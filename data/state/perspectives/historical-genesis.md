# Historical Genesis

## Summary

Ask how this problem or concept came to exist — what prior problem it was solving, what it replaced, and what it inherited without examination. Origin stories reveal hidden assumptions that have calcified into invisible constraints.

## Full Text

Every concept, theory, and problem formulation has a history. It was invented by someone, in a particular context, to solve a particular problem. Understanding that genesis reveals what the concept was designed to do — and what it was never designed to do, but is now being asked to do anyway.

This matters because inherited frameworks carry invisible constraints. A concept that was useful for one purpose gets extended to new domains, and the original constraints travel with it even when they no longer apply. The history makes those constraints visible.

Applied to a problem, this perspective asks:

- When did this problem first appear in its current form? What problem was being solved before it was formulated this way?
- What did this concept or framework replace? What was lost in the replacement, and what was gained?
- Who had an interest in framing the problem this way? What would the problem look like from the perspective of someone who found this framing inconvenient?
- What was assumed to be constant when this concept was developed that might now vary?
- Is the current formulation of the problem an answer to a question that is no longer being asked?

The discipline is not to commit the genetic fallacy — an idea is not wrong because of its origin. But origins are informative about scope. A framework developed to explain one class of phenomena may be silently assuming features of that class when applied elsewhere.

Historical genesis is most useful when a problem feels stuck, when the same debate recurs without progress, or when the terms of debate feel fixed in ways that are hard to question from inside them.
