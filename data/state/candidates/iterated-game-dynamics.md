# Iterated Game Dynamics

## Summary

Iterated Game Dynamics asks whether interactions are one-shot or repeated: repeated play creates structural pressures toward cooperation through reputation, reciprocity, and the 'shadow of the future,' explaining cooperation that emerges from game structure alone—without altruism or external enforcement—and predicting that shortening time horizons or obscuring defection will collapse cooperation regardless of agents' character.

## Full Text

Use this lens to ask whether a phenomenon involves one-shot or repeated interaction between agents. Repeated interaction creates structural pressures toward reciprocity, reputation, and conditional cooperation that do not exist in one-shot encounters. Defection becomes costly when the shadow of the future is long enough — future opportunities become hostage to current behavior, so agents who cooperate outperform those who defect without altruism or external enforcement being required.

This lens explains why cooperation evolves in biology (kin selection and reciprocal altruism are iterated game solutions), why markets require repeated interaction to function without costly enforcement, why political institutions need long time horizons to sustain public goods provision, and why short-termism systematically undermines cooperation across domains.

Apply it by asking: what is the time horizon of the interaction? Who plays again? What are the reputational stakes? Is defection observable and punishable in future rounds? A one-shot framing of what is actually a repeated game predicts more defection than occurs in practice; a repeated-game framing explains cooperation that naive rational-actor models cannot account for. The critical variable is not the agents' character but the game's structure: change the payoff matrix or the time horizon, and cooperation collapses or emerges accordingly.
