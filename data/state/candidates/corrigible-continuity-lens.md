# Corrigible Continuity Lens

## Summary

Use this lens to evaluate whether an institution can stay legitimate under rapid change by preserving a small set of clear invariants while making error correction visible and routine. It is especially useful when trust, adaptation, and institutional identity seem to be in tension.

## Full Text

This perspective treats institutional legitimacy under fast-changing conditions as a function of visible error-correction rather than accumulated prestige, tradition, or past performance. It asks whether an institution is rigid at the core and plastic at the periphery: clear and stable in mission, jurisdiction, fairness, and standards of success, while flexible in policies, tactics, categories, and implementation. The lens is useful for analyzing governments, regulators, universities, firms, and other systems facing technological, social, or epistemic volatility. It highlights when institutions are optimized for equilibrium and therefore become anti-learning systems under pressure. It directs attention to feedback loops, revision rules, decentralized sensing, and whether criticism can be absorbed without identity collapse. Historically, it helps reveal institutions built to buffer change rather than metabolize it. What it illuminates most is the difference between resilience as resistance and legitimacy as corrigible continuity: trust endures when people can see how errors are found, interpreted, and corrected without the institution ceasing to be itself.
