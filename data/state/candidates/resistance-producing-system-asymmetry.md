# Resistance Producing System Asymmetry

## Summary

Antibiotic failure outpaces antibiotic discovery because the system is structured to generate resistance faster than it generates durable new treatments. Resistance is driven by fast reinforcing selection and transmission loops, while antibiotic innovation is slowed by costly development, stewardship constrained sales, weak revenues, and centralized bottlenecks.

## Full Text

Antibiotic failure outpaces antibiotic discovery because the surrounding system is structurally optimized to produce resistance faster than it is optimized to produce durable treatment options. On the resistance side, the stock is the global reservoir of resistance genes and resistant organisms, with large continuous inflows from antibiotic exposure in humans, animals, and the environment, plus transmission and horizontal gene transfer. This forms a reinforcing loop: more antibiotic use selects more resistance, treatment failure leads to broader or longer use, and that selects still more resistance. On the innovation side, the stock is the pipeline of effective new antibiotics, but its inflows are slow, costly, and fragile because discovery is hard, trials are expensive, stewardship limits volume, and reserve use weakens revenue. That creates a balancing loop that suppresses investment. The result is a structural mismatch: resistance evolves through decentralized, massively parallel experimentation, while drug development depends on centralized, sequential, capital constrained institutions. Therefore faster discovery alone is low leverage unless the resistance generating structure is changed.
