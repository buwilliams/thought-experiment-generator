## Summary

Use this lens to distinguish capability bottlenecks from epistemic bottlenecks: once a system can generate many plausible hypotheses, durable knowledge growth depends more on evaluation and promotion structures than on additional scale. Ask whether progress is constrained by substrate capacity, or by the rules that detect error, apply criticism, and retain corrections.

## Full Text

This perspective treats epistemic progress as a systems problem of variation, criticism, and selective retention. It says that once an AI system has enough substrate capacity to generate many candidate representations, the main bottleneck shifts from raw capability to structure: the evaluation mechanisms that expose error and the promotion rules that decide what survives, spreads, or gets optimized further. More compute expands the space of possible outputs, but does not by itself create a stronger gradient toward truth.

Use this lens when assessing AI research strategy, model scaling, benchmark design, training pipelines, or organizational learning systems. It is especially useful for diagnosing why systems become more fluent or capable without becoming more reliable, explanatory, or truth-tracking. The key question is not just “can the system generate ideas?” but “what transforms ideas into criticized, corrected, retained knowledge?”

This perspective illuminates the difference between intelligence and knowledge, between search volume and error elimination, and between scaling a generator and improving a truth-seeking process. Its main leverage point is redesigning feedback loops, adversarial testing, and retention criteria so that criticism, not mere production, governs progress.
