## Full Text

This perspective treats an explanation as a structure of dependencies rather than a pile of details. Its central question is: which parts are load-bearing? To find out, systematically vary the explanation’s components—remove one, invert one, or swap one for an opposite or near-equivalent—and observe what breaks. If the explanation still works with little loss, that part was likely descriptive decoration or an implementation detail. If changing it forces broad revision in what the explanation predicts, entails, or rules out, it is structurally necessary.

Use this lens when assessing theories, causal stories, strategic plans, product rationales, policy arguments, or scientific models. It is especially useful for separating genuine mechanism from storytelling, and for locating where the real explanatory burden sits. The perspective highlights dependency density, counterfactual sensitivity, exclusion power, and compression with necessity. It illuminates an explanation’s constraint architecture: not just why something happens, but why nearby alternatives do not. What survives variation is incidental; what resists variation is structural.
