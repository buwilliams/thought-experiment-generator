# Discovered: Run 001

## Summary

Use this lens to shift debates about AI from “does it merely rearrange tokens or truly understand?” to “can it participate in error-correction?” It treats knowledge as explanatory content that improves through conjecture, criticism, and revision, not as something determined by a system’s training origin.

## Full Text

This perspective treats the key divide in intelligence not as memory vs understanding, or rearrangement vs knowledge, but as correlation reproduction vs participation in error-correction. It suggests that asking whether a token-predicting system can “really know” inherits a misleading frame from older debates about symbol manipulation and stored information. The more useful question is whether a system can generate candidate explanations, encounter counterexamples, preserve criticism, compare alternatives, and improve.

Use this lens when evaluating AI systems, research programs, institutions, or workflows that produce plausible outputs but may lack mechanisms for noticing and correcting failure. It helps distinguish systems that mirror past regularities from systems embedded in cycles of conjecture, test, revision, and learning. It also redirects design attention: instead of demanding some vague essence of “understanding,” examine the surrounding structure of feedback loops, criticism, memory of errors, and revision. What this perspective illuminates is that a mechanism like token prediction does not determine epistemic destiny; knowledge grows where outputs are exposed to error-correction and selected for explanatory resilience.
