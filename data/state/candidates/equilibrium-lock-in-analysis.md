# Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis

## Summary

Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis identifies structural traps where no single agent can improve their outcome by acting alone, explaining why systems resist reform even when all parties prefer change — and distinguishes these Nash equilibrium constraints from contingent failures that better decisions could fix.

## Full Text

Use this lens to identify what cannot change in a system without coordinated deviation by multiple agents simultaneously. Nash equilibria — positions from which no agent benefits from unilateral departure — reveal structural lock-ins that persist even when all parties prefer a different outcome. Coordination failures, regulatory capture, political gridlock, and institutional stagnation are often not failures of will or information but equilibrium traps: each agent's best response, given what others are doing, is to maintain the status quo.

This lens is useful for explaining why systems resist reform despite widespread dissatisfaction, why dominant norms persist without enforcement, why markets fail to provide public goods even when participants want them, and why races to the bottom in standards occur even among competitors who would prefer higher standards. It distinguishes structural inevitabilities (equilibria that persist under rational agency) from contingent failures (which could be fixed by better decisions within the current structure).

Apply it by mapping the payoff structure: what does each agent do if everyone else maintains the current pattern? If the answer is the same, you have identified an equilibrium. The interesting diagnostic question is then: what kind of shock — a change in payoffs, new information, external coordination, or a change in who the agents are — could shift the system to a different equilibrium? Dominant strategies are especially diagnostic: if an agent has a dominant strategy regardless of others' choices, that behavior is structurally inevitable, not a matter of character or culture.
