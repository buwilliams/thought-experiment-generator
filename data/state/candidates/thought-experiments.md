# Thought Experiments

## Summary

Construct a hypothetical scenario that isolates the key variable of the problem, assume it is real, and follow the logic strictly to its conclusion — not to where you expect it to go, but where it actually leads.

## Full Text

A thought experiment is not a metaphor or an analogy. It is a rigorous logical operation: you construct a hypothetical situation, stipulate its conditions, and then reason from those conditions as though they were actual. The power comes from isolation — by controlling all variables except one, you force the problem to reveal its structure.

Einstein did not imagine riding a beam of light to be poetic. He did it because it produced a logical contradiction with Maxwell's equations, and that contradiction was the finding. The strangeness of the scenario was load-bearing.

Applied to a problem, this perspective asks:

- What scenario, if stipulated as real, would make the problem impossible to ignore?
- What are the minimum conditions needed to make the scenario work? Stipulate only those.
- If you follow the logic of those conditions strictly — not toward a comfortable conclusion, but wherever the premises lead — what do you arrive at?
- What does that arrival imply about the problem outside the thought experiment?

The discipline is to not normalize the scenario. When the logic leads somewhere strange, that strangeness is information. Resist the urge to explain it away. Ask instead what it would mean if the strange thing were true.

Thought experiments are most useful when a problem seems to resist direct analysis — when the concepts involved are too abstract, too large, or too entangled to examine directly. The hypothetical gives you leverage.
