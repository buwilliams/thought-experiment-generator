# Bayesian Inference Structure

## Summary

Bayesian inference structure distinguishes P(data|hypothesis) from P(hypothesis|data) — the direction of the conditional matters, and every inference implicitly assumes a prior. Use this lens to check whether an argument correctly reverses the conditional and to surface hidden base-rate assumptions that determine whether apparent evidence is actually informative.

## Full Text

The fundamental question of inference is not "how probable is this data?" but "how probable is my hypothesis given this data?" These questions have different structures, and conflating them — treating the probability of data given a hypothesis as direct evidence about the probability of the hypothesis given the data — is the source of systematic errors in reasoning across science, medicine, law, and everyday judgment. The correct structure is Bayesian: posterior probability is proportional to likelihood times prior. Every inference implicitly involves a prior, whether stated or hidden.

Priors are not a weakness of this framework but its central strength: they make background assumptions explicit and updateable, rather than concealed and frozen. A p-value answers the question "how surprising is this data if the null hypothesis were true?" — it does not answer "how likely is the null hypothesis given this data?" Treating statistical significance as evidence about hypothesis probability commits what might be called the inverse probability error: reading a forward conditional as if it ran in reverse.

Use this lens to ask: which direction does the conditional probability run in this argument? What prior is this inference implicitly assuming, and what would a different prior imply? When a study reports a significant result, ask: significant given what null, and with what base rate for the phenomenon being studied? The base rate matters enormously — in a domain where true effects are rare, even a low false-positive rate produces mostly false positives among significant results. This lens applies wherever evidence is evaluated: legal reasoning, medical diagnosis, scientific inference, forecasting, and everyday belief revision. The test is whether the argument correctly reverses the conditional.
