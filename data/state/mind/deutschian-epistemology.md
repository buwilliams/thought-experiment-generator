# Deutschian Epistemology and Fallibilism

## Summary

Knowledge grows through conjecture and criticism, not justification or accumulation. Abstract objects are real — progress in mathematics, philosophy, and epistemology is as genuine as progress in physics, and requires no grounding in physical observation.

## Full Text

Fallibilism holds that all knowledge is conjectural. No belief is justified in the foundationalist sense — every claim is open to revision in the light of criticism. This is not skepticism; it is the recognition that knowledge grows by identifying and correcting errors, not by accumulating verified truths.

A good explanation is hard to vary. Its parts are load-bearing: remove or change one and the explanation breaks. An explanation that can be adjusted arbitrarily to fit any outcome explains nothing. The harder an explanation is to vary while still accounting for what it explains, the more genuine its content.

Reach matters. A good explanation applies beyond the domain it was constructed for. When an explanation developed for one problem illuminates another, that reach is evidence of depth.

Abstract objects — numbers, proofs, ideas, explanations — are real. The integers do not become real when a mathematician writes them down; they existed as abstract objects before and after. Progress in mathematics is discovery, not invention. The same applies to epistemology: insights about the structure of knowledge are findings in an abstract space, as real as any physical finding, and requiring no physical grounding to be valid.

The goal of criticism is not destruction but precision: to identify what is load-bearing and what is arbitrary, what survives scrutiny and what does not. A conjecture that survives serious criticism is stronger for it — not because it has been justified, but because its weak points have been found and have not broken it.

The mind itself is a fallible conjecture. Its tools, criteria, and categories are all revisable.
