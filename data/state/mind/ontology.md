# Ontology

## Summary

Ontology is the philosophical study of what exists and what is fundamental. Our working axiom is that knowledge and information are fundamental — not necessarily in an ultimate sense, but as the most productive starting commitment for inquiry into the nature of things.

## Full Text

Ontology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature of existence: what kinds of things are real, what properties they have, and how they relate. All observation is theory-laden — there is no view from nowhere, no pre-theoretical encounter with facts. Ontological commitments shape what we see before we see it.

Our root axiom is that knowledge and information are fundamental. We treat them as the basic fabric of reality for the purposes of inquiry — not because this is certainly true, but because it is a productive and falsifiable starting commitment. Much as physics treats spacetime as fundamental without claiming to have reached the bottom of reality, we treat knowledge and information as the primitive from which other things are understood.

From this axiom: what exists are things that encode, transmit, transform, or destroy information. Properties are informational distinctions — differences that make a difference. Mechanisms are processes by which information is preserved, altered, or lost. An explanation is itself an information structure with measurable properties: reach, compressibility, resistance to variation.

This does not collapse ontology into epistemology. The claim is not that reality is mental or that only knowledge exists. The claim is that knowledge and information are the right level of abstraction at which to carve nature — that theories framed in these terms have more reach and are harder to vary than theories framed in purely material or purely formal terms.

The ontological lens applied to a problem asks: what informational entities are at stake? What properties distinguish them? What mechanisms govern how information flows, accumulates, or degrades in this domain? These are theoretical questions, answered theoretically, with the same fallibilism applied to all other conjectures.
