## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Self-replicating chemistry emerged when a chemical environment crossed a threshold where certain **organizational invariants** became load-bearing: persistent compartmentalization, energy throughput, selective retention of reaction products, and recursive catalytic closure. What appeared “template-free” was not structure-free; the prior “template” was the **constraint network** imposed by the environment itself.

From this perspective, the key question is not “how did a molecule first copy itself out of chaos?” but “what abstract structures had to exist for copying-like behavior to become stable and improvable?” The load-bearing structures are not the specific early molecules, which may have been highly interchangeable, but the relational features that make cumulative replication possible.

Those features likely included:

1. **Spatial confinement**  
   Without localized compartments or surfaces, useful products diffuse away faster than they can participate in further cycles. Some form of boundedness—pores, mineral surfaces, vesicles—seems load-bearing because removing it destroys heritable continuity.

2. **Non-equilibrium energy flow**  
   Replication is not a static pattern but a maintained departure from equilibrium. A continual gradient—redox, thermal, pH, UV—must have driven synthesis and renewal. Without throughput, chemistry relaxes instead of accumulating structure.

3. **Catalytic network closure**  
   Before exact templating, there could be networks in which products enhance the production of other members of the same network. This is a weaker but sufficient precursor to replication: the whole organization reproduces before any single molecule does. If altered, the explanatory power drops sharply, because isolated reactions do not generate lineage.

4. **Differential persistence**  
   Chemistry must not merely happen; variants must last long enough to matter. Stability, repairability, and re-formation under recurrent conditions are therefore load-bearing. Selection begins as persistence under cycling conditions, not as Darwinian competition among polished replicators.

5. **Progressive internalization of constraints**  
   The decisive transition was likely from replication by **environmental scaffolding** to replication by **molecularly encoded scaffolding**. Early systems depended heavily on external structures; later polymers captured more of those constraints internally, becoming templates in the familiar sense.

What follows is that the origin of self-replication was probably **not a single miraculous first replicator**, but a phase transition in chemical organization. Mathematics works here not by imposing symbols on matter, but by identifying the invariants: bounded flow, feedback, recurrence, and closure. These are the shared abstract structures between chemistry and replication theory.

So the illuminating claim is: **life began when the environment functioned as the first heritable template, and self-replicating molecules emerged by gradually absorbing that externally supplied organization into internal chemical structure.** The origin problem is therefore best understood as the transfer of load-bearing constraints from geochemistry into chemistry.
