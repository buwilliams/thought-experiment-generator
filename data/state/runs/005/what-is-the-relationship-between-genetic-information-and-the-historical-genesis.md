## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The apparent mismatch between genome size/gene count and organismal complexity exists because “genetic information” is not best understood as a complete blueprint for the organism, but as one component in a historically layered control system that evolved to solve earlier problems of reliable reproduction, regulation, and adaptation. Complexity in organisms therefore depends less on the amount of encoded sequence than on how inherited information is *used, regulated, and embedded* in developmental and cellular systems that predate any individual genome.

This perspective becomes clearer if we ask: what problem did genomes originally solve? Not “encode a complex organism,” but more basic problems: preserving successful chemical constraints across generations, coordinating metabolism, and enabling replication with variation. Early hereditary systems were solutions to continuity, not to architectural completeness. As multicellular organisms evolved, they did not replace this older function with a clean “program for building a body.” Instead, they accumulated layers: regulatory networks, cell–cell signaling, developmental pathways, chromatin dynamics, spatial organization in the egg, and environmental triggers.

So the modern assumption that more complex organisms should require proportionally more genetic information inherits an unexamined metaphor from engineering and literacy: the genome as a detailed specification. But that metaphor was imported after the fact. Historically, genomes were recruited into increasingly elaborate systems that already had path-dependent constraints. What they encode is often not complexity directly, but *rules for modulating pre-existing processes*.

That helps explain why gene number correlates weakly with organismal complexity. A small set of genes, if embedded in richly recursive regulatory circuits and developmental contexts, can generate large phenotypic complexity. Conversely, large genomes may contain repetitive, vestigial, or weakly constrained elements inherited from prior evolutionary episodes, because evolution modifies what exists rather than redesigning from scratch. Genome size therefore reflects not just functional specification, but history.

What follows is that “genetic information” must be treated as relational rather than absolute. Its informational content depends on the interpreter system: transcriptional machinery, epigenetic marks, cellular architecture, maternal contributions, and ecological context. The organism is not encoded by DNA alone; it is reconstructed by a lineage-specific developmental system in which DNA is a stable, central, but non-sovereign component.

The hidden assumption exposed by the origin-story lens is that encoding and construction are the same kind of thing. They are not. Genes are better understood as interventions in a self-organizing inherited process than as a one-to-one description of the final form.

So the relationship is this: genetic information constrains and channels organismal complexity, but does not scale with it in any simple quantitative way, because complexity resides in the evolved organization of the whole developmental system, of which the genome is only one historically accreted layer.
