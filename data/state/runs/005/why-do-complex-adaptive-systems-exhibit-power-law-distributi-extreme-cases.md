## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law and scale-free behavior when their generative rules remain meaningful under repeated extension across scales — especially near boundaries of **vanishing frequency** and **unbounded size**. A power law appears when the system has no intrinsic characteristic scale for allocating growth, failure, attention, or connectivity, so the same structural rule keeps applying as quantities approach zero or infinity.

Push the problem to the edge:

- At the **small end**, many events, agents, or links are barely consequential.
- At the **large end**, a few become enormous, dominant, or highly connected.
- The question is: what kind of mechanism can govern both extremes without switching rulebooks?

A Gaussian world cannot. It presupposes a characteristic scale and rapidly suppresses extremes. A power law does the opposite: it allows the tail to remain structurally continuous with the body. The rare giant event is not a different kind of thing; it is the same process continued farther.

This suggests the key explanatory constraint: **scale-invariant update rules**. If the probability of gaining resources, links, influence, or survival depends primarily on what one already has *in relative terms*, then multiplying size does not change the logic of interaction. Preferential attachment is one example, but the deeper point is broader: whenever adaptation is driven by feedback that is insensitive to absolute scale, repeated iteration produces distributions with no privileged size.

Why should complex adaptive systems drift toward such rules? Because they are often operating near boundaries where local optimization fails:

- finite agents competing over open-ended opportunity,
- many small perturbations with occasional cascades,
- bounded resources but unbounded combinatorial possibilities,
- continual selection among entities whose future depends on accumulated past success.

At these boundaries, systems that encode a fixed “typical” scale are brittle. New scales of organization or disruption appear and break the model. Systems that survive are those whose rules can recurse across levels: node to cluster, event to cascade, adaptation to meta-adaptation. Power laws are then not merely statistical accidents but traces of a deeper invariance: **the system can propagate structure across magnitudes without needing a new explanatory mechanism at each magnitude**.

What follows:

1. **Power laws signal scale-recursive feedback**, not just heterogeneity.
2. **Scale-free behavior is evidence of missing characteristic scale in the governing interaction rule**, even if physical constraints eventually truncate the tail.
3. **Breakdowns from power-law to exponential or log-normal tails identify real boundaries** — capacity limits, saturation, regulation, modular compartmentalization, or timescale separation.
4. Therefore, the right question is not “why do big events happen?” but “why does the system permit the same adaptive logic to persist from the smallest events to the largest?”

So the boundary reveals the structure: complex adaptive systems look scale-free when their causal grammar is itself scale-indifferent, and the observed power law is the footprint of that invariance under growth, selection, and cascading feedback.
