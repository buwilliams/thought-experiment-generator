## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The complexity of an organism is not determined mainly by the raw amount of genetic information it contains, but by the structure of the error-correcting developmental system built on top of that information.

Genes are better understood as part of a control architecture than as a literal blueprint whose size should scale with organismal complexity. The familiar puzzle — that genome size and even gene count correlate poorly with apparent biological complexity — is exactly what we should expect if progress in living systems comes less from adding more encoded bits and more from improving how those bits are interpreted, reused, regulated, and corrected across development.

What matters, then, is not just **capacity** but **inquiry-like structure**: mechanisms that generate possibilities, test them locally, suppress failures, stabilize successes, and preserve useful results. In biology, this appears as gene regulation, feedback loops, modular developmental programs, cell signaling, redundancy, repair, and selection at multiple levels. A relatively compact genome can produce a highly complex organism if it specifies a rich system for conditional expression and recursive refinement. The leverage lies in the organization of these processes, not in genome length alone.

This reframes “genetic information” itself. Much of what an organism becomes is not explicitly itemized in DNA. It emerges from interactions among genes, cellular machinery, physical constraints, and environmental inputs, all under a regime that is robust to error. Development is less like reading off a static description and more like executing a layered process that can correct, canalize, and coordinate outcomes despite noise. Complexity therefore depends heavily on the **explanatory depth** of the developmental program: how many differentiated structures and adaptive responses can be generated from a reusable set of rules.

A load-bearing implication is that if you changed only genome size while holding regulatory architecture fixed, complexity would not reliably increase. But if you altered the structure of regulation, memory, and correction — for example by enabling new forms of modular reuse or more reliable cell-type stabilization — complexity could increase dramatically without a proportional rise in raw sequence content. That asymmetry shows which details are explanatory and which are not.

So the relationship is real but indirect: genetic information constrains organismal complexity, yet complexity scales chiefly with how that information participates in a system that generates and corrects developmental “conjectures.” DNA provides the inherited, compressible basis; the organism’s complexity comes from the higher-order machinery that interprets and iteratively stabilizes form. In short, biology’s leverage is not more code, but better structured use of code.
