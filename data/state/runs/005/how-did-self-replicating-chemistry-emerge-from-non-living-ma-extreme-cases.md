## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If you push prebiotic chemistry to the boundary condition of **near-zero prior specificity**—no template, no code, no finely tuned sequence—then the first “self-replicators” were probably **not discrete molecules copying themselves**, but **whole chemical regimes that reproduced their own boundary conditions**. In other words, replication began as the recurrence of a **self-maintaining dynamical organization**, and only later narrowed into templated molecular copying.

At the limit of “no prior template,” asking how a specific polymer first copied itself is likely the wrong unit of analysis. A lone molecule in a well-mixed soup faces the maximal problem: dilution, side reactions, degradation, and combinatorial explosion. Pushed to the extreme, that scenario reveals its hidden assumption: it presupposes the very thing to be explained—localized, repeated, constrained interaction. Without those constraints, chemistry diffuses faster than it remembers.

So the relevant boundary is not molecule/non-molecule but **chemistry with memory vs chemistry without memory**. Memory appears when matter is arranged so that its present state **biases the production of similar future states**. That can happen before sequence templates exist. Examples in principle include mineral pores, wet-dry cycles, redox gradients, or lipid compartments: structures that trap products, recycle intermediates, and feed back on reaction rates. These systems do not “copy” a molecule; they **copy a context**.

What follows is that the earliest heredity was likely **distributed and analog**, not digital. The inherited thing was a pattern of concentrations, catalytic biases, compartment properties, and environmental coupling. A protocell or reaction network “reproduced” when growth and division carried forward enough of that organization to re-establish the same regime. Selection could act on such regimes even if no single component was faithfully replicated.

At the opposite limit—maximum fidelity, digital code, gene-like templates—we see enormous error-correction and elaborate translation machinery. That edge reveals that modern replication is not primitive simplicity but a highly evolved compression of prior chemical organization into symbolic form. The boundary case suggests a trajectory: **from environmental scaffolds, to autocatalytic networks, to compartmentalized regimes, to templated polymers, to digital heredity**.

The load-bearing claim is this: **self-replication did not emerge by a miraculous first template appearing in inert matter; it emerged when certain chemical systems crossed a threshold where they could regenerate the conditions of their own persistence faster than those conditions decayed.** Once a regime could reproduce its own constraints, increasingly precise molecular roles became selectable. Templates were not the start of heredity; they were an optimization of an older, looser kind of heredity already present in organized chemistry.

So the illumination from the boundary is: life began not with “a molecule that copied itself,” but with **matter that learned to make its own context recur**.
