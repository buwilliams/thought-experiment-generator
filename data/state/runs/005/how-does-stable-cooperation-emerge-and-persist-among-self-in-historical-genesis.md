## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation without central enforcement emerges not because self-interest is overcome, but because “self-interest” itself gets redefined by inherited social and informational structures that evolved to solve the prior problem of repeated vulnerability.

The origin-story lens shifts the question. “How can selfish agents cooperate?” already inherits a misleading picture: isolated individuals facing one-off dilemmas. But that framing is historically downstream of a deeper problem: agents who were repeatedly exposed to predation, uncertainty, and dependence needed ways to make one another’s future behavior more predictable. Cooperation arose as a solution to the problem of **managing interdependence over time**, not as a miraculous exception to selfishness.

What did this replace? It replaced unstable arrangements based on immediate force, evasion, or opportunism alone. Those strategies work locally but destroy the informational conditions needed for ongoing exchange. In repeated settings, agents need not become altruistic; they need mechanisms that convert today’s action into tomorrow’s expectations. Reputation, signaling, reciprocity, shared norms, role obligations, and punishment by peers are inherited solutions to that prior problem.

What was inherited without examination? The modern formulation often assumes:
1. agents are fundamentally atomized,
2. preferences are fixed and private,
3. enforcement must be centralized to count as real enforcement.

These assumptions hide the fact that decentralized cooperation usually depends on **distributed enforcement systems** already embedded in interaction: memory, observability, network structure, norm transmission, and exclusion from future benefits. These are not decorative add-ons; they are load-bearing. Remove shared memory, for example, and reputation collapses. Remove repeated interaction and reciprocity loses grip. Remove legible signals and agents cannot distinguish cooperators from exploiters. Stable cooperation persists when the structure makes defection informationally expensive and future access valuable.

So the key explanatory move is this: cooperation persists when institutions—formal or informal—alter the payoff landscape by changing what counts as rational self-interest. A cooperator in such a system is not “being nice against their interests”; they are acting within an ecology where maintaining trust, eligibility, and interpretability is part of their interest.

What follows is that the persistence of cooperation is less about moral transformation than about the historical accumulation of **informational infrastructures**. Trust is not a feeling but a social technology; norms are compressed knowledge about which behaviors preserve mutual predictability. Where cooperation fails, the cause is often not excess selfishness but the erosion of the inherited structures that made long-run self-interest legible.

Illumination: the real contrast is not selfishness versus cooperation, but **thin, short-horizon self-interest** versus **structurally extended self-interest**. Stable cooperation emerges when systems evolve that force agents to live with the consequences of being known.
