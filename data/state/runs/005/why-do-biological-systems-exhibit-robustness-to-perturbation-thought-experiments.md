## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Suppose the key variable is **modularity of information-processing**: biological systems are built from semi-independent subsystems whose internal interactions are dense, but whose external interfaces are sparse and standardized. Assume this is real and ask what follows.

If perturbations occur, modularity localizes damage. A mutation, environmental shock, or developmental fluctuation mostly propagates within the affected module rather than across the whole organism. Robustness follows not because the system is rigid, but because its causal architecture contains failure. Redundancy, feedback control, buffering, and repair can then operate within modules at manageable scale.

Now follow the same structure under variation. Because interfaces are constrained but internals are relatively free, a module can change substantially without requiring compensating redesign everywhere else. That means mutations can accumulate behind a stable interface. Most changes are neutral or deleterious, but some generate new capacities while preserving system viability. Evolvability follows from the same feature that produced robustness: perturbations are prevented from becoming global catastrophes, so exploration is possible.

This suggests the apparent tension between robustness and evolvability is partly mistaken. They are not opposites to be traded off directly. Under a non-modular architecture, they do conflict: if every part depends intricately on every other, robustness requires freezing the whole, and change is usually destructive. But under modular organization, robustness and evolvability become complementary consequences of the same structural fact. Robustness protects ongoing function; that protected function creates the slack in which variation can be tested.

The deeper claim is that evolution selects not merely for traits, but for **architectures of variation**. Systems that survive long-term are those whose organization makes many perturbations non-fatal and many modifications non-global. This includes gene regulatory networks, protein domains, developmental programs, immune repertoires, and ecological symbioses. In each case, the load-bearing feature is not “backup parts” alone, but constrained interfaces plus internal freedom.

A further implication follows. The most evolvable systems should not be maximally robust in the sense of resisting all change. They should be **selectively robust**: stable against perturbations at the level of organismal function, yet permissive of hidden or compartmentalized variation underneath. Canalization on the outside, plasticity or degeneracy on the inside.

So the conjecture is:

**Biological systems are robust while remaining evolvable because evolution preferentially builds hierarchically modular architectures with buffered function and constrained interfaces, allowing perturbations to be absorbed locally while enabling internal variation to accumulate and occasionally reconfigure into adaptive novelty.**

If this is right, robustness and evolvability are not two properties to be balanced after the fact. They are co-products of a particular causal organization of biological information.
