## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges not primarily from altruistic preferences or raw cognitive capacity, but from the presence of **error-correcting social structures** that make defection easier to detect, punish, remember, and route around than cooperation is to exploit.

From this lens, the key question is not “why would selfish agents cooperate?” but “what institutional and informational arrangements cause mistaken trust, unnoticed cheating, and repeated coordination failure to be corrected faster than they accumulate?” Cooperation persists when a group builds a system that continuously improves at answering that question.

What matters, then, is a layered structure:

1. **Conjecture generation:** Agents try strategies for exchange, signaling, reciprocity, coalition-building, and norm formation.
2. **Criticism:** Others test these strategies through repeated interaction, comparison, gossip, auditing, reputation, and observation of outcomes.
3. **Memory:** Information about past behavior persists in reputations, records, norms, and shared stories.
4. **Selection:** Agents preferentially interact with reliable partners and exclude or discount defectors.
5. **Correction:** Norms, sanctions, forgiveness rules, and institutional adaptations update the cooperative regime when exploitation appears.

Stable cooperation is therefore best understood as a **knowledge system**, not just an incentive equilibrium. A cooperative order survives when it contains enough distributed knowledge to identify who is trustworthy, under what conditions, using which signals, and how to respond when those signals fail.

This shifts the explanatory burden. Repeated games, kinship, and mutual gain are enablers, but they are not the deepest leverage. The leverage lies in structures that reduce the cost of criticism and increase the fidelity of social memory: transparent actions, legible commitments, decentralized reputation, modular punishment, and norms that distinguish error from betrayal. These make cooperation self-repairing.

What follows is that cooperation will fail less from “too much selfishness” than from **epistemic breakdown**: when agents cannot tell who defected, cannot preserve records, cannot coordinate on sanctions, or cannot revise bad norms. In such conditions, defection is not merely tempting; it is systematically undiscoverable or uncorrectable.

So the persistence of cooperation without central enforcement depends on whether the group has evolved a structure in which:
- cooperative behavior is visible enough to be rewarded,
- defection is legible enough to be criticized,
- reputational knowledge is durable enough to inform future choice,
- and rules are revisable enough to close newly discovered loopholes.

In short: **cooperation among self-interested agents is an emergent product of distributed error correction.** Self-interest does not prevent cooperation; it supplies the motive to participate in systems that reliably identify good partners and suppress exploitative strategies. Where such systems improve, cooperation scales. Where they decay, cooperation collapses back into noise, fear, or predation.
