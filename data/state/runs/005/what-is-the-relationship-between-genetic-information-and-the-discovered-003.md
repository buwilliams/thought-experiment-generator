## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is not one of direct specification but of **constraint architecture**: genes are load-bearing insofar as they encode stable interfaces, regulatory invariants, and error-correcting developmental procedures that make complex organisms reproducible. Organismal complexity therefore depends less on genome size or raw sequence content than on the structure of the **information-processing system** in which genetic information participates.

From this perspective, the key abstract structure is not “DNA as blueprint” but **multi-level control under feedback**. If we change the idea that genes primarily specify parts, many observations become easier to explain: why genome size correlates weakly with apparent complexity; why small regulatory changes can have large morphological consequences; why development is robust despite noise; and why similar gene sets can support very different organisms. These are signs that what is load-bearing is a system of **regulatory constraints**, not a one-to-one list of encoded features.

Genetic information matters because it helps instantiate the invariants that development must preserve: timing, spatial differentiation, cell identity, repair, and adaptive responsiveness. A complex organism is one in which many subsystems must coordinate across scales without collapsing into error. The genome contributes to this not by containing a detailed description of the final form, but by encoding a generative process that recruits cellular machinery, environmental inputs, and developmental history into a constrained unfolding. Complexity is thus distributed across genome, epigenetic state, cellular context, and organism-environment interaction, but the genome remains load-bearing where it defines the reusable rules and interfaces that keep this whole process coherent.

What follows is that increases in organismal complexity should correlate most strongly with increases in **regulatory depth, modularity, and combinatorial reuse**, rather than simply with more genes. Information that is causally central will often be the information that controls when, where, and in what context other information is expressed. In systems terms, complexity grows when evolution discovers architectures with richer feedback loops and more powerful forms of hierarchical control.

This also reframes heredity. What is inherited is not just sequence, but access to a developmental system whose abstract organization is partly stabilized by sequence. Genes are load-bearing where altering them forces compensatory changes across many levels of the system; those are the points where abstract structure is real and explanatory. So the right relationship is: **genetic information sets the conserved constraints that make biological complexity generable, but organismal complexity itself emerges from the organized interaction of that information with larger developmental and ecological systems.**
