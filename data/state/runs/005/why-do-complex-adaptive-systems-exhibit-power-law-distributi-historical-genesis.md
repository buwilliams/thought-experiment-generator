## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Power laws in complex adaptive systems are often not “natural signatures of complexity” in the abstract, but historical residues of a more specific problem: how to keep growing, allocating attention, and coordinating under ignorance without requiring global optimization. Scale-free structure is what remains when systems repeatedly solve the problem of **where to attach next** using cheap local rules that reuse past success as evidence.

**Origin-story lens:**  
Ask what problem a power-law-generating rule solved. In many domains, the predecessor state is not a scale-free network but a coordination bottleneck: too many possible connections, too little information, too much cost to evaluate all options. A system facing continual growth must answer questions like: Which node gets the next link? Which idea gets the next citation? Which site gets the next visit? Which firm gets the next partner? Exhaustive comparison is impossible. So systems adopt a compressed heuristic: attach to what is already visible, connected, used, or trusted.

That heuristic replaces a harder one: assessing intrinsic quality directly. It inherits an unexamined assumption from earlier survival problems — that **past accumulation is an adequate proxy for future value**. Once embedded, this assumption becomes structural. New entrants are filtered through existing visibility; feedback amplifies prior gains; the system no longer merely reflects differences but manufactures them.

What follows is not just skew but a characteristic kind of skew. If growth continues while attachment is biased by existing degree, then multiplicative advantage compounds across scales. The resulting power law is a historical trace of the system having solved local search and coordination by outsourcing judgment to prior popularity.

This explains why scale-free behavior appears across domains with very different contents but similar growth constraints. The load-bearing detail is not “complexity” alone; it is the conjunction of:

1. **Ongoing entry or growth**,  
2. **Local decision rules under bounded knowledge**, and  
3. **Feedback that treats accumulated connections as information**.

Change any of these and the explanation changes. Remove growth and the tail can saturate. Remove feedback and the distribution tends toward thinner-tailed forms. Replace popularity-proxy rules with costly global evaluation and scale-free structure weakens or disappears.

So the deeper illumination is this: power laws are often less about an eternal law of self-organization than about a path-dependent solution to an epistemic problem. Complex adaptive systems become scale-free because they inherit a historically successful but crude answer to uncertainty: **follow what has already been selected**. The distribution is the visible fossil of that rule.
