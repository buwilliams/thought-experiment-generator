## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law distributions when their generative rules preserve a small set of scale-invariant abstract structures: cumulative advantage, heterogeneous local interaction, and continual variation under feedback. These are the load-bearing features. “Power laws” are not mainly a surface regularity to be explained by this or that domain-specific mechanism, but the visible trace of systems whose dynamics do not privilege a characteristic scale.

From this perspective, the key question is not “why do many different systems happen to produce the same distribution?” but “what structural constraints must remain intact for scale-free outcomes to survive explanation?” Several candidates are interchangeable; a few are not.

What appears load-bearing:

1. **Positive feedback on prior success**  
   Growth rates depend on current size, connectivity, fitness, or attention. This “rich-get-richer” form is load-bearing because removing it usually introduces a characteristic scale and collapses the heavy tail.

2. **Ongoing entry, mutation, or perturbation**  
   New agents, strategies, nodes, or states must continually enter. Without this flow, cumulative advantage tends to freeze into oligarchy or saturation rather than sustain a broad scaling regime.

3. **Local rules with global aggregation**  
   Agents respond to limited information, but the system aggregates many such interactions. This matters because scale-free behavior is not centrally imposed; it emerges from repeated decentralized updates.

4. **Absence of a strong scale-setting constraint**  
   If there is a hard capacity, optimal size, or dominant length/time scale, then power-law behavior is truncated or replaced by thinner-tailed forms. So the relative weakness of such constraints is itself load-bearing.

What is *not* load-bearing is the specific substrate: firms, cities, proteins, websites, ecosystems, and citation networks can differ enormously while preserving the same abstract structure. That is why mathematics is effective here: the same invariants govern many realizations.

This reframes power laws as the outcome of a symmetry: approximate **scale invariance**. If the explanatory core survives coarse-graining—if aggregating micro-events leaves the same form of interaction and feedback—then no single scale dominates, and multiplicative disparities accumulate across levels. The resulting distribution is often power-law or close to it.

What follows is a testable claim: whenever a supposed scale-free system loses one of these load-bearing structures—especially cumulative advantage, continuous novelty, or scale-indifference in constraints—the power-law should weaken, acquire a cutoff, or transition to another family. Conversely, models that differ in narrative detail but retain these structures should converge on similar heavy-tailed behavior.

So the deeper explanation is not that complex adaptive systems “like” power laws. It is that many such systems are organized around abstract generative constraints that are themselves scale-free. Power laws are what remains visible when those constraints dominate.
