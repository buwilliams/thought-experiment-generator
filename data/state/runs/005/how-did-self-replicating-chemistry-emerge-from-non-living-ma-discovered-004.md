So self-replication likely emerged first as **network-level recurrence** before molecule-level templating. The earliest “replicators” may have been autocatalytic sets, compositional assemblies, or surface-bound cycles whose identity was distributed across interacting components. Such systems are low-fidelity, but fidelity is not the initial requirement. What matters first is **selectable continuity**: enough persistence that better error-correcting structures can accumulate.

What follows is that the transition to template-based replication was probably not the origin of heredity but a later refinement that **compressed and stabilized memory**. Templates became advantageous because they improved the criticism process: variants could be copied with enough continuity for selection to distinguish better from worse chemistries across generations. In this view, RNA-like templating was not a miraculous first step but a high-leverage upgrade within an already evolving chemical ecology.

The load-bearing part of this conjecture is the claim that **selection requires memory before it requires exact copying**, and that prebiotic chemistry could instantiate memory in distributed, environmental, and cyclical forms. Remove that, and the story collapses back into a lottery for a fully formed replicator. Keep it, and the emergence of life becomes less a singular accident and more a phase change: matter organized into systems capable of retaining partial knowledge about how to keep going.
