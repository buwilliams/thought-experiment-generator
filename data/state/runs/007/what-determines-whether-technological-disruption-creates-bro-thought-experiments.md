## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Imagine a society in which a powerful new technology appears, but we isolate one variable: **who can copy and recombine the know-how needed to use it**. Hold everything else as equal as possible.

Now split the scenario in two.

In the first, the technology is physically productive but epistemically closed: the crucial knowledge is proprietary, legally fenced, organizationally concentrated, and difficult for others to adapt. Firms and workers outside the controlling nodes can buy outputs, but cannot meaningfully participate in improvement, substitution, or competing deployment. The gains from disruption then flow through a narrow channel. Prices may fall somewhat, and some spillovers occur, but the main effect is to increase the bargaining power of whoever controls the scarce, non-copyable knowledge and the complementary bottlenecks around it. Wealth concentrates not because technology “naturally” centralizes, but because the system’s feedback loops reward ownership of exclusion points.

In the second, the same technology is epistemically open: the relevant knowledge can be learned, criticized, modified, and deployed by many actors across firms, regions, and classes. Then disruption behaves differently. Competition increases, complementary innovations proliferate, and institutions must bid for participation from a wider base of capable contributors. Wages, firm formation, and local adaptation rise not automatically, but because the productive knowledge is not locked inside a few load-bearing chokepoints. Gains spread because many people can become creators, not merely consumers.

So the conjecture is:

**Technological disruption creates broad prosperity when the knowledge required to use and improve the technology is widely transmissible and institutionally contestable; it creates concentrated gains when that knowledge is enclosed behind bottlenecks that are hard to copy, challenge, or route around.**

What follows is important. The key determinant is not “technology” alone, nor even capital ownership in the abstract, but the **structure of access to error-correcting participation** in the new production system. If many can enter, test ideas, and retain enough upside to continue, prosperity broadens. If only a few can do so, disruption amplifies concentration.

This isolates a deeper variable: whether the social system treats knowledge as a generative commons or as a defended extraction point. Education, IP law, financing, labor mobility, interoperability, antitrust, open standards, and organizational design matter insofar as they alter that one structural fact.

The collision between the perspective and the problem therefore illuminates this: **prosperity distribution is downstream of who gets to participate in the creation and correction of knowledge around the new technology.** Broad prosperity is what disruption looks like when entry into that process is wide; concentrated gains are what it looks like when entry is narrow.
