## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a market containing only one missing variable: **excludability**. Hold everything else favorable to provision. Let all participants (1) know the good is valuable, (2) prefer that it exist, (3) have enough wealth to help fund it, and (4) face low transaction costs. Now assume the good, once produced, benefits everyone in the relevant group whether or not they paid.

Follow the logic.

In an ordinary market exchange, payment and benefit are structurally linked: if you do not pay, you do not receive. That linkage is load-bearing. It lets private willingness to pay become revenue, and revenue become a production signal.

For a public good, that linkage is absent. The participant’s decision is no longer “pay and get” versus “don’t pay and don’t get.” It becomes “pay and get” versus “don’t pay and still get, if enough others pay.” Once that is true, each individual’s preference for the good does **not** translate into an equal preference to personally fund it. The rational comparison is at the margin: “Will my contribution change the outcome enough to justify my cost?” In large groups especially, the answer is often no, even when the person strongly wants the good provided.

So the key variable is not selfishness, ignorance, or malice. It is the **decoupling of contribution from access**. Markets are mechanisms for coordinating excludable rival commitments through price. When excludability disappears, the market’s feedback loop breaks: demand remains real, but it is not expressed as reliable purchasing power. Producers observe insufficient revenue, not insufficient value.

This explains the apparent paradox: all participants can prefer provision in the collective sense while individually withholding payment in the private sense. Their preferences are not inconsistent; they are being filtered through a structure that rewards waiting for others to bear the cost.

What follows is stronger than “markets fail sometimes.” The underprovision is not an accident at the edge of the system; it is the expected consequence of using a mechanism whose core signal depends on exclusion to allocate goods defined by non-exclusion. A market can discover who values a good only when non-payers can be denied it. If denial is impossible or undesirable, willingness-to-pay data become systematically distorted downward.

So the conjecture is:

**Markets underprovide public goods because the market signal requires a binding connection between payment and benefit, while public goods sever that connection. Therefore collective preference can exist without generating sufficient revenue, and underprovision follows even under ideal participant attitudes.**

What this illuminates is that the problem is structural, not moral. The remedy, if any, must restore the missing coupling by some other means: taxation, compulsory contribution, club formation, patronage, norms, or institutions that convert diffuse benefit into enforceable commitment.
