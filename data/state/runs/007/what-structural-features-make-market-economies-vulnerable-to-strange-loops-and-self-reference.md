## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Market economies become vulnerable to winner-take-all dynamics when their core feedback structures form a **strange loop between success, visibility, preference, and rule-setting**: agents win not just by producing value, but by becoming the standard through which value is recognized, which then feeds back into further winning. At that point, competition ceases to be merely horizontal rivalry within a market and becomes recursive control over the market’s own self-description.

The key structural feature is **self-referential selection**. In many markets, especially informational, cultural, financial, and platform markets, demand depends on prior demand: people buy what others already buy, invest in what others already invest in, and trust what already looks trusted. This creates a loop:

1. **Initial advantage** produces  
2. **greater visibility and legitimacy**, which produces  
3. **more adoption**, which produces  
4. **more data, capital, and bargaining power**, which allows the winner to  
5. **reshape the criteria of selection**—ranking systems, standards, access, pricing expectations, supply chains, or regulation—thereby reinforcing the original advantage.

That loop is “strange” because the system’s outputs become inputs into its own evaluation function. The market is no longer just selecting winners; winners increasingly determine how selection occurs.

Several structural features make this loop possible:

- **Network effects:** value rises with the number of users, so popularity directly creates quality-like advantages.
- **High fixed costs, near-zero marginal costs:** once scale is reached, small leads become hard to challenge.
- **Positional and attention goods:** when visibility itself is scarce, being seen causes more being seen.
- **Reputation cascades:** trust and legitimacy become recursive; people trust what others are seen trusting.
- **Data and learning effects:** incumbents improve faster because they have more users, making the market “learn” in their favor.
- **Rule plasticity:** dominant firms can influence standards, interfaces, contracts, and policy, turning contingent success into institutionalized advantage.

From the strange-loop perspective, winner-take-all outcomes are not explained mainly by greed or even by efficiency, but by **recursive closure**: the market folds back on itself so that performance, perception, and governance become mutually reinforcing. This also explains why such dynamics feel both rational and unjust. Each local step may look reasonable—choose the best-known platform, back the safest asset, hire the most credentialed candidate—but the aggregate structure amplifies tiny differences into durable hierarchy.

What follows is that vulnerability to winner-take-all dynamics is highest where markets are forced to **model themselves through their participants’ beliefs**. The more market actors must infer quality from the choices of other actors, the more likely the system is to become self-amplifying. The deepest leverage, then, is not merely redistribution after concentration, but interruption of the loop: interoperability, portability, anti-self-preferencing rules, decentralized discovery, and institutions that prevent winners from becoming the arbiters of what counts as winning.
