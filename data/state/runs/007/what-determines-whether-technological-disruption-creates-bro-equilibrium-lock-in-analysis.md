## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption produces broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined less by the technology itself than by the **equilibrium structure of bargaining, ownership, and coordination around it**. Broad prosperity emerges when the surrounding institutions let many actors adopt, complement, and bargain over the new capabilities without being individually punished for doing so. Concentrated gains emerge when the system locks into an equilibrium where each actor’s locally rational move reinforces centralization.

**Why this perspective bites:**  
The key question is not “Who made better choices?” but “What can no one afford to do unilaterally?” In many disruptions, workers, firms, consumers, investors, and governments may all prefer a more widely shared outcome, yet still reproduce concentration because deviation is too costly for any one participant.

**Structural traps that create concentrated gains:**
1. **Winner-take-most platform dynamics:**  
   If scale, data, network effects, or standards advantages make one platform more valuable as it grows, then users, suppliers, and complementors each rationally converge there. No single actor can defect to a more distributed ecosystem without losing access, demand, or interoperability.

2. **Weak labor bargaining under rapid substitution:**  
   Even if firms would tolerate slower automation or more gain-sharing in principle, any one firm that does so may lose on cost; any one worker who resists replacement may simply be replaced. The result is an equilibrium of adoption without broad sharing.

3. **Capital-biased ownership of complements:**  
   If the scarce bottlenecks are compute, IP, data, finance, or distribution, then returns flow to owners of those bottlenecks. Others can contribute, but cannot capture much value alone because they lack leverage over the choke points.

4. **Policy coordination failures:**  
   A single jurisdiction that taxes rents, strengthens labor standards, or compels interoperability may fear capital flight or competitive disadvantage. So even mutually preferred reforms fail if no actor can move first safely.

**What follows:**  
Technological disruption yields broad prosperity when institutions alter the game so that decentralized actors can improve their position **without requiring universal virtue or synchronized courage**. The load-bearing variables are:
- **Diffusion costs:** Can many firms and workers realistically use the technology?
- **Countervailing bargaining power:** Can labor, suppliers, and users claim part of the surplus?
- **Openness of complements:** Are interfaces, standards, and inputs accessible?
- **Ownership dispersion:** Who owns the bottlenecks and downstream gains?
- **Multi-actor coordination capacity:** Can policy move at a level matching the scale of the technology?

**Illumination:**  
This reframes inequality from disruption as often being an **equilibrium lock-in problem**, not merely greed, ignorance, or bad leadership. If so, exhortation is weak leverage. The relevant reforms are those that change payoffs and feasible strategies: interoperability rules, broadened capital ownership, portable benefits, stronger collective bargaining, anti-monopoly action, public option infrastructure, and coordination across jurisdictions.

So the determinant is not simply innovation rate, but whether the post-innovation equilibrium is one where **sharing gains is individually stable** rather than collectively desirable but strategically unreachable.
