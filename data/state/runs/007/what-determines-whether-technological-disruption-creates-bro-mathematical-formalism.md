## Conjecture

Conjecture: Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined mainly by the topology of the economic network through which the new technology propagates — specifically, by how ownership, complementarity, and bargaining power are distributed across the graph.

Mathematically, model the economy as a network \(G=(V,E)\). Nodes are agents or institutions — workers, firms, platforms, capital owners, governments. A technological disruption is a transformation \(T\) that changes production functions, transaction costs, and information flows on this graph. Let total surplus after disruption be \(S(T)\). The central question is not just whether \(S(T)\) rises, but how the increment \(\Delta S\) is partitioned.

That partition depends on three structural variables:

1. Ownership concentration \(O\): who controls the key bottleneck assets created or amplified by \(T\) — IP, compute, data, platforms, logistics, capital.
2. Substitutability/complementarity \(C\): whether labor and small firms become more complementary to the new technology or more substitutable by it.
3. Mobility/bargaining \(B\): how easily agents can switch partners, organize, retrain, or access the new productive frontier.

We can then express the broadness of gains \(P\) as something like
\[
P \sim f(-O,\,+C,\,+B),
\]
where higher \(P\) means prosperity is more widely distributed.

The key invariant is this: if disruption increases total productivity but leaves the allocation structure dominated by bottlenecks, then gains concentrate. Formally, if \(T\) raises \(S\) while also increasing centrality and scarcity rents at a small set of nodes, then the network’s payoff distribution becomes more unequal even under aggregate growth. This is not a contingent moral fact but a structural consequence of surplus flowing to high-centrality nodes.

By contrast, broad prosperity arises when \(T\) is “diffusive”: it lowers entry barriers, creates many complementary niches, and weakens chokepoints. In graph terms, prosperity broadens when disruption increases the degree and effective participation of many nodes rather than merely amplifying the eigenvector centrality of a few.

A useful threshold claim follows: there exists a regime change when complementarity and bargaining power outrun ownership concentration. If
\[
C + B > O
\]
in the relevant structural sense, gains tend to spread; if
\[
O > C + B,
\]
gains tend to compress upward. The exact functional form is less important than the relation: concentrated control over non-substitutable bottlenecks dominates distributional outcomes unless countered by widespread complementary participation and credible outside options.

So the determining factor is not “technology” alone, nor “policy” alone, but the mathematical structure of surplus routing. Technological disruption creates broad prosperity when it behaves like a general-purpose transformation on an open network; it creates concentrated gains when it behaves like a rent amplifier on a bottlenecked network. The deepest leverage, therefore, lies in changing the graph: ownership dispersion, interoperability, skill portability, competition, and institutions that convert users and workers from peripheral nodes into co-owners or indispensable complements.
