## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture persists not mainly because safeguards are weak or officials are corrupt, but because many anti-capture institutions sit inside a **lock-in equilibrium** where each actor’s individually rational strategy reproduces dependence on the regulated industry.

The key collision is this: anti-capture design usually targets **bad incentives at the margin** — ethics rules, transparency, cooling-off periods, oversight boards — but capture is often generated by a deeper strategic structure. Regulators need information, technical expertise, implementation cooperation, political cover, and career pathways. Industry can supply all of these at lower immediate cost than any alternative. So even a well-intentioned regulator who wants independence faces a unilateral-action problem: refusing industry access, expertise, or personnel makes the agency less effective, slower, and more politically vulnerable unless others simultaneously supply substitutes.

That creates an equilibrium trap:

- **Regulators** rely on industry for usable knowledge and administrable rules.
- **Politicians** prefer low-budget oversight and industry-mediated policy because it reduces fiscal and political costs.
- **Industry** invests heavily in influence because concentrated benefits justify the expense.
- **Publics and diffuse beneficiaries** cannot match that investment because their gains are fragmented and attention is scarce.

No single actor can defect cheaply. A regulator who resists industry input may produce worse rules or lose institutional support. A politician who fully funds independent expertise bears visible cost for diffuse future benefit. Individual firms that refrain from influence risk disadvantage relative to competitors who continue. Citizens cannot rationally sustain comparable monitoring effort. Thus the anti-capture system is formally committed to independence while operationally forced into dependence.

What follows is that capture should be understood less as a contingent moral failure and more as a **recurrent equilibrium outcome of asymmetric knowledge, concentration of stakes, and underprovision of independent capacity**. The institution’s explicit purpose does not dissolve this; indeed, formal anti-capture rules can stabilize the equilibrium by legitimizing the appearance of independence while leaving the dependency structure intact.

The load-bearing part of the explanation is not merely “industry has money.” Money matters because it helps solve multiple coordination problems at once: producing expertise, maintaining presence, shaping agendas, and offering post-regulatory careers. If one removed revolving doors but not informational dependence, capture would mutate rather than disappear. If one increased transparency but not independent analytic capacity, influence would become more visible without becoming less necessary.

So the illuminating claim is: **systematic regulatory capture is a lock-in phenomenon produced by the absence of credible, collectively financed substitutes for industry-supplied inputs.** Reform fails when it tries to improve virtue within the existing equilibrium rather than changing the game so that independence is individually sustainable. The highest-leverage interventions therefore are structural: public expert capacity, countervailing representation for diffuse interests, rule designs less reliant on firm-specific discretion, and career systems that make nonindustry dependence viable.
