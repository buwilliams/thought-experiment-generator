## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods not mainly because participants are irrational or insufficiently motivated, but because public-good provision is a **computationally irreducible coordination problem**: the system cannot, in general, infer the provision outcome everyone prefers from decentralized bids and exchanges alone without effectively running the full strategic interaction step by step. Since each agent’s best move depends on others’ moves, and the good is non-excludable, willingness to pay is not directly computable from observed market behavior. The market’s local rule—voluntary exchange of excludable benefits—therefore generates a stable underprovision pattern.

### What this perspective illuminates
A standard market transaction compresses preference into a price because buyer and seller can isolate who gets what. With public goods, that compression fails. The key information is not just “Do people want it?” but “What would each contribute conditional on what everyone else contributes?” That is a higher-order, interdependent computation.

If computational irreducibility holds here, there is no general shortcut from:
- many individuals preferring the good,
- to a decentralized market outcome that funds it.

The system must process strategic responses in sequence: each person waits to see whether others will pay, shades revealed demand, and exploits non-excludability. “Everyone wants the park” does not algorithmically yield “the park gets funded,” because the generative rule of the market is not collective preference aggregation but bilateral exchange under private incentives.

### What follows
1. **Underprovision is structural, not accidental.**  
   The market is running the wrong kind of computation. It is excellent at coordinating rival, excludable goods because the local feedback signal—price—tracks individual benefit and cost. For public goods, price lacks sufficient load-bearing information because benefits spill across non-payers.

2. **Preference agreement is insufficient.**  
   Even unanimous preference for provision does not collapse the strategic computation. The issue is not whether the good is valued, but whether the system contains a rule that converts distributed valuation into binding contribution.

3. **Free-riding is not merely moral failure.**  
   It is the expected behavior of agents operating inside a rule-set that cannot shortcut the dependency network. Each participant’s rational calculation includes others’ likely payments, so the collective outcome emerges from recursive withholding.

4. **Institutional design becomes the minimal generative rule.**  
   If no shortcut exists inside voluntary exchange, then successful provision requires adding a different rule: taxation, compulsory contribution, matching schemes, assurance contracts, club structures, or other mechanisms that alter the computation. These are not patches on an otherwise sufficient market; they are alternative algorithms.

### Load-bearing claim
The crucial constraint is **non-excludability interacting with decentralized strategic dependence**. Remove either one—make benefits excludable, or make contributions binding and collective—and the underprovision pattern changes. That suggests the explanation is not decorative but structurally tight.

So the deepest lesson is: markets underprovide public goods because, for this class of goods, decentralized exchange cannot in general compute the collectively preferred outcome more cheaply than agents’ full strategic interaction—and that interaction systematically selects too little provision.
