## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods not mainly because participants misvalue them, but because public goods place agents inside an **equilibrium lock-in**: each participant prefers a world where the good is funded, yet no individual can unilaterally move the system there without bearing costs others can avoid. The underprovision is therefore a structural trap, not merely a motivational or informational failure.

What is load-bearing here is the combination of two features: **non-excludability** and **diffuse benefit**. Because people cannot easily be excluded from enjoying the good, any one person’s contribution creates benefits that spill to non-contributors. And because the benefit to each individual is only a fraction of the total, the private payoff from contributing is typically below the private cost, even when the **total social payoff** is strongly positive. So for each agent, “wait and free-ride” is locally rational.

This creates a Nash-type constraint: if others are not contributing, my contribution is wasted or insufficient; if others are contributing, I can benefit without paying. In neither case does unilateral action dominate. Thus everyone can sincerely prefer the collectively funded outcome while still choosing not to fund it. The market is not “failing” in the sense of malfunctioning relative to its own rules; it is accurately expressing incentives that do not align private action with joint preference.

What follows is important: better intentions, more moral exhortation, or even better price discovery will not reliably solve the problem, because the obstacle is not primarily ignorance but **strategic dependence**. The system resists reform because each actor’s best response is conditioned on expectations about others, and those expectations reinforce non-provision. This is lock-in.

The distinction illuminated by this perspective is between **equilibrium constraints** and **contingent failures**. If underprovision were mainly due to confusion, coordination mistakes, or temporary distrust, then improved information or leadership might suffice. But where the public-good structure is load-bearing, those interventions only help at the margins. Durable provision requires altering the game so that individual incentives no longer reward defection — for example through taxation, compulsory contribution, bundling public goods with excludable benefits, repeated-game enforcement, or institutions that create credible assurance of broad participation.

So the deeper claim is: **public-goods underprovision is a case where markets reveal the limits of voluntary bilateral exchange as a coordination technology.** Markets excel when benefits and costs can be internalized to transacting parties. They systematically stall when value is jointly created but privately unrecoverable. The issue is not that people do not want the good, but that the pathway from preference to provision is blocked by an equilibrium no one can escape alone.
