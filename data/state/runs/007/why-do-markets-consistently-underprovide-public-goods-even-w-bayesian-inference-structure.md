## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods not mainly because people “don’t value them enough,” but because the market’s inference mechanism cannot reliably convert widespread *conditional preferences* into effective *payment signals*. From a Bayesian lens, the key mistake is reversing: “many people would benefit if the good existed” is treated as if it implied “many people will pay enough for it under decentralized exchange.” That inference fails because the prior probability that any one person’s payment is pivotal is extremely low.

More formally, participants often have high **P(I want this good | it is provided)** and even high **P(I would contribute in a world where others contribute)**. Markets, however, require something closer to **P(I will pay now | my payment may not determine provision, and I can benefit anyway)**. For public goods, those are not the same conditional. Non-excludability and non-rivalry make free-riding rational: the evidence “I prefer the outcome” is weak evidence for “I will reveal that preference through purchase.”

The hidden prior is the base rate of **individual pivotality**. In private goods markets, paying is tightly linked to receiving; the prior that my purchase matters to my own access is high. In public goods, the prior that my marginal contribution changes the outcome is often tiny. So even strong aggregate preference does not update individual willingness-to-pay enough to generate provision. The market interprets low payment as low value, but that is a bad posterior because it ignores the base rate structure created by the good’s public character.

This also explains why the problem is systematic rather than accidental. The issue is structural: the price system is excellent at aggregating information when exclusion ties benefit to payment. It is poor at aggregating information when benefit leaks regardless of payment. In Bayesian terms, observed demand is a biased signal of true social value whenever the mapping from “values outcome” to “pays for outcome” is weak. Public goods are exactly such cases.

What follows is that solving underprovision requires changing the inference environment, not merely exhorting generosity. Mechanisms like taxation, matching funds, assurance contracts, subscription thresholds, or quadratic funding work because they alter the prior and likelihoods: they increase the probability that contribution is pivotal, or they better translate dispersed benefit into actionable funding signals. These institutions do not override preferences; they make the relevant evidence legible.

So the illumination is this: market failure for public goods is an epistemic failure of decentralized payment signals under the wrong conditional structure. The market is not discovering that people do not want the good. It is often discovering only that, given low pivotality and guaranteed spillovers, people will not pay in ways that truthfully reveal how much they want it.
