## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Technological disruption creates **broad prosperity** when the key complements needed to use, adapt, and benefit from the new technology are **widely copyable and widely accessible**; it creates **concentrated gains** when the decisive complements are **bottlenecked** — scarce data, compute, capital, network position, regulatory privilege, land, distribution, or tacit organizational know-how.

Push the problem to the boundary and the structure appears.

At one extreme, imagine a technology whose use costs approach zero, whose knowledge is easy to transmit, and whose complements are abundant. Then each improvement can be copied, criticized, recombined, and deployed by many actors. Competition spreads gains. Prices fall, capability rises, and prosperity broadens because no one can hold the critical leverage point for long.

At the other extreme, imagine a technology that is infinitely powerful in principle but usable only through one narrow gateway — one platform, one data reservoir, one legal franchise, one fabrication chain, one scarce skill elite. Then even if the technology raises total output enormously, gains concentrate at the chokepoint. The disruption does not primarily reward invention; it rewards control over access.

So the hidden variable is not “how advanced” the technology is. It is **where the load-bearing scarcity moves after the disruption**.

Technological change always destroys some constraints and exposes others. If it relaxes labor, coordination, or information constraints but leaves ownership of the new bottleneck concentrated, then wealth pools there. If it dissolves bottlenecks faster than institutions can recreate them, then gains diffuse.

This suggests a systems view: prosperity distribution is determined by the interaction of three feedback loops:

1. **Replication loop** — how fast the technology and the know-how to use it spread.  
2. **Bottleneck capture loop** — how strongly control over complements can be defended and compounded.  
3. **Adaptation loop** — how quickly workers, firms, and institutions can reorganize around the new possibilities.

Broad prosperity occurs when replication and adaptation outrun bottleneck capture. Concentrated gains occur when capture outruns diffusion.

At the limit, this becomes almost taut: if everyone can use and improve the technology, prosperity broadens; if only a few can, gains concentrate. But the boundary case reveals what policy and strategy often miss: the distributive outcome is decided less by the invention itself than by the **architecture of access** around it.

Therefore: to predict who benefits from disruption, look for the new non-substitutable complement. To change who benefits, do not merely accelerate innovation; alter the bottleneck — through interoperability, education, open standards, competitive market structure, abundant infrastructure, and institutions that make adaptation faster than enclosure.

The edge case reveals the rule: **disruption distributes gains according to the scarcity it leaves behind.**
