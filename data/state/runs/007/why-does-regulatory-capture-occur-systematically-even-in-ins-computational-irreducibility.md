## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture persists not mainly because safeguards are too weak, but because regulation is a computationally irreducible adaptive game: the institution cannot reliably predict, in compressed form, how regulated actors will exploit rules, relationships, and information asymmetries. As a result, any fixed anti-capture design is eventually outcomputed by the very system it governs.

What is true on this view is that “prevent capture” is not a one-time design problem with a stable solution. It is an ongoing process in which regulators, firms, legislators, courts, and professional networks co-evolve. The institution is explicitly designed against known failure modes — conflicts of interest, bribery, revolving doors, lobbying pressure — but the relevant behavior is generated by many interacting local rules: career incentives, expertise dependencies, reporting burdens, legal ambiguity, budget constraints, political oversight, and industry coordination. Because these interactions are irreducible, the institution cannot derive in advance all the paths by which influence will be laundered into “normal procedure.”

What follows is that capture should be expected to reappear in new forms after each reform. Close the bribery channel, and influence shifts into expertise monopolies. Limit revolving doors, and capture moves into consultant ecosystems, trade associations, shared epistemic frameworks, or dependence on industry-generated data. Tighten formal independence, and capture may intensify through upstream appointment politics or downstream judicial review. This is not incidental evasion; it is what an adaptive, high-dimensional system does when constrained locally.

This perspective illuminates why institutions designed to prevent capture often become more procedural yet not less captured. In irreducible systems, increasing rule complexity does not necessarily increase control; it may instead enlarge the state space in which strategic actors can search for compliant-looking influence. The regulator must effectively simulate more of the environment to distinguish genuine compliance from adversarial adaptation, while industry can specialize narrowly in discovering exploitable paths. That asymmetry favors capture.

The load-bearing point is dependence on regulated actors for interpretation. Modern sectors are too complex for regulators to understand without industry expertise, data, and personnel. That dependence is not a removable flaw but a generative condition of regulation itself. Once the regulator must learn the system from the system’s participants, influence enters not only through corruption but through model-shaping: what counts as risk, feasibility, efficiency, evidence, and enforceability.

So the practical implication is not “design better static safeguards,” but “build institutions for permanent adversarial iteration”: rotation of epistemic sources, redundant monitoring, randomized audits, public contestation of industry models, and structures that reduce single-channel dependence. Capture occurs systematically because the problem is not merely moral hazard; it is irreducible adaptation in a system where the governed are often better positioned than the governor to compute the consequences of the rules.
