## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined less by the technology itself than by the *institutional inheritance* into which it arrives: specifically, whether the social arrangements that evolved to solve earlier coordination problems still route the gains from new knowledge to a narrow set of bottlenecks.

The origin question matters because “technological disruption” is not appearing in a vacuum. Modern firms, capital markets, intellectual property regimes, labor contracts, urban zoning, higher education credentials, and platform governance were all built to solve prior problems: financing large fixed-cost industry, controlling quality, managing labor at scale, reducing uncertainty, and coordinating scarce physical assets. Those arrangements often replaced more local, dispersed, or informal systems. But they also inherited assumptions from the industrial era: that capital is scarce, production is centralized, monitoring must be hierarchical, and scale requires ownership concentration.

When a new technology arrives, it interacts with those inherited structures. If the key complements to the technology — capital, compute, data, legal rights, distribution, land access, licensing, credentialing — are controlled through concentrated bottlenecks created for older problems, then the disruption’s gains will concentrate. The technology may be productivity-enhancing in principle, but the system’s feedback loops route rents upward. Network effects, IP protections, and financialized ownership then reinforce this concentration.

Broad prosperity happens when disruption bypasses or dissolves those bottlenecks faster than incumbents can reassert them. This is more likely when the new technology reduces dependence on scarce centralized assets, lowers entry barriers, makes knowledge more transferable, and is matched by institutions designed for diffusion rather than control: abundant housing, competitive markets, portable benefits, broad capital ownership, open standards, education that teaches adaptation rather than credential scarcity, and policy that treats knowledge spillovers as a public good rather than a private moat.

So the hidden variable is not “innovation intensity” but *which old problem the surrounding institutions still think they are solving*. If labor law still assumes the factory, antitrust still assumes price effects in goods markets, education still assumes one-shot frontloaded training, and IP still assumes innovation requires strong exclusion, then even democratizing technologies will be forced through centralized channels. The result is concentrated gain not because concentration is technologically necessary, but because old solutions remain load-bearing after their original problem has changed.

What follows is that the prosperity question is fundamentally historical and structural. To predict distributional outcomes, ask: Which inherited bottlenecks does this technology depend on? Which obsolete constraints are being preserved by policy, ownership, or culture? And are the gains encoded as knowledge that can spread, or as control over access points that can be fenced?

In short: technological disruption broadens prosperity when it is allowed to behave like distributed knowledge; it concentrates gains when legacy institutions convert that knowledge into ownership of chokepoints.
