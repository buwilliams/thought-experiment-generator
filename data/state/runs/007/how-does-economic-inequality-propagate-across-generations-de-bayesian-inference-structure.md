## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Economic inequality persists across generations not because redistribution “fails” in the abstract, but because many redistributive systems condition on *observed outcomes* while the true transmission process operates through *latent priors*—family-level starting conditions, social networks, norms, expectations, and risk buffers. Bayesianly: policy often targets \(P(\text{bad outcomes} \mid \text{disadvantage})\), but persistence is driven by differences in \(P(\text{advantage} \mid \text{same observed outcomes})\) and by unequal priors over future success before outcomes appear.

The key collision is between redistribution as a response to visible deprivation and inequality as a process generated earlier, upstream, and probabilistically. Two children may show the same current income, grades, or employment status, yet the posterior probability of future success differs because institutions and decision-makers implicitly condition on hidden base rates: family wealth, neighborhood quality, school reputation, professional contacts, fallback options, and inherited confidence about taking risks. These priors affect loans, internships, housing, marriage markets, legal outcomes, and willingness to endure short-term failure. Redistribution after income realization does little to equalize these posterior trajectories.

A common conditional-reversal error obscures this. Observers see that many poor adults received some public support and infer that support should have equalized opportunity. But \(P(\text{received redistribution} \mid \text{poor})\) tells us little about \(P(\text{escaping poverty} \mid \text{received redistribution})\) unless we also know the base rates and what unobserved disadvantages remain. If the recipients are disproportionately drawn from families facing multiple compounding constraints, then modest transfers can coexist with high persistence. The evidence is not that redistribution is irrelevant; it is that selection into need already encodes deep prior disadvantage.

Intergenerational propagation is therefore best understood as a Bayesian updating system with asymmetric information and unequal priors. Wealthier families can repeatedly generate favorable evidence about their children—better credentials, cleaner records, safer experimentation—because they can subsidize mistakes and purchase time. Poorer families face noisier signals and harsher penalties for early negative evidence. Institutions then update on these signals as if they were individual merit, when in fact they are partially artifacts of inherited priors. This creates a feedback loop: prior advantage produces better evidence, which produces more favorable posterior judgments, which then justifies further allocation.

So the explanatory leverage lies in shifting from downstream equalization of outcomes to upstream equalization of priors and of error tolerance. Redistribution aimed only at income is structurally too late and too narrow. To interrupt propagation, mechanisms must alter the hidden base rates that shape inference itself: early childhood conditions, school quality, housing stability, access to low-cost capital, social insurance that permits risk-taking, and institutions that discount inherited signal advantages. Inequality survives because society keeps mistaking posterior success for individual information rather than inherited prior probability.
