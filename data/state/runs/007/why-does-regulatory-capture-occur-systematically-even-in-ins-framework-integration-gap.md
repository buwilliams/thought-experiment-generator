## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture recurs not mainly because rules are too weak or actors are too corrupt, but because the institution is built on a weakly integrated model of control: it treats formal oversight, information production, and operational dependence as separable layers when in practice they are dynamically coupled. Capture is the “cosmological constant problem” of governance — a persistent anomaly signaling that the theory of how regulation relates to the regulated system is structurally incomplete.

### What is true from this perspective
Institutions designed to prevent capture usually focus on visible safeguards: conflict-of-interest rules, transparency, rotation, procedural checks, ethics codes. But these are like numerical patches on top of an unexamined linkage problem. The load-bearing question is: **how does the regulator obtain the knowledge required to govern without becoming dependent on the regulated system’s categories, data, expertise, and time horizons?**

That link is fundamental. In complex sectors, regulators cannot act without information, models, and implementation pathways that are largely produced by the industry itself. So the institution’s “microscopic layer” — daily epistemic inputs, personnel pipelines, benchmark metrics, legal interpretations, technical standards — flows directly into the “macroscopic layer” of public authority. If the regulator’s knowledge substrate is supplied by the regulated, then formal independence at the top cannot remain independent in practice.

### What follows
Regulatory capture is systematic when three feedback loops reinforce one another:

1. **Epistemic dependence:** firms know more than regulators about the system being regulated, so regulators adopt industry framings.
2. **Operational dependence:** regulators need firms to implement policy, so they avoid demands that would disrupt cooperation.
3. **Career and legitimacy dependence:** regulators rely on industry for future employment, professional status, and signals of competence, so dissent from industry norms is penalized.

These loops mean anti-capture features often fail because they operate at the wrong layer. They constrain overt corruption while leaving intact the deeper coupling through which judgment itself is shaped.

### What this illuminates
Capture is not best understood as a moral failure supervening on an otherwise sound design. It is the predictable output of a system whose knowledge flows are endogenous to the actors being constrained. The relevant analogy is not “bad apples bypass safeguards” but “a theory fails because quantities assumed independent are actually linked.”

### Implication
A genuinely anti-capture institution would need **structural separation in knowledge production**, not just in decision authority: independent technical capacity, adversarial model-building, alternate career tracks, citizen or cross-sector counter-expertise, randomized audits, and funding streams not mediated by regulated actors. The test of a serious reform is load-bearing integration: if you change who supplies the regulator’s knowledge, does decision behavior change in specific, predictable ways? If not, the reform is probably cosmetic.
