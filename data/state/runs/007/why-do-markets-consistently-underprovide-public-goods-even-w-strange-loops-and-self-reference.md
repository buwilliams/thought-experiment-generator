## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods because public-goods situations create a **strange loop between individual choice and collective conditions**: each participant’s payoff depends on a social world that is itself generated by everyone’s individually rational choices, including their own. The market treats agents as choosing *within* a fixed environment, but in public-goods cases agents are also choosing the environment itself. This self-reference produces a structural blind spot.

A private good does not force much looping: I buy, I get. But a public good—clean air, national defense, basic research, herd immunity—exists only if enough agents act as contributors to the very system from which all benefit. Each participant asks: “Should I pay, given whether the good will exist?” But the answer depends on whether others pay, who are asking the same question, including about me. The hierarchy collapses: chooser and chosen context become the same system. That is the strange loop.

What follows is not merely “free riding” in the usual sense, but a deeper mismatch of representational levels. Market prices encode exclusion and rivalrous exchange well; they do poorly when the object being produced is the **shared background condition** for exchange itself. Public goods are often meta-goods: they alter the whole game rather than delivering a separable item within it. Because no individual transaction can capture the full recursive effect—my contribution slightly changes the world in which everyone values contributing—the market systematically underreads the value created.

This explains why unanimous preference for provision does not solve the problem. Participants may all prefer the world where the public good exists, yet each local decision is made from inside uncertainty about whether that world will be brought about. The preference is for a higher-order state of the system; the action available is a lower-order private expenditure. Markets are strong at coordinating across the lower level, weak at bootstrapping the higher one.

The load-bearing claim is: **underprovision is not just incentive failure but self-modeling failure**. A market composed of individually optimizing agents lacks a built-in mechanism for agents to bind themselves as co-authors of the collective state they all prefer. Institutions like taxation, regulation, norms, clubs, and constitutions work when they “close the loop”: they convert the higher-order preference (“we want this world”) into a rule that changes lower-order action for everyone at once.

So the illumination is this: public goods are where economic agency becomes reflexive. The market falters not because participants are irrational, but because the problem lives at a level where the system must, in effect, act on itself.
