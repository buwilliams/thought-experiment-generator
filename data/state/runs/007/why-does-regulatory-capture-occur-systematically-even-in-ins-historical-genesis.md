## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture recurs because “anti-capture” institutions inherit the deeper function of regulation from earlier political arrangements: not merely constraining industry, but stabilizing a continuing relationship between state power and the regulated sector. The institution is designed to prevent crude bribery or overt favoritism, but it preserves the older necessity that regulators must *know*, *predict*, and *coordinate with* the industry they govern. That inherited dependency is load-bearing, and it recreates capture in subtler forms.

Origin-first, regulation did not arise in a vacuum as a neutral public-interest machine. It emerged to solve prior failures of courts, legislatures, and dispersed market discipline to manage complex, technical, fast-moving sectors. It replaced episodic punishment with continuous supervision. But continuous supervision requires information, expertise, and implementation capacity. Those are concentrated inside the industry itself.

So the institution inherits an unexamined assumption: to regulate an industry effectively, one must remain in dense contact with it. Anti-capture reforms usually target the visible pathologies of that contact — conflicts of interest, revolving doors, gifts, lobbying transparency failures. But they do not eliminate the underlying structural dependence. The regulator still relies on the regulated for data, technical models, feasibility judgments, personnel pipelines, and definitions of what counts as a problem.

That creates a system with reinforcing feedback loops:

- **Information asymmetry:** industry knows more than regulator.
- **Dependence:** regulator must consult industry to act.
- **Agenda shaping:** what is measurable and discussable comes preformatted by industry categories.
- **Personnel convergence:** expertise is trained in the same professional culture.
- **Risk minimization:** regulators prefer cooperation with incumbents because visible failure is punished more than invisible accommodation.

What follows is that capture is not mainly a moral failure of individuals. It is a recurrent property of a design trying to do two incompatible things at once: remain independent of industry while being epistemically dependent on industry. Preventive rules can block some channels of influence, but unless they alter the knowledge structure, influence reappears through whichever channels remain. This is why reforms often displace capture rather than remove it: from bribery to consultancy, from lobbying to standards-setting, from direct pressure to shared assumptions.

What this perspective illuminates is that “capture” names not just corruption but a deeper ontological fact about institutions: if one subsystem is tasked with controlling another while drawing its knowledge, language, and career incentives from that subsystem, the controller becomes partially constituted by what it controls.

So the hidden assumption to criticize is: *oversight can be independent without independent knowledge production*. If that assumption is false, then the leverage point is not stricter ethics alone, but building rival epistemic infrastructures — independent data generation, adversarial technical capacity, shorter model chains, diversified recruitment, and institutional designs that reduce the regulator’s need to see the world through industry-built abstractions.
