## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Economic inequality persists across generations because redistribution is mostly applied to the **visible “income layer”** of the social system, while the **load-bearing couplings** that convert family advantage into durable life chances operate in deeper layers — such as knowledge, expectations, social networks, institutional fit, health, geography, and control over time. The problem is not just that redistribution is too small; it is that the system is **weakly integrated**: the policy layer and the inheritance-of-advantage layer are linked only loosely.

From this perspective, inequality is like a bad theory interface. We observe a large-scale outcome — persistent class stratification — and try to correct it with transfers. But the key question is: **which microscopic family-level advantages actually gravitate into macroscopic inequality?** Not every resource is equally load-bearing. Some forms of inherited advantage are fungible and easily taxed; others are structural and regenerate themselves through feedback loops.

The likely load-bearing links are these:

1. **Capability formation is path-dependent.**  
   Early differences in language exposure, nutrition, stress, schooling quality, and norms of self-advocacy compound over time. Later redistribution cannot fully substitute for missed developmental windows.

2. **Institutions reward encoded, not merely purchased, advantage.**  
   Schools, firms, and professions often select for behaviors, credentials, and dispositions disproportionately cultivated in affluent households. This means advantage is not simply transferred; it is *recognized and amplified* by institutions.

3. **Wealth protects against volatility.**  
   Families with assets can absorb shocks, wait out bad opportunities, move neighborhoods, fund unpaid internships, or take educational risk. Redistribution of annual income often does little to equalize this intertemporal control.

4. **Networks transmit opportunity nonlinearly.**  
   Access to recommendations, insider knowledge, and trust-based openings is not easily replaceable with cash. These are high-leverage channels because small network differences can produce large career differences.

5. **Political feedback stabilizes the structure.**  
   Inequality itself increases the capacity of advantaged groups to shape taxation, zoning, schooling, licensing, and inheritance rules, which then reproduce inequality. The system defends its own gradients.

What follows is that redistributive mechanisms fail when they treat inequality as a **stock deficit** rather than a **reproductive process**. A transfer can reduce current hardship without altering the generative structure that remakes inequality in the next generation.

So the illuminating claim is: **intergenerational inequality persists not because redistribution is absent, but because redistribution is aimed at downstream aggregates while inheritance operates through upstream, tightly coupled developmental and institutional channels.** A theory or policy is better integrated if changing family resources forces changes in schooling access, neighborhood effects, time security, social capital formation, and political influence — not just tax liabilities. The real leverage lies where micro-level advantage is converted into socially ratified durability.
