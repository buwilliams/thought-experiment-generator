## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains depends less on the raw power of the technology itself than on the structure of the **social interpreter systems** that make it economically meaningful: institutions, labor markets, education systems, ownership rules, infrastructure, and norms. Like a genome, a technology is not a self-executing blueprint for prosperity. It is one information-bearing component inside a layered developmental control system. Broad prosperity arises when the surrounding system can interpret and distribute the new productive possibilities across many agents; concentrated gains arise when the interpreters channel those possibilities through narrow bottlenecks.

### What this perspective illuminates
The wrong question is often: *How much capability does the technology encode?*  
The better question is: *What problem did this technological form originally solve, and what institutional machinery is required to translate that capability into widespread benefit?*

Many disruptive technologies initially solve a local coordination or efficiency problem for firms: reduce labor costs, improve prediction, lower transaction friction, scale control. If the existing interpreter systems are designed mainly to protect incumbent ownership and capture, then the technology’s benefits will be expressed as higher rents, market concentration, and labor displacement. If interpreter systems instead include broad access to complementary skills, competitive markets, diffusion channels, bargaining power for workers, and public investment, then the same underlying technology can generate rising productivity with widely shared gains.

### Layered regulation matters more than encoded quantity
The key determinant is not “how advanced” the technology is, but how many **layers of regulation and feedback** stand between invention and livelihood. These include:

- **Ownership and IP regimes**: who can legally use, modify, and profit from the technology  
- **Capital access**: whether complementary investment is concentrated or diffuse  
- **Labor-market institutions**: whether workers can capture productivity gains  
- **Education and retraining**: whether people can become competent interpreters of the new system  
- **Competition policy**: whether disruption opens ecosystems or reinforces gatekeepers  
- **Public infrastructure**: whether adoption requires private chokepoints

These layers are load-bearing. Change them, and the same technology leads to different distributional outcomes.

### What follows
1. **Technological disruption is not destiny.** Distributional outcomes are developmentally constructed.  
2. **Concentrated gains are often a symptom of interpreter asymmetry.** A few actors control the institutions that make the technology usable at scale.  
3. **Broad prosperity requires co-evolution.** Societies must adapt their interpreter systems as fast as the technology evolves.  
4. **The highest-leverage interventions are institutional, not merely inventive.** Open standards, antitrust, portable benefits, worker training, public options, and shared ownership mechanisms are prosperity-shaping “developmental regulators.”

### Bottom line
Technological disruption creates broad prosperity when the social system can **interpret, diffuse, and reciprocally regulate** the new productive information across the population. It creates concentrated gains when those interpretive layers are narrow, proprietary, and bottlenecked. Prosperity, then, is not encoded in the technology; it is expressed by the developmental system around it.
