## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Economic inequality persists across generations not mainly because redistribution is too small, but because modern redistributive systems were historically built to correct **income shortfalls within an already unequal social structure**, rather than to rewrite the mechanisms that reproduce advantage itself. They solve the later-stage symptom inherited from industrial capitalism — insufficient consumption, poverty, instability — while leaving intact the earlier-stage inheritance systems that allocate competence signals, social trust, family time, neighborhood quality, institutional access, and capital ownership.

Put historically: redistribution emerged as a response to the visible failures of market societies — destitution, labor unrest, extreme deprivation. What it replaced was not inherited hierarchy itself, but harsher forms of neglect or patronage. So it inherited a hidden assumption: that inequality is acceptable so long as its worst effects are softened after the fact. That assumption becomes an invisible constraint.

Once viewed as a system, this matters. Intergenerational inequality is reproduced through **stocks** that accumulate before the state redistributes annual flows of money: wealth, educational quality, health, expectations, networks, and low-risk opportunities. Families with advantages convert them into durable positional goods for children. Redistribution usually enters later and more narrowly, through taxes and transfers tied to yearly income. It therefore acts downstream on one flow while the upstream stocks continue compounding.

This explains why inequality propagates even under redistribution:

1. **Timing mismatch:** advantage is transmitted early in life; redistribution often compensates late.  
2. **Object mismatch:** states mostly redistribute money; families transmit money plus culture, attention, contacts, and risk buffers.  
3. **Positional conversion:** private advantage buys scarcity — better schools, safer areas, elite credentials — that cannot be equalized merely by cash transfers.  
4. **Political feedback:** concentrated advantage reproduces influence, which shapes redistributive mechanisms to remain palliative rather than transformative.

The load-bearing claim is that redistributive institutions were not designed to abolish inherited advantage, only to civilize its consequences. If that claim changes, the whole explanation changes: one would then expect redistribution to target wealth transmission, childhood environments, institutional segregation, and political capture much more directly than most systems do.

So the illuminating point is this: “despite redistribution” is slightly misleading. Inequality persists **through a settlement in which redistribution and reproduction are complementary**. One stabilizes the social order enough to preserve the other. Redistribution reduces breakdown; it does not necessarily interrupt inheritance.

If true, the implication is not simply “redistribute more,” but “redesign the inheritance architecture of advantage.” Otherwise policy keeps treating a structural reproduction system as though it were merely a recurring shortage of money.
