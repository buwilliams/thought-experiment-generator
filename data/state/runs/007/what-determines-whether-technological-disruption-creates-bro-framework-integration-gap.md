## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined less by the technology itself than by how tightly the economy’s “microscopic” innovation layer is integrated with the “macroscopic” distribution layer. Broad prosperity arises when the institutions that translate local gains in capability into system-wide income, bargaining power, and access are load-bearing parts of the same structure. Concentrated gains arise when those links are weak, so productivity can rise without forcing corresponding changes in wages, ownership, or public capacity.

From the given perspective, this is analogous to the cosmological constant problem: the key issue is not just a numerical mismatch between “more technology” and “more prosperity,” but a weak integration between two theories of the economy — one describing how value is created, the other how value gravitates through society. If those layers are only loosely connected, extraordinary micro-level advances can coexist with stagnant median livelihoods.

The load-bearing links include:

1. **Ownership structure:**  
   If the assets embodying the new technology are narrowly owned, then gains mechanically accumulate there unless countervailing institutions exist. Ownership is not cosmetic; changing it changes the whole distributional consequence.

2. **Labor complementarity vs substitution:**  
   Technologies that amplify many workers’ capabilities tend to spread gains; technologies that deskill, centralize control, or replace labor without new bargaining channels concentrate gains. The technical design of the innovation matters because it constrains who remains economically necessary.

3. **Market structure and feedback loops:**  
   In networked, data-rich, low-marginal-cost sectors, winner-take-most dynamics can make concentration self-reinforcing. Scale advantages, data advantages, and political influence become positive feedback loops that decouple social productivity from shared prosperity.

4. **Institutional transmission mechanisms:**  
   Tax systems, antitrust, labor law, education, housing, and public infrastructure determine whether productivity improvements propagate into broad capabilities or remain trapped in firms and asset prices. These are not after-the-fact patches; they are part of the effective theory linking innovation to mass welfare.

So the decisive variable is the **integration strength** between innovation and distribution. In a strongly integrated system, improvements in productive knowledge force broad downstream effects: tighter labor markets, diffusion of skills, falling costs in essentials, rising fiscal capacity, wider ownership, stronger complements to human work. In a weakly integrated system, the same innovation can mainly inflate rents, monopoly positions, and capital income.

What this perspective illuminates is that “technology creates wealth” is too weak an explanation. The real question is: **what structural couplings make newly created knowledge gravitate into the social body rather than collapse into a few balance sheets?** Policies or institutional designs that alter those couplings are high-leverage because they change not a single outcome, but the translation rule from disruption to distribution.
