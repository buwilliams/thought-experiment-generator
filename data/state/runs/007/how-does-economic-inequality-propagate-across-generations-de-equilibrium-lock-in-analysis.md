## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A large share of intergenerational economic inequality persists not because redistribution is absent, but because society is locked into a **multi-level equilibrium** in which each major actor responds rationally to inequality in ways that reproduce it. Redistribution softens outcomes at the margin, while the underlying game keeps re-creating unequal starting positions.

**What is structurally true:**  
Parents, schools, employers, neighborhoods, lenders, and political coalitions are all adapting to inequality at once. Affluent families invest heavily in positional advantages for their children — housing in high-opportunity areas, tutoring, networks, internships, cultural fluency. Poorer families, facing tighter constraints and higher risk, make defensive choices that protect short-term stability over long-term mobility. Schools and employers, unable to directly observe merit or future potential, rely on proxies that are themselves shaped by family resources: credentials, polish, extracurriculars, recommendations, location. Politicians can sustain redistributive programs that relieve deprivation without threatening the advantage-preserving strategies of organized, influential groups.

This forms an equilibrium lock-in:  
- **If one affluent family disarms** from status competition, its child loses relative position.  
- **If one school or employer stops using elite proxies**, it bears selection risk unless others do too.  
- **If one locality broadens access to scarce opportunities**, incumbent residents face crowding or asset dilution and resist.  
- **If one politician pursues deeper equalization**, concentrated losers mobilize more effectively than diffuse beneficiaries.

So even where many actors would prefer a more mobile and less unequal society in the abstract, no actor can safely move first at acceptable cost.

**What follows:**  
Redistribution mostly acts **downstream**, after advantages have already been converted into developmental, educational, spatial, and social capital. Cash transfers can reduce hardship, but they do not by themselves break the competitive equilibria that govern school quality, housing access, inheritance, credential sorting, and social network transmission. Thus inequality propagates because redistributive flows are offset by stronger reproducing flows embedded in family strategy, market screening, and local political control.

**What this perspective illuminates:**  
The key distinction is between **contingent policy weakness** and **equilibrium constraint**. It is tempting to explain persistence by saying redistribution is too small, badly targeted, or politically compromised. Often true — but incomplete. The deeper trap is that redistribution can be politically sustainable precisely because it leaves intact the mechanisms through which advantage is privately secured and publicly defended. In that sense, some redistributive mechanisms are not failed alternatives to structural reform; they are part of the equilibrium that substitutes compensation for transformation.

**Implication:**  
To reduce intergenerational inequality, reform must target the **coordination structure**, not just the income distribution: decouple life chances from exclusionary housing markets, reduce scarcity in high-opportunity institutions, weaken inheritance of political and educational advantage, and replace status proxies with broader access and better evaluation. Otherwise redistribution will continue to mop up harms while the system reliably reproduces them.
