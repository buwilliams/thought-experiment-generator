## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined less by the technology’s intrinsic capabilities than by the **interaction structure** linking innovation, ownership, bargaining power, institutional adaptation, and diffusion. Prosperity is broad when the system converts productivity gains into widely distributed income, capability, and market access through reinforcing feedback loops; gains concentrate when the system routes those gains through ownership and control bottlenecks that compound advantage.

### What this perspective shifts
A component-level explanation asks: *What does the technology do?*  
A systems-level explanation asks: *Through what network of relationships do its effects propagate?*

The same technology can produce opposite social outcomes under different structures because its consequences emerge from feedback loops among:

- **Asset ownership**: who owns the platform, models, data, capital, IP
- **Labor market structure**: worker substitutability, bargaining power, mobility
- **Market structure**: competition vs winner-take-most dynamics
- **Institutional response speed**: education, regulation, taxation, antitrust, labor law
- **Diffusion channels**: whether complementary tools and know-how spread cheaply or remain gated

### Core mechanism
Technological disruption increases the value of certain complementarities. The crucial question is whether those complementarities are **widely accessible** or **structurally scarce**.

If the gains require scarce, defensible control points — compute clusters, proprietary data, platforms, distribution, capital-intensive deployment — then disruption tends to create **positive feedback loops of concentration**:
more scale -> better products/data/cost position -> more users and capital -> stronger barriers -> more scale.

If, instead, the gains depend on complements that can be widely acquired — skills, interoperable tools, open standards, competitive access to infrastructure, new firm formation — then disruption tends to create **positive feedback loops of broadening**:
better tools -> more participants become productive -> more entry and wage pressure -> wider demand -> more innovation serving broad markets.

### What follows
Broad prosperity is therefore not the automatic result of higher productivity. It is an emergent property of a system whose feedback loops distribute adaptive capacity. Concentrated gains arise when productivity growth outruns institutional and competitive mechanisms that would otherwise diffuse its benefits.

This explains why “skill-biased” or “capital-biased” accounts are incomplete on their own. Skills and capital matter, but their effects depend on system structure: education systems, credential bottlenecks, financing access, labor protections, IP regimes, and platform governance. These are load-bearing constraints, not decorative context.

### Illuminating implication
The highest-leverage interventions are structural, not exhortative. If one wants disruption to generate broad prosperity, the key is not merely to accelerate invention but to alter the interaction topology: reduce choke points, widen access to complements, preserve competition, increase labor and entrepreneurial bargaining power, and speed institutional adaptation.

So the determining factor is: **does the surrounding system amplify diffusion or capture?** Technological disruption becomes broadly prosperous when the social-economic network is organized to make productive power generalizable rather than accumulable at a few control nodes.
