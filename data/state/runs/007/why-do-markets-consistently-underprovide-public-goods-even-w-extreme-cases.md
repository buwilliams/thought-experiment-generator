## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Markets underprovide public goods not because participants lack desire for them, but because in the limit of large numbers the connection between *individual payment* and *individual benefit* goes to zero. At that boundary, preference ceases to function as an effective allocating signal.

Push the problem to the edge:

- If a good is perfectly private, then one buyer’s payment secures one buyer’s benefit. Preference is tightly coupled to purchase.
- If a good is perfectly public, then one unit benefits everyone, whether they paid or not. The good’s benefits are non-excludable and non-rival.

Now take the number of participants toward infinity. For any one individual, the marginal effect of their contribution on total provision becomes negligible, while their marginal benefit from others’ contributions remains positive. So even if *everyone prefers the world in which the good is provided*, each person confronts a local decision in which paying looks dominated by waiting. The market aggregates willingness to pay, but here willingness to pay is structurally severed from willingness to benefit.

This reveals the hidden structure: a market works when it can use prices to compress dispersed preferences into transactions. But for public goods, the relevant knowledge is not “Who wants this enough to buy it for themselves?” but “Who wants a world in which everyone is jointly supplied?” That is a different object. The market’s signal system is tuned to private appropriation, not shared outcome-production.

At the extreme, imagine national defense, clean air, or epidemic surveillance with millions of beneficiaries. If one person abstains, provision is almost unchanged for them; if all abstain, the good disappears. So the failure is not merely moral weakness or irrationality. It is a feedback problem: the system rewards withholding at the individual level while requiring coordinated contribution at the collective level. The equilibrium is underprovision because the incentive gradient points the wrong way.

What follows is stronger than the standard free-rider story. The issue is not just that some people can defect. It is that, as non-excludability and scale increase, *preference becomes non-load-bearing* in market allocation. A unanimous preference for provision does not imply provision, because the mechanism cannot translate that preference into action under those boundary conditions.

So the conjecture is:

**Public goods are underprovided whenever the ratio of individual decisiveness to collective benefit falls below the threshold at which payment can be incentive-compatible.**  
Beyond that boundary, market exchange ceases to be a knowledge-using discovery process for those goods and becomes a mechanism that systematically erases the very preference it is supposed to register.

Hence the leverage point is not exhorting participants to care more, but altering the structure so that contribution and benefit are recoupled: taxation, club boundaries, compulsory insurance, matching schemes, assurance contracts, or institutions that convert “everyone wants this” into binding coordinated action.
