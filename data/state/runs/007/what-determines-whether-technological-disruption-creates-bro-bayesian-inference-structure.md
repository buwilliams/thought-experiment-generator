## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains is largely determined by the **prior distribution of bargaining power, asset ownership, and institutional access** before the disruption occurs. The technology itself is not strong evidence for broad prosperity; it becomes such evidence only under priors where gains can diffuse.

Using the Bayesian lens, many arguments commit a reversal like this:

- True but weak: **If broad prosperity is present, we often observe high technological dynamism**  
  \(P(\text{tech disruption} \mid \text{broad prosperity})\) may be high.
- Illicit jump: therefore **tech disruption implies broad prosperity**  
  \(P(\text{broad prosperity} \mid \text{tech disruption})\).

That inference fails unless the prior probability of broad diffusion is already substantial and unless the likelihood ratio strongly favors it over concentration. But in many real economies, the prior is not neutral. If ownership of capital, data, networks, intellectual property, and political influence is already concentrated, then new technology is more likely to **amplify** those existing asymmetries than dissolve them.

So the key hidden base-rate question is: **In this society, how often do productivity gains historically pass through to wages, broad asset ownership, lower prices, public goods, and new opportunity, rather than being captured by incumbents?** That base rate matters more than optimistic stories about the technology’s abstract potential.

This suggests a systems claim: disruption interacts with a preexisting structure of stocks and flows.

- **Stocks:** capital ownership, skills, market concentration, housing access, social insurance, legal rights.
- **Flows:** profits, wages, taxes, credit, training, entry of new firms.
- **Feedback loops:** scale advantages, data network effects, regulatory capture, skill premia, political reinforcement of winners.

Broad prosperity is more likely when the system contains mechanisms that convert innovation rents into distributed gains: competitive markets, labor bargaining power, diffusion of know-how, broad-based capital ownership, antimonopoly enforcement, portable benefits, education/training pipelines, and taxation that recycles rents into public capacity. Concentrated gains are more likely when technology plugs into winner-take-all markets, weak labor institutions, restrictive IP or platform control, and policy systems responsive mainly to incumbents.

The load-bearing constraint is this: if you change the institutional prior while holding the technology roughly fixed, the social outcome changes substantially. But if you keep concentrated ownership and weak diffusion institutions fixed, changing the rhetoric around “innovation” changes little. That indicates the **distributional structure**, not the mere existence of disruption, is carrying the explanatory weight.

So the collision between Bayesian reasoning and this problem illuminates a disciplined answer: **technology is evidence filtered through priors**. Disruption produces broad prosperity when the prior social architecture makes broad diffusion probable; otherwise, the same disruption is more likely to produce concentrated gains.
