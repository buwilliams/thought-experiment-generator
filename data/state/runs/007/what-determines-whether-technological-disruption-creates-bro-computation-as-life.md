## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption produces broad prosperity or concentrated gains is determined mainly by **how the new computation introduced by the technology is distributed across a society’s replication-and-selection system**: prosperity broadens when the capacity to process information, copy successful adaptations, and participate in feedback loops is widely accessible; gains concentrate when those computational capacities are bottlenecked inside a few firms, platforms, protocols, or asset owners.

From this perspective, technology is not primarily “machines replacing labor,” but a **reorganization of who gets to compute, who gets to copy, and who gets to learn from the results**. Every disruption changes the substrate on which economic adaptation happens. It alters the costs of information processing, coordination, prediction, design, and replication. The decisive question is therefore not simply whether productivity rises, but **where the new informational leverage sits in the system**.

If a disruption creates tools that many actors can use to generate, test, and propagate improvements — for example through open standards, diffuse education, interoperable infrastructure, low-cost capital access, and contestable markets — then adaptive capacity spreads. More agents can run meaningful “search” over economic possibilities. More local knowledge enters the system. More people can become producers, not just consumers or subordinate operators. In computational terms, the system gains **parallelism**: many semi-independent nodes explore, innovate, and replicate. Broad prosperity follows because the gains from improved computation are embodied in many livelihoods and organizations.

If instead the disruption depends on centralized data access, proprietary models, closed platforms, IP chokepoints, network effects, regulatory capture, or scarce complementary assets, then the computation is concentrated. The economy still becomes more productive, but adaptation is funneled through a small number of nodes that own the key training data, infrastructure, interfaces, or legal rights. In that case, society’s search process narrows. Others may supply inputs, but cannot easily replicate the high-value computation or retain the learning it generates. Gains then compound at the bottlenecks.

The load-bearing constraint here is that **ownership of outputs matters less than control over recursive learning loops**. A one-time invention can be taxed or redistributed after the fact; but if a small group controls the process by which models improve, users are monitored, workflows are standardized, and future advantages are trained into the system, concentration will reappear. Broad prosperity therefore requires distributing not just income, but **the means of ongoing adaptation**.

So the collision between this computational perspective and the problem illuminates a sharper rule:  
**Technological disruption is egalitarian when it decentralizes the ability to compute-and-adapt; it is oligarchic when it centralizes the feedback loops through which adaptation compounds.**

Policy follows structurally from this: the highest-leverage interventions are those that widen participation in learning loops — education, open technical standards, data portability, anti-monopoly enforcement, interoperable infrastructure, broad capital access, and institutions that let workers and small firms retain and recombine the knowledge generated through use.
