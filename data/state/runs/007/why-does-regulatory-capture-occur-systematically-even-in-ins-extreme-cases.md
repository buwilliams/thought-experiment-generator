## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Regulatory capture occurs systematically because any regulator that remains in sustained contact with a regulated domain is pushed, at the limit, to depend on that domain for the very information, categories, timing, and feasible action-space required to regulate it. The institution may be designed to block bribery, conflicts of interest, or overt political pressure, but unless it can generate an independent knowledge base and independent operational capacity, it will be captured structurally rather than corruptly.

**Boundary test:**  
Push regulation toward the extreme case where the industry is highly complex, fast-moving, and information-dense, while the regulator is slower, thinner, and episodic. At that boundary, the regulator cannot even perceive the object of regulation except through models, data, personnel, and problem-definitions supplied by the regulated entities. “Oversight” then becomes a feedback loop inside the industry’s epistemic frame. Capture is no longer a deviation from the system’s purpose; it is the default outcome of informational dependence.

Push the opposite extreme: imagine a regulator with total independent expertise, independent measurement infrastructure, long time horizons, and the power to experiment without relying on industry cooperation. In that limit, capture weakens dramatically. So the load-bearing variable is not mainly ethics or institutional intent; it is whether the regulator can think and act without borrowing the regulated system’s cognition.

**What follows:**  
1. **Anti-capture rules aimed only at incentives are insufficient.**  
   Bans on gifts, revolving-door restrictions, and transparency rules matter, but they attack peripheral channels. If the regulator must still ask the industry what is happening, what matters, and what is possible, capture survives in sanitized form.

2. **Capture is a systems outcome, not primarily a moral failure.**  
   The relevant stocks and flows are expertise, data, personnel, and agenda-setting authority. When these flow predominantly from industry to regulator, the regulator’s behavior will converge toward industry-compatible judgments even with honest actors.

3. **Institutional design often worsens the problem at the margin.**  
   Efforts to prevent arbitrary state power often fragment regulators, cap salaries, shorten political time horizons, and impose procedural burdens. At the limit, these safeguards reduce independent capacity more than they reduce industry influence, making capture more likely.

4. **The hidden structure is epistemic asymmetry.**  
   The industry lives inside the system continuously; the regulator samples it intermittently. Continuous participants accumulate tacit knowledge, adaptive speed, and problem-framing power. The outsider becomes dependent on the insider’s map.

**Illumination:**  
Regulatory capture persists because “preventing capture” is usually framed as excluding bad motives, when the deeper issue is excluding dependence. A regulator that cannot reproduce, contest, and revise the knowledge of the regulated domain from outside it will eventually regulate in terms the industry can live with. The edge case reveals the rule: where one side monopolizes understanding, it also, in practice, governs.
