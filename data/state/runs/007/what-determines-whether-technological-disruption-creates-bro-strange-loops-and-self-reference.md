## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains depends on who controls the *strange loops* of the economy: the recursive systems by which technology changes institutions, institutions change who can build and benefit from technology, and those beneficiaries then redesign the institutions again.

The key issue is not just ownership of tools, capital, or skills in isolation. It is whether the social system becomes **self-representing at many levels** or only at the top. In disruptive periods, firms, platforms, states, and labor markets increasingly model the population through data, prediction, and coordination systems. If ordinary participants are only *inputs* to these models, gains concentrate. If they are also *authors* of the models that shape them, prosperity broadens.

From the strange-loop perspective, disruption creates a hierarchy: technology alters production; altered production reshapes bargaining power; bargaining power reshapes law, norms, and access; those rules then determine the next generation of technology. Traversing these levels returns to the same question: **who gets to participate in the recursive redesign of the system?** Broad prosperity appears when the loop closes through widely distributed agency — education, capital access, interoperable tools, labor voice, political accountability, and institutional adaptability. Concentration appears when the loop closes through a narrow class that can see, optimize, and revise the system while others are merely optimized.

This illuminates why productivity growth alone does not guarantee shared gains. Productivity is a lower-level fact. Distribution depends on higher-level self-reference: who notices the gains, encodes them into contracts and policy, and feeds those rules back into the next cycle. A society with strong strange loops connecting workers, consumers, entrepreneurs, and policymakers can correct imbalances because those affected can become represented in the system’s own self-model. A society with broken loops — opaque platforms, weak civic institutions, captured regulation, non-portable reputations, exclusion from ownership — cannot. Then disruption amplifies asymmetry: the system becomes better at modeling people than people are at modeling and governing the system.

So the determinant is not “technology” versus “policy” but the **architecture of recursive representation**. Broad prosperity requires institutions that make the economy reflexive in a distributed way: feedback loops that are legible, contestable, and revisable by many participants. Concentrated gains arise when self-reference is monopolized.

In systems terms, the leverage point is the design of the loop itself: ownership structures, standards, governance rights, bargaining mechanisms, and educational pathways that decide whether people remain objects inside technological systems or become subjects who can act back on them. The deepest divide is between disruptions that create **shared self-awareness** in the economy and disruptions that create **centralized self-awareness**.
