## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Market economies become vulnerable to winner-take-all dynamics when they contain **self-reinforcing advantages that are individually rational to feed but collectively harmful to escape**. The lock-in is not just that some firms get ahead; it is that once scale, attention, data, liquidity, reputation, or network position begin compounding, **every participant’s best response is to route even more activity toward the current leaders**, even if most participants would prefer a less concentrated market structure.

From an equilibrium lock-in perspective, the key structural features are:

1. **Strong increasing returns to scale**  
   When average costs fall with size, buyers prefer the biggest provider because it is cheaper or better; sellers then join the biggest platform because that is where buyers are; investors back leaders because returns are safer there. No single participant can defect to a smaller rival without paying a penalty. Concentration becomes the stable equilibrium.

2. **Network effects and coordination dependence**  
   The value of a product depends on how many others use it. This creates a trap: even if users dislike domination by one platform, each user’s best move is still to remain where everyone else already is. Reform requires coordinated migration, which decentralized markets rarely produce spontaneously.

3. **Cumulative advantage through data, brand, and capital access**  
   Success generates assets that improve future success: more users produce more data; more data improve the product; better products attract more users. Likewise, brand lowers customer acquisition costs, and capital markets offer cheaper financing to incumbents. These feedback loops are load-bearing: remove them and dominance weakens; keep them and competition tends to tip.

4. **High switching costs and ecosystem bundling**  
   Lock-in is intensified when moving away from a dominant firm means losing contacts, compatibility, reputation, learned routines, or complementary services. Then even dissatisfied users rationally stay put. The equilibrium persists not because everyone approves, but because unilateral exit is too costly.

5. **Positional competition for scarce attention**  
   In markets where visibility is scarce, small early leads can snowball. Consumers use popularity as a quality proxy; platforms amplify what already performs; advertisers and creators follow the traffic. This does not merely reward quality — it structurally converts relative advantage into runaway concentration.

What follows is that winner-take-all outcomes are often **not best explained by greed, irrationality, or isolated policy mistakes**, but by **equilibrium structures in which decentralised best responses reproduce concentration**. This matters because it changes the reform target. If the problem is lock-in, then better intentions or more entry alone will not fix it. The leverage points are structural: interoperability, portability, anti-bundling rules, limits on data advantages, alternative coordination mechanisms, and institutions that reduce the cost of collective switching.

So the illumination is: market economies are vulnerable to winner-take-all dynamics where competition itself becomes a mechanism for **amplifying incumbency**, and where no actor can rationally choose the more competitive outcome unless many others move at once.
