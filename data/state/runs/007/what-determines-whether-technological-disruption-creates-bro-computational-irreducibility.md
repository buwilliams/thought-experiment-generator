## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption yields broad prosperity or concentrated gains depends less on the technology’s raw productivity than on the **computational structure of the social system that absorbs it**: specifically, whether access, bargaining power, and adaptation can be recomputed locally by many actors, or only through long, path-dependent sequences controlled by a few institutions.

From the perspective of computational irreducibility, disruption is not something whose distributive effects can be cleanly inferred from first principles — “automation increases output, therefore everyone benefits” or “platforms create monopolies, therefore gains concentrate” are too compressed. The actual outcome is generated by many interacting updates: firm strategy, labor-market adjustment, legal interpretation, capital allocation, standards formation, network effects, education, urban geography, and political bargaining. In many cases, there is no shortcut around this process. The distributional result must be “run” through the institutional system.

What follows is that broad prosperity is more likely when the system’s generative rules let many participants adapt **without requiring centralized permission or rare strategic position**. Examples of such rules include portable skills, open standards, competitive capital access, labor mobility, interoperable platforms, diffused ownership, and institutions that lower the number of sequential gates between innovation and participation. These reduce the degree to which benefits must pass through a narrow computational bottleneck.

Concentrated gains arise when disruption compounds through **irreversible or hard-to-replicate sequences**: winner-take-most network effects, proprietary data flywheels, control of key infrastructure, IP thickets, regulatory capture, or labor displacement that requires long retraining chains with weak support. In such systems, small early advantages cannot be bypassed; everyone else must traverse the same costly sequence step by step, while incumbents already occupy the critical states. The result is not merely inequality as a side effect, but concentration as the natural output of the system’s update rule.

This reframes policy. The key question is not “How do we predict all downstream effects of a new technology?” but “What are the minimal rules by which gains propagate?” If the social operating system routes value through a few nodes, disruption will likely enrich those nodes no matter how productive the technology is. If the rules allow rapid, parallel recombination by many actors, prosperity spreads.

So the leverage point is structural: redesign institutions to make economic adaptation more **parallel, modular, and reversible**. Broad prosperity depends on whether people can join the new computation early and cheaply, not merely on whether the computation produces abundance in the aggregate. Unpredictability remains — irreducibility means we cannot fully forecast each disruption — but we can still improve the generative rule that governs who gets to benefit as the system unfolds.
