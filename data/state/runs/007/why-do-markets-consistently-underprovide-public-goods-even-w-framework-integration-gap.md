## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods because the institutional “coupling” between individual willingness to pay and collective provision is structurally weak, in the same way the cosmological constant problem suggests a weak coupling between microscopic vacuum contributions and macroscopic gravitating stress-energy. The key issue is not merely a numerical gap between “what people want” and “what gets provided,” but a bad integration between layers of description.

At the microscopic layer, each participant may genuinely value the good. But at the macroscopic layer, provision requires a mechanism that converts dispersed preferences into binding, revenue-backed action. For private goods, markets supply this coupling: exclusion, rivalry, and price make an individual’s payment load-bearing for production. For public goods, that link breaks. One person’s payment is not tightly connected to one person’s benefit, and one person’s refusal does not exclude them from consumption. So the market lacks a constraint that would force individual preference to aggregate into collective provision.

This reframes the classic free-rider problem. Free riding is not just bad incentives in an otherwise sound system; it is evidence that the mapping from micro motives to macro outcomes is underconstrained. If I can benefit whether or not I contribute, then my expressed valuation is not load-bearing in the production decision. A system built from such weakly load-bearing contributions will systematically fail to track shared preference.

What follows is that underprovision is not an anomaly within ordinary market logic but a prediction of its structure. Markets are optimized for goods whose benefits and costs can be localized to transacting parties. Public goods violate that architecture by generating benefits as non-excludable system-wide spillovers. The market therefore “sees” only the privately capturable fraction of total value. The missing provision is not hidden irrationality; it is value that exists at the system level but has no adequate transmission channel into decentralized bids.

This perspective also sharpens what counts as a real solution. Mere subsidies or moral appeals are often patch-like: they try to offset the numerical shortfall without repairing the broken coupling. More integrated institutions — taxation, compulsory contributions, matching rules, assurance contracts, club-like partial excludability, constitutional commitments — are stronger because they alter the structural link between individual action and collective outcome. In a load-bearing design, changing contribution incentives forces changes in provision, and changing provision rules forces changes in contribution behavior.

So the illumination is this: public-goods failure is a problem of ontology and integration. Preference, payment, and provision are treated by markets as if they belong to one coherent system, but for public goods they do not. Underprovision persists because the bridge from individual valuation to collective production is too weakly built to carry the explanatory load.
