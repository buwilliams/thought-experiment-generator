## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Economic inequality persists across generations because wealth and status function less like a stock of resources that can be periodically rebalanced and more like an information-bearing control layer embedded in a broader developmental system. Redistribution alters some downstream resource flows, but unless it also changes the “interpreter systems” that make inherited advantage actionable, inequality is reconstructed.

From this perspective, the key question is not just “what is transferred?” but “what problem did inherited advantage originally solve?” Historically, family wealth, class position, and social standing solved the problem of developmental reliability under uncertainty: they stabilized offspring trajectories by securing nutrition, safety, training, alliances, and access to institutions. Over time, societies evolved many interpreter systems that convert these signals into durable outcomes: schools that reward prior cultural familiarity, neighborhoods that concentrate peer effects and safety, employers that read credentials and mannerisms as competence, legal systems that protect asset continuity, and social networks that transmit opportunities before markets visibly clear.

So inequality propagates because these interpreter systems are layered. Money is one signal among others, but it is powerful because it can buy entry into environments that recursively generate more legible signals of merit. Families do not merely pass on wealth; they shape developmental contexts — language exposure, expectations, risk tolerance, institutional fluency, health, timing, and contacts — that institutions then interpret as talent or deservingness. Redistributive mechanisms often target the stock variable of income after the fact, while leaving intact the upstream control architecture that governs capability formation and opportunity recognition.

What follows is that inequality is not mainly reproduced by a single barrier but by a multi-layer regulatory system with feedback loops. Early advantage increases the probability of landing in institutions that amplify advantage; those institutions then certify outcomes in ways that appear individually earned, which politically stabilizes the very interpreter systems doing the conversion. Meanwhile, disadvantaged families face noisier developmental conditions, so equal formal access does not produce equal effective access.

This lens illuminates why modest redistribution can reduce hardship without equalizing life trajectories. It changes available fuel but not the developmental program that determines who can convert fuel into compounding gains. The load-bearing claim here is that if interpreter systems were different — if schools, hiring, housing, inheritance law, and early-childhood environments did not convert small inherited differences into large trajectory divergences — then the same level of private wealth inequality would propagate less strongly. Conversely, if one only increases transfers while preserving the interpreters, inequality reappears in adapted form.

Therefore, the structural leverage is upstream: redesign the institutions that decode family background into future advantage, not just the tax-and-transfer layer that compensates after decoding has already occurred.
