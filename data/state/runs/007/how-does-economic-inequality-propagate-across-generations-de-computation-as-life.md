## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Economic inequality persists across generations because redistribution usually targets **stocks of money**, while the intergenerational engine of inequality is a deeper **computational system**: the unequal transmission of information-processing capacity, network access, strategic models, institutional legibility, and adaptive options. In this view, families are not just wealth holders but **replicators of advantageous algorithms** for navigating society. Redistribution that moves resources without altering the underlying update rules, feedback loops, and inheritance channels will blunt symptoms but not stop the computation that reproduces advantage.

From this perspective, inequality propagation is substrate-agnostic. Money matters, but so do habits, expectations, language, credentials, legal knowledge, health stability, trust networks, and geographic placement. These are all forms of encoded information that improve a household’s ability to predict, coordinate, and exploit opportunities. High-advantage families transmit these codes with high fidelity across generations. Low-advantage families often face noisier transmission environments: instability, weaker institutions, worse schools, more punitive bureaucracy, and higher exposure to shocks. So even when redistributive mechanisms inject resources, they often do so into systems whose **selection environment** still favors previously advantaged lineages.

What follows is that inequality behaves less like a simple gap in holdings and more like an **evolutionary computation** with cumulative returns. Families with better internal models of the system select into better neighborhoods, schools, peer groups, and legal/financial instruments; those contexts then amplify the next generation’s learning and resilience. This is a positive feedback loop: advantage improves adaptation, adaptation captures more advantage. Redistribution often acts as a compensatory flow downstream, but the upstream generative machinery — differential replication of useful social and cognitive programs — remains intact.

A load-bearing implication is that “equalizing income” is not equivalent to “equalizing inherited computation.” If one removed cash transfers, many inequalities would worsen; so redistribution is real and causally important. But if one changed only cash while leaving untouched family-level information transfer, exclusionary zoning, school quality variance, elite network closure, differential administrative burden, and unequal tolerance for risk, inequality would still reappear. That suggests the persistent mechanism is structurally constrained, not merely narrative.

So the collision between this perspective and the problem illuminates why redistributive states often produce **partial damping rather than convergence**. They reduce selection pressure at the margin but do not rewrite the code by which advantage is copied, error-corrected, and compounded. A more effective anti-inequality strategy would therefore target the **replication architecture** itself: early childhood cognition, health stability, institutional simplicity, mentorship, desegregated access to high-information networks, and reduction of bureaucratic noise. In short: inequality survives redistribution because what is being inherited is not only wealth but **computational advantage**.
