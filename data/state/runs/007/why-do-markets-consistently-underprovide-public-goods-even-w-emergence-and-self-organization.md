## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods not mainly because individuals lack goodwill or information, but because the interaction structure of market exchange cannot reliably convert dispersed preferences for *shared outcomes* into effective demand. The failure is emergent: it arises from the feedback pattern linking exclusion, payment, and benefit.

A market works well when the good is structurally coupled to individual purchase: *I pay, I get*. That coupling creates a reinforcing loop between willingness to pay, producer revenue, and continued supply. Public goods break that loop. Their benefits are non-excludable and non-rival, so *whether or not I pay*, I still receive most of the benefit once the good exists. The system-level result is that each participant’s best local move is to under-contribute relative to their global preference.

This is not just “free riding” as an individual moral defect. It is a structural misalignment in the network of incentives. The relevant emergent property is: **collective preference fails to aggregate into provision because the market only registers preferences that can be attached to exclusion-backed transactions**. Preferences for a shared world-state — clean air, national defense, basic research, herd immunity — are real, but the market’s signaling architecture filters them out unless they can be privatized.

What follows is that even universal preference for provision does not solve the problem. If everyone prefers that the public good exist, each still confronts the same local feedback: their own payment has diffuse impact, while their own savings are concentrated and immediate. In system terms, the costs are individualized and salient; the benefits are distributed and delayed. That asymmetry generates a stable equilibrium of underprovision.

This also explains why merely improving altruism, information, or price discovery often has weak effects. Those change node-level properties, but the failure is load-bearing in the interaction structure. Unless contribution is linked to universal participation, assurance, coercive pooling, or some other mechanism that rewires the feedback loops, the system continues to produce underprovision.

So the explanatory shift is this: the puzzle is not “Why won’t people pay for what they want?” but “Why would a transaction system built to mediate excludable private exchange be expected to supply non-excludable collective conditions?” Once framed at the system level, underprovision is no longer paradoxical. It is the normal emergent behavior of a market architecture whose information and incentive channels are tuned to private goods, not shared ones.

Therefore, effective provision of public goods requires institutions that alter the interaction structure — taxation, mandates, clubs, cooperative commitments, matching schemes, or state production — because the missing ingredient is not preference but a mechanism that makes collective benefit legible and binding within the system.
