## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Markets underprovide public goods not mainly because people are selfish, but because the interaction structure around public goods is usually too weakly iterated, too anonymously observed, and too weakly coupled to future rewards for cooperative provision to stabilize.

From an iterated-game perspective, cooperation emerges when today’s contribution affects tomorrow’s treatment. Public goods break that linkage. A person can enjoy the good whether or not they paid, so defection is hard to punish through ordinary market exchange. The key structural fact is not just “free riding” in the static sense, but that the repeated game is badly designed for reciprocity.

In ordinary private-goods markets, exchange is tightly reciprocal: if I do not pay, I do not get the good. The game is effectively self-enforcing in each round. With public goods, consumption is non-excludable, so the rounds do not naturally carry usable signals about who cooperated and who defected. That destroys the informational basis for contingent strategies like “I’ll contribute if others do” or “I’ll stop cooperating with defectors.” If defection cannot be clearly observed, remembered, and answered in later rounds, the shadow of the future is too weak to sustain contribution.

What follows is that even when all participants prefer a world where the public good exists, each participant faces a repeated incentive to wait for others to bear the cost. This is not a contradiction in preferences; it is a prediction about equilibrium under poor feedback and low enforceability. Everyone may prefer the cooperative outcome, while no one can reliably make their contribution conditional on others’ future behavior in a way that protects them from exploitation.

This perspective also explains why public-good provision improves when institutions lengthen time horizons and sharpen observability: stable communities, reputation systems, clubs, subscription models, local governance, and taxation all partially transform the public-goods game into one with clearer repeated consequences. They do not merely “add morality”; they alter the game so that contribution becomes linked to future standing, access, or reciprocal treatment.

So the underprovision is illuminated as a structural failure of reciprocity. Markets excel where exchange can be broken into repeated, monitorable, bilateral bargains. Public goods sit where that architecture collapses: benefits are diffuse, defections are cheap and often invisible, and future interactions do not reliably discipline present non-contribution. Shorten horizons further, increase anonymity, or blur who paid, and provision should fall even if participants remain equally civic-minded. Conversely, make contribution visible and future-relevant, and cooperation should rise without needing to assume better people.
