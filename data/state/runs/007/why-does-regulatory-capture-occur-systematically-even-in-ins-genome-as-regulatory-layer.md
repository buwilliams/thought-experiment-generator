## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture recurs because rules and formal mandates are not the true “blueprint” of governance; they are only one information-bearing layer inside a larger evolved control system whose effective behavior is determined by the institutions that interpret, update, and enforce them. Anti-capture design fails when it treats statutory text, ethics rules, or agency structure as sufficient code, while the real regulatory phenotype is produced by layered interpretation: staffing pipelines, professional norms, industry-generated data, legal doctrines, budget dependencies, revolving-door career incentives, and crisis-response routines.

From this perspective, the key question is: **what problem did each governance component originally solve?** Many features that later enable capture were adaptive solutions to real coordination problems. Agencies rely on industry expertise because the environment is too complex for complete internal knowledge. They standardize around industry categories because action requires legible objects of regulation. They develop stable relationships with regulated firms because compliance depends on ongoing information exchange rather than one-shot command. They defer to technical specialists and incumbent actors because constant adversarial reconstruction of knowledge is too costly. These arrangements solve the problem of making regulation possible at all.

But once those interpreter systems exist, the formal anti-capture layer becomes underdetermining. A rule prohibiting favoritism still needs operational definitions of evidence, risk, feasibility, and “reasonable compliance.” Those meanings are supplied by surrounding systems, and those systems are precisely where regulated parties can exert selective pressure. In genome terms, the statute is not enough; expression depends on the regulatory network around it. Capture therefore appears not mainly as corruption added from outside, but as **developmental canalization** of institutions toward the actors that provide the most usable inputs to their interpretive machinery.

What follows is that capture is systematic where there is a persistent asymmetry in **control over regulatory context**: who trains staff, supplies models, defines metrics, threatens exit, offers post-agency employment, litigates ambiguities, and shapes what counts as realistic. Under those conditions, even sincerely designed safeguards are reinterpreted through a surrounding control architecture that was built to solve other problems—competence, speed, predictability, administrability.

This explains why simply adding more rules often fails. More encoded constraint increases dependence on interpreters, and if those interpreters are already developmentally coupled to the regulated domain, complexity can deepen capture rather than prevent it. The load-bearing claim here is that capture is not primarily a failure of moral intent or insufficient legal detail; it is a structural consequence of **layered regulatory dependence**.

So the leverage point is not just stricter prohibition, but redesign of the interpreter system: independent knowledge generation, non-industry career ladders, public-interest counter-expertise, randomized audits, adversarial review structures, different budget feedback loops, and institutional memory insulated from regulated parties. In short: anti-capture succeeds only when the control system that gives rules meaning is itself made less developmentally dependent on the regulated actors.
