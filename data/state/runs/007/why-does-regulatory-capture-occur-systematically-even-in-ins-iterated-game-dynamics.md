## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture is systematic because regulation is not a one-shot design problem but an iterated game between regulators and regulated firms, and the repeated structure gives the regulated side stronger incentives, denser feedback, and longer strategic memory than the public or the formal anti-capture safeguards. What looks like “failure of rules” is often the equilibrium of a repeated interaction.

**Why this follows from the perspective:**

In an iterated setting, cooperation emerges where actors expect future encounters, can observe each other’s behavior, and can reward or punish over time. Regulators and firms meet repeatedly, exchange information continuously, depend on each other operationally, and face a long horizon of future interaction. This creates the structural conditions for stable cooperation between them. But that “cooperation” need not serve the public purpose of the institution; it can instead become tacit collusion.

The regulated industry has concentrated stakes, so it can sustain a long game: invest in relationships, provide expertise, offer career paths, and reward interpretive flexibility over many rounds. Regulators, by contrast, often have limited information and rely on firms for data, technical understanding, and implementation. This repeated dependence creates reciprocity pressures: today’s adversarial enforcement risks tomorrow’s information drought, bureaucratic obstruction, reputational conflict, or loss of future employment options. In repeated games, players accommodate those who can retaliate in future rounds.

Meanwhile, the public—the nominal principal—plays a much weaker iterated game. Citizens are diffuse, inattentive, and usually observe only coarse outcomes with long delays. Their horizon is politically punctuated rather than continuous. So the regulator’s effective repeated counterpart is not “the public” but the industry. Anti-capture mechanisms often fail because they are designed as static constraints—formal independence, ethics rules, transparency requirements—inside a dynamic game where the real leverage comes from repeated interaction, information asymmetry, and future reciprocity.

This also predicts when capture worsens: when enforcement depends heavily on firm-supplied expertise; when oversight is opaque; when personnel circulate repeatedly between regulator and industry; when political time horizons are short; and when public monitoring is noisy. Shortening the regulator-public “shadow of the future” while lengthening the regulator-industry one shifts equilibrium further toward capture.

So the key illumination is: regulatory capture persists not despite institutional design, but because the institution sits inside a repeated game whose payoff structure anti-capture rules do not alter enough. If you want less capture, don’t just add prohibitions; restructure the iteration—change who regulators repeatedly depend on, increase independent information channels, extend accountability horizons, and make defections against the public legible and punishable across rounds.
