## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Economic inequality persists across generations because redistribution acts mostly on **first-order holdings** (income, transfers, taxes), while intergenerational advantage is reproduced through a **strange loop of self-reference**: wealth, status, and institutional power recursively shape the very categories, expectations, and mechanisms by which redistribution is designed, interpreted, and limited. The system does not merely distribute resources; it distributes the capacity to define what counts as a resource, what counts as merit, and what levels of inequality are normal.

More concretely: a hierarchy of levels appears to exist — individuals earn, governments tax, institutions redistribute, families transmit opportunity. But traversing these levels returns us to the starting point, because advantaged families do not only pass on money; they pass on **models of the system** and privileged positions within it. Those models then influence schooling, law, neighborhoods, social networks, financial literacy, political access, and cultural confidence. These, in turn, affect who captures future income and who can insulate assets from redistribution. Thus redistribution occurs inside a loop already bent by inherited self-reference.

The load-bearing claim is that **inequality is not primarily a stock problem but a recursive control problem**. If it were only about stocks, sufficient transfer should flatten outcomes over time. But unequal agents also possess unequal powers of self-representation: they can anticipate policy, reorganize assets, purchase positional goods, shape institutions, and convert one form of capital into another faster than redistributive systems can track. The wealthy transmit not just wealth but **strategies for preserving wealth under the description “fair competition.”** This makes the system self-stabilizing.

What follows is that redistributive mechanisms often fail not because they are absent, but because they intervene at the wrong logical level. They treat inequality as an output to be corrected after the fact, while the loop reproducing inequality operates at the level of **identity formation, institutional legibility, and rule-shaping**. Children inherit a place in a feedback structure: some are raised inside institutions that mirror back efficacy and belonging; others encounter institutions that encode them as risky, deficient, or expensive. That recursive difference compounds.

This perspective illuminates why modest redistribution can coexist with durable hierarchy: the system can refund losses while preserving the **self-reinforcing architecture of advantage**. A transfer may reduce hardship without altering who gets to navigate, predict, and redesign the system. So long as some groups inherit a superior ability to model and influence the game, inequality reappears even after partial correction.

Therefore, durable equalization would require interventions that break the loop of self-referential advantage: not only taxing outcomes, but democratizing the capacities by which people understand, access, and recursively reshape the institutions that generate those outcomes.
