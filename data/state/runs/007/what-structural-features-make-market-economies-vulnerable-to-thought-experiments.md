## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a minimal market economy with one key variable isolated: **consumer choice is even slightly sensitive to relative popularity**. Hold everything else ordinary — many buyers and sellers, legal competition, no corruption, no explicit monopoly privilege, roughly similar product quality.

Assume this sensitivity is real. Then each additional customer does two things at once: it raises current revenue **and** makes the seller more attractive to future customers. Popularity becomes not just an outcome of success but an input into further success.

Follow the logic. A firm that gets an early lead — by luck, timing, branding, distribution, or a small quality edge — gains extra demand. That demand funds lower prices, better placement, more data, more complementary services, and stronger reputation. Those improvements attract still more users. This is a **positive feedback loop**. The market no longer rewards only “who is best” but “who is already ahead.”

From that point, several structural features make winner-take-all dynamics likely:

1. **Increasing returns to scale**: if serving additional customers gets cheaper or more valuable at scale, large firms improve faster than small ones.
2. **Network effects**: if a product is more useful because others use it, market share itself becomes quality.
3. **High fixed costs, low marginal costs**: once the system is built, one firm can serve almost everyone cheaply, making concentration efficient in the short run.
4. **Data and learning effects**: more users generate more information, which improves the product, which attracts more users.
5. **Switching costs and lock-in**: even mild user friction stabilizes early winners and prevents correction.
6. **Attention scarcity**: buyers cannot evaluate everything, so they use proxies like rank, brand, or installed base, amplifying visible leaders.

The crucial structural point is that **competition changes character**. In ordinary textbook competition, success is self-limiting: a firm grows, rivals imitate, profits compress. But in this scenario, success is self-reinforcing. The market ceases to be mainly a selection mechanism among alternatives and becomes an **amplifier of small asymmetries**.

So the vulnerability is not simply “big firms become powerful.” It is deeper: market structure can convert contingent early differences into durable dominance whenever **demand, cost, and coordination all bend in favor of the already-large**.

What follows is that winner-take-all outcomes are not necessarily signs of superior merit nor merely policy failures. They can arise from the internal logic of markets with strong reinforcing feedbacks. The load-bearing variable is not greed, nor even inequality, but the presence of **self-reinforcing accumulation mechanisms**. Remove those, and dominance becomes harder to sustain. Add them, and concentration can emerge even under fair rules.
