## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Winner-take-all dynamics are not an accidental excess of markets; they arise where markets inherit institutional solutions designed to solve older coordination problems — especially standardization, trust, and scale — and those solutions quietly convert ordinary advantages into self-reinforcing dominance.

Ask how the modern market came to exist. It did not emerge to maximize dispersion of opportunity. It evolved to solve prior problems: how strangers trade, how quality is made legible, how transactions become cheap, and how production scales beyond local craft. To solve those problems, markets built infrastructures: common standards, property rights, firms, brands, financial capital, mass media, platforms, and legal regimes that make exchange comparable and repeatable.

Those inherited structures carry a hidden bias. They reduce friction by making goods, sellers, and consumers commensurable — but once comparison is easy, small differences in visibility, reputation, or cost can be amplified across the whole system. A local edge no longer stays local.

The load-bearing structural features are these:

1. **High fixed costs with low marginal costs.** This originated as a solution to scale and coordination in industrial production and now in software, media, and platforms. Once the costly system is built, serving one more customer is cheap, so the largest player can spread costs over the whole market and undercut rivals.

2. **Standardization and interoperability.** These solved the problem of fragmented exchange. But standardization also makes markets more contestable on a single dimension — price, speed, visibility, compatibility — enabling one firm to dominate if it gets slightly ahead.

3. **Network effects.** They solve the trust and matching problem: people go where others already are. But this creates positive feedback loops. The largest network becomes better simply by being larger.

4. **Capital concentration.** Large pools of finance evolved to fund scale-intensive enterprise. But access to capital lets firms survive losses, buy distribution, acquire rivals, and turn temporary advantage into permanent position.

5. **Reputation and attention bottlenecks.** Brands, rankings, and platforms arose because buyers cannot evaluate everything. These filters reduce search costs, but they funnel attention to a few visible winners, making discovery itself unequal.

6. **Legal modularity of the firm.** The corporation solved continuity, risk pooling, and long-horizon investment. But it also enables accumulation across markets, allowing dominant firms to leverage power from one domain into another.

What follows is that winner-take-all outcomes are strongest where these inherited solutions align into reinforcing loops: scale lowers cost, low cost attracts users, users increase network value, network value attracts capital, capital buys more attention and acquisitions.

So the real vulnerability is structural: markets designed to solve coordination at scale often embed mechanisms that transform efficiency gains into dominance. The hidden assumption is that reducing friction is unambiguously good. But in many markets, friction was also a dispersal mechanism. Remove it entirely, and you do not get neutral competition; you get runaway cumulative advantage.
