## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture persists not mainly because bad actors infiltrate good institutions, but because the regulator–industry arrangement is itself a coupled system whose feedback structure systematically produces convergence. Anti-capture design aimed at the character or independence of individual officials leaves intact the interaction pattern that generates capture.

**What is true from this perspective**

A regulator is not outside the industry it regulates. It sits inside a dense network of information, incentives, career pathways, legal challenges, lobbying, crisis response, and performance metrics. In that network, the regulated industry is typically:

- more informed about operational details,
- more continuously present than diffuse publics,
- better able to reward or punish,
- and better able to define what counts as a “realistic” policy.

These are not merely advantages possessed by components; they are structural asymmetries in repeated interaction.

**The emergent mechanism**

Capture emerges from several reinforcing feedback loops:

1. **Information dependence loop**  
   The more complex the sector, the more regulators depend on firms for usable knowledge. That dependence makes industry categories and assumptions the default frame. Regulation then becomes calibrated to industry legibility, increasing future dependence.

2. **Access and attention loop**  
   Concentrated firms have high stakes and engage continuously; the public has diffuse stakes and engages sporadically. Regulators therefore receive more feedback, warnings, data, and relationship-maintenance from industry than from citizens. What is most present becomes what seems most urgent and concrete.

3. **Career circulation loop**  
   If expertise is produced largely inside the industry, then staffing regulators requires drawing from industry-trained people, and post-regulatory careers often return there. Even without corruption, this aligns worldview, professional norms, and perceived feasibility with industry interests.

4. **Accountability asymmetry loop**  
   Regulators are punished sharply for visible disruption, legal defeat, market instability, or service failures, but are punished weakly for long-run public harms that are diffuse and hard to attribute. This selects for accommodation over confrontation.

These loops generate an emergent property: the regulator increasingly optimizes for system stability as defined by incumbents, which is then experienced as “capture.”

**What follows**

Institutions explicitly designed to prevent capture often fail because they target the wrong level. Ethics rules, recusals, formal independence, and transparency can matter, but if they do not alter the feedback structure, they are absorbed by the system. A formally independent agency can still be informationally dependent, professionally colonized, and asymmetrically accountable.

**What this illuminates**

Capture is best understood less as a moral deviation than as an attractor state of a particular governance architecture. The load-bearing explanation is the structure of repeated interactions under unequal concentration of knowledge, attention, and consequence. Therefore the main leverage is structural: diversify knowledge supply, create durable counter-parties for public interests, alter career pathways, change what regulators are measured and punished for, and reduce dependence on incumbent-defined expertise. If the interaction structure remains the same, capture will recur even with better people and better rules.
