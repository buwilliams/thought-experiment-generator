## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Whether technological disruption produces broad prosperity or concentrated gains depends less on the technology itself than on whether the surrounding economic game is **iterated, legible, and reputation-sensitive** across firms, workers, platforms, investors, and the state. When the gains from innovation are embedded in repeated relationships, actors have incentives to share surplus, invest in counterparties, and preserve future cooperation. When disruption is organized as a sequence of effectively one-shot interactions, gains concentrate in the hands of those who can defect early, hide extraction, or exit before retaliation arrives.

**Why this follows from iterated game dynamics:**  
In repeated games, today’s treatment of workers, suppliers, customers, and communities affects tomorrow’s access to talent, trust, regulatory tolerance, and complementary cooperation. That creates structural pressure toward strategies like profit-sharing, training, long-term contracting, interoperability, and restraint in monopoly behavior. Broad prosperity is not mainly a moral achievement here; it is the equilibrium outcome of a system where participants expect to meet again and can condition future cooperation on present conduct.

By contrast, concentrated gains emerge when disruption shortens horizons or breaks visibility. If founders can cash out before downstream harms appear, if platforms can atomize labor into replaceable gigs, if financial markets reward quarterly extraction over long-term ecosystem health, or if consumers cannot observe exploitative or exclusionary practices, then the game becomes closer to one-shot. In that structure, the rational strategy is to capture network effects, suppress bargaining power, externalize adjustment costs, and leave others with the losses.

**What this illuminates:**  
The key variable is not “innovation vs regulation” but the **shadow of the future**. Institutions that lengthen time horizons and make defection observable tend to diffuse technological gains. These include portable benefits, worker voice, antitrust against lock-in, transparent supply chains, long-duration ownership, open standards, and tax or legal rules that tie innovators’ fortunes to the long-run performance of the wider system. Such mechanisms convert disruption from a raid into an ongoing relationship.

**Prediction:**  
Broad prosperity will arise where technological adopters cannot prosper without maintaining repeated cooperation with those displaced or subordinated by the technology. Concentrated gains will arise where technology enables scalable defection: hiding surplus extraction, reducing counterparties to interchangeable units, accelerating exit, and severing accountability.

So the decisive question is: **Does the disruption create a durable cooperative game among stakeholders, or does it let winners treat everyone else as a one-time opportunity?** That structural distinction, more than intent or ideology, determines the distribution of gains.
