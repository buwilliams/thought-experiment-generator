## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Regulatory capture persists not mainly because institutions fail to *forbid* bias, but because they systematically misinfer what observed behavior means under uncertainty: they treat visible procedural compliance as strong evidence of independence, while underweighting the prior probability that regulators and regulated firms will become informationally and socially entangled over time.

In Bayesian terms, institutions often reason as if:

- “This agency has ethics rules, disclosure requirements, staggered terms, and formal transparency”
- therefore
- “This agency is probably not captured.”

But that confuses **P(safeguards | independence)** with **P(independence | safeguards)**. Anti-capture features are indeed likely in genuinely independent institutions. But they are also likely in captured or capture-prone ones, because modern legitimacy requires them. So the data are weakly discriminating. A captured institution can easily display the same formal markers as an uncaptured one.

The neglected term is the **prior**: in any setting where regulators must repeatedly depend on the regulated for expertise, data, implementation feedback, and future career pathways, the base rate of capture pressure is high. If the prior probability of capture is already substantial, then procedural safeguards update us only modestly unless they change the underlying incentive and information structure.

This illuminates why capture is systematic even in institutions designed against it. The design often targets the wrong likelihood. It asks: “What would an independent regulator look like?” and then builds those appearances. But the real question is: “What observations would be much less likely if capture were occurring?” Those are harder to produce cosmetically. For example:

- persistent decisions contrary to concentrated industry interests,
- independent in-house technical capacity,
- career structures not dependent on industry exit options,
- adversarial information pipelines rather than consultative monopolies,
- outcome measures showing benefits to diffuse publics despite elite opposition.

These are more informative because they lower **P(observation | capture)**, not merely raise **P(observation | independence)**.

From a systems view, the high prior for capture comes from structure: concentrated benefits for firms, diffuse costs for the public, asymmetric expertise, revolving doors, and repeated interaction. These generate a feedback loop in which industry supplies the information used to evaluate the regulator, shaping both policy and the evidence by which policy is judged. That makes capture self-confirming: the institution sees “competence,” “pragmatism,” and “stakeholder alignment,” and updates toward trust, even though those observations are also highly probable under capture.

So the load-bearing claim is this: **regulatory capture is systematic because anti-capture design usually operates at the level of symbolic indicators, while the Bayesian base rates are set by deeper structural dependencies.** Unless design alters those priors and produces evidence that would be unlikely under capture, institutions will continue mistaking legibility for independence.
