## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a world in which redistributive policy works unusually well on the obvious variable: income. Taxes are progressive, public education is free, healthcare is universal, and poor families receive transfers sufficient to prevent deprivation. Now isolate the key remaining variable: **control over the conditions under which knowledge, expectations, and opportunities are formed**.

Assume that this control is real and unequally distributed. Some families can reliably place children in high-information environments: homes with time, stability, adult attention, dense social networks, institutional fluency, low chaos, and strong expectations of agency. Other families, even after transfers, live with greater volatility: insecure housing, fragmented time, weaker institutional trust, fewer effective models of long-term planning, and less access to people who can convert abstract possibilities into concrete opportunities.

Follow the logic. Redistribution mainly alters **flows of money**. Intergenerational inequality is reproduced by **stocks of navigational capacity and positional access**: habits of explanation, confidence in dealing with institutions, inherited networks, knowledge of which risks are survivable, and ownership of scarce gateways such as neighborhoods, credentials, internships, and reputation. These stocks are not easily purchased at the margin, and they compound through feedback loops.

The child with inherited navigational capacity uses public systems better, not because the systems are formally biased at every point, but because they are complex and require interpretation. Equal schools do not produce equal outcomes if one child arrives with better error-correction machinery: adults who notice problems early, contest bad placements, arrange enrichment, normalize delayed gratification, and translate failure into iteration rather than identity. Redistribution softens losses; it does not equalize the ability to convert institutions into advantage.

Then add scarcity. Many desirable outcomes are positional: admission to selective programs, access to safe neighborhoods, elite signaling, influential mentors. If redistribution raises the floor without changing the scarcity structure, advantaged families redirect effort into the next bottleneck. Inequality therefore migrates. It is reproduced less through brute deprivation than through superior control of adaptation to whatever rules currently matter.

So the conjecture is: **economic inequality persists across generations despite redistribution because the main propagating variable is not money itself but unequal inherited command over knowledge-rich environments and positional systems, which function as self-reinforcing stocks.** Redistributive mechanisms often reduce suffering without breaking these feedback loops. To interrupt transmission, policy would have to target the production of agency, stability, institutional legibility, and access to non-monetary gateways — not merely post hoc income differences.

What this perspective illuminates is that inequality is not just a gap in resources; it is a gap in the capacity to shape the pathways through which resources become durable advantage.
