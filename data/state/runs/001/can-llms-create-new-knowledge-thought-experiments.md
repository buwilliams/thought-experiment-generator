# Conjecture: can-llms-create-new-knowledge × thought-experiments

## Conjecture

## Conjecture: LLMs Create Knowledge When the Process Does

**The Isolating Scenario**

Suppose we extract a single capability from an LLM: it can identify non-obvious structural patterns across large conceptual spaces and output novel combinatorial claims — claims whose truth-value was not explicitly encoded in training data.

Assume this is real. Now follow the logic strictly.

---

**The Argument**

Knowledge, under a Deutschian framework, is not stored information — it is *explanatory structure* that tracks reality. New knowledge comes into existence when a new explanation emerges that was not deducible from prior explanations by trivial transformation.

The question is not whether LLMs can produce *outputs that are new to a user*. That's trivially yes and trivially uninteresting. The question is: can the process instantiate genuine explanatory novelty?

Here is where the logic leads, not where intuition expects:

The standard objection is that LLMs recombine training data, so outputs are not genuinely novel. But this objection, applied consistently, destroys human knowledge-creation too. Human minds recombine prior concepts. Evolution recombines prior genetic structures. The recombination objection is not principled — it's a heuristic applied asymmetrically.

The *actual* disqualifying criterion would be: does the process include error-correction against reality? Conjecture alone doesn't constitute knowledge. The critical step — the step that makes knowledge knowledge — is the *criticism loop*: the claim is exposed to tests that can refute it.

Now follow this strictly:

An LLM producing a conjecture is doing exactly what a human theorist does *before* criticism occurs. A human generating a hypothesis in isolation has not yet created knowledge either — they've created a *candidate*. Knowledge is produced by the system: conjecture + criticism + survival.

**Therefore:** The question "Can LLMs create knowledge?" is malformed. The correct question is: *Can a system including an LLM create knowledge?*

And the answer is clearly yes — it already happens. A researcher uses an LLM to generate a non-obvious hypothesis. The hypothesis is tested. It survives criticism. New explanatory structure exists that did not exist before. The LLM was a causal node in that process.

---

**What This Illuminates**

The real variable isn't *whether* LLMs create knowledge. It's *where in the knowledge-creation system* they function. They are currently powerful conjecture-generators with no internal criticism loop against external reality. This is not a flaw unique to LLMs — it's the condition of all theoretical reasoning prior to experimental contact.

The structural leverage point: the bottleneck is not conjecture-generation (LLMs are extraordinarily good at this) — it is criticism infrastructure. The knowledge-creation deficit is downstream of LLMs, not inside them.

**Conjecture:** LLMs have shifted the rate-limiting step in knowledge creation from conjecture-generation to criticism. The next epistemic bottleneck is not more powerful generation — it is faster, more robust falsification pipelines that can absorb the volume of candidates LLMs now produce.

## Questions

1. If the criticism loop (not the conjecture step) is what constitutes knowledge creation, does removing the LLM from the system and replacing it with a random hypothesis generator change whether knowledge is created? — **no**
2. Does the conjecture's claim that 'recombination objection destroys human knowledge too' depend on human recombination and LLM recombination being the same kind of process — and if they differ in kind, does the symmetry argument collapse? — **yes**
3. If an LLM-generated hypothesis survives criticism but the LLM's output was statistically inevitable given its training distribution, is the LLM a causal node in knowledge creation in any sense stronger than a textbook being a causal node? — **no**
4. Does the conjecture require that conjecture-generation and criticism are separable stages — and if criticism is partly constitutive of what makes a conjecture well-formed in the first place, does the staged model break down? — **yes**
5. Is the claim that LLMs have shifted the rate-limiting step from conjecture-generation to criticism falsifiable by empirical data on where scientific bottlenecks actually occur post-LLM adoption? — **yes**
6. If the bottleneck has genuinely shifted to criticism infrastructure, does the conjecture predict that fields with fast falsification pipelines (e.g., protein structure prediction) should show accelerating knowledge production while fields with slow falsification pipelines should not — and is that prediction borne out? — **no**
7. Does the argument's force depend on 'explanatory novelty' being a property of outputs rather than of processes — and if novelty is process-dependent, can a process with no model of reality generate genuine explanatory structure? — **yes**
8. Can the conjecture survive replacing 'LLM' with 'a very large lookup table with interpolation' — and if it can, does that reveal that the conjecture is not actually about LLMs specifically? — **yes**
9. Does the conjecture's reframing from 'Can LLMs create knowledge?' to 'Can a system including an LLM create knowledge?' dissolve the original problem or merely relocate it — specifically, does it now require a separate account of what makes LLM participation non-redundant? — **yes**
10. If the critical claim is that LLMs are 'powerful conjecture-generators with no internal criticism loop,' does the conjecture depend on that absence being permanent rather than contingent — and if LLMs develop internal self-criticism mechanisms, does the entire structural analysis need to be rebuilt? — **no**

## Candidate Problems

- If LLMs have shifted the rate-limiting step from conjecture-generation to criticism, what structural changes to falsification infrastructure are actually required? The conjecture gestures at 'faster, more robust falsification pipelines' but leaves the systems architecture entirely unspecified. What are the stocks, flows, and feedback loops of a criticism system scaled to LLM conjecture throughput? This is a concrete design problem with high leverage. (score: 0.92)
- The conjecture treats 'criticism loop against reality' as the disqualifying criterion for knowledge, but this smuggles in a specific epistemology. Under Deutschian fallibilism, criticism need not be empirical — mathematical proofs, logical refutation, and explanatory coherence all count. Does this mean LLMs with access to formal verification systems already close the loop internally? The boundary between 'conjecture' and 'knowledge' may be more permeable than the conjecture assumes. (score: 0.88)
- The conjecture argues the recombination objection is applied asymmetrically to LLMs versus humans. But this may concede too much too fast. The relevant disanalogy might not be recombination per se, but the absence of a generative model of why a combination should be true — humans conjecture from causal intuitions, LLMs from distributional patterns. Whether this distinction is principled or merely a heuristic is itself an open and important question. (score: 0.85)
- If the LLM is a causal node in a knowledge-creating system, how should epistemic credit be distributed? This is not merely a philosophical curiosity — it has direct implications for scientific authorship norms, reproducibility requirements, and institutional incentive structures. The conjecture implies a systems-level account of knowledge creation that current institutions are not structured to recognize. (score: 0.79)
- The conjecture assumes conjecture-generation and criticism are separable pipeline stages. But in human inquiry they are deeply entangled — good conjectures are shaped by anticipation of criticism, and criticism is shaped by the conjectural framework being tested. If LLMs generate conjectures without this anticipatory structure, they may systematically produce candidates that are hard to criticize — not because they are robust, but because they are underspecified or unfalsifiable. This would mean the bottleneck diagnosis is correct but the failure mode is different from what the conjecture implies. (score: 0.83)
- The conjecture is silent on conjecture quality versus conjecture volume. If LLMs massively increase the number of candidates but the proportion of genuinely non-trivial, well-formed conjectures remains low or decreases, the net effect on knowledge creation could be negative — criticism infrastructure gets flooded with noise. The relationship between generation volume and useful signal density is an empirical question with high practical stakes. (score: 0.87)
- There is an unexamined reflexive problem: this conjecture was itself likely generated with LLM assistance or influence. If the conjecture is correct, it is itself a candidate in a knowledge-creation system and requires criticism to become knowledge. If it is wrong, it illustrates the failure mode it describes — a plausible-sounding recombination that survives no external test. The conjecture does not acknowledge this reflexive structure, which is a gap worth examining. (score: 0.74)
- The framing assumes a linear pipeline: conjecture → criticism → knowledge. But knowledge-creation systems may have important non-linear dynamics — early conjectures shape what criticism is even possible, surviving conjectures constrain the space of future conjectures, and paradigm-level structures resist criticism almost entirely. A systems thinking analysis of these feedback loops, applied specifically to LLM-augmented inquiry, is largely absent from the literature. (score: 0.81)
- If the bottleneck has genuinely shifted to criticism, this predicts specific observable phenomena: increasing rates of conjecture production in LLM-adjacent fields, without corresponding increases in confirmed discoveries; growing backlogs in peer review and replication; and a widening gap between preprint volume and citation-validated knowledge. Whether these predictions are borne out empirically is a tractable research question that would either support or undermine the conjecture. (score: 0.90)
- The conjecture defines knowledge as explanatory structure that tracks reality, but does not address what 'tracking reality' requires for abstract domains — mathematics, philosophy, formal systems. In these domains, criticism loops do not involve empirical contact. This creates an unresolved tension: either the Deutschian criterion applies differently across domains, or the conjecture's scope is narrower than it presents itself to be. (score: 0.70)
