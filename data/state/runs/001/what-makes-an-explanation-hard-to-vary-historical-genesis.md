## Conjecture: Explanatory Rigidity as Inherited Constraint

The question assumes we already know what "explanation" is — but explanations were not designed; they evolved as replacements for something else.

Early explanations were narrative: gods acted, heroes decided, and the *specific actors mattered*. Swap Zeus for Poseidon and you got a different story, not an equivalent account. Narratives were maximally load-bearing by construction — every element was causally downstream of a specific agent with specific motivations.

The Scientific Revolution replaced narrative with *mechanism*: impersonal forces, general laws, substitutable particulars. The great achievement was showing that *which* apple fell didn't matter — only the mass and distance did. Mechanism deliberately made most details freely swappable. This was not discovered; it was a design choice inherited from the problem mechanism was solving (escaping arbitrary particularity).

Here is the hidden assumption that calcified: we absorbed the idea that *good* explanation tends toward swappability — that particulars are noise and structure is signal. The question you're asking now was pre-answered before it was asked. We are asking how to identify "rigid" explanations as if rigidity is a special case requiring diagnosis, when in fact rigidity was the *original default* and swappability was the invented one.

**The conjecture:**

An explanation is fully load-bearing when its parts are *individuated by their causal role* — remove or swap any element and you alter what the explanation is tracking, not just how it is described. The structural property that produces this is **causal specificity**: each part occupies a position in the mechanism that no other part could occupy without changing the explanandum.

An explanation permits free swapping when its parts are *role-fillers*: they are instances of a type, and the type is what does the explanatory work. The structural property here is **type-generality**: the explanandum is insensitive to which instance fills each role.

The diagnostic question is therefore: *does swapping a part change what is being explained, or only who/what is doing the explaining?*

But here is what the origin story reveals that the framing obscures: **most real explanations are hybrid**, and the assumption that we should sort them cleanly into "rigid" vs "swappable" is itself the inherited ghost of mechanism's original polemic against narrative. The demand for clean sorting is an artifact of the problem mechanism was solving, not a feature of explanatory structure itself.

The more productive structural distinction is: *which elements are load-bearing relative to which questions?* A detail swappable in one inquiry is rigid in another. Evolutionary explanations are insensitive to which specific mutation arose (swappable) but deeply sensitive to the selective environment (load-bearing). The "same" explanation changes character depending on what question is being placed upstream.

**What follows:** Diagnosing explanation rigidity requires first diagnosing what problem the explanation replaced — because that ancestry determines which elements were frozen into place before the explanation was ever examined, and which were always understood as provisional placeholders.
