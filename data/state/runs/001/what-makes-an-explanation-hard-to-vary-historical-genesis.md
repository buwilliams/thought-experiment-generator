The apparent flexibility of such details reveals not richness but underconstraint.

To ask how this problem came to exist is to notice that “load-bearing” is borrowed from engineering, where a part matters because removing it changes what the structure can withstand. That metaphor likely arose to solve a prior problem in epistemology: how to distinguish explanatory content from ornament, prediction from curve-fitting, mechanism from redescription. It replaced looser standards like elegance, vividness, or mere empirical adequacy. But it also inherited an unexamined assumption: that the important question is whether a part is necessary *within the finished account*, rather than why that part entered the account in the first place.

From the origin-story perspective, a part is load-bearing not just if deleting it breaks the explanation, but if it exists because it blocks a known error, resolves a prior contradiction, or enables the account to survive a specific criticism. Such parts are constrained by the problem-history of the theory. They are difficult to swap because alternatives have already, in effect, been tried and failed. By contrast, freely swappable details are often remnants of the route by which the explanation was assembled: analogies, labels, local parametrizations, visualization choices, or inherited metaphysical packaging. These persist because they were never exposed to severe criticism.

What follows is a structural criterion: **load-bearing parts are those connected to the explanation’s counterfactual dependence on the errors it was designed to exclude**. If changing a detail reopens an old problem, destroys reach, or severs links to other solved problems, that detail is genuinely structural. If changing it leaves the explanation’s problem-solving power intact, then the detail was not carrying explanatory weight; it was scaffolding, style, or historical residue.

This illuminates why mature explanations often feel inevitable in retrospect: their parts are not arbitrary because each was selected against a landscape of criticism. It also explains why some accounts are endlessly “reframeable” without loss: they have not yet encountered enough constraint to force specificity.

So the hidden assumption exposed by the origin story is that necessity can be read off statically from the present form of an explanation. Better: necessity is genealogical and systemic. The difference between load-bearing and swappable parts is the difference between constraints imposed by a history of solved problems and details that survived only because nothing ever made them answer for themselves.
