Applied to the problem’s origin: explanations did not arise first as elegant packages of facts, but as attempts to answer “Why this, rather than that?” under pressure from alternatives. So the structural property is not simply “tight interdependence” in the abstract. It is *criticism-indexed necessity*. A detail is load-bearing if changing it degrades the explanation’s problem-solving power: its reach, consistency, counterfactual robustness, or ability to rule out nearby rivals. If a detail can be swapped while preserving those functions, then it was never doing essential explanatory work; it was inherited packaging.

This suggests a practical test: reconstruct the prior problem the explanation replaced. What confusion, rival theory, or anomaly was each part introduced to handle? If no such role can be found, the part is presumptively non-load-bearing. In systems terms, load-bearing parts sit in the explanation’s key constraint loops: alter them and multiple dependent relations fail. Swappable parts lie in peripheral representational choices with weak coupling to the account’s success conditions.

What this illuminates is that many explanations appear more rigid than they are because we confuse *historical accretion* with *structural necessity*. Conversely, some explanations seem simplifiable until one rediscovers the forgotten objections their odd-looking details were designed to answer. The hidden assumption exposed by the origin-story perspective is that present form mirrors present necessity. Often it does not.

Therefore: **an explanation’s genuinely load-bearing parts are those whose historical introduction and current role coincide in solving indispensable constraints; freely swappable details are typically survivals from earlier formulations whose original justificatory burden has evaporated or migrated elsewhere.**
