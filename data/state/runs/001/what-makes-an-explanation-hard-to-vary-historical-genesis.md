## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The distinction tracks the *history of the explanation’s construction*: an explanation has “all load-bearing” parts when it was shaped to solve a specific explanatory failure under strong criticism, whereas details are freely swappable when they are residues of inheritance — borrowed framing, metaphor, notation, or narrative scaffolding that survived because it was convenient, not because the explanation depends on it.

Applied genealogically, the question becomes: *what problem was this explanation created to solve, and what earlier forms did it replace?* Explanations do not begin as minimal necessity-structures. They usually accrete from prior representational systems: myths become mechanisms, mechanisms become equations, equations become models with pedagogical stories attached. In that transition, some elements are retained without examination. Those inherited elements often masquerade as essential.

So the structural property at issue is not just internal coherence but **counterfactual dependency under criticism**. A part is load-bearing if, when altered, the explanation loses one of the constraints that originally made it explanatory: predictive reach, solution to a contradiction, unification of cases, or resistance to rival accounts. A part is swappable if changing it leaves those achievements intact. In that case, the part belongs more to the explanation’s lineage than to its logic.

This suggests a test: reconstruct the explanatory problem-history. Ask:
1. What anomaly or limitation called this explanation into being?
2. Which features were introduced specifically to answer that problem?
3. Which features were inherited from earlier formulations, teaching conventions, or available metaphors?
4. If a feature is changed, which solved problem reappears?

What follows is that “load-bearing” is not a stylistic property but a **relation between components and the explanation’s origin-constraints**. Parts are load-bearing when they are the minimal surviving adaptations to past criticism. Freely swappable details are often path-dependent ornament: they persist because no criticism has yet targeted them, not because they do real explanatory work.

This also illuminates why mature explanations often look more rigid than they are. Their contingent historical packaging becomes fused with their genuine necessity. Conversely, some explanations seem elegantly modular only because their core has already been abstracted away from its historical scaffolding.

So the hidden assumption exposed by the origin perspective is this: we often treat present explanatory form as if it were designed from first principles. It usually was not. It is a patched artifact. To distinguish essential structure from replaceable detail, examine not the explanation alone, but the sequence of problems that selected its current form. The load-bearing parts are those that criticism could not remove without reviving the old failure.
