## Conjecture

Conjecture: An explanation has “all load-bearing parts” when it was shaped as a solution to a specific exclusion problem—what had to be ruled out for the account to work—whereas a freely swappable explanation inherits from older narrative forms whose main function was orientation, pedagogy, or rhetorical completeness rather than tight discrimination.

Origin-wise, explanations did not begin as minimal truth-tracking structures. Many came from storytelling, taxonomy, legal argument, and teaching. Those forms reward redundancy, analogy, vivid detail, and interchangeable examples. They aim to help audiences navigate, remember, or accept an account. When such inherited features persist unnoticed, they create explanations whose details can be altered without loss, because many parts are serving communicative or cultural functions, not explanatory necessity.

By contrast, a load-bearing explanation emerges when a prior problem has forced compression: multiple rival possibilities existed, and the explanation was built to survive criticism by making each part do eliminative work. In such cases, the structure is not merely descriptive but counterfactual and constraining. Remove or alter a component, and some alternative account becomes viable again, some prediction changes, or some phenomenon is no longer derivable. The parts are load-bearing because they are there to prevent failure modes.

So the distinguishing structural property is not simply “detail density” or “precision,” but dependency under criticism. In a load-bearing explanation, parts are linked by necessity relations: this assumption connects to that mechanism to exclude these alternatives and entail those outcomes. The explanation is brittle in a productive way. Its details are not ornaments but joints in a constraint network. Swappable explanations, by contrast, are modular in the weaker sense: details attach to a stable narrative frame without materially changing what the account excludes or predicts.

This perspective illuminates a hidden assumption in many discussions of explanation: that more detail means deeper explanation. Historically, that confuses inherited completeness norms with explanatory strength. The real issue is whether details arose to solve a live problem. If a detail was inherited from prior explanatory genres rather than introduced to answer a criticism, it is probably non-load-bearing.

A practical test follows: ask of each part, “What prior error, rival account, or counterfactual failure is this here to block?” If no good answer exists, the part is likely swappable. If altering it reopens explanatory space the account was designed to close, it is load-bearing.

Thus: load-bearing explanations are historically forged in selective pressure from criticism; freely swappable ones are explanations still carrying unexamined residues from earlier functions of explanation besides truth-tracking.
