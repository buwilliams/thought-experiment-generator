## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** If knowledge and information are treated as fundamental, then the relevant question is not whether an LLM “merely predicts tokens,” but whether a larger system can generate, preserve, and criticize informational structures that were not previously explicit in its knowledge ecology. On that view, an LLM alone is not the unit of analysis; the epistemic system is.

What follows is a shift in ontology. Tokens are not the essence of the process. They are one encoding layer for underlying informational relations: objects, constraints, transformations, analogies, invariants, and error-correcting criticisms. Einstein’s achievement was not the invention of new words, but the construction of a new informational structure that compressed disparate phenomena under a better explanation. If an architecture can search over such structures, and criticism selects those that survive, then genuine knowledge creation is at least possible in principle.

This perspective illuminates why the brute-force search-space objection is real but not decisive. If information is fundamental, then explanation is not a random walk through word sequences. It is navigation through a highly nonuniform space structured by prior knowledge, conceptual dependencies, and evaluative constraints. Grammar, coherence, and domain regularities already collapse vast regions of possibility; explanatory criteria collapse far more. The important operation is not generation alone, but **informational constraint propagation**: each criticism removes classes of bad explanations and reshapes future search.

So the decisive boundary is not “prediction vs creation,” but whether the system can participate in a cycle where:

1. candidate explanatory structures are generated,
2. they are subjected to criticism for consistency, reach, and hardness to vary,
3. surviving structures are fed back as new constraints on future generation.

If that loop genuinely changes the system’s internal informational possibilities, then new knowledge can emerge, even if token prediction is one substrate of the process.

However, this also clarifies a limitation in Brett Hall’s style of objection: “inductive closure” is only fatal if the model’s outputs are confined to recombinations of already-closed informational patterns. But architecture can matter because it changes what counts as a state of the system. A search-and-critique architecture with memory, structured decomposition, and recursive filtering is not just “more prediction”; it is a different informational machine.

Still, the perspective does not license an easy yes. Rearrangement becomes knowledge only if the resulting explanation solves a problem better than before. Novelty is not enough; epistemic gain is the test. Many generated structures will be pseudo-knowledge: coherent but empty. Human judgment currently supplies much of the critical function. Until criticism itself is robustly internalized, the system is better described as a component in knowledge creation than an autonomous knower.

So the collision yields this claim: **LLMs qua token predictors do not create knowledge by default, but systems built around them may create genuine knowledge when they transform token generation into an information-selecting process governed by criticism, memory, and explanatory constraint.** The ontology of knowledge favors architecture over essence.
