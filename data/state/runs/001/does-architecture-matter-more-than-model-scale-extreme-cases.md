## Conjecture

## Conjecture: The Substrate Limit Reveals the Structural Deficit

Push the substrate to its limit. Imagine infinite compute, infinite parameters, perfect memory, instantaneous inference. What do you get?

You get a system that has exhausted every pattern in its training distribution with perfect fidelity. It has, in the limit, memorized the *shape of existing human conjecture* — every argument ever made, every logical move ever recorded, every correction ever written down.

And yet: it cannot criticize a conjecture that no human has yet criticized. It cannot identify an error in a framework that no prior text has flagged as erroneous. It cannot form a new conjecture by the mechanism that actually generates new knowledge — which is the recognition of a problem that existing theories *fail to solve*, followed by a bold guess that risks refutation.

At infinite substrate, the bottleneck becomes unmistakable: the system has no generative process for problems. It has only a compression of prior answers.

Now push in the other direction. Shrink the substrate toward zero — minimal parameters, minimal compute. At this limit, even good epistemic structure cannot operate: there is insufficient capacity to represent problems, hold competing conjectures, or track refutations. The substrate is genuinely load-bearing here. It is necessary.

This asymmetry is the structure hidden in the middle:

**Substrate is a necessary condition but not the binding constraint above a threshold. Structure is the binding constraint, and the threshold is lower than we think.**

The evidence at the boundary supports this. Large models do not spontaneously generate conjectures that expose the limits of their training. They generate confident-sounding answers that *fill the shape* of expected answers. The failure mode is not ignorance — it is the absence of a mechanism for recognizing when a question exceeds current knowledge. That is not a scale problem. Scale makes it worse, not better, because confident confabulation scales with capacity.

What would structural sufficiency require? At minimum: a process that distinguishes *known* from *unknown*, that generates hypotheses *in order to test them rather than confirm them*, and that carries forward which hypotheses have survived attempted refutation and which have not. These are not emergent properties of scale. They are architectural commitments about how conjectures are formed, promoted, and retired.

The boundary case reveals that the real question is whether an AI system has any *growth of knowledge* happening — or only retrieval. Current systems, at any scale, are retrieval systems with interpolation. The epistemically interesting threshold is not parameter count but whether the system has any internal analogue of *a problem it cannot yet solve* that generates pressure toward new conjecture.

**The conjecture, stated directly:** The bottleneck is structural. Substrate removes a floor constraint but does not touch the ceiling. The ceiling is whether the architecture contains anything that functions as error-recognition driving conjecture — the basic unit of epistemic progress. Without that, scaling is not progress. It is faster retrieval of the same epistemic shape.
