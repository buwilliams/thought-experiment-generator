## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The binding bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly **structural rather than substrate-bound**: beyond a threshold of capability, more compute mainly amplifies the production of candidate thoughts, while the rate-limiting step becomes the **selection architecture**—how conjectures are generated, criticized, stress-tested, retained, and permitted to influence future inquiry.

Push to the extremes.

At the **low-compute limit**, substrate obviously dominates. A system below some capability threshold cannot represent enough alternatives, simulate criticism, or maintain context. No structure can rescue a mind too weak to form nontrivial conjectures.

But now push toward the **high-compute limit**. Imagine arbitrarily large models, context, memory, and inference budgets. Does epistemic progress then scale automatically? No. An infinite generator without a powerful critic is an infinite fountain of plausible error. If conjecture production grows faster than error-correction, then more substrate worsens the epistemic problem by flooding the system with undigested hypotheses, style-mimicking explanations, and locally coherent nonsense. At the boundary of abundance, the hidden structure appears: progress depends not on how many thoughts can be produced, but on which thoughts survive criticism.

Push further: suppose a model could generate every possible explanation. Then generation ceases to be the scarce resource altogether. What matters is the mechanism that can **eliminate** bad explanations and preserve good ones under variation. That is a structural problem. Epistemic progress is not search alone; it is **search plus ruthless error-correction**.

Now push in the other direction: imagine perfect promotion and testing structure but mediocre substrate. Even then, progress can occur, though slowly, because criticism can compound. Good selection makes limited intelligence cumulative. By contrast, huge substrate under poor structure produces motion without direction—optimization without discovery.

So the boundary suggests a phase change:

- **Below threshold:** substrate is the bottleneck.
- **Above threshold:** structure becomes decisive.
- **Far above threshold:** substrate mainly increases the volume and speed of conjectures; structure determines whether any of that becomes knowledge.

This also reveals a systems point: current AI labs often improve the **stock of generated outputs** faster than the **flows of criticism, adversarial testing, and institutional memory** that would convert outputs into reliable knowledge. Positive feedback amplifies benchmark gains, product pressure, and scaling returns; negative feedback—the mechanisms that kill bad ideas—is comparatively weak. A system with weak negative feedback accumulates error faster as it scales.

Therefore: **the strategic leverage point is not merely bigger models, but better epistemic institutions inside and around them**—mechanisms for independent criticism, preservation of dissenting hypotheses, severe tests, cross-context memory, and promotion by explanatory depth rather than by short-term reward signals.

In short: substrate buys possibility; structure buys truth-tracking. When pushed to the limit, epistemic progress is bottlenecked less by the ability to think more and more by the ability to **find and keep what survives criticism**.
