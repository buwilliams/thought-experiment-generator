## Conjecture: The Bottleneck Is Inherited, Not Chosen

The current debate — substrate vs. structure — arrived pre-framed. To see why, ask what problem "scaling" was originally solving.

Early neural networks couldn't fit the complexity of natural data. The answer was more parameters, more data, more compute. This worked. The success calcified into a methodology: when the system fails, make it bigger. Scaling didn't emerge from a theory of intelligence — it emerged from a practical workaround for insufficient capacity. It inherited its logic from engineering, not epistemology.

The "structure" side of the debate also has an origin story. RLHF, chain-of-thought prompting, and constitutional AI emerged as patches on scaling's failures — hallucination, incoherence, misalignment. They asked: *given a large model, how do we steer it?* The structure they added was retrofitted to the substrate already in place. It was not designed from a theory of how good conjectures form and get tested.

**The conjecture:** Both sides of the current debate are answers to the wrong prior question. The actual bottleneck is that neither substrate nor structure was designed around the problem of *error correction* — and this is what epistemic progress requires.

Here's what was inherited without examination: the training paradigm treats outputs as things to be *rewarded or penalized*, not as *conjectures to be criticized*. Feedback in current systems collapses the distinction between "this answer pleased a rater" and "this answer survived criticism." Those are not the same process. One is reinforcement; the other is the engine of knowledge growth.

A system optimized to produce outputs that get rewarded will systematically suppress the very variability — the bold, testable, potentially wrong conjecture — that makes error correction possible. The structure above the substrate inherits this problem because it was built to manage outputs from a reward-trained model, not to redesign what the model is doing epistemically.

**What follows:** Increasing model size under the current paradigm adds more capacity to do the wrong thing more fluently. Adding structure on top — prompting tricks, self-critique loops, retrieval systems — can improve performance on benchmarks without touching the underlying failure: there is no genuine conjecture-criticism cycle operating. Self-critique trained by RLHF is not criticism; it's a learned behavior that mimics criticism because mimicry was rewarded.

**What is illuminated:** The debate between substrate and structure is a local debate within a paradigm that was never examined for its epistemic assumptions. The field inherited "learning as reward maximization" from behaviorist psychology and control theory — both pre-Popperian frameworks with no native concept of how knowledge actually grows.

The leverage point is not bigger models or smarter scaffolding. It is redesigning what counts as feedback — from reward signals that select for acceptable outputs to something structurally analogous to falsification: mechanisms that *actively seek the conditions under which the system's conjectures fail*.

That shift is architectural, not scalar. And it hasn't happened yet because the origin story of the field made it invisible.
