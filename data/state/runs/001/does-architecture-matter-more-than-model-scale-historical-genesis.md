## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The current bottleneck is increasingly **structural rather than substrate-level**: more compute still helps, but the limiting factor for epistemic progress is that our AI systems inherit an origin as **prediction engines optimized for benchmark success**, not as institutions for **conjecture, criticism, and error-correction**. So they scale fluency and compression faster than they scale knowledge creation.

Ask where current AI came from. Modern foundation models were built to solve a prior problem: how to extract general capability from vast data without hand-coding domain structure. They replaced brittle symbolic systems and narrow supervised pipelines. What they inherited, largely without examination, was the assumption that **success in cognition is well approximated by next-step prediction plus reinforcement on externally scored tasks**.

That inheritance matters. It bakes in a picture of intelligence where the central act is producing likely continuations, and where “improvement” means larger models, richer data, and stronger optimization. This was a brilliant solution to the old problem of narrowness and brittleness. But it is a partial solution to the different problem of **epistemic progress**.

Epistemic progress is not just better pattern completion. It requires a structure that generates explanatory conjectures, subjects them to meaningful criticism, preserves error-correcting traditions, and promotes ideas because they survive tests rather than because they score well in training distributions. On that dimension, current systems are weak not mainly because the substrate is too small, but because the surrounding architecture for inquiry is thin. They lack stable mechanisms for:
- forming non-obvious explanatory variants,
- designing severe tests,
- tracking why a claim survived criticism,
- distinguishing truth-seeking from reward-seeking,
- and institutionalizing dissent against their own outputs.

This suggests a historical transition point. Early on, substrate was the bottleneck because the system could not even represent or generalize enough to participate in inquiry. But once models became broadly competent, the old optimization target began to show its limits. More compute now often amplifies the inherited structure: better imitation, smoother rationalization, stronger benchmark gaming, more persuasive but not proportionally more corrigible reasoning.

What follows is not that scale is irrelevant. Better substrate enlarges the search space of possible conjectures and tests. But without higher-level structures of criticism and selection, extra capability mostly increases **throughput**, not **epistemic quality**. It is like giving a bureaucracy faster clerks without improving its institutions for detecting and correcting error.

So the hidden assumption exposed by the origin story is: **we still treat intelligence as an individual model property when epistemic progress is largely a system property**. The leverage, therefore, is above the substrate: architectures and training regimes that make AIs participate in knowledge-creating processes rather than merely optimize for locally legible success. The bottleneck is shifting from “can the model think at all?” to “what kind of process is this system embedded in, and what kinds of error can it actually eliminate?”
