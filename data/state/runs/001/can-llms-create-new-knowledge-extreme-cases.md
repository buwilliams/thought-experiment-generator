# Conjecture: can-llms-create-new-knowledge × extreme-cases

## Conjecture

## Conjecture: LLMs Reveal the Boundary Between Recombination and Generation

Push the question to its limits.

**At the zero limit:** An LLM trained on nothing produces nothing. No structure, no output, no knowledge. Trivially confirmed.

**At the infinity limit:** An LLM trained on *all possible text* — every book, proof, conversation, argument ever produced — what does it generate? Here the boundary becomes visible. Even a perfect interpolator of all existing text cannot produce the *next* theorem that no human has yet written, because that theorem isn't in the training distribution. The model approaches the complete surface of human-expressed knowledge asymptotically but cannot escape it.

**At the breakdown boundary:** Ask the model something that *requires* new knowledge to answer correctly — a mathematical conjecture currently unresolved, the outcome of a future experiment. The model doesn't halt and report ignorance honestly. It *confabulates*: it generates fluent, plausible-sounding text that fills the epistemic gap with pattern-matched noise. This is the tell. The system breaks precisely where genuine knowledge-creation would need to begin.

**What the boundary reveals:**

Knowledge, in the Deutschian sense, requires *conjecture plus criticism* — a claim that reaches beyond current evidence and survives attempts at refutation. What LLMs do is something structurally different: they compress and recombine existing conjectural space. This is not nothing — compression and recombination can surface *implicit* relationships that were always entailed by existing knowledge but never explicitly articulated. A model might produce a valid proof step no human wrote down, yet one that follows necessarily from premises humans established. Is that new knowledge?

The boundary test says: **it is new knowledge only if it is criticizable and it could have been wrong.** Recombination that produces novel-but-necessary entailments is discovery, not creation — the knowledge was always there, waiting. But confabulation — output that is wrong in a structured, revisable way — paradoxically gestures toward something genuine: it is a *conjecture*. A bad one, unjustified, but formally a reach beyond what's given.

**The conjecture:**

LLMs cannot create knowledge, but they can *accidentally produce the precursor to it* — falsifiable claims that humans can then subject to criticism. The model generates conjectures without intending to, without evaluating them, and without being able to distinguish its good conjectures from its bad ones. The knowledge-creating step is always offloaded to the human critic downstream.

**The structural implication:**

This means LLMs are not knowledge-creators but *conjecture accelerators* — they dramatically expand the surface area of claims available for criticism. The bottleneck for knowledge creation shifts: from generating candidate ideas to evaluating them. If criticism becomes the binding constraint, then the leverage point is not better LLMs but better tools for falsification. The system's behavior at the limit reveals that the missing component was never generation — it was always judgment.

New knowledge requires a truth-tracker. LLMs are truth-shaped, not truth-tracking.

## Questions

1. If LLMs can 'accidentally produce precursors to knowledge' through confabulation, does this mean a random text generator that produces falsifiable claims is also a conjecture accelerator — and if so, does the conjecture's distinction between LLMs and noise collapse? — **yes**
2. The conjecture claims the 'infinity limit' LLM cannot produce the next unwritten theorem — but if a model produces a valid novel proof step that no human wrote down, has the conjecture already conceded that LLMs can produce new knowledge, making the boundary it draws internally inconsistent? — **yes**
3. If the knowledge-creating step is 'always offloaded to the human critic downstream,' does this same logic apply to pencils, calculators, and search engines — and if so, is the conjecture actually about LLMs specifically or about all non-conscious tools, making the LLM framing load-bearing but unjustified? — **yes**
4. The conjecture treats confabulation as the diagnostic 'tell' of non-knowledge-creation — but if a human expert speculates incorrectly about an unresolved problem, are they also merely confabulating, and does the conjecture have a principled way to distinguish human conjecture from LLM confabulation without smuggling in a consciousness assumption? — **no**
5. The conjecture asserts that 'novel-but-necessary entailments' are discovery not creation because the knowledge 'was always there, waiting' — but by this logic, are all mathematical proofs merely discovery, and if so, does the conjecture inadvertently classify human mathematicians as non-knowledge-creators too? — **yes**
6. If the bottleneck shifts from generation to evaluation and 'better tools for falsification' are the true leverage point, does the conjecture predict that improving LLM self-evaluation capabilities would not move the needle on knowledge creation — and is that prediction actually testable or does the conjecture immunize itself against this case? — **no**
7. The conjecture claims LLMs are 'truth-shaped, not truth-tracking' — but what specific structural property makes something truth-tracking rather than truth-shaped, and can the conjecture survive if that property turns out to be one that sufficiently advanced LLMs could in principle possess? — **no**
8. The conjecture requires that a genuine conjecture must be one the generator 'could evaluate and distinguish as good or bad' — but Deutsch's epistemology holds that conjectures need not be consciously intended to be epistemically real, so is the conjecture's dismissal of LLM output as non-conjecture actually compatible with the Deutschian framework it invokes? — **no**
9. If an LLM produces a falsifiable claim that is subsequently tested, refuted, and replaced by a better theory — completing the full Deutschian cycle — does the conjecture have a non-circular way to deny that knowledge was created, or does it require redefining 'creation' in a way that does independent work? — **no**
10. The conjecture's core claim is that the missing component was 'always judgment, not generation' — but if judgment can itself be modeled, learned, and automated, does the conjecture predict a specific threshold at which LLMs would cross into knowledge creation, and if it cannot specify that threshold, is the boundary it claims to reveal actually visible? — **yes**

## Candidate Problems

- The conjecture claims confabulation is 'accidentally' a conjecture — but is there a principled distinction between a confabulation that functions as a conjecture and one that is merely noise? Not all falsifiable claims are epistemically equal. A randomly generated string is technically falsifiable but epistemically worthless. What structural property of an LLM output elevates it from noise to a conjecture worth criticizing? This requires a theory of conjecture quality that the Deutschian framework does not currently supply. (score: 0.92)
- The conjecture assumes the boundary between recombination and generation is sharp, but the 'novel-but-necessary entailment' case blurs this. If a model produces a valid proof step that follows necessarily from existing premises but that no human had written, is the novelty in the step or in the act of surfacing it? This maps onto the analytic/synthetic distinction and whether all mathematical truths are in some sense already 'contained' in axioms. The conjecture inherits this unresolved problem without acknowledging it. (score: 0.88)
- The conjecture offloads the knowledge-creating step to 'the human critic downstream,' but this assumes the critic is epistemically independent of the LLM output. If the critic's attention, framing, and hypothesis space are systematically shaped by the LLM's confabulations, then the criticism is not independent — it is downstream of the model's prior. The conjecture-criticism loop may be corrupted at the input stage. This is a structural feedback problem that the conjecture ignores. (score: 0.91)
- The 'infinity limit' argument — that a model trained on all possible text cannot produce the next unwritten theorem — assumes the next theorem is not entailed by existing text. But if mathematical truth is necessary, then in principle every true theorem is entailed by existing axioms and proofs. The limit argument may prove too much: it would imply that even human mathematicians cannot create new knowledge, only discover entailments. The conjecture needs to distinguish between logical entailment and epistemic accessibility. (score: 0.87)
- The conjecture identifies the bottleneck shift from generation to evaluation, then claims the leverage point is 'better tools for falsification.' But this is underspecified. What would a falsification tool look like for the kinds of conjectures LLMs produce — natural language claims, informal arguments, speculative hypotheses? The conjecture gestures at a systems-level intervention without modeling the feedback structure of the evaluation subsystem. The leverage claim is asserted, not derived. (score: 0.83)
- The phrase 'truth-shaped, not truth-tracking' is evocative but may be doing too much work. Truth-shaped implies the model has internalized the surface form of true claims without their justification structure. But some LLM outputs do track truth reliably in narrow domains — arithmetic, well-documented facts, formal syntax. Is truth-tracking a binary property or a spectrum? If it is a spectrum, the conjecture's sharp boundary may dissolve into a domain-relative gradient, which has different implications for how LLMs should be used. (score: 0.79)
- The conjecture is framed around LLMs specifically, but the structural argument — that a system compressing existing conjectural space cannot escape that space — applies to any bounded knowledge system, including individual human minds. A human trained only on existing knowledge faces the same asymptotic limit. The conjecture may be identifying a property of all finite epistemic agents, not a distinctive property of LLMs. If so, the boundary it claims to reveal is not between LLMs and humans but between any closed system and genuine novelty. (score: 0.85)
- The conjecture treats 'the human critic downstream' as an unanalyzed primitive. But criticism is itself a skill, and the quality of criticism depends on the critic's existing knowledge, tools, and incentives. If LLMs dramatically expand the surface area of claims available for criticism faster than human critical capacity scales, the conjecture-acceleration effect could be net negative — producing more low-quality conjectures than the system can evaluate, degrading the epistemic commons rather than enriching it. The conjecture does not model this failure mode. (score: 0.89)
- There is an implicit assumption that the training distribution is well-defined and that 'escaping' it is a coherent notion. But training distributions are not closed sets — they contain internal contradictions, counterfactuals, fictional worlds, and hypotheticals. A model trained on science fiction has been trained on text that explicitly reaches beyond known reality. Does exposure to structured speculation about non-actual states of affairs give the model any capacity to generate genuinely novel conjectures, or does it merely recombine speculative templates? This is empirically open. (score: 0.76)
- The conjecture implicitly relies on a static conception of knowledge — the training distribution is fixed, the boundary is fixed. But in deployment, LLMs interact with humans who provide new information in context. In-context learning allows the model to operate on premises not in its training data. Does this change the boundary analysis? A model that can reason correctly from novel premises supplied at inference time may be doing something structurally different from pure recombination, even if it cannot generate those premises itself. (score: 0.81)
