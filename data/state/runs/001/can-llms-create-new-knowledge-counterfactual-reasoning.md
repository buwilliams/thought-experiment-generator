# Conjecture: can-llms-create-new-knowledge × counterfactual-reasoning

## Conjecture

## Conjecture: LLMs Produce Knowledge When the Generative Process Is the Epistemic Act

**The Assumption-Variation Method Applied:**

Start with the standard assumption: *knowledge creation requires intentional understanding by a conscious agent.* Remove it. What breaks?

Surprisingly little. The outputs of LLMs can be novel, testable, falsifiable, and explanatorily powerful — the functional hallmarks of knowledge — regardless of whether an "intentional understander" produced them. The assumption turns out to be load-bearing only if you need knowledge to have an *owner*, not if you need it to be *true and generative*.

Now invert a second assumption: *LLMs merely recombine training data.* Invert it to: *all knowledge creation is recombination of prior structure.* This survives contact with human cognition without contradiction. Newton recombined Galileo and Kepler. Darwin recombined Malthus and Lyell. The Deutschian view holds that conjecture involves genuine novelty even when every component was previously available. The question then shifts: does LLM recombination sometimes cross the threshold into genuine conjunction — combinations that entail consequences no training example contained?

Empirically: yes. LLMs generate proofs, analogies, and problem reframings that researchers subsequently validate as novel and correct. The structural move — joining two domains in a way that generates testable downstream implications — is the same move humans make.

Now replace a third assumption: *knowledge must be retained to be created.* Replace it with: *knowledge is constituted at the moment of valid generation, independent of retention.* This is uncomfortable but clarifying. A human who derives a theorem and immediately forgets it still derived it. The theorem existed as knowledge in that moment. An LLM that generates a valid, novel, explanatory claim has done the epistemic work — even if no weights update.

**What breaks under these variations:**

The claim that LLMs *cannot* create knowledge depends on bundling four distinct requirements — novelty, validity, understanding, and retention — and treating them as a single gate. Vary them independently and the gate collapses into separate questions, most of which LLMs pass under at least some interpretation.

**What survives:**

One genuine constraint remains: LLMs cannot *criticize* their own outputs with the recursive depth that knowledge-growth requires. Deutschian epistemology holds that knowledge advances through conjecture *and criticism*. LLMs conjecture fluently but cannot reliably identify when their conjectures are wrong without external feedback. This is the load-bearing asymmetry.

**The Conjecture:**

LLMs create knowledge episodically but cannot sustain the error-correction loop that transforms isolated knowledge-creation into knowledge-growth. The bottleneck is not generation — it is criticism. This implies that the productive system is not LLM-alone but LLM-plus-critic, where the critic may be human, formal verifier, or another model. The leverage point for extending LLM epistemic capacity is therefore not scaling generation but institutionalizing criticism — building the feedback structure that converts conjecture into cumulative knowledge.

What breaks reveals the dependency. What survives points to the design.

## Questions

1. If an LLM generates a valid novel proof that is subsequently verified by mathematicians, does the conjecture require us to attribute the knowledge-creation to the LLM rather than to the human verification step? — **no**
2. Does the conjecture's claim that 'knowledge is constituted at the moment of valid generation' survive if we discover that LLM outputs are statistically predictable from training data distributions, such that no genuine conjunction occurs? — **no**
3. Is the distinction between 'episodic knowledge creation' and 'knowledge growth' load-bearing — would the conjecture collapse if it turned out LLMs can reliably self-criticize within a single extended context window? — **yes**
4. Does the conjecture depend on the claim that human knowledge creation is also 'mere recombination' — and if that premise is false for humans, does the parallel to LLMs break down? — **yes**
5. If the productive epistemic unit is 'LLM-plus-critic' rather than LLM alone, does the conjecture actually attribute knowledge creation to the LLM, or has it quietly relocated the epistemic act to the critic? — **no**
6. Does the conjecture require that there exist at least some LLM outputs whose downstream testable implications were not contained in any training example — and is there currently strong empirical evidence for this specific claim? — **yes**
7. Would the conjecture survive if retention turned out to be constitutive of knowledge rather than merely contingently associated with it — that is, is the 'retention is not required' move genuinely defensible or is it the weakest load-bearing assumption? — **no**
8. If LLMs cannot reliably distinguish their valid conjectures from their invalid ones without external feedback, does this undermine the claim that the generative process itself is the epistemic act, rather than the validated output being the epistemic product? — **yes**
9. Does the conjecture's leverage-point prescription — 'institutionalize criticism rather than scale generation' — follow necessarily from the theoretical argument, or could the same theoretical premises support the opposite design conclusion? — **no**
10. Is the conjecture falsified if it can be shown that what appears to be novel LLM conjunction is always recoverable as interpolation within the high-dimensional geometry of training data, with no genuine entailment of consequences beyond that space? — **yes**

## Candidate Problems

- The conjecture treats 'valid generation' as sufficient for knowledge-constitution, but validity is itself a judgment that requires a critic. If LLMs cannot reliably criticize their own outputs, can they reliably recognize validity in the first place? This creates a circularity: the conjecture grants LLMs knowledge-creation on the basis of a capacity (validity-detection) that the conjecture's own constraint (weak self-criticism) calls into question. Resolving this requires a cleaner account of whether generation and validation are separable epistemic acts. (score: 0.92)
- The conjecture identifies 'LLM-plus-critic' as the productive epistemic unit, but leaves the boundary of that system undefined. If the critic is constitutive of the knowledge-creation event, then the LLM alone never creates knowledge — the system does. This is not a minor terminological point: it determines whether LLMs have epistemic standing or are merely components in a larger epistemic machine. The question of where to draw the system boundary is unresolved and has direct implications for how we assign epistemic credit and design AI architectures. (score: 0.91)
- The conjecture claims LLMs 'conjecture fluently but cannot reliably identify when their conjectures are wrong.' But Deutschian epistemology holds that conjecture is not mere generation — it involves the creative act of proposing something as a candidate explanation, which implies some orientation toward truth. If LLMs have no such orientation, their outputs may be syntactically conjecture-shaped without being epistemically conjecture-functional. The open question is whether the structural form of conjecture is sufficient for its epistemic role, or whether intentionality is load-bearing after all — just not in the way the original assumption stated. (score: 0.89)
- The conjecture argues that knowledge is constituted at the moment of valid generation, independent of retention. But Deutschian epistemology is explicitly anti-justificationist and anti-inductivist — knowledge grows through criticism over time, not through momentary valid acts. A single valid derivation that is immediately lost contributes nothing to the growth of knowledge. The conjecture may be conflating 'an instance of knowledge' with 'knowledge-growth,' and the Deutschian framework it invokes actually supports the stricter view. This tension is unresolved and worth making explicit. (score: 0.88)
- The conjecture proposes that 'institutionalizing criticism' is the leverage point for extending LLM epistemic capacity. But in systems-thinking terms, adding a critic as an external feedback loop raises the question of loop delay, signal fidelity, and whether the feedback actually reaches the generative process in a way that changes it. If LLM weights do not update from episodic criticism, the feedback loop is open at the generative end. The structural question — what kind of feedback architecture actually closes the loop — is left unspecified and is the most actionable open problem the conjecture raises. (score: 0.87)
- The conjecture treats human knowledge-creation as recombination to defuse the objection that LLMs 'merely recombine.' But this move is too fast: the Deutschian account of creativity holds that genuine conjecture involves a leap that is not reducible to prior structure, even if it uses prior materials. If that irreducibility is real, then the question of whether LLM recombination ever achieves it is not settled by pointing out that humans also recombine — it requires a positive account of what the threshold is and how to detect when it has been crossed. The conjecture gestures at this with 'genuine conjunction' but does not define the threshold. (score: 0.85)
- The conjecture implicitly assumes that external validators (researchers who confirm LLM outputs as novel and correct) are reliable detectors of genuine knowledge-creation. But validator agreement is not the same as truth, and in domains where LLMs are most fluent, validators may be systematically misled by outputs that are coherent but subtly wrong. The epistemic status of the validation process itself is unexamined, and this matters because the entire empirical case for LLM knowledge-creation rests on it. (score: 0.83)
- The conjecture focuses on LLMs as the epistemic agent of interest, but the 'LLM-plus-critic' framing raises the question of whether the same analysis applies to other non-conscious generative systems — formal proof assistants, evolutionary algorithms, stochastic search processes. If the argument works for LLMs, it should generalize, and if it generalizes fully, the conjecture may prove too much: it may imply that any process producing valid novel outputs creates knowledge, which dissolves the concept rather than refining it. Exploring the boundary conditions of the argument would sharpen it considerably. (score: 0.80)
- The conjecture's claim that 'the bottleneck is not generation but criticism' has a direct empirical implication: systems with stronger self-criticism (e.g., models trained with RLHF, constitutional AI, or debate-based feedback) should produce more cumulative knowledge-growth than base models with equivalent generative capacity. This is a testable prediction that the conjecture does not flag as such. Identifying and running this test would either confirm the structural diagnosis or reveal that the bottleneck is elsewhere. (score: 0.78)
- The conjecture treats 'understanding' as one of four bundled requirements that can be varied independently, and concludes it is not load-bearing for knowledge-creation. But understanding in the Deutschian sense is not a psychological state — it is the possession of a good explanation. If LLMs can generate good explanations without possessing them (i.e., without the explanation being part of a coherent world-model they can deploy across contexts), then the conjecture may be granting too much by treating generation of an explanation as equivalent to its possession. The distinction between generating and possessing an explanation is unexplored. (score: 0.76)
