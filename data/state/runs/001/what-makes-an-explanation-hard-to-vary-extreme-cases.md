## Conjecture

Conjecture: An explanation’s parts are all load-bearing iff, when the explanation is pushed to its boundaries, each part participates in preserving its counterfactual structure; freely swappable details are precisely those that do not constrain what would happen at the limits.

Push the problem to extremes. First, drive the explanation toward zero: remove one detail at a time. If the account still generates the same distinctions, predictions, or problem-solution structure, that detail was ornamental or merely implementation-specific. If removing it collapses the explanation’s ability to answer “why this rather than that?”, then it was load-bearing.

Now push toward infinity: vary the details across as many alternative realizations as possible. If the explanation survives wide substitution—different materials, agents, scales, or surface mechanisms—then what remains invariant is its true structure. The swappable details were never doing explanatory work; they were only one instantiation. But if substitution breaks the explanation, then those details were not superficial after all: they were carrying necessary constraints.

At the edge, the distinction becomes clearer. A load-bearing explanation is tightly compressed: its parts are mutually constraining, and the account loses explanatory power under deletion or arbitrary replacement. It has low freedom of permutation because its elements are connected by necessity, not by narrative convenience. Its hallmark is counterfactual fragility at the level of the parts but robustness at the level of the organizing principle. You may not swap out a key part without changing the “why,” but you may redescribe the whole in equivalent terms.

A non-load-bearing explanation has the opposite profile. It is robust to part-substitution because many of its details are independent of the core explanatory work. It often contains detachable illustrations, historical contingencies, or local embodiments. Such explanations can be improved by stripping them down until only the generative constraints remain. What is left is the real explanation; the rest is scaffolding, mnemonic aid, or rhetoric.

So the structural property at issue is not simply necessity, but constraint contribution. A part is load-bearing when it closes off nearby alternative accounts—when it rules out error, not merely decorates truth. Explanations differ by how much of their content is involved in this error-preventing function.

This suggests a practical test: examine an explanation under extreme deletion and extreme substitution. The irreplaceable residue marks the actual explanatory structure. The freely swappable remainder reveals where the account is representationally loose—useful perhaps, but not constitutive.

What the boundary reveals is that explanation is not a heap of true statements. It is a structure for excluding wrong possibilities. In an explanation whose parts are all load-bearing, nearly every part helps perform that exclusion. In one with swappable details, much of the content can change because it was never part of the exclusion machinery to begin with.
