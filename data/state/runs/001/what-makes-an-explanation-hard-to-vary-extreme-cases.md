## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** An explanation whose parts are all load-bearing is one that has been pushed close to a boundary condition where variation is no longer tolerated: remove, alter, or permute almost any part and the explanation loses predictive reach, coherence, or problem-solving power. By contrast, an explanation with freely swappable details is operating far from constraint; its apparent content lies mostly in a loose pattern or label, not in a tightly interlocked structure.

Pushing the problem to the limit clarifies the distinction. Take an explanation and imagine driving its components toward extremes:

- **Toward zero:** delete one part at a time.
- **Toward infinity:** multiply alternative substitutions for each part.
- **Toward the boundary:** ask whether the account still explains the same phenomenon under severe perturbation.

If the explanation survives wide substitution, then many of its details were not doing explanatory work. They were decorative, heuristic, or merely one implementation among many. The real explanation is whatever remains invariant across those substitutions. In other words, the load-bearing structure is not the visible wording but the deeper constraint pattern.

If, however, changing even a small element collapses the account, that can mean two different things. Either the explanation is genuinely precise — each part closes off a real class of errors — or it is brittle in a bad way, overfit to a narrative with no independent grip on reality. The boundary test separates these: a good load-bearing explanation is fragile **with respect to arbitrariness** but robust **with respect to criticism**. Each part is necessary because it rules out a live alternative, not because the story has been ornamented into irreplaceability.

So the key structural property is **constraint density**: how many independent possibilities each part excludes, and how strongly the parts mutually determine one another. In a load-bearing explanation, parts are coupled by necessity; they form something like a proof, mechanism, or design in which each component answers a specific explanatory problem created by the others. In a swappable explanation, parts are weakly coupled; they can be exchanged because they are not resolving sharp constraints, only filling roles in a generic template.

At the limit, the distinction becomes stark:

- A **maximally load-bearing** explanation approaches a minimal sufficient structure: nothing can be removed without loss, and nothing can be altered without changing what follows.
- A **maximally swappable** explanation approaches a shell: many retellings fit equally well because the account has not descended to the level where reality pushes back.

What the boundary reveals is that explanatory strength is not primarily about detail versus simplicity. It is about whether details are **forced**. The mark of a deep explanation is not that it contains many parts, but that its parts are there because the problem leaves no freedom to replace them arbitrarily.
