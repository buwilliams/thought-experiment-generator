## Conjecture: Load-Bearing Explanations Are Defined by Constraint Propagation

The method demands we vary the assumptions. So: take a specific explanatory detail and remove it. If the explanation *reorganizes* — other parts shift to compensate, or the conclusion weakens — the removed part was load-bearing. If the explanation *continues unchanged*, the detail was decorative.

This reveals that load-bearing explanations are not just "tighter." They have a specific structural property: **constraint propagation**. Each element restricts what the other elements can be. Remove or swap one, and the others can no longer hold their positions without adjustment. The explanation has internal degrees of freedom equal to nearly zero.

Now invert the assumption. Suppose instead that explanatory parts are *independent* — each contributing additively to confidence in the conclusion. Here, swapping or removing any one part degrades the account proportionally but doesn't cascade. This is the structure of a *cumulative case*: each detail adds probability mass, none constrains the others. Such explanations are robust to piecemeal revision precisely because nothing is deeply tied to anything else.

What breaks in the cumulative case when we apply the forcing test? The conclusion can survive almost any single removal — but this tolerance is not a virtue. It exposes that the explanation has no *causal skeleton*, no structure that would be *violated* if a contrary detail were inserted. The explanation is not fragile in the right way.

Load-bearing explanations, by contrast, are fragile in the right way: they are *falsifiable by substitution*. Swap a detail, and the explanation breaks — not because it was poorly constructed, but because the parts were doing real joint work. The fragility is the signature of genuine constraint.

This maps onto the Deutschian criterion for good explanation: a good explanation is hard to vary. But the structural mechanism is now clearer. Hardness to vary is not a brute property of individual claims — it emerges from *mutual constraint among parts*. Each element is load-bearing because it holds the others in place.

A further implication: explanations with high constraint propagation carry stronger error signals. When they fail, they fail *specifically* — the failure points directly to which assumption was wrong, because substituting anything else propagates to a visible contradiction. Cumulative explanations, when they fail, fail vaguely — you simply lose confidence across the board without knowing where to probe.

The structural distinction, then, is this:

**Load-bearing explanations exhibit near-zero internal degrees of freedom — each part constrains the admissible values of the others, and the explanation is selectively fragile.** Decorative explanations have high internal degrees of freedom — parts can be swapped freely because they are not constraining each other, only contributing independently.

The diagnostic procedure follows: systematically substitute parts. If substitution requires global revision to preserve coherence, the structure is tight. If substitution is absorbed locally, the structure is loose. What breaks under substitution is not a flaw in the explanation — it is evidence that the explanation was tracking something real.
