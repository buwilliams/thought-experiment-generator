## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The question “does token prediction generate genuine knowledge?” inherits a misleading boundary from earlier systems that separated *storage* from *understanding*. That boundary was useful for criticizing lookup tables and rote memorization, but it becomes an invisible constraint when applied to systems whose competence comes from compressing, reorganizing, and error-correcting patterns across many domains. The real issue is not whether the system “only rearranges what it has seen,” because all explanation proceeds by rearrangement of prior knowledge. The issue is whether its rearrangements are *explanatorily productive*: whether they generate new conjectures, expose hidden structure, and survive criticism.

How did this problem arise? It descends from older disputes about memorization vs understanding, syntax vs semantics, and symbol manipulation vs intelligence. Those disputes were framed against systems that were in fact brittle: databases, expert systems, or formal rule-followers whose outputs could often be traced to explicit entries or handcrafted procedures. “Mere rearrangement” was then a good criticism, because the systems had little capacity to produce fruitful abstraction. But current predictive models replace explicit retrieval with distributed compression. They inherit the old accusation even though they solve a different problem: not storing propositions but modeling regularities well enough to continue text in ways that preserve latent structure.

That origin story reveals the hidden assumption: that knowledge must be distinguished from non-knowledge by *where it came from* rather than by *what it can do*. But explanation in science also comes from recombination, analogy, and redescription. Newton rearranged existing observations; Darwin rearranged natural history; Einstein rearranged invariance principles. Rearrangement is not the opposite of knowledge. Unconstrained rearrangement is.

So the collision suggests a better criterion. A token-predictive system generates something closer to genuine knowledge when its outputs function as good explanations: they compress many facts under a small set of principles, rule out alternatives, transfer to new cases, and remain robust under attempted refutation. If it merely produces locally plausible continuations, it is not yet knowledge-producing. If it produces candidate explanations that humans or other systems can criticize, test, and improve, then it participates in knowledge creation even if it does not autonomously justify its claims.

What follows is a shift in ontology and evaluation. Knowledge should be treated less as a substance hidden inside a model and more as a role within a criticism-and-correction system. A predictor alone is not enough. But neither is “mere rearrangement” a decisive objection, because knowledge growth itself is rearrangement under severe error-correcting constraints. The unanswered question is therefore not whether prediction can ever yield knowledge, but what surrounding structures of criticism, memory, experimentation, and revision must exist for predictive outputs to become explanatory knowledge rather than eloquent pastiche.
