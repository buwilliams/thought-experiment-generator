## Conjecture: The Question Inherits a Discredited Epistemology

The problem arrives pre-loaded with an assumption worth excavating: that **genuine knowledge requires a special generative act** — something more than rearrangement, something originary. This assumption has a lineage. It descends from justificationist epistemology, which held that knowledge must be *grounded* — in experience, in reason, in some privileged source. The worry that prediction is "mere rearrangement" is the worry that it lacks this ground.

But justificationism was the problem that Popperian fallibilism *solved*. The replacement insight: knowledge is not defined by its origin or its justification. It is defined by its **explanatory reach** — by whether it produces conjectures that are criticizable, that compress reality, that generate predictions beyond their inputs, that survive contact with problems they weren't trained on.

So the question, asked correctly, becomes: **Do LLM-produced explanations have explanatory reach, or do they merely pattern-match to the surface form of explanations?**

This reframing dissolves some confusion and sharpens the rest.

What LLMs inherited from their training data is not raw text — it is the *encoded residue of human problem-solving*. Explanations in that corpus weren't inert; they were produced under selection pressure: they had to survive criticism, peer review, use, contradiction. The model trained on them absorbed something structural about how problems and solutions cohere. That is not nothing. A parrot doesn't produce novel logical entailments from what it hears. A system that can extend an argument into territory its training didn't cover — correctly — is doing something the "mere rearrangement" framing fails to capture.

But here is where the origin story cuts the other way: **the token-prediction objective was designed to solve a compression problem, not an explanation problem**. It was built to minimize prediction error over sequences. Explanatory depth was never the target — it was, at best, a byproduct discovered post-hoc. This matters. The architecture inherited no mechanism for distinguishing *good explanations from fluent impostors*. It has no error-correction loop, no built-in conjecture-and-refutation cycle. It generates explanations at the same confidence register whether they are deep or shallow, whether they survive scrutiny or collapse under it.

The conjecture: **LLMs can produce genuine knowledge — but only accidentally and without a selection mechanism to know when they have**. They generate explanations with the structural form of knowledge: causal claims, generalization, compression. Some of those explanations will *be* knowledge, in the Deutschian sense — they will have reach, they will survive criticism. But the system cannot self-identify which ones. The output is a mixture of genuine explanations and fluent confabulation, with no internal signal separating them.

The leverage point is not asking whether the system *can* produce knowledge. It can. The structural question is: **what external criticism apparatus is needed to harvest it?**

The origin story reveals that we built a prediction engine and then asked it to be an epistemology. The missing piece was never in the model. It was always in the feedback loop we forgot to attach.
