## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If knowledge and information are taken as fundamental, then the key structural difference between a genuine explanation and a merely plausible narrative is **informational compressibility under variation**: in a real explanation, most parts are constrained by the informational role they play in preserving the explanatory structure, whereas in a bad explanation, many parts are interchangeable because they do not participate in the structure that generates the account’s consequences.

Put differently: an explanation with “all load-bearing parts” is one in which the information is **mutually constraining**. Its components are not just present; they encode dependencies that narrow what else can be true. Change one part, and many downstream inferences fail or must be revised. A freely swappable account lacks this network of constraints. Its details decorate rather than determine.

From this perspective, the relevant distinction is not primarily linguistic, nor even psychological, but **ontological at the level of information**. Explanations are structures of constrained information. The more those constraints bind the parts together and connect them to counterfactuals, the more the explanation carries knowledge.

That illuminates your LLM proposal. The enormous raw search space of word sequences is mostly irrelevant, because knowledge creation is not search over strings as such. It is search over **informational structures** with high internal constraint. The tractability comes from moving from surface combinatorics to deeper invariants: objects, relations, properties, symmetries, thought-experiment structure, and criticism. These are not conveniences; they are the units in which explanatory information is actually organized.

So the property you want to test for is something like:

1. **Counterfactual fragility:** if a component is altered, does the explanation lose its ability to account for the phenomenon?
2. **Constraint propagation:** does each component restrict the allowable form of the others?
3. **Generative reach:** does the structure entail or illuminate phenomena beyond the original target?
4. **Compression of arbitrariness:** are there few free choices left once the core idea is fixed?

On this view, “hard to vary” can be sharpened: it means **low freedom of informational substitution without loss of explanatory power**.

This also reframes whether LLMs can create knowledge. They can, in principle, if embedded in an architecture that does not merely sample fluent text but systematically constructs and criticizes high-constraint informational structures. The decisive issue is not whether the model “understands” in some intrinsic sense, but whether the overall system can generate candidate structures whose parts become increasingly load-bearing under criticism.

So the collision of perspectives yields this:  
**Knowledge creation is the production of informational structures with dense internal constraint and broad external reach.** An explanation’s parts are load-bearing when they are necessary nodes in that constraint network; they are swappable when they are informationally idle. The problem, then, is measurable in structural terms, not mystical ones.
