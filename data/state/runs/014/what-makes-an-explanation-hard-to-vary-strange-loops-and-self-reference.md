## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
An explanation has “all load-bearing parts” when it forms a **closed self-referential constraint network**: its components do not merely add information, but mutually define the conditions under which the explanation counts as an explanation at all. By contrast, an explanation whose details can be swapped freely is structurally **open and modular**: its parts are illustrative or implementation-level, not constitutive of the explanatory loop.

From the strange-loops perspective, the key distinction is whether the explanation contains a **return path** from higher-level claim back to the lower-level details that generated it. In a load-bearing explanation, if you climb from details to principle and then descend again, you are forced back to those details or to something tightly equivalent. The explanation “closes” on itself. Its parts are necessary because each one helps stabilize the loop by which the account represents both the phenomenon and the standards of adequacy for explaining it.

In a swappable explanation, the ascent to generality does not constrain the descent. The higher-level claim underdetermines the lower-level realization. You can replace examples, metaphors, mechanisms, or framing devices without breaking the account because they are not part of a self-supporting loop; they are attached to it.

A useful structural test follows:

1. **Counterfactual sensitivity:** If a part is altered, does the explanation merely change style, or does it lose explanatory reach, coherence, or self-consistency?
2. **Recurrence:** Does the global claim depend on the local parts in such a way that the parts are re-derived when the claim is applied back to itself?
3. **Constraint density:** Are the components independently variable, or does changing one force revisions across the whole account?
4. **Self-application:** Can the explanation explain why these parts, rather than interchangeable ones, are needed?

When these properties are high, the explanation behaves like a strange loop: every level constrains every other, and the account’s adequacy depends on preserving the recursive structure. This is why such explanations often feel elegant but brittle: remove one beam and the whole loop collapses.

Thought experiment: imagine two explanations of consciousness. One says, “Consciousness is integrated information,” and the examples can vary widely. Another says, “Consciousness arises when a system models itself in a recursive hierarchy that feeds back into its own operations.” In the second, self-model, recursion, hierarchy, and feedback are not decorative. Remove any one, and the claim no longer generates the phenomenon it aims to explain. The parts are load-bearing because the explanandum is itself a loop.

So the deeper structural property is this: **load-bearing explanations are those in which the phenomenon and the explanation share the same recursive organization**. Freely swappable explanations describe; load-bearing explanations reenact.
