Let the data-generating process be a latent-state system:

- latent state \(z_t \in Z\)
- observation \(x_t \in X\)

- emission \(x_t \sim E(z_t)\)

A next-token predictor learns \(p(x_{t+1}\mid x_{\le t})\). The question is: when must optimal prediction internally recover variables equivalent to the true invariants of \(z_t\)?

## Conjecture

Next-token prediction forces learning of latent variables corresponding to real domain invariants **iff** the minimal sufficient statistic for predicting future tokens from past tokens is isomorphic, up to an information-preserving transformation, to the invariant structure of the underlying process.

More concretely, prediction is sufficient when four conditions hold:

1. **Predictive insufficiency of surface statistics**  
   No finite collection of shallow observables of \(x_{\le t}\) can achieve optimal prediction. The Bayes-optimal predictor requires a compressed state
   \[
   s_t = f(x_{\le t})
   \]
   such that
   \[
   p(x_{>t}\mid x_{\le t}) = p(x_{>t}\mid s_t),
   \]
   and \(s_t\) is not reducible to local token correlations.

2. **Invariant dependence of future on latent state**  
   The future depends on aspects of \(z_t\) that are stable under observational variation. Formally, there exists an equivalence relation on latent states such that if two states yield identical future distributions, they are equivalent; the quotient \(Z/{\sim}\) is the predictive state. These equivalence classes are the real invariants relevant to the task.

3. **Identifiability through temporal variation**  
   Distinct invariant states must induce distinguishable future token distributions:
   \[
   z_t \not\sim z'_t \implies p(x_{>t}\mid x_{\le t}, z_t) \neq p(x_{>t}\mid x_{\le t}, z'_t).
   \]
   If multiple latent organizations produce the same predictive distribution, prediction alone cannot force the “real” one.

4. **Capacity and optimization reach the minimal predictor**  
   The learner must be able to represent and find this minimal predictive state. Otherwise sufficiency fails in practice even if it holds in principle.

## What follows

This makes the issue mathematical: prediction does not recover latent structure because it is “smart,” but because the loss function makes certain invariants unavoidable. The relevant object is not the hidden state itself, but the **minimal predictive state**—the coarsest partition of histories preserving future distributions.

So prediction learns real invariants exactly when those invariants are the unique low-complexity solution to the predictive compression problem.

## Illumination

The boundary case is decisive: if the world contains real structure but that structure is not needed to predict token continuations, next-token prediction need not learn it. Conversely, if invariant structure is the only way to compress history without predictive loss, then prediction pressure selects for it.

So the conjecture can be sharpened:

**Prediction is sufficient only when the domain’s real invariants are identical to its minimal sufficient predictive statistics, up to reparameterization.**
