## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** *Epistemic progress is best defined as an increase in a system’s capacity to generate, preserve, and apply error-correcting explanations across novel conditions; it can be measured by the structure of that capacity rather than by output fluency, benchmark scores, or popularity. Viewed through the lens of public-goods underprovision, genuine epistemic progress is systematically undersupplied because explanation quality is partly non-excludable, its benefits are diffuse, and institutions therefore reward legible answer production over investment in shared error-correction.*

This perspective clarifies the distinction the problem assumes: **answer production** is a rival, marketable output; **epistemic progress** is a partially public infrastructure. A fluent answer can be sold, ranked, or liked. But the underlying work that makes knowledge grow—better problem decomposition, sharper criticism, reusable conceptual distinctions, stronger tests, preserved negative results, and mechanisms for detecting and correcting error—spills over to others. Because those benefits are hard to fully appropriate, individually rational actors underinvest in them.

So the key measurement question becomes structural: **what features indicate that a system has improved its error-correcting knowledge infrastructure?** Not “How often does it answer well on known tasks?” but:

- **Novelty robustness:** does performance survive domain shift, adversarial variation, or previously unseen problems?
- **Explanatory compression:** can the system account for more with fewer arbitrary patches?
- **Error detectability and recoverability:** when wrong, does it expose the source of error and support correction?
- **Criticism yield:** does the system make itself vulnerable to informative refutation rather than merely optimizing for pass rates?
- **Transfer of understanding:** do improvements in one area increase capacity in others without retraining on every case?
- **Preservation of negative knowledge:** are failed paths, counterexamples, and refuted conjectures retained in ways that prevent rediscovery of the same mistakes?

A thought experiment sharpens this. Imagine two communities. One optimizes only for benchmarkable outputs; the other invests heavily in public epistemic goods: shared standards of criticism, archives of failures, interpretable models, and mechanisms for stress-testing explanations. In the short run, the first may appear superior because output metrics are privately capturable. But under novel conditions, the second adapts faster and avoids repeated error. What looked like “lower performance” was actually investment in the substrate of progress.

Thus, benchmark success and popularity are not just noisy proxies; they can be **anti-indicators** when they reward private answer production while masking collective underprovision of error-correction. Epistemic progress should therefore be measured by whether the system escapes the stable equilibrium of producing persuasive answers without improving the shared means of finding and fixing mistakes.

In short: **epistemic progress is growth in the public infrastructure of error-correcting explanation.** Its measurement should target that infrastructure directly.
