## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The current bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly **structural rather than substrate-based**: once a threshold of capability is reached, better conjecture-formation, criticism, testing, and selection become a **public good** that is systematically underprovided by individually rational actors. As a result, the field can remain in a stable equilibrium of impressive capability growth but weak epistemic progress.

Here the public-goods lens matters because “better epistemics” is often **non-excludable and diffuse in benefit**. If one lab develops clearer standards for falsification, better benchmark design, stronger error-correction loops, or institutions that reward criticism over hype, the gains spill across the whole field. Competitors benefit; downstream users benefit; even critics benefit. But the costs are concentrated: slower product cycles, reduced room for strategic ambiguity, and fewer incentives to publish flattering but weakly tested claims. So each actor is pushed toward investing in substrate improvements they can privately capture—compute, scale, proprietary data, engineering optimizations—rather than in shared epistemic infrastructure.

This perspective distinguishes **underprovision** from mere confusion or laziness. The issue is not primarily that people do not know how to test ideas, nor that they lack motivation in general. It is that the system rewards privately appropriable advances more reliably than it rewards improvements to the communal machinery of criticism. Even actors who sincerely prefer a more truth-tracking ecosystem can rationally defect if unilateral investment in better epistemics imposes local costs while creating global benefits.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two worlds with identical models and compute. In World A, labs compete only on benchmark wins, demos, and deployment speed. In World B, the same labs share strong norms and institutions for adversarial testing, replication, anomaly reporting, and prestige for finding errors rather than merely shipping. If epistemic progress differs sharply between the worlds—and it likely would—then the bottleneck cannot be mainly in substrate. It lies in the structure above it: the feedback loops governing which conjectures are generated, which survive criticism, and which get amplified.

What follows is not that substrate no longer matters. More compute can still expose new phenomena and enlarge the space of testable ideas. But beyond some point, adding substrate without improving epistemic structure mainly increases the volume of outputs, claims, and seductive performance gains—without proportionately increasing knowledge. In systems terms, the stock of capability grows faster than the stock of reliable criticism, so error can compound.

So the illuminating claim is: **AI epistemic progress is bottlenecked where incentives fail to fund the public good of better criticism.** The leverage point is not just larger models, but institutions and norms that make epistemic improvements more excludable, more rewarded, or more mandatory—so that individually rational behavior no longer traps the field in a no-provision equilibrium for truth-seeking infrastructure.
