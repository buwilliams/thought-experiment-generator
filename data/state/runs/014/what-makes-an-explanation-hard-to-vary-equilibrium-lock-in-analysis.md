## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
An explanation is **all-parts-load-bearing** when its elements are held in a kind of *epistemic equilibrium lock-in*: each part’s role is defined by its necessity for preserving the explanatory payoff, and no single element can be altered without forcing compensatory changes elsewhere or collapsing the account’s ability to explain the target phenomenon. By contrast, an explanation has **freely swappable details** when many of its components are not equilibrium-constrained but merely contingent implementations of a more abstract structure.

The distinguishing structural property is therefore not “complexity” or “amount of detail,” but **degree of mutual dependence under counterfactual substitution**.

From the lock-in perspective, the key question is: if one agent-like component of the explanation — an assumption, mechanism, term, or narrative step — “defects” and changes while the rest stay fixed, does the explanatory system still function? If not, the account is structurally trapped in a tight equilibrium: each part is load-bearing because unilateral deviation is explanation-destroying. This resembles a Nash equilibrium because no local replacement improves or even preserves the account on its own. Reform requires coordinated revision across multiple parts.

An explanation with swappable details has a different structure. Its success depends mainly on a **higher-order invariant** — a causal relation, mathematical dependency, functional mechanism, or organizing principle — while lower-level details are interchangeable realizations. Here, changing one part need not break the whole, because the account is not locked into that specific component. The part serves the structure; it does not constitute it.

This illuminates a practical diagnostic:

1. **Hold the explanatory target fixed.**  
2. **Vary one part at a time.**  
3. Ask whether the rest of the explanation can remain unchanged while retaining comparable explanatory power.  
   - If no: the part is load-bearing.  
   - If yes: the part is replaceable.  
4. Then ask whether multiple replacements are possible because they instantiate the same deeper relation. If so, the true load-bearing element is the abstract relation, not the concrete detail.

The perspective also distinguishes two failure modes that are often confused. Some explanations appear rigid because they are genuinely tightly coupled: every element is doing indispensable work. Others appear rigid only because the explainer has not identified the correct abstraction level. In the latter case, apparent necessity is a contingent presentation artifact, not a real structural constraint.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem suggests: **load-bearing explanations are characterized by strong interlocking dependence and low unilateral substitutability; flexible explanations are characterized by stable invariants and high peripheral substitutability.** What matters is whether necessity resides in the specific parts themselves or in the relational pattern they jointly realize.
