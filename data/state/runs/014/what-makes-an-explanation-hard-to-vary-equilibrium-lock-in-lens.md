## Conjecture

A useful conjecture is this:

**An explanation is “all load-bearing” when its components function like contributors to a public good under tight complementarity: each part supplies a non-substitutable constraint, and removing or altering one part collapses the explanatory outcome. By contrast, an explanation with swappable details has slack, redundancy, or multiple realizability: its parts contribute in ways that are substitutable, so the account survives local variation.**

What this lens illuminates is that the difference is not mainly about elegance or level of detail. It is about **structure**.

In a public-goods frame, underprovision happens when benefits are diffuse and contributions are non-excludable, so each actor can rationally withhold contribution even though all would prefer the fully funded result. Translate that into explanation: an account is fully load-bearing when each element plays the role of a necessary contribution to a jointly produced result. The explanation works only if all the required constraints are “funded.” If one is missing, the result is not a weaker version of the same explanation; it becomes a different, inadequate one.

So the distinguishing properties are:

1. **Necessity of parts**: each component rules out error that no other component rules out.
2. **Low substitutability**: details are not interchangeable because each carries unique explanatory work.
3. **Strong complementarity**: the parts produce explanatory power together, not additively but conjunctively.
4. **Fragility under perturbation**: changing one detail breaks the account rather than merely restyling it.

Where details can be swapped freely, the explanation has a different structure:
- multiple details perform the same function,
- the real explanatory work is being done at a higher level of abstraction,
- many local variations preserve the same causal or logical pattern.

A thought experiment makes this clearer. Imagine two bridges. In one, every cable is uniquely placed and exactly tensioned; remove any one and the bridge fails. In the other, the bridge is overengineered; many cables could be replaced or removed without collapse. Both bridges stand, but only the first is “all load-bearing.” Explanations differ similarly. Some are precise conjunctions of indispensable constraints. Others are robust templates padded with replaceable illustrations.

This also distinguishes structural underprovision from confusion. If people disagree about whether a detail matters, that may reflect ignorance. But if an explanation’s success truly depends on non-substitutable contributions, then dismissing “small” details creates a stable failure: each omitted detail seems dispensable in isolation, yet the explanatory public good never materializes. The account appears under-motivated when it is actually under-specified.

So the conjecture is: **load-bearing explanations are defined by irreducible constraint architecture, whereas swappable explanations are defined by redundancy and invariance across detail-level substitutions.** The test is counterfactual: what breaks when a part is changed, and can another part do its job?
