## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** *Epistemic progress is best defined as growth in the load-bearing explanatory structure of a system’s cognition: the extent to which its representations constrain possible answers, survive criticism, and support counterfactual variation without collapsing into ad hoc repair. It can therefore be measured not by output fluency or benchmark success, but by how tightly integrated, revisable, and world-discriminating its internal explanations are.*

This perspective matters because fluency, benchmarks, and popularity mostly evaluate **answer production under familiar selection pressures**. Those can be satisfied by shallow pattern completion. If the details inside a system are interchangeable without affecting what it can answer, then the “knowledge” is a narrative shell: performance may be high, but the explanatory core is weak.

So the key distinction is between:

- **Production competence:** ability to emit acceptable answers.
- **Epistemic competence:** possession of internal structures whose details are *load-bearing*.

A detail is load-bearing if changing it forces compensating changes elsewhere or reduces the range of phenomena the system can explain. In a real explanatory structure, parts co-constrain each other. That mutual constraint is the sign of knowledge rather than mimicry.

From this, epistemic progress can be operationalized as increases in at least four properties:

1. **Counterfactual dependence:**  
   If a central representation is altered, do many predictions, explanations, or plans change in a principled way? If yes, the structure has explanatory grip.

2. **Compression with constraint:**  
   Does the system explain more with fewer independent assumptions *without* making details arbitrary? Mere compression is not enough; the compressed core must force consequences.

3. **Error-correction quality:**  
   When the system fails, can it localize the failure to specific assumptions and revise them non-arbitrarily? Progress appears when corrections improve the whole structure rather than patch isolated outputs.

4. **Transfer by necessity, not analogy:**  
   Can the same internal structure generate competence in novel domains because the explanation genuinely applies there? Success that transfers only after surface remapping is weaker than success forced by shared underlying structure.

This implies a family of measurements independent of popularity or benchmarks:

- **Intervention tests on representations:** perturb internal components and measure downstream reorganization.
- **Explanatory ablation:** remove purported “knowledge” and test whether broad competence degrades coherently.
- **Revision discipline:** quantify whether new data leads to minimal, global, principled updates versus proliferating patches.
- **Cross-context invariance:** test whether the same internal commitments constrain behavior across tasks with different surfaces but shared deep structure.

The illuminating consequence is this: **epistemic progress is not primarily more correct outputs, but more non-arbitrary internal necessity.** A system progresses when its successful performances become increasingly hard to preserve under arbitrary rewrites of its internals. In that condition, answers are no longer free-floating products; they are the visible consequences of an integrated explanatory engine.

So the conjecture is that the right unit of epistemic measurement is **load-bearingness of cognition**: how much of a system’s success depends on specific, mutually constraining internal explanations rather than interchangeable heuristics.
