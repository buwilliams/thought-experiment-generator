## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The main bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly *not* the substrate, but the *computational organization of criticism* above it: how candidate explanations are generated, exposed to error, selected, retained, and recombined. More compute enlarges the search; better epistemic structure changes what is searchable and what survives.

From the given perspective, intelligence and evolution are both computation under selection. So the key question is not “how much raw computation do we have?” but “what selection dynamics are acting on informational variants?” A giant model is a substrate capable of vast transformation, but epistemic progress requires a specific kind of computation: conjecture-and-criticism. If the system mostly predicts, imitates, or locally optimizes, then more scale mainly amplifies interpolation over existing informational regularities. That can improve performance dramatically without proportionally improving explanation-formation.

A thought experiment: imagine two systems with equal total compute. System A is a monolithic next-token predictor scaled to the limit. System B is smaller, but organized into a population of generators, critics, experiment-designers, memory structures, and promotion rules that reward error-correction rather than mere predictive fit. If the task is epistemic progress—finding better explanations, not just better outputs—System B should eventually outperform A, because it implements richer selection over hypotheses. The difference is not metaphysical but computational: B has more powerful *evolutionary structure*.

This reframes model size as analogous to available energy in biology: necessary, often limiting at first, but not sufficient to explain major transitions. Major epistemic advances occur when new higher-level selection mechanisms appear—genes, sex, science, peer criticism, writing, markets of ideas. Each is a structural innovation in how information is varied and selected. By analogy, AI progress will bottleneck where current systems lack institutions of criticism: stable world models, explicit conjecture generation, adversarial testing, cross-episode error retention, and promotion mechanisms keyed to explanatory depth rather than benchmark success.

What follows is a systems claim: current frontier AI may be trapped in a loop where the stock of parameters grows rapidly, but the flow of corrigible knowledge grows slowly because feedback is shallow. Benchmarks, RLHF, and static corpora mostly select for usefulness, plausibility, and compliance—not for surviving severe criticism. So the leverage point is above the substrate: redesign the feedback loops by which hypotheses are generated, tested against reality and rival hypotheses, and preserved only when they solve problems without creating worse ones.

This does not imply compute no longer matters. Substrate still sets the feasible space of structures. But once a threshold of representational and search capacity is crossed, returns shift from scaling the processor to scaling the *epistemic architecture*. In short: **for AI epistemic progress, compute is increasingly the enabler; structure is the selector; and selection is where knowledge actually grows.**
