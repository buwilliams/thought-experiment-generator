## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Epistemic progress should be defined as an increase in an agent’s or community’s **capacity to improve beliefs across repeated inquiry cycles**, especially under criticism, revision, and future reuse—not as immediate answer quality, benchmark score, or social approval. From an iterated-game perspective, knowledge-seeking is not a one-shot output task but a repeated interaction between conjectures, tests, errors, and revisers. What matters is whether a system becomes a better long-run cooperator with reality and with future inquirers.

**Core claim:**  
A belief-forming system shows epistemic progress when its outputs and practices make future correction, coordination, and discovery more likely. This is structurally analogous to cooperation in repeated games: good epistemic behavior is sustained when present claims remain accountable to future scrutiny. Fluency and popularity can win in one-shot settings, just as defection can win in a single prisoner’s dilemma, but they are poor indicators of long-run epistemic fitness.

**What follows from this collision:**  
If answer production is separated from knowledge-seeking, then epistemic progress is best measured by variables that track the **shadow of the future** in inquiry:

1. **Error-correction responsiveness**  
   How reliably does the system update when shown counterevidence or contradiction? Progress means shorter lag to revision, fewer repeated errors, and more targeted correction.

2. **Cross-temporal consistency under scrutiny**  
   Do claims made now remain stable or improve when revisited later with new evidence? A system making disposable, context-fragile answers is behaving like a short-horizon defector.

3. **Inspectable reasoning and defeasibility**  
   Are claims presented in ways that permit others to test, challenge, and refine them? Epistemic progress rises when outputs expose their vulnerabilities instead of obscuring them behind persuasive fluency.

4. **Generativity for future inquiry**  
   Does a claim help others ask better questions, design better tests, or compress reality more fruitfully? Progress is not just “being right” but enabling more productive next rounds.

5. **Reputation grounded in track record of correction, not charisma**  
   In repeated games, cooperation depends on visible history. Likewise, epistemic standing should depend on how often a source survives criticism, retracts cleanly, and improves over time.

**Measurement implication:**  
Use **longitudinal audit** rather than snapshot evaluation. Track revision quality, retained accuracy after challenge, recurrence of known mistakes, calibration over time, and downstream usefulness for other investigators. The key unit is not isolated performance but **trajectory across rounds**.

**Prediction:**  
When institutions shorten time horizons, hide defections from truth-seeking (e.g. unretracted errors, opaque methods, attention rewards for confidence), apparent success will decouple from epistemic progress. Fluency and popularity then dominate, just as cooperation collapses in repeated games when future accountability weakens.

So epistemic progress is best understood as **improved performance in the iterated game of inquiry**: becoming more corrigible, more testable, and more useful to future rounds of knowledge creation.
