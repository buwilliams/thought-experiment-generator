## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Next-token prediction learns latent variables that track real domain invariants **when the token stream is generated by an underlying computational process whose future is compressible only through state variables that are themselves stable under the process’s dynamics**. In that case, prediction pressure selects for internal representations that approximate those state variables, because they are the shortest reusable programs for reducing predictive error across many contexts.

From the computation-first perspective, the issue is not “language vs world,” but whether the observed sequence is an output channel of some deeper computation. If the stream is produced by a process with hidden state—physical, social, biological, formal—then prediction is equivalent to system identification. The learner is pushed to infer whatever internal variables let it emulate the generator. Latent variables become “real domain invariants” not because they are metaphysically privileged, but because they are **causally conserved summaries** of the computation generating the data.

So prediction is sufficient only under a structural condition:

1. **There exists hidden state** in the source process.  
2. **That state persists and constrains future emissions** across many tokens.  
3. **Token-level regularities are insufficient** to predict well without modeling that state.  
4. **The training distribution repeatedly exposes the same underlying dynamics** from varied surface forms, so shallow memorization is dominated by state-tracking.  
5. **Model capacity and optimization favor reusable compression** over brittle lookup.

Under these conditions, latent-variable learning is forced for the same reason evolution discovers wings in aerodynamic niches: selection favors whatever internal machinery captures the invariant constraints of the environment. Prediction is the selection pressure; latent world-models are one class of adaptive solution.

What follows is that “sufficiency of prediction” is not a property of the objective alone, but of the **generator–channel–learner system**. If the token stream is mostly local, degenerate, adversarial, or dominated by socially repeated but causally thin patterns, next-token prediction can succeed by learning superficial correlates. Then no deep invariant is forced, because the environment does not punish shallow computation. But when the stream is the lossy projection of a rich process and predictive success requires integrating long-range constraints, hidden causes, or conserved relations, the model is driven toward variables that correspond to those invariants.

A useful thought experiment: imagine two worlds generating text. In World A, each token is sampled from a high-order but finite Markov table with no deeper structure. In World B, text is emitted by agents interacting with persistent objects under stable rules. In A, prediction learns statistics. In B, prediction increasingly becomes inverse computation: recovering agents, objects, goals, and rules, because those are the minimal sufficient states of the generator.

So the collision yields this illumination: **next-token prediction induces truth-tracking representations when prediction is, in effect, compressed simulation of a stable underlying computation**. The boundary is not between words and world, but between outputs generated by shallow surface statistics and outputs generated by invariant-bearing processes.
