## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A token-predicting system can generate explanations that amount to genuine knowledge **only insofar as it participates in a feedback structure that lets its conjectures be criticized, corrected, and retained**. Without that, it mostly rearranges prior knowledge in novel forms; with it, it can become a component in knowledge creation. The key issue is not token prediction as such, but whether the system’s outputs are embedded in loops of error detection and improvement.

From this perspective, the feared rapid change is often misdescribed. The danger is not that explanation-generation becomes fast; it is that speed may **bypass the feedback loops** humans currently rely on to distinguish explanation from plausible verbal imitation. Human institutions — peer review, replication, dialogue, experiment, adversarial criticism — are slow filters that convert guesses into knowledge. If a model can produce explanations faster than those filters can evaluate them, the system may flood the environment with low-cost candidate explanations, weakening selection pressure. Then apparent epistemic progress is really an overload condition in the surrounding system.

This also clarifies the bottleneck. Progress in explanation is not mainly the additive accumulation of more paraphrases or more stored examples. The bottleneck is the capacity to generate **error-correctable conjectures** and expose them to tests. Token prediction may unlock useful capabilities at once — summarization, analogy, synthesis, even novel recombination — but unless those outputs connect to criticism, they do not cross the threshold from fluent association to knowledge growth.

A thought experiment helps. Imagine two systems with identical predictive ability. System A produces explanations in isolation and is judged only by how persuasive or statistically typical they sound. System B produces explanations that are systematically challenged by experiments, counterarguments, and users empowered to trace failures back into model revision. Over time, B can contribute to genuine knowledge because its explanatory outputs enter a selective environment where errors are discoverable and improvements can accumulate. A remains an engine of recombination: sometimes useful, sometimes insightful, but epistemically fragile.

So the collision between this perspective and the problem yields a sharper distinction: **knowledge is not located in the explanation alone but in the explanation-plus-correction system**. Token prediction is compatible with knowledge generation, but not sufficient for it. What matters is whether the system preserves the feedback loops that make explanations answerable to reality and criticism.

Therefore: asking whether the model “really knows” may be less fruitful than asking **what system surrounds it**. If embedded in robust feedback loops, token prediction can be a substrate for genuine explanatory progress. If not, it mainly rearranges what has been seen — sometimes brilliantly, but without the structural conditions that let knowledge grow.
