## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The main bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is not the substrate by itself, but the **structure above it**: the feedback architecture by which conjectures are generated, criticized, selected, and retained. More compute matters, but mainly when it helps instantiate better epistemic loops. Rapid gains become dangerous or disappointing not because they are fast, but because they can outpace the feedback processes that distinguish error-correcting progress from mere capability amplification.

Here is the collision of perspectives.

If we ask a systems question, model size and compute are **stocks**: reservoirs of representational and search capacity. They can raise the ceiling of what an AI system can do. But epistemic progress depends on **flows and feedback loops**: how candidate explanations are formed, how errors are exposed, how criticism is preserved, and how good methods are promoted over attractive failures. A larger stock without improved feedback often yields more fluent conjectures, not more knowledge.

From a fallibilist perspective, knowledge grows through **conjecture and criticism**, not through accumulation alone. So if a model becomes bigger but still lacks structures for targeted criticism, adversarial testing, causal attribution of mistakes, and institutional memory of failed ideas, then scaling mostly increases the volume and polish of outputs. It does not reliably improve truth-tracking. In that case, the limiting factor is structural: the system cannot convert capability into knowledge at a higher rate.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two systems:

- **System A:** 100x more compute, but evaluation remains based on short-horizon benchmarks, popularity signals, and weak post hoc filtering.
- **System B:** same substrate as today, but embedded in a strong epistemic structure: explicit hypothesis tracking, systematic adversarial criticism, environments that reveal error over time, mechanisms that reward corrigibility and explanatory depth, and promotion based on demonstrated error-correction rather than surface performance.

System A will likely look more impressive faster. But System B is more likely to produce cumulative epistemic progress, because it has tighter negative feedback against falsehood and better retention of genuine improvements. The bottleneck, therefore, is whichever factor most constrains error-correcting loops. Today that appears to be structure more than substrate.

What follows is that some feared rapid AI change is dangerous not because speed itself is bad, but because speed can **remove or bypass the human and institutional feedback loops** on which current safety and governance depend. Similarly, progress may appear substrate-driven when in fact compute is merely resolving one bottleneck in a broader system; if the higher-level epistemic structure remains weak, gains plateau into unreliability.

So the deeper claim is: **substrate improvements unlock capability, but structural improvements determine whether that capability becomes knowledge.** If we want epistemic progress rather than just stronger pattern production, the highest-leverage interventions are in the architecture of criticism, testing, memory, and selection.
