## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Next-token prediction learns latent variables that track real domain invariants **only when prediction error cannot be driven low by shallow correlations alone** — that is, when the data-generating process structurally forces successful prediction to pass through compressed, reusable state variables that remain stable across many contexts.

From the requested lens, the key issue is not whether prediction is “powerful” in general, but **what feedback loops the objective preserves or removes**. Next-token prediction gives one feedback loop only: reward for local predictive accuracy. If a domain allows that loop to close using surface statistics, then the system has no structural reason to discover deeper invariants. Fast improvement in loss can then be misleading: it reflects removal of a performance bottleneck, not acquisition of world structure.

So the relevant condition is a **bottleneck mismatch**:

- If the easiest route to lower loss is memorization, stylistic continuation, or exploiting short-range regularities, then those strategies absorb optimization pressure.
- If, however, the domain has long-range dependencies, partial observability, and repeated hidden causes whose effects recur across contexts, then low loss depends on inferring latent state. In that case, latent-variable learning is not an optional elegance but a structural necessity.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Compare two corpora:

1. A corpus where every sentence can be predicted from local co-occurrence and genre patterns.
2. A corpus generated by an underlying simulator where visible tokens are sparse emissions from stable hidden variables, and future emissions depend on those hidden variables more than on recent token surface form.

In the first case, next-token prediction can improve additively through many shallow heuristics. In the second, there is a **governing bottleneck**: unless the model infers hidden state, many predictions remain systematically bad. Solving that bottleneck unlocks many capabilities at once — consistency, long-horizon prediction, counterfactual robustness, and transfer.

Thus the condition is not merely “the task is hard enough,” but more specifically:

- **Information about latent causes must be distributed across observations** so that integrating evidence pays off.
- **The latent causes must be reusable** across many prediction situations, making abstraction compression-efficient.
- **Surface shortcuts must be insufficient** at the scale of performance demanded.
- **Training and architecture must preserve gradients across the relevant horizon**, so the objective can actually reinforce latent-state construction.

This also illuminates why sufficiency claims are fragile. Prediction alone does not guarantee invariant discovery unless the environment denies cheap alternatives. Current safety and interpretability debates often miss this systems point: the danger is not rapid capability gain per se, but capability gain achieved by bypassing the feedback loops that would reveal whether models learned genuine structure or merely exploited dataset regularities.

So the conjecture is: **next-token prediction is sufficient for learning real domain invariants only in domains where invariant-tracking is the dominant error-correcting pathway available to optimization**. Where cheaper predictive loops exist, prediction will often select them instead.
