## Conjecture

Conjecture: At the limit, the bottleneck is structural, not substrate.

Push both variables to extremes.

First, send substrate toward infinity: arbitrarily large models, effectively unbounded compute, vast memory, perfect retrieval, endless synthetic data. What happens? You do not automatically get epistemic progress. You get a system able to generate, compress, imitate, and recombine at enormous scale. But scale alone does not tell it which errors matter, which conjectures are interesting, which tests are severe, or which apparent successes are artifacts of the evaluation loop. Without structures for criticism, error-correction, and promotion of better explanations over merely better predictions, infinite substrate tends toward infinite elaboration of current biases. The failure mode at the boundary is not ignorance but self-sealing sophistication.

Now send substrate toward zero while preserving structure: very limited compute, small models, narrow memory, but a strong process for forming bold conjectures, exposing them to hard tests, tracking failure, and updating what gets reused. Progress becomes slow and local, but not impossible. Science itself historically advanced under severe substrate constraints because its epistemic structure was good enough to create knowledge growth from error correction. This suggests substrate is a rate limiter; structure is the possibility condition.

Now push structure to zero while substrate remains high: huge models, no genuine criticism, weak test design, reward by benchmark gaming, popularity, or short-term usefulness. Then “progress” collapses into selection for outputs that survive local incentives. The system may look powerful while becoming epistemically stagnant. It accumulates answers without improving the process that distinguishes truth from persuasive error.

Push structure toward infinity: idealized conjecture generation, adversarial testing, transparent criticism, and promotion based on explanatory power and error detection. Even on finite substrate, such a system keeps converting mistakes into knowledge. More compute still helps, but mainly because the structure knows how to spend it.

So the hidden structure revealed at the boundary is this: substrate expands the reachable search space; structure determines whether search becomes knowledge. Compute is a stock. Epistemic method is a feedback loop. Stocks can be increased directly; feedback loops determine whether increases compound into progress or merely scale failure.

Therefore: for current AI, the deeper bottleneck is above the substrate — in the institutions, objectives, interfaces, and selection mechanisms by which conjectures are generated, criticized, tested, and retained. More substrate can mask this bottleneck for a while by buying breadth and fluency. But at the edge, where normal gains saturate, the decisive variable is whether the system contains a functioning error-correcting epistemology. If not, scaling approaches a ceiling of polished confusion. If yes, even modest systems can continue to improve.
