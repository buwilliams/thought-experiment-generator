## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The main bottleneck is increasingly **in the structure above the substrate**, because epistemic progress in AI is a **collective-action problem**: generating, testing, and elevating better conjectures is costly and diffuse in benefit, while scaling substrate is legible, privately rewardable, and institutionally easy to enforce.

From this perspective, “more compute” and “bigger models” are not just technical choices; they are the **stable norm** of the field because they solve coordination problems for labs, funders, benchmark-makers, and prestige systems. Everyone can observe FLOPs, parameter counts, benchmark deltas, and product launches. These are easy to reward, easy to defend internally, and easy to narrate externally. So the norm reproduces itself.

By contrast, improvements in the structure above the substrate — better conjecture formation, adversarial criticism, sharper problem decomposition, replication, interpretability tied to theory, institutionalized error-correction — are **hard to meter and easy to free-ride on**. Who pays for them? Usually a lab that may not capture the gains, because better epistemic methods spill over to rivals and the whole field. Who benefits? Often everyone, later. Is defection privately rewarding? Yes: a lab can skip costly criticism and still harvest benchmark wins or product revenue. That means the norm of “epistemic discipline” is structurally fragile unless institutions make it unavoidable.

Thought experiment: imagine two worlds with equal compute growth. In World A, labs must publicly pre-register capability claims, run adversarial evaluations, preserve negative results, and reward internal criticism. In World B, they need only ship benchmark gains. World A will likely produce fewer flashy claims in the short run but more reliable knowledge about what systems can do, why they fail, and which research directions are dead ends. The substrate is identical; the epistemic output differs because the **social machinery selecting and transmitting conjectures** differs.

What follows is not that substrate is unimportant. If models are too weak, higher-order epistemic structure has little to work on. But once substrate crosses a threshold where systems can produce rich behavior, the marginal return to more scale declines unless the surrounding institutions can distinguish real understanding from accidental success. Otherwise the field gets trapped in a norm-equilibrium of **cheap demonstration, expensive criticism**.

So the illuminating claim is: the debate is misframed if it asks “compute or ideas?” in isolation. The reason compute appears bottleneck-like is that the field has built powerful institutions for funding, enforcing, and celebrating substrate expansion, while leaving conjecture-formation and criticism under-incentivized. The true bottleneck is therefore **structural selection pressure**: what kinds of claims, methods, and researchers the system reliably rewards. If you change that, epistemic progress per unit compute likely rises sharply.
