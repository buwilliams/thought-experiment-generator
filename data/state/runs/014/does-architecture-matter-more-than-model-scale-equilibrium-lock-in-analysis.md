## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The present bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly **structural rather than substrate-limited**: more model size and compute still improve capability, but the main reason progress stalls on truth-seeking is that the surrounding system is locked into an equilibrium that rewards **plausible output, benchmark gains, and product utility** more than **genuine conjecture-criticism cycles**.

From an equilibrium lock-in perspective, this matters because many actors would benefit from better epistemic performance—labs want more reliable systems, users want truth, evaluators want robustness, researchers want scientific insight—but **no single actor can unilaterally shift the regime** without incurring local losses. A lab that diverts resources from scale to deeper mechanisms for conjecture generation, adversarial testing, error preservation, and criticism-friendly evaluation may lose on visible metrics, shipping speed, and investor confidence. Evaluators who use harder truth-tracking standards risk becoming irrelevant if the field coordinates around easier benchmarks. Users often cannot distinguish polished confabulation from genuine knowledge creation at point of use. So the system keeps selecting for what is legible and monetizable, even if most participants would prefer a world with stronger epistemic machinery.

What follows is that the substrate-vs-structure question is partly misframed. Compute is not irrelevant; it is a **stock** that enlarges the search space of possible hypotheses and behaviors. But whether that enlarged space yields epistemic progress depends on the **feedback loops above the substrate**: how models are prompted to form conjectures, how errors are surfaced, whether criticism is preserved rather than trained away into style conformity, and what gets rewarded in training, publication, deployment, and procurement. In systems terms, current loops are optimized for **output acceptability**, not **knowledge growth**.

A useful thought experiment: imagine a frontier model with effectively abundant compute but inserted into today’s incentive structure. It would likely become better at producing persuasive, benchmark-winning, commercially useful answers—yet still be weak at initiating bold explanatory conjectures, exposing its own errors, or participating in institutions that reward refutation over fluency. Now imagine only modestly better models placed in a structure explicitly built for conjecture and criticism: adversarial debate, persistent error libraries, evaluation by out-of-distribution explanation quality, incentives for discovering where the model fails, and promotion mechanisms tied to corrigibility rather than confidence. The second world plausibly yields faster epistemic progress.

So the illumination is: **substrate advances are being mistaken for the bottleneck because they are the easiest unilateral move inside the current equilibrium.** Structural reform looks harder not necessarily because it is less important, but because it requires coordination across labs, evaluators, product teams, and users. Therefore the strongest prediction is not “compute no longer matters,” but: **returns to scale on epistemic progress will increasingly diminish unless the higher-level selection environment changes.** The bottleneck is where the equilibrium traps reform.
