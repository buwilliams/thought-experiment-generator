## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
When applied to the problem of defining epistemic progress apart from fluency, benchmark scores, or popularity-weighted success, **Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis suggests that current evaluation regimes are trapped in a coordination equilibrium that rewards answer production because it is legible, cheap, and individually rational to optimize, even if all serious participants would prefer systems that genuinely improve reasoning.** As a result, the main obstacle is not lack of conceptual access to better metrics, but a structural trap: no single lab, evaluator, or buyer can afford to shift first to “reasoning-quality” measures unless others do too.

What is true in this collision is that **fluency and benchmark success function as equilibrium-compatible proxies**, not necessarily as knowledge-sensitive ones. They persist because they solve local incentives: they are easy to compare, market, and automate. But if the conjectured distinction between *answer production* and *reasoning improvement* is real, then these proxies systematically select for systems that produce acceptable outputs under existing tests rather than systems that correct errors, generate better conjectures, preserve criticism, or become less wrong over time.

From this perspective, **epistemic progress should be defined structurally as an increase in a system’s capacity to undergo error-correcting improvement**, not as an increase in its presentational competence. A model, method, or institution has made epistemic progress to the extent that it can:

1. **Expose its own errors to criticism**,  
2. **Revise internal representations or procedures in ways that reduce future error**,  
3. **Transfer improvements across domains rather than merely fit a test distribution**, and  
4. **Resist regression into persuasive but non-corrective output production**.

This implies that measurement should target **counterfactual learnability and corrigibility**, not just observed performance. Useful indicators would include: performance gains after criticism, robustness under adversarial reframing, ability to identify conditions of likely failure, retention of corrections across contexts, and improvement in solving novel problems after sparse feedback. These are signs that the system is not merely generating outputs but participating in a process of knowledge growth.

The equilibrium insight illuminates why such measures remain underused: they are typically slower, noisier, harder to standardize, and less marketable than benchmarks. So the absence of adoption should not be read as evidence that reasoning-sensitive metrics are impossible or unimportant. It may instead indicate a **Nash lock-in around superficial epistemic proxies**.

Therefore the key implication is: **if we want to measure epistemic progress independently of fluency, we should stop asking “How impressive are the answers?” and ask “How does the system change when confronted with error?”** The latter tracks knowledge growth; the former mostly tracks equilibrium fitness within existing evaluation institutions.
