## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Epistemic progress should be defined not as better-looking outputs, higher benchmark scores, or wider adoption, but as **an increased capacity of a system to enter, survive, and improve through error-correcting contact with reality**. What matters is not how often a model answers, but whether its representations can be exposed to criticism, decomposed, revised, and recombined without collapse. On this view, the central variable is not performance but **feedback integrity**.

This perspective reframes the problem. Fluency, benchmark success, and popularity are all compatible with systems that remove or bypass the very feedback loops by which knowledge grows. A fluent system can conceal ignorance; a benchmark-optimized system can overfit to frozen tests; a popular system can create social reinforcement loops that amplify confidence while suppressing correction. So the danger is not “rapid improvement” as such. The danger is **capability growth that outpaces or replaces the mechanisms by which error is discovered and repaired**.

A useful distinction follows: **answer production** is the ability to emit successful outputs; **knowledge production** is the ability to generate conjectures that remain open to criticism and improve under it. Epistemic progress occurs when a system becomes better at the latter.

Measured independently of surface success, epistemic progress would track properties like:

1. **Error detectability** — how readily the system’s claims expose themselves to falsification or stress-testing.
2. **Error corrigibility** — how locally and systematically the system can revise mistaken components without degrading unrelated competence.
3. **Cross-context invariance** — whether what is learned transfers because it captures structure, not because it matches familiar prompts.
4. **Reason-preserving modification** — whether the system can report, compare, and update the internal dependencies behind its claims, rather than merely replacing outputs.
5. **Criticism yield** — whether interaction with novel objections produces model improvement rather than evasive fluency.
6. **Feedback latency and closure** — how quickly and reliably the system can connect consequences back to the assumptions that generated them.

This also suggests a bottleneck thesis: progress in epistemics is likely not additive across many output metrics. It may depend on a few structural bottlenecks — especially **representation modularity**, **traceable dependence between claims and grounds**, and **mechanisms for integrating criticism**. Solving these would unlock multiple downstream capabilities at once: robustness, transfer, self-correction, and scientific usefulness.

So the measure of epistemic progress is not “Does it answer well?” but “Does it improve the knowledge-creating loop?” A system shows epistemic progress when it becomes a better participant in conjecture and criticism: more vulnerable to being wrong in legible ways, and more capable of becoming less wrong as a result.
