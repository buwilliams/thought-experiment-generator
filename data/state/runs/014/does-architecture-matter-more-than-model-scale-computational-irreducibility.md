## Conjecture

Conjecture: **The main bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly structural rather than substrate-level, because conjecture generation, criticism, and promotion form a computationally irreducible search process that cannot be bypassed merely by scaling the underlying model.**

If computational irreducibility is the right lens, then the hope that “more compute + bigger models” will straightforwardly yield faster epistemic progress is limited. For many domains, especially open-ended inquiry, there is no compressed shortcut to the actual process of generating candidate explanations, exposing them to error, and iterating. The system must, in effect, run that search. Scaling substrate helps run more of it, but does not replace it.

What follows is a distinction between **capacity** and **epistemic dynamics**. Substrate improvements increase capacity: larger search space coverage, better memory, more simulations, more candidate hypotheses. But if the structure above the substrate poorly forms conjectures, weakly tests them, or promotes them according to proxies like plausibility, consensus, or benchmark reward, then extra compute mostly amplifies the wrong dynamics. The irreducible process is not “token generation” or “parameter optimization” alone, but the whole loop by which explanations are proposed and selected.

This reframes current AI limitations. Apparent unpredictability in epistemic performance is not just because models are not yet large enough; it may be because genuine knowledge growth depends on institutional and algorithmic structures that force confrontation with error. In irreducible domains, you do not get to jump from data to truth through a hidden closed-form solution. You need a generative rule for inquiry itself: how to produce bold variants, design severe tests, preserve useful criticism, and prevent premature convergence.

A useful thought experiment: imagine two systems with equal compute. One is a massive predictor optimized to continue patterns; the other is smaller but embedded in a structure that explicitly tracks explanatory commitments, seeks refutations, archives failed ideas, and reallocates attention based on criticism rather than mere success signals. In computationally irreducible domains, the second may produce more epistemic progress, because it better instantiates the necessary search process rather than merely accelerating surface-level pattern completion.

So the collision of perspectives suggests: **substrate matters until it is sufficient to sustain rich search, but beyond that point the bottleneck shifts to the architecture of epistemic iteration.** The leverage lies in higher-level structures: curricula for conjecture, mechanisms for adversarial testing, memory of criticism, and promotion rules aligned with error-correction rather than performance mimicry.

In short: if inquiry is irreducible, then scaling is fuel, not a substitute for the engine.
