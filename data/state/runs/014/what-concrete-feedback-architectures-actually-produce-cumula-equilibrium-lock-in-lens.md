## Conjecture

Conjecture: In AI, cumulative epistemic error-correction is underprovided not mainly because builders are confused about its value, but because the key feedback goods are public within and across organizations: once created, criticism protocols, durable audit trails, reproducible evaluations, and shared failure taxonomies benefit many actors diffusely while imposing concentrated costs on the team that builds and maintains them. Therefore the default equilibrium is not “everyone forgot to do science,” but a stable no-provision or thin-provision regime in which local optimization dominates and error-correcting loops remain ornamental.

What is true on this view is structural. A lab can privately capture gains from capability improvements, product speed, and benchmark wins. But it captures only part of the value of robust adversarial criticism, long-horizon monitoring, and institutional memory, because these reduce systemic error, repeated mistakes, and collective delusion in ways that spill over to future teams, adjacent products, and even competitors. So individually rational actors fund just enough “feedback” to satisfy immediate engineering needs or governance optics, not enough to build cumulative criticism.

What follows is a diagnostic distinction: many things called feedback are not epistemic loops. Preference optimization, RLHF-style polishing, leaderboard iteration, and post-hoc dashboards can increase performance while suppressing discoverable error. They are often control loops aimed at short-term objective attainment, not truth-tracking loops aimed at exposing and preserving falsification. The missing architectures are those that make criticism durable, legible, and action-forcing.

The concrete feedback architectures that plausibly generate cumulative error-correction therefore share four properties:

1. Persistent memory of failure: immutable incident logs, versioned rationales, and replayable traces so criticism survives personnel turnover and model churn.

2. Adversarial independence: evaluators, red teams, or critique models whose incentives are not aligned with shipping pressure, so criticism is not absorbed into the optimization target too early.

3. Cross-case aggregation: taxonomies and registries that turn one-off failures into reusable abstractions, allowing generalization rather than anecdotal patching.

4. Closure mechanisms: thresholds, gates, or mandatory design responses that force criticized systems to change, preventing feedback from becoming mere commentary.

A thought experiment clarifies the point. Imagine two labs with equally smart researchers. One has superb local metrics but no durable archive of failed hypotheses, no independent critics, and no mechanism binding critique to redesign. The other has mediocre raw speed but preserves failures, rewards serious refutation, and propagates lessons across projects. Over time, only the second accumulates epistemic capital. The first merely re-optimizes around recurring blind spots.

So the illumination is this: the core problem is not adding more feedback channels, but provisioning the public goods that convert criticism into cumulative knowledge. The leverage point is institutional architecture, not motivational exhortation.
