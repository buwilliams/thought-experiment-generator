## Conjecture

Conjecture: the main bottleneck is increasingly in the structure above the substrate, because “more substrate yields more epistemic progress” is not a tightly constrained explanation, while “progress depends on how conjectures are generated, criticized, and selected” is.

Why this perspective points there: ask which details are load-bearing. In a substrate-first story, many details are swappable. If we change model size, training FLOPs, or context length, the explanation usually survives by saying progress scales with “capability.” If one ingredient underperforms, another can be substituted: more data, better hardware, longer training, larger inference budgets. That makes the account descriptively flexible but weakly explanatory. It does not tell us what kind of error-correction is missing, only that “more” helps.

By contrast, a structure-first explanation has parts that constrain each other. If conjecture formation is poor, then increasing compute mainly amplifies low-quality hypothesis generation. If testing is weak, the system cannot distinguish elegant falsehoods from hard-won truths. If promotion is misaligned, good ideas lose to persuasive or benchmark-optimized ones. Change any one of those parts and compensating changes are required elsewhere: stronger testing can partly rescue noisy conjecture generation; better promotion can surface minority but fertile lines of inquiry; absent these, scale mostly increases throughput, not knowledge creation. These details are load-bearing because each alters what the system can account for.

A useful thought experiment: imagine two labs with equal compute. One has crude but explicit institutions for proposing bold hypotheses, adversarially criticizing them, preserving dissenting alternatives, and rewarding explanations that survive severe tests. The other has 10x more training budget but optimizes primarily for next benchmark gains and persuasive outputs. If the first lab systematically produces more corrigible models, better transfer outside training distributions, and faster recovery from failure, then substrate was not the main bottleneck. The key difference would be structure: the feedback loops that turn errors into knowledge.

What follows is not that substrate is irrelevant. It is load-bearing up to the point where models can represent, retain, and manipulate rich conjectures at all. Below that threshold, structure cannot express itself. But once that threshold is crossed, substrate becomes a permissive condition, while epistemic structure becomes the selecting condition. More compute broadens the search; better structure changes the search’s objective and error-correction dynamics.

So the collision yields this claim: for current and near-future AI epistemic progress, substrate is necessary but increasingly non-explanatory at the margin; the decisive bottleneck is the meta-structure that governs conjecture, criticism, and promotion. The strongest evidence would be interventions that improve truth-tracking without major scale increases: better adversarial evaluation, mechanisms for preserving rival explanations, and reward structures tied to surviving criticism rather than merely producing plausible answers.
