## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** An explanation has “all load-bearing parts” when it was originally constructed to solve a *specific failure of variation* in prior accounts, whereas an explanation with freely swappable details inherits the structure of older explanatory forms designed for *classification, persuasion, or compression* rather than for surviving criticism.

Apply the origin question: where did “explanations” come from as a distinct practice? They emerged not merely to describe regularities, but to answer dissatisfaction with earlier forms: myth, taxonomy, analogy, narrative, and curve-fitting. Those earlier forms often worked by substitutability. If one god, metaphor, parameterization, or anecdote could be replaced by another while preserving the apparent fit, the account still functioned socially or predictively. What they solved was intelligibility or coordination, not necessarily truth-tracking.

A genuinely explanatory structure arose to solve a different prior problem: *why this and not otherwise?* That problem selects for internal necessity. In such an account, the parts are constrained by their role in excluding alternatives. Remove or alter one element and the explanation no longer answers the question it was built to answer.

So the structural distinction is this:

1. **Historical function:**  
   - **Swappable-detail accounts** descend from representational forms whose job was to summarize appearances.  
   - **Load-bearing explanations** descend from problem-solving structures whose job was to rule out rival possibilities.

2. **Counterfactual dependence:**  
   In a load-bearing explanation, each major part participates in a network of counterfactual commitments. Change the part, and the explanation changes its implications, scope, or compatibility with the phenomenon. In a swappable account, details are decorative because the account’s success does not depend on them.

3. **Inheritance of unexamined assumptions:**  
   We often still inherit from pre-explanatory traditions the idea that an explanation is “good” if it sounds coherent, matches the data, or offers a familiar mechanism. But those criteria tolerate replaceability. They mistake *fit* for *constraint*. The hidden assumption is that explanatory detail is additive ornamentation rather than a set of mutually dependent necessities.

4. **Test of origin-sensitive structure:**  
   Ask of each part: *What prior explanatory failure was this element introduced to prevent?* If no such failure can be named, the part is likely swappable. If the part exists because without it the account reverts to an older, criticized inadequacy, it is load-bearing.

What follows is that “load-bearingness” is not about complexity or precision alone. It is about whether the explanation was shaped by a history of criticism that made each retained component necessary. The best marker is not descriptive richness but **non-arbitrariness under variation**.

So: explanations whose parts are all load-bearing are those that bear the scars of the problems they were invented to solve. Freely swappable explanations are often fossils of earlier epistemic functions still masquerading as explanation.
