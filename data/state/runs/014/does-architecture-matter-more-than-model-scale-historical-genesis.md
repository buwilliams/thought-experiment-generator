## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The main bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly **structural rather than substrate-bound**: more model size and compute still matter, but they are now often being used to compensate for inherited defects in how systems generate, criticize, retain, and elevate conjectures.

Why this perspective points there: ask where the current paradigm came from. Modern scaling-centered AI emerged as a solution to an earlier problem: brittle, hand-built symbolic systems could not cope with messy reality. Deep learning replaced explicit epistemic structure with statistical absorption of vast regularities. That solved one problem brilliantly — feature engineering and narrow formalism — but it also inherited an unexamined assumption: that better prediction is a sufficient proxy for better knowledge formation.

That assumption calcified. If a model improves with scale, then the field naturally treats the substrate as the primary lever. But historically, “more substrate” was standing in for something else: a way to extract useful behavior without having solved the harder problem of how to form and criticize explanations. Scale was not just power; it was a workaround.

So the key question is: what prior limitation is scale still solving, and what is it no longer solving? Scale helps when the system lacks enough representational capacity, training signal, or search breadth. But once models can already produce many candidate answers, the limiting factor shifts to **selection and correction**: which conjectures get surfaced, what criticism they survive, how error is localized, how abstractions are stabilized, and how better procedures are retained rather than rediscovered.

On that view, current AI inherits a pre-epistemic architecture: next-token prediction, benchmark optimization, preference tuning, and shallow retrieval. These are effective descendants of the original goal — produce useful outputs from data — but they do not by themselves constitute a strong machinery for knowledge growth. They are weak on adversarial criticism, explicit problem decomposition, preserving discovered explanations, and institutional memory across episodes of reasoning. The system often generates plausible continuations rather than bold conjectures exposed to real refutation.

What follows is not that substrate is irrelevant. Rather: **substrate improvements now have diminishing epistemic returns unless paired with better higher-level structures of conjecture and criticism**. A larger model inside a poor epistemic loop mainly becomes a more fluent defender of untested ideas. A smaller model inside a strong loop can outperform its raw capability by making error-correction cumulative.

So the hidden historical inheritance is this: AI still treats intelligence as if the central problem were recovering competence from data scarcity and hand-design. But if the frontier problem has become knowledge creation, then the bottleneck is upstream of scale — in the architecture of epistemic institutions above the model: how hypotheses are proposed, attacked, revised, archived, and promoted. In short, substrate got us past brittleness; structure is what would get us to progress.
