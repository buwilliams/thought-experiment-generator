## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Epistemic progress should be defined as an increase in a system’s *error-correcting power* over a domain: its ability to generate claims that survive criticism, expose its own failure modes, and improve under adversarial contact. Measured this way, progress is not “better answers” in isolation but lower-cost, more reliable *knowledge maintenance*.

From the collective-action perspective, fluency, benchmark scores, and popularity are bad proxies because they are cheap to display and privately rewarding to optimize, while genuine error-correction is costly and often locally punishing. A model, lab, or institution gets immediate gains from appearing competent; it pays immediate costs for admitting uncertainty, surfacing anomalies, preserving dissent, and building mechanisms that let critics win. So if we want an independent notion of epistemic progress, we should track the structures that make truth-seeking unavoidable rather than optional.

That suggests a definition:

**Epistemic progress = improvement in the social-technical system that produces, transmits, and revises knowledge under criticism.**

This can be measured by asking:

1. **Who pays to detect error?**  
   If criticism depends on unpaid volunteers or reputational martyrs, the system is weak. Progress occurs when error detection is institutionalized and funded.

2. **What happens to defectors?**  
   If agents can gain by bluffing, cherry-picking, or laundering confidence into status, then answer production has outrun truth-tracking. Progress means reducing the private reward for epistemic defection.

3. **Can the system preserve and use negative knowledge?**  
   Not just what works, but what failed, why it failed, and under what conditions. A system that forgets its errors is performing intelligence, not accumulating it.

4. **Does criticism change future behavior?**  
   Benchmark success may show competence at static tasks; epistemic progress shows up as durable updates to methods, models, and institutions after failure.

5. **Is transfer robust under hostile conditions?**  
   Knowledge is stronger when it remains usable across contexts, users, and adversaries—not just in curated demonstrations.

So the key independent metrics are not output quality alone but **correction capacity**: anomaly yield, reproducibility under distribution shift, calibration under adversarial testing, retention of failed hypotheses, diversity and survival of criticism channels, and the latency between discovered error and system update.

The answer-production / truth-tracking distinction becomes sharper here. Answer production is what a system outputs when rewarded for legibility and success signals. Truth-tracking is what a system does when forced to bear the cost of being wrong over time. The latter requires enforcement: archives, audit trails, adversarial evaluation, incentives for replication, and norms that protect inconvenient criticism.

What this perspective illuminates is that epistemic stagnation is often not ignorance but an equilibrium: everyone benefits from fluent performance, while too few are paid to maintain the costly machinery of error correction. Therefore, independent measures of epistemic progress should focus on whether institutions make criticism compulsory, memory durable, and defection unattractive. That is where knowledge grows.
