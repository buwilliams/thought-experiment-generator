## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Epistemic progress is best defined not as improved output quality on tasks, nor as social uptake, but as an increase in the **system’s capacity to generate, preserve, and improve error-correcting explanations under novel criticism**. What matters is not the performance of isolated answers, but the interaction structure between conjecture, criticism, revision, memory, and transfer.

From the emergence perspective, the key mistake is to look for epistemic progress in components: a model’s fluency, a benchmark score, a citation count, or even a single correct answer. These are local behaviors. They can rise while the system’s knowledge-generating structure stagnates or worsens. A system can become more persuasive, faster, and more popular while becoming *less* capable of discovering and correcting its own errors.

So the explanatory target should shift from outputs to the **feedback architecture** of inquiry. A system exhibits epistemic progress when its internal and external interactions increasingly do the following:

1. **Expose errors rather than hide them**  
   Progress occurs when the structure makes falsehoods easier to detect, isolate, and revise.

2. **Retain criticism and revisions**  
   Corrections must not be one-off patches. The system should preserve what was learned from failure and make it reusable.

3. **Support cross-context transfer**  
   Explanations improved in one domain should constrain and illuminate others. Knowledge is not a bag of successes but a network of mutually constraining ideas.

4. **Generate fruitful new problems**  
   Progress is visible when better explanations create new, non-arbitrary questions and sharper discriminations, not merely more answers.

5. **Improve under adversarial novelty**  
   A genuinely epistemic system does not just interpolate on familiar tasks; it reorganizes successfully when criticism arrives from outside its training or prestige environment.

This suggests measurement should focus on **structural indicators of error-correction**, not surface competence. For example:

- How well does the system revise beliefs after targeted criticism?
- Do corrections propagate coherently across related claims?
- Can it distinguish explanation from mere regularity-fitting?
- Does it preserve the history of why a view changed?
- Under novel counterexamples, does it degenerate into patchwork or produce deeper unification?

A useful thought experiment is to compare two systems. System A produces brilliant answers, high benchmark scores, and widespread approval, but cannot explain its revisions, localizes fixes, and repeatedly reintroduces old mistakes in new settings. System B is less fluent and less popular, but systematically identifies its own failure modes, integrates criticism into general principles, and improves its handling of unseen problems. On the proposed definition, **B shows more epistemic progress** even if A looks more successful by ordinary metrics.

So the conjecture is: **epistemic progress is an emergent property of an inquiry system’s feedback structure, measurable by the increasing quality of its error-correction dynamics, not by the immediate impressiveness of its outputs.** The distinction between answer production and knowledge creation is therefore real: the former can improve without the latter, because knowledge resides in the system’s evolving organization of criticism and correction.
