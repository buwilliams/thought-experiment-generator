## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Next-token prediction learns latent variables that track real domain invariants **only when those invariants are part of a predictive equilibrium the model cannot evade by exploiting cheaper correlational shortcuts**. The decisive condition is not merely that the invariant exists, but that the training distribution and objective make *all locally available non-invariant strategies unstable or inferior*. Where shortcut strategies remain individually sufficient on the observed data, learning the invariant is not forced.

From an **equilibrium lock-in** perspective, the issue is structural. Gradient descent, finite model capacity, dataset composition, and the token-level loss together form a system with multiple equilibria. One equilibrium is “world-modeling”: internal states organize around stable latent causes because those causes compress and predict across contexts. Another is “surface mimicry”: the model relies on lexical, positional, stylistic, or frequency-based proxies that achieve low loss without representing the deeper variable. If the proxy equilibrium gives adequate reward, no single update step has reason to “defect” toward the harder invariant-representing solution. The training process becomes locked into a shallow basin.

So prediction is sufficient only when the task distribution **removes the viability of those shortcut equilibria**. This happens under conditions like:

1. **Cross-context variation with invariant preservation:** the same latent factor must explain many superficially different sequences.  
2. **Proxy breakdown:** superficial cues are unreliable or adversarially scrambled, so token-level success cannot be maintained by local heuristics.  
3. **Long-range predictive dependence:** future tokens depend on hidden state in a way that cannot be reconstructed from recent surface statistics alone.  
4. **Compression advantage:** representing the latent variable is a simpler, more reusable solution than memorizing many context-specific correlations.  
5. **Sufficient exploration/optimization power:** training must be capable of reaching the invariant-based basin rather than getting trapped in an easier local optimum.

A useful thought experiment: imagine two corpora generated by the same hidden variables. In Corpus A, every latent state is paired with a stable stylistic marker; in Corpus B, those markers are randomized across episodes while the latent dynamics remain. In A, next-token prediction can succeed from style alone, so invariant learning is not forced. In B, style shortcuts collapse, and prediction pressure shifts toward discovering the hidden state. The difference is not in the objective but in the equilibrium structure of the data-generating environment.

Thus the conjecture sharpens: **prediction does not inherently force realism about latent structure; it forces whatever internal organization is necessary to escape all cheaper predictive equilibria**. Real domain invariants emerge when they are the only stable bargaining point between data structure and optimization. Where alternative equilibria exist, failure to learn invariants is not a contingent mistake but a structural consequence of the training game.
