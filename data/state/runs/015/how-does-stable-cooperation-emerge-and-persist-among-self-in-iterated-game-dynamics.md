## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges when the interaction structure makes future consequences matter more than short-term gains from defection. In repeated games, cooperation is not explained primarily by virtue or altruism, but by a system of incentives in which agents expect to meet again, can observe each other’s behavior, and can condition future actions on past conduct. Under those conditions, cooperation becomes a self-reinforcing equilibrium.

The key collision between iterated game dynamics and this problem is that “without central enforcement” does not mean “without enforcement.” Repetition itself generates endogenous enforcement through **reciprocity, reputation, and retaliation**. If I defect today, I may gain immediately, but I risk losing profitable cooperation tomorrow. The “shadow of the future” converts isolated choices into a feedback loop: present behavior updates others’ expectations, which alters future treatment, which changes the payoff of current action. Cooperation persists when that loop is strong enough.

This implies that the stability of cooperation depends less on agents’ internal morality than on several structural features:

1. **Repeated interaction:** Agents must expect future encounters. If the game becomes effectively one-shot, defection dominates.
2. **Observability or memory:** Others must be able to detect, remember, or infer defection. Hidden defection weakens reciprocity.
3. **Partner responsiveness:** Agents must be able to reward cooperation and punish defection, even minimally, by withholding future benefits.
4. **Sufficiently long horizons:** If actors discount the future too steeply, immediate temptation overwhelms delayed loss.
5. **Network continuity:** If agents can disappear, reset identity, or constantly switch partners, reputation loses force.

From a systems perspective, cooperation is therefore an emergent property of **informational and temporal structure**. Reputation functions as a stock of social capital; cooperative acts increase it, defections deplete it. Reciprocity is the feedback mechanism that links that stock to future payoffs. The absence of central authority is not fatal if local feedbacks are reliable enough.

What follows is a practical prediction: cooperation will collapse not mainly when people become more selfish, but when institutions or environments **shorten time horizons, reduce visibility, fragment memory, or sever repeated ties**. Anonymous markets, high turnover, weak record-keeping, and unstable identities systematically favor opportunism. Conversely, even among narrowly self-interested agents, cooperation can arise if the system preserves reputational information and keeps future interaction salient.

So the explanatory center shifts: the question is not “Why are self-interested agents cooperative?” but “What repeated-game structures make cooperation payoff-maximizing?” The answer is that stable cooperation persists when defection is made legible and consequential across time.
