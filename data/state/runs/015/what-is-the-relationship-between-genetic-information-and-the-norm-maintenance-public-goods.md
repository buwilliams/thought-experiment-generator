## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is not primarily “more genes → more complex organism,” but “more effective systems for enforcing, interpreting, and stabilizing developmental coordination → more complex organism.” Genes function less like a full blueprint and more like a sparse rule-set embedded in a costly, self-maintaining collective-action system of cells.

From the given perspective, an organism is a multi-level cooperation problem. Billions of cells must divide, specialize, signal, and refrain from defecting. The central question becomes: what in the genome helps sustain this coordination, and what other structures bear the burden of enforcing it?

That shifts attention away from raw gene count toward the **institutions of development**: regulatory networks, timing systems, chromatin states, error-correction, signaling pathways, spatial gradients, apoptosis, immune surveillance, and tissue-level constraints. These are the mechanisms that make compliance with the organismal plan hard to evade and deviation costly. A complex organism therefore need not encode every detail explicitly in DNA; it can encode rules that generate and police orderly interaction among cells.

So the genome’s contribution to complexity is more like a constitution than a census of parts. What matters is not just how much information is stored, but how it structures flows of information during development and maintenance. Small genomic changes in regulatory control can reorganize large downstream patterns because they alter the terms of cellular coordination.

This perspective also clarifies why **defection** is central. For an individual cell, unconstrained proliferation or refusal to specialize can be privately rewarding. Cancer is the paradigm case. Thus, organismal complexity rises only insofar as the system can afford stronger enforcement against cellular defection. More complex organisms are not just more elaborately encoded; they are more heavily governed. Part of what genetic information encodes is the capacity to make multicellular order robust against cheating.

It follows that the “complexity gap” between genome size and organism complexity is expected. Much of the organism’s apparent complexity is offloaded onto self-organizing developmental processes and environmental regularities, provided institutions exist to keep those processes within viable bounds. The genome does not specify the mature organism line by line; it specifies a process whose participants are incentivized, constrained, and corrected into producing one.

So the illuminating claim is: **genetic information scales organismal complexity indirectly, by encoding the rules and enforcement mechanisms of cellular collective action, not by directly listing every structural feature of the final organism.** Complexity therefore depends less on the amount of genetic information than on the architecture of its interpretation and the unavoidable institutions that maintain cooperation across scales.
