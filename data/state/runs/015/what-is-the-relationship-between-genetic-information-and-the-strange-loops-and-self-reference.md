## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is not linear or directly descriptive; instead, complexity arises when genetic instructions participate in *strange loops* of interpretation, regulation, and self-modification across hierarchical levels of the organism. Genes do not simply encode a complex body; they help construct a system that increasingly *reads, regulates, and reconstructs itself*, and it is this recursive closure that generates high complexity from comparatively limited genomic information.

### Collision of perspective and problem
If we approach biology through strange loops, the key fact is that DNA is not a one-way blueprint. A genome is interpreted by cellular machinery that the genome itself helps build. The “reader” of the code is partly produced by the code being read. This is already a loop: genes specify proteins, proteins build regulatory networks, those networks determine which genes are expressed, and expression changes the machinery that will interpret future expression.

At low levels, genetic information looks like sequence. At higher levels, it becomes developmental timing, spatial patterning, cell identity, tissue architecture, behavior, and environmental responsiveness. But traversing upward does not leave the genome behind; the higher-level organism feeds back into the lower-level reading of the genome through epigenetics, signaling, niche construction, hormonal states, neural activity, and selection on developmental pathways. The organism is, in part, a system for controlling how its own genetic text is interpreted.

### What follows
1. **Genome size is a poor proxy for complexity.** More base pairs do not straightforwardly mean a more complex organism, because complexity depends less on stored symbols than on the depth and richness of recursive interpretive loops acting on them.

2. **Regulation matters more than raw code volume.** The decisive variable is the architecture of feedback: gene regulatory networks, developmental constraints, reuse of modules, and context-sensitive expression. A modest genome can generate great complexity if embedded in sufficiently rich self-referential control structures.

3. **Development is computation, not transcription.** Organismal complexity is produced through iterative, path-dependent execution. The genome provides generative constraints and update rules, not an explicit parts list for the finished organism.

4. **Complexity is distributed across levels.** It is misleading to ask how much complexity is “in the genes alone.” The relevant information is spread across genome, cellular machinery, maternal inheritance, developmental environment, and the organism’s own feedback on itself. The loop, not any single level, is the causal unit.

### Illumination
This perspective reframes the gene–organism relationship: genetic information is more like the seed of a self-interpreting hierarchy than a compressed picture of the final creature. Organismal complexity appears when biological systems become recursive enough that the products of genetic expression participate in governing the conditions of their own future production. In short: **genetic information enables complexity not by containing it explicitly, but by bootstrapping strange loops that amplify, stabilize, and elaborate form.**
