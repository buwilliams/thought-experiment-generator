## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The key lock-in in abiogenesis was not initially the absence of replication, but the absence of any *individually favorable path* from loose chemistry to template-based self-replication. In prebiotic chemistry, many molecular arrangements could transiently cooperate to produce persistence, catalysis, or compartment stability, but no single molecule or reaction pathway could “defect” into full heredity on its own and gain a durable advantage. This implies that the origin of life was structurally blocked by an equilibrium in which chemistry kept cycling through locally stable, non-heritable regimes.

What follows is that “self-replicating chemistry” likely did not appear as a single invention — e.g. one miraculous replicator — but as a *system-level transition* that altered payoffs across a network of reactions. Before templating, catalytic molecules, energy gradients, and compartments could each improve local persistence, yet these improvements were not enough to stabilize cumulative evolution. The equilibrium trap was: useful products diffused away, catalysts were not reliably reproduced, compartments lacked faithful internal inheritance, and no component could solve this alone.

From the equilibrium-lock-in perspective, the decisive event was the emergence of a coupled structure in which several constraints were relaxed *simultaneously*:  
- **Compartmentalization** retained products and aligned the fate of cooperating molecules.  
- **Metabolic closure or autocatalytic cycles** increased local production of relevant building blocks.  
- **Template-like polymers** converted chemical recurrence into heritable recurrence.  

Only when these were linked did a new equilibrium become available — one where molecules that contributed to replication also improved the persistence of the whole protochemical system that recreated them. This is a coordination transition, not merely a chemical optimization.

This illuminates why origin-of-life debates often stall when asking which came first: metabolism, compartments, or genes. The lock-in analysis suggests this is partly a false sequencing problem. If each element alone is insufficient to escape the non-life equilibrium, then asking for a solitary first mover misdescribes the barrier. The real question is: what minimal coupled arrangement changed the incentive landscape of prebiotic chemistry so that replication became self-reinforcing rather than constantly undermined by dilution, side reactions, and decay?

It also distinguishes necessary structural barriers from contingent failures. The problem was not simply that chemistry “hadn’t yet gotten lucky enough” to produce a replicator. Rather, many plausible prebiotic chemistries may have been trapped in attractors where individually beneficial reactions did not compose into open-ended evolution. Life emerged when chemistry crossed from a regime of isolated local advantages to one of *mutually stabilizing constraints*, creating a new equilibrium in which heredity, variation, and selection could persist.

So the deepest claim is: **the origin of self-replicating chemistry was an escape from a coordination lock among prebiotic processes, not the standalone triumph of one molecule.**
