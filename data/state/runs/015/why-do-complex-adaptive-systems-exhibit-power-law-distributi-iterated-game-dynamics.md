## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law distributions and scale-free behavior when their local interactions are effectively *iterated games* with asymmetric visibility and memory: repeated encounters reward agents for attaching to already-proven partners, strategies, nodes, or signals, so past success recursively increases future interaction rates. Power laws then arise not from a static “preference for the large” alone, but from the game-theoretic structure of repeated selection under uncertainty.

**Reasoning from the perspective:**  
In a one-shot world, each interaction is largely independent. Agents have little reason to privilege established partners except immediate payoff, so link formation and resource allocation are more evenly distributed or bounded by local constraints. But in repeated play, the “shadow of the future” makes reliability, reputation, and reciprocation strategically valuable. Agents preferentially re-engage with those who have already demonstrated cooperative value. That creates a feedback loop:

1. **Early success or visibility** gives an agent more partners.  
2. **More partners** generate more opportunities to demonstrate reliability or extract value.  
3. **That history** makes others more likely to choose the same agent in future rounds.  
4. The cycle repeats, amplifying small early differences into highly unequal degree, influence, or wealth distributions.

This is a structural mechanism for **preferential attachment** recast as iterated-game dynamics. What looks like “the rich get richer” is often “the repeatedly trusted get repeatedly chosen.” Scale-free behavior appears because there is no characteristic cap on how much repeated interaction can accumulate around successful nodes; the interaction process compounds across rounds.

This perspective also explains why power laws are common in systems where cooperation and coordination matter: markets, social networks, citation networks, ecosystems, and technological platforms all involve repeated selection under uncertainty. Agents economize on search and defection risk by reusing relationships with known payoff histories. The distribution’s tail reflects the cumulative advantage produced by reputation-mediated repeated play.

**What follows:**  
- **Power laws should strengthen** when interactions are more repeated, histories are more legible, and partner choice is flexible.  
- **They should weaken** when horizons shorten, memory is erased, identities are unstable, or defections cannot be tracked—because the strategic value of reputation collapses.  
- Apparent scale-freeness is therefore partly a measure of how much the system lets the past condition the future.

**What this illuminates:**  
Scale-free structure is not just a statistical artifact or growth rule. It can be the frozen record of countless micro-level repeated games. Hubs are not merely large; they are often nodes around which cooperation, expectation, and repeated trust have been recursively organized. In this view, complex adaptive systems become scale-free because iteration turns interaction history into a self-reinforcing selection mechanism.
