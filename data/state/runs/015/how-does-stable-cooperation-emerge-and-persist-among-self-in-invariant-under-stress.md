## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges when the interaction structure imposes repeated variation, local observability, and consequences for partner choice, because under those conditions predictive success requires learning **agent-level and norm-level invariants** rather than exploiting one-off opportunities for defection.

From the stated lens, the key question is not “why would selfish agents ever cooperate?” but “under what conditions does minimizing loss on future interactions force agents to model stable structure?” In a one-shot or weakly coupled environment, shortcuts work: defecting can harvest immediate payoff, and simple heuristics keyed to superficial cues may perform well enough. Cooperation is then fragile because prediction does not need to discover anything deep about trustworthiness, reciprocity, reputation, or norm enforcement.

But when agents face **repeated interaction, shifting partners, memory, and the possibility of exclusion or retaliation**, superficial correlations fail. An agent that relies on cheap exploitation or brittle pattern-matching incurs losses across varied contexts. To do well, it must learn more invariant features: who reciprocates, which signals are reliable, which behaviors update reputation, and which local norms persist across encounters. In this setting, cooperation is not primarily altruism; it is a compression of stable regularities in the social environment.

What follows is that persistent cooperation depends less on moral transformation than on a **generative social structure** with three properties:

1. **Variation:** agents meet across multiple contexts and counterparties, so exploitative shortcuts do not generalize.
2. **Intervention/consequence:** actions change future opportunities through reputation, retaliation, repair, or ostracism.
3. **Generalization pressure:** long-run payoff matters enough that agents benefit from modeling durable patterns rather than extracting immediate gains.

These conditions create selection pressure for strategies that track real invariants of the social system. Reciprocity, fairness norms, and commitment devices survive not because they are intrinsically virtuous, but because they become the best predictors of future returns in an environment where shallow strategies overfit.

This also illuminates why cooperation often collapses. If anonymity rises, memory is erased, mobility is too high, signaling is cheap, or punishment is noisy, then low-loss shortcuts become available again. Agents can profit by defecting without learning or respecting deeper structure. In systems terms, cooperation is sustained by feedback loops that make present behavior informative about future payoffs.

So the deeper claim is: **stable cooperation without central enforcement is an epistemic achievement of the system**. It appears when the ecology of interaction makes genuine social invariants legible and payoff-relevant. Where the structure does not force that discovery, self-interest can coast on shortcuts, and cooperation remains thin, local, or illusory.
