## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement **when the environment makes cooperative behavior computationally legible**: actions must leave durable traces, relevant actors must be able to observe or cheaply infer those traces, signals must be hard enough to fake relative to the benefits of cheating, and interactions must be sufficiently repeated or networked that reputational updates feed back into future payoffs. Under those conditions, enforcement is implemented not by a sovereign punisher but by a distributed computation: the system stores memory, updates trust, routes cooperation, and excludes defectors.

From the computational perspective, central enforcement and decentralized enforcement are not categorically different. They are alternative architectures for solving the same problem: **how to transform local observations into behavior-shaping constraints**. A state does this through concentrated sensing, record-keeping, adjudication, and coercion. A decentralized infrastructure does it through distributed logs, reputation systems, cryptographic attestations, mutual monitoring, protocol rules, and repeated-game feedback. In both cases, the substrate is information processing under selection.

What follows is that substitution depends less on ideology than on **informational boundary conditions**:

1. **Persistent memory:** past actions must remain available long enough to matter.  
2. **Observability:** defections must be detectable, or at least inferable with acceptable error.  
3. **Signal reliability:** identities, commitments, and reports must resist cheap forgery.  
4. **Repetition or relational closure:** actors must expect future interactions, directly or indirectly.  
5. **Low enough interpretive ambiguity:** the relevant norm must be machine-readable or socially legible enough for distributed updating.

Where these conditions hold, decentralized systems can outperform central ones, especially in environments too fast, granular, transnational, or heterogeneous for centralized authorities to monitor effectively. They can also be more evolvable: local rule variations can be tried in parallel, and successful coordination protocols can spread by selection.

Their hard limits appear where the computational problem becomes underdetermined. If harms are **hidden, one-shot, identity-fluid, high-lag, or semantically contested**, decentralized informational enforcement loses traction. A reputation system cannot discipline what it cannot observe; a ledger cannot resolve what is not formally specifiable; cryptographic proof cannot establish all socially meaningful facts; distributed consensus does not eliminate the need for costly judgment where evidence is ambiguous. In such settings, central enforcement is not merely a stronger version of the same thing but a different computational strategy: it concentrates authority to resolve ambiguity, compel disclosure, absorb investigatory cost, and impose sanctions even when local informational feedback is insufficient.

So the limit is not “decentralization versus centralization” in the abstract. The limit is whether the target domain permits **enough valid distributed computation of norm-relevant reality**. Where reality is legible, repeated, and attestable, decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute substantially for central enforcement. Where reality is opaque, manipulable, or irreducibly interpretive, they cannot fully substitute; they must either hybridize with centralized institutions or fail.
