## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents does not primarily emerge by overcoming self-interest, but by historically transforming what counts as “self-interest” through inherited information structures — reputation, norms, roles, accounting systems, and shared narratives — that were originally invented to solve repeated coordination and uncertainty problems. What persists is not cooperation as such, but social machinery that makes defection locally costly, legible, and self-undermining.

Asked genealogically, the problem itself is already loaded. “How can cooperation arise without central enforcement?” presumes a baseline world of isolated, preference-maximizing individuals to which cooperation must be added. But this framing is itself an inheritance from legal, economic, and game-theoretic abstractions that replaced older pictures of persons as already embedded in kinship, ritual, reciprocity, and status systems. In many historical settings, agents were never merely self-interested atoms facing a cooperation problem; they were constituted by institutions that pre-structured incentives, identities, and expectations.

So the prior problem was less “how to get selfish individuals to cooperate” than “how to make behavior predictable enough that ongoing exchange, coexistence, and mutual vulnerability become tolerable.” The solutions that emerged — custom, oath, taboo, credit, reputation, honor, punishment, and later contract — were information technologies before they were moral ideals. They reduced uncertainty across time.

What these systems inherited without examination is the assumption that cooperation depends on monitoring defections. But the deeper leverage may be elsewhere: not in punishing bad actors harder, but in designing environments where contributions are visible, memory is durable, partner choice is possible, and future interaction matters. In systems terms, cooperation stabilizes when feedback loops link present behavior to future opportunity. Reputation is a stock; gossip, records, and observation are flows; trust and exclusion are feedback effects. Where these loops are weak, defection is rational. Where they are strong, “self-interest” is redefined by the system’s structure.

This also explains persistence. Cooperation lasts not because equilibrium has been intellectually solved, but because institutions store knowledge about past failures. Norms are compressed history: they encode prior discoveries about what repeatedly destroyed group viability. Their opacity is a feature of cultural evolution. Participants may obey rules without knowing the original problem the rules solved.

What follows is that the central contrast — cooperation with or without central enforcement — is misleading. Durable cooperation usually depends on **distributed enforcement** embedded in social, informational, and material structure. Central authority is just one late and specialized descendant of older mechanisms for making conduct observable and consequences recursive.

So the illuminating claim is: **stable cooperation emerges when a system evolves memory, legibility, and selective continuation of interaction, such that exploiting others damages the exploiter’s own future pathway through the system.** The key question is not “why cooperate?” but “what inherited structures make non-cooperation a losing strategy over time?”
