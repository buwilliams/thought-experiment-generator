## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only in the limit where the system can manufacture a functional equivalent of “omniscient memory”: durable records, cheap verification, high observability of defections, and repeated interaction dense enough that future consequences dominate immediate gains. Their hard limit appears at the boundary where action becomes cheap to hide, identity becomes cheap to shed, and harms become fast, diffuse, or irreversible relative to the network’s capacity to detect and respond.

Pushed to the extremes, the structure becomes clearer:

- At one extreme, imagine **perfect memory, perfect observability, stable identity, and infinite repetition**. In that world, decentralized enforcement is nearly sufficient. Reputation becomes enforcement. Signaling becomes credible because lies are permanently exposed. Cooperation can be sustained by distributed refusal, exclusion, and reciprocal adjustment. Central coercion adds little because the informational substrate already carries consequence through time.

- At the opposite extreme, imagine **zero memory, zero observability, one-shot interaction, and disposable identities**. Then decentralized infrastructure collapses as an enforcement substitute. No record accumulates, no threat is credible, no signal can be anchored, and no future exists against which present cheating can be priced. In this limit, central enforcement is not merely helpful but structurally different: it supplies consequences from outside the informational game.

This suggests the core condition: decentralized systems do not replace enforcement by eliminating coercion; they replace it by **moving coercion into the temporal and relational structure of information**. Memory is the key stock; observability and verification are its inflows; forgetting, anonymity, and noise are its outflows. Repeated interaction is the feedback loop that converts record into discipline.

The hard limits follow from boundary cases:

1. **Irreversible harms:** If damage occurs before reputation can update—fraud, sabotage, violence, systemic contagion—informational punishment is too late.
2. **Low observability domains:** Where actions are private, technically opaque, or deniable, decentralized memory records signals about signals, not reality.
3. **Cheap pseudonymity / identity reset:** If actors can defect and re-enter at low cost, memory no longer attaches to persons or assets.
4. **Sparse future dependence:** In transient, one-shot, or highly mobile environments, the shadow of the future is too weak to discipline behavior.
5. **Coordination thresholds:** Even with information, decentralized punishment may fail when response requires synchronized collective action; free-riding reappears at the enforcement layer itself.
6. **Adversarial manipulation:** If signaling channels can be flooded, forged, or strategically gamed, the infrastructure produces counterfeit trust rather than trust.

So the deeper claim is: **decentralized informational infrastructures are strongest where they can simulate continuity—of identity, record, and future consequence—and weakest where the world permits discontinuity.** Central enforcement becomes necessary not simply when people are untrustworthy, but when the informational environment cannot bind actions to enduring consequences. The boundary is not moral but structural.
