What follows is that the origin of life is better understood as a transition from **linear causation** to **recursive causation**. Nonliving chemistry mostly dissipates. Living chemistry begins when dissipation is locally harnessed into structures that repeatedly re-create the conditions for their own persistence. This is a strange loop because the system is made of molecules obeying local rules, yet at the network level it feeds back downward, selecting which local events continue to occur. Traversing upward from molecules to metabolism/compartmentalization and back downward returns you to the same chemistry, now biased by the organization it produced.

This also reframes the “without a prior template” objection. A prior *sequence template* may not have existed, but a prior *organizational template* could have: recurring geochemical environments, mineral surfaces, wet-dry cycles, and compartmental microenvironments supplied stable scaffolds within which self-reinforcing loops could form. The first replicator, then, was likely not a thing but a **loop that copied its own generative context**.

So the conjecture is: **life began when chemical systems became strange loops — when networks of reactions started reproducing the constraints that reproduced the networks.** Genetic templates were not the origin of replication but the refinement of a deeper self-referential organization already present in prebiotic chemistry.
