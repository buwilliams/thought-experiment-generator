## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement **when the system’s interaction structure makes defection legible, memory persistent, signals hard to fake, and future relationships consequential**. Their efficacy is an emergent property of the network, not of the virtue or rationality of individual participants. Where these conditions hold, enforcement is displaced from a centralized coercive node into distributed feedback loops of observation, reputation, exclusion, and reciprocal adjustment. Where they fail, no amount of local goodwill compensates: the system ceases to generate enforceable order.

More precisely, decentralized enforcement works when five structural conditions align:

1. **Persistent memory:** actions leave durable records, whether social, technical, or institutional. Without memory, repeated interaction cannot accumulate consequences.
2. **Sufficient observability:** relevant behavior is visible, directly or indirectly, to those who can respond to it. Hidden defections break the feedback loop.
3. **Credible signaling:** participants can communicate quality, compliance, or intent in ways that are costly to fake or externally verifiable. Cheap talk does not stabilize trust.
4. **Repeated interaction or reputational spillover:** present behavior must affect future opportunities. If interactions are one-shot or identities are disposable, decentralized sanction loses force.
5. **Coordinated response capacity:** network members must be able to update on shared information and alter behavior accordingly — refusing service, withdrawing trust, rerouting exchange, or broadcasting warnings.

Under these conditions, enforcement emerges from the **structure of connected expectations**. The “policing” function is performed by feedback: observed action updates shared belief; shared belief changes future access; changed access alters incentives. Order is thus not located in a sovereign center but in the system’s capacity to remember, compare, and propagate consequences.

The hard limits also appear structurally. Decentralized informational enforcement breaks down when behavior is **opaque**, harms are **diffuse or delayed**, identities are **cheaply reset**, signals are **easily counterfeited**, or the network is too **fragmented** to converge on common updates. It also struggles where coordination against defectors is a public good: everyone benefits from sanctioning, but no one bears the local cost. In such cases, the system cannot reliably close the loop between violation and consequence.

A deeper limit is that informational infrastructures can often govern **eligibility, trust, and association**, but not always **physical compliance**. They are strongest where participation in valued networks is voluntary and conditionable; weakest where actors can extract gains despite reputational loss, or where immediate coercion is required.

So the substitution is never absolute. Central enforcement is most replaceable in environments where order depends primarily on **informational feedback**, and least replaceable where order depends on **monopolized force, adjudication under ambiguity, or action against actors outside the network’s consequence structure**. The key insight is that enforcement capacity is not a thing possessed only by states; it can emerge from distributed memory and signaling — but only when the system’s interaction topology makes consequences inescapably recursive.
