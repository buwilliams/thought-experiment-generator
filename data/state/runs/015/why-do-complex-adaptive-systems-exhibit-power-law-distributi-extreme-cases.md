## Conjecture

Conjecture: Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law distributions and scale-free behavior when their core adaptive processes remain operative across arbitrarily many scales, so that no characteristic size is stably selected by the system’s own dynamics. At the boundary where “typical scale” disappears, power laws emerge as the generic visible trace of adaptation under repeated amplification, constraint, and selection.

Push the problem to the limit: imagine a system growing toward infinity in size, while local agents continue to adapt using only local information. Now push the smallest unit toward zero: every agent still follows the same kind of rule — copy success, attach to what is already useful, exploit available pathways, conserve costly links, respond to feedback. If the same generative logic survives both enlargements and refinements, then the system has no privileged middle scale. In that case, exponential or Gaussian forms become unstable descriptions, because those presume a characteristic scale produced by averaging or decay. But adaptation in an open, heterogeneous environment constantly breaks such averaging.

At the upper boundary, success breeds visibility, visibility attracts interaction, interaction breeds further success: a positive feedback loop. At the lower boundary, extinction, cost, and competition prune most agents or links before they grow. Between these boundaries, multiplicative growth plus selective pruning produces many small events and a few giant ones. This is exactly the regime in which power laws appear.

What is illuminated at the edge is that scale-free behavior is not primarily a “distributional fact” but a structural consequence of systems poised between unbounded reinforcement and bounded resources. Systems thinking makes this clearer: the relevant structure is a reinforcing loop nested inside balancing loops. Reinforcement alone would drive monopoly or blow-up; balancing alone would produce uniformity or fixed scale. Power laws arise when neither loop fully closes the dynamics — when adaptation keeps reopening the possibility that the already-large become larger, while constraints keep most trajectories small.

Computational irreducibility matters here too. In such systems, one should not expect a compact predictive formula for which node, species, strategy, or cascade becomes dominant. The microscopic path is historically contingent. But the minimal generative rule can still be simple: repeated local adaptation under heterogeneous opportunity and feedback, with no global scale imposed. The power law is then the coarse-grained residue of innumerable irreducible histories.

So the conjecture is: scale-free structure appears when a complex adaptive system is open-ended enough that adaptation does not converge to a characteristic scale, yet constrained enough that growth remains selective rather than uniform. Power laws are the signature of this boundary condition — where the system can propagate advantage across scales without ever normalizing it into a stable average.
