## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges not primarily when agents become less selfish, but when the **boundary conditions of defection are made locally self-defeating** — that is, when repeated interaction, memory, and network visibility push exploitation to its limit and cause it to collapse under its own informational consequences.

### Collision of perspective and problem
Push the problem to extremes:

- At the limit of **one-shot, anonymous, perfectly mobile interaction**, cooperation should vanish. Defection dominates because information cannot accumulate and consequences do not return to the actor.
- At the opposite limit — **infinite repetition, perfect memory, persistent identity, and dense observability** — defection becomes progressively less viable, because every short-term gain is converted into long-term exclusion, retaliation, or missed future gains.

The hidden structure appears at the boundary between these extremes: cooperation is sustained when a system allows **information about past behavior to persist and circulate faster than the benefits of opportunism**.

### What is true
Self-interest alone does not destroy cooperation. It destroys cooperation only in environments where behavior leaves no durable trace. Where actions generate reputational memory, partner selection, and conditional response, self-interest can favor cooperation because cooperative agents become better long-run bets.

Thus, cooperation is not mainly a moral achievement but a **systems effect** produced by:
1. **Repeated encounters**  
2. **Recognizable identity**  
3. **Memory or record of past behavior**  
4. **Selective association or exclusion**  
5. **Sufficiently low noise that cooperation and defection can be distinguished**

### What follows
The key threshold is not “Are agents altruistic enough?” but “Is the future thick enough to matter?” If the shadow of the future approaches zero, cooperation decays. If it becomes long and legible, cooperation can become the rational equilibrium for selfish agents.

Pushed further: if punishment is too costly, cooperation still fails. But if exclusion, refusal, and reputation are cheap, then enforcement becomes distributed rather than centralized. Stable cooperation therefore tends to arise first not through coercion, but through **decentralized feedback loops** that make defection reduce future options.

### What is illuminated
The persistence of cooperation depends on **the conservation of social information**. A society without central enforcement can still stabilize cooperation if it preserves knowledge of who did what to whom. In that sense, the fundamental resource is not virtue but **informational continuity**.

At the edge, this reveals a general rule:

> **Cooperation persists when the system converts local acts into durable informational consequences that feed back onto future payoffs.**

Without that loop, self-interest fragments the system. With it, self-interest can help maintain order.

So the deepest answer is structural: stable cooperation emerges when the environment is arranged such that defection wins only in the short run, while cooperation compounds across time.
