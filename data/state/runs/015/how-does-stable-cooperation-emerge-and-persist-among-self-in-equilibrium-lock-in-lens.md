## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents without central enforcement emerges when the interaction is structured so that cooperation ceases to function as a pure non-excludable public good and becomes, instead, a system of partially excludable, iterated, reputation-mediated benefits. In that case, the problem is not “why do selfish agents cooperate?” but “what institutional feedback loops convert diffuse gains into locally capturable returns?”

From the specified lens, the key distinction is between **mere reluctance** and **structural underprovision**. If cooperation produces benefits that are broadly shared and hard to withhold from defectors, then failure to cooperate need not reflect confusion, irrationality, or weak prosocial motivation. It may instead be the equilibrium result of individually rational action in a non-excludable setting: each agent prefers the cooperative world, but no agent can justify paying enough to sustain it alone. This is a classic no-provision trap.

So the central explanatory move is structural. Stable cooperation does not persist because self-interest disappears; it persists because the system changes the **payoff topology**. Repeated interaction, observability, partner choice, reputation, contingent reciprocity, and club-like boundaries all function as mechanisms that **relocalize benefits and costs**. They make cooperation less diffuse, defection more legible, and future gains more conditional on present behavior. In systems terms, these are feedback loops that amplify cooperative acts into downstream private advantages and convert opportunism into future exclusion or loss.

What follows is that “cooperation without central enforcement” is often misdescribed. It is usually not cooperation without enforcement altogether, but cooperation enforced by **decentralized informational and relational structure**. Gossip, memory, norms, network closure, and selective association are enforcement technologies. Their role is to solve underprovision by making at least some of the returns to cooperation excludable and some of the costs of defection unavoidable.

This also illuminates why cooperation is fragile in large anonymous populations. As benefits become more diffuse, monitoring weaker, and exclusion harder, the system drifts back toward the public-goods problem. Appeals to trust or virtue alone then have limited leverage, because the failure mode is structural, not primarily psychological. Conversely, even modest institutional changes can have outsized effects if they alter excludability, visibility, or repetition.

Thus the deepest answer is: **stable cooperation emerges when a social system evolves local mechanisms that prevent cooperation from remaining a purely diffuse good.** Persistence depends less on altruistic preference and more on whether the environment generates self-reinforcing loops in which cooperating is individually sensible because access, status, future exchange, or protection depends on it. The cooperation problem is therefore best understood not as overcoming selfishness, but as redesigning the structure that otherwise makes underprovision the rational outcome.
