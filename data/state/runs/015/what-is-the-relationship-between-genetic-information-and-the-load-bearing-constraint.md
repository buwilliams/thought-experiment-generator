## Conjecture

Conjecture: Genetic information is not a direct measure of organismal complexity; rather, it is a constrained set of generative instructions embedded in a larger, multilevel developmental system. The load-bearing parts of the explanation are the features that, if altered, force changes across many levels of biological organization: gene regulation, developmental timing, cell-cell signaling, and environmental responsiveness. By contrast, raw genome size or gene count are largely non-load-bearing details, since they can vary widely without proportionate changes in organismal complexity.

This perspective matters because “an organism is complex because it has more genetic information” is too interchangeable to explain much. If one swaps in a different genome size, gene count, or amount of noncoding DNA, many organisms remain comparably complex. That means those quantities alone are not carrying the explanatory weight. The explanation breaks unless we include the architecture of how genetic information is used.

What is load-bearing is the organization of information flow. Genes do not encode a finished organism in a one-to-one blueprint sense. They participate in regulatory networks that control when, where, and how gene products are expressed. If you change a core regulatory element, compensating changes are often required elsewhere in development; otherwise morphology, physiology, or viability shifts. That is a sign of real explanatory constraint. Complexity in the organism therefore follows less from the sheer amount of encoded sequence and more from the structure of interactions that sequence helps instantiate.

This suggests the relationship is indirect and systems-dependent. Genetic information sets up developmental rules, but organismal complexity emerges from iterative processes: gene expression, feedback loops, physical self-organization, stochastic variation, and environmental inputs. If any of these are removed from the explanation, the remaining story cannot account for phenomena like identical genomes producing different cell types, or similar gene counts across organisms with very different forms. So the genome is load-bearing, but only as part of a causally integrated system.

A useful formulation is: organismal complexity depends more on the compressive power and regulatory leverage of genetic information than on its bulk. A relatively compact genome can generate high complexity if it specifies a rich developmental program with reusable modules, hierarchical control, and context-sensitive regulation. This is analogous to a short generative rule producing elaborate output: the explanatory burden lies in the rule structure, not the length of the rule’s description alone.

So the relationship is not “more DNA -> more complex organism,” but “the more deeply genetic information constrains and coordinates a multiscale generative process, the more organismal complexity it can produce.” Genetic information is best understood as a set of intervention points in a developmental system, not as a scalar reservoir of complexity.
