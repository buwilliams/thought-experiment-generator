## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only where they change the game from a one-shot defection equilibrium into a repeated, observable coordination problem in which reputational memory, credible signaling, and low-cost exclusion make cooperation individually rational. Their hard limit appears where these informational mechanisms cannot alter payoffs enough to break lock-in: when agents cannot reliably observe defections, cannot preserve or transfer memory, cannot distinguish honest from strategic signaling, or cannot impose decentralized consequences on violators.

From an **equilibrium lock-in** perspective, the key question is not whether participants *want* cooperation, but whether any one participant can safely act as if cooperation will be reciprocated. In many decentralized settings, they cannot. If information about past behavior is sparse, manipulable, or local, then trust is not a public stock but a fragmented private guess. Under those conditions, each actor faces the same trap: cooperate and risk exploitation, or defect and remain safe. Central enforcement is then not merely administratively convenient; it is the only available mechanism for changing payoffs at scale.

Decentralized informational infrastructure works when it supplies the functional equivalents of enforcement without requiring a sovereign:  
1. **Memory**: persistent records of behavior;  
2. **Observability**: defections become legible to relevant others;  
3. **Signal credibility**: costly or hard-to-fake markers distinguish cooperators;  
4. **Repetition**: agents expect future interactions or future dependence on current reputation;  
5. **Distributed sanction capacity**: others can refuse trade, access, interoperability, or recognition.

These conditions create a feedback loop. Better memory and observability strengthen reputation; stronger reputation makes signaling meaningful; meaningful signals support repeated cooperation; repeated cooperation increases the value of maintaining standing. At that point, decentralized enforcement can be self-stabilizing.

But the hard limits are structural, not merely technical. Informational systems cannot substitute for enforcement where harms are **non-local, delayed, anonymous, or irreversible**. If the injured parties cannot identify violators, coordinate sanctions, or recover losses through exclusion alone, then information does not close the incentive gap. Likewise, when identity is cheap to reset, pseudonymity is unconstrained, communities are weakly repeated, or strategic actors can flood the system with counterfeit signals, decentralized memory loses force. The equilibrium remains locked in opportunism because no single honest actor can make trust pay.

A deeper limit is that informational infrastructures discipline mainly through **future deprivation**; they are weak against actors who can extract large one-time gains, externalize costs broadly, or exit after defection. Central enforcement remains necessary where rule-breaking produces concentrated private benefit and diffuse public harm, especially when sanctions require coercive recovery rather than mere refusal.

So the illumination is this: decentralized information can replace central enforcement only where it does enough **payoff engineering** to make mutual discipline an equilibrium. Where it cannot, calls for “better transparency” mistake an equilibrium constraint for an information deficit.
