## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Self-replicating chemistry did not begin with a fully specified molecular “template,” but at the boundary where matter is driven far from equilibrium and local persistence becomes the only stable outcome. At that edge, chemistry is selected not for complexity first, but for **repeatability of constraint**: reaction networks that recreate the conditions of their own continuation outcompete networks that merely occur once.

Pushed to the limit, the question becomes: what happens in a chemical world with **zero prior biological information** but **maximum flux** of energy and materials? Most reactions dissipate and vanish. But some sets of reactions alter their environment — pH, concentration gradients, catalytic surfaces, compartment boundaries — in ways that make the same reactions more likely to happen again. That is the minimal precursor of replication: not copying a sequence, but **reconstructing a generative niche**.

At the opposite limit, imagine perfect equilibrium. Nothing persists except stasis; no replication can originate because no process preferentially recurs. So the origin of self-replication must lie near the boundary between chaos and freeze-out: enough instability for novelty, enough recurrence for memory. The first “template” was therefore likely **distributed and dynamical**, not a molecule but a coupled system: cycles, surfaces, compartments, and gradients mutually regenerating one another.

What follows is that heredity begins before digital encoding. A protocell-like droplet, mineral pore, or autocatalytic cycle can “inherit” structure if its division or reformation statistically preserves the organization that recreates the same flow pattern. This is analog, low-fidelity heredity. Once such systems exist, any molecule that sharpens recurrence — for example by catalyzing a key step or stabilizing a boundary — is selected. Only later does high-fidelity templating emerge as a superior compression of this prior ecological memory.

So the hidden structure revealed at the boundary is this: **replication is a special case of self-maintaining causation**. Life did not start when a molecule copied itself exactly; it started when a chemical arrangement became better at causing the reappearance of arrangements of its own kind than its rivals were. Sequence templating was not the first cause but a later refinement that made this process more accurate, modular, and evolvable.

This reframes the origin problem. The key step is not “How did a template appear from nowhere?” but “What chemical systems can produce persistent recurrence before templating exists?” The answer is likely: systems where energy flow, confinement, and catalysis create feedback loops that preserve organization across cycles. In that view, non-living matter does not need to leap directly into molecular self-copying. It only needs to cross the smaller boundary where chemistry begins to remember itself.
