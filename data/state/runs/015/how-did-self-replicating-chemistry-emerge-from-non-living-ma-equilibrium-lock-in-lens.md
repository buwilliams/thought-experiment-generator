## Conjecture

Conjecture: the key barrier was not “lack of complexity” or “insufficient drive toward life,” but a structural underprovision problem in prebiotic chemistry. The crucial goods for replication — stable informational polymers, catalytic support, compartmentalization, energy coupling, and error-correction-like stabilization — were partly non-excludable and diffusely beneficial within local chemical environments. Any molecular lineage that produced them paid local costs while nearby, non-producing chemistries could often benefit. That makes a no-provision equilibrium plausible: chemistry can remain rich and reactive yet fail to cross into open-ended self-replication because the enabling conditions are public goods at the mesoscale.

What this illuminates is a shift from asking “which molecule first replicated?” to “what structures made the provision of replication-supporting goods locally excludable enough to persist?” In a well-mixed environment, catalysts, activated monomers, favorable pH gradients, or membrane-like boundaries are easily exploited or dissipated. Individually “successful” reactions may therefore deplete shared resources or free-ride on transient order without stabilizing a replicating regime. The absence of life need not imply confusion in the chemistry; it may reflect a stable underprovision trap.

What follows is that origin-of-life transitions likely required structural changes that altered the payoff landscape, not just better molecules. Spatial localization, porous mineral compartments, wet-dry cycling, ice eutectic phases, lipid vesicles, and surface adsorption would matter because they convert diffuse benefits into more captive ones. A protocell boundary, for example, is not merely a container; it is an institutional innovation in chemical terms. It makes catalytic products, metabolic intermediates, and template copies more excludable to the lineage contributing to their production. Likewise, coupling replication to metabolism or compartment growth reduces free-riding by tying benefits to producers.

This perspective also distinguishes underprovision from mere rarity. A template-free emergence of replication may have been blocked less by the improbability of a single miraculous replicator than by the failure to assemble a system that solved local public-goods problems. Once structures existed that retained and concentrated benefits, imperfect replicators could improve through variation and selection. Before that, selection may have favored fast-consuming, non-contributory chemistries that prevented cumulative complexity.

So the conjecture is: self-replicating chemistry emerged when prebiotic environments accidentally created governance-like structures — compartments, surfaces, cycles, and coupling constraints — that internalized the benefits of informational and catalytic innovations. Life began not when matter discovered a template in isolation, but when chemistry escaped a no-provision equilibrium and made the supports for replication sufficiently ownable, persistent, and recursively reinforcing.
