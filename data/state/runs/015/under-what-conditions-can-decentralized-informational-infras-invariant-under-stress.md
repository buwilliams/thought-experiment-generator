## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement **only when they force interaction histories to reveal stable behavioral structure across time and contexts**—that is, when reputation, auditability, and signaling are rich enough that opportunistic actors cannot keep winning through cheap shortcuts. They fail where low-loss mimicry is easier than genuine cooperativeness.

More concretely: decentralized systems work when repeated interaction, shared memory, and observability create a selection environment in which superficial compliance does not generalize. If defection in one context predictably contaminates future opportunities in many others, then the system “learns” real invariants about agents: reliability, reciprocity, competence, restraint. In that regime, information can substitute for coercion because prediction is pressured to track underlying dispositions rather than surface signals.

This yields several conditions.

1. **Persistent memory:** Histories must be durable enough that behavior is not cheaply reset. Without continuity of identity or credible linkage across episodes, the infrastructure cannot distinguish stable types from one-off performances.

2. **Sufficient observability:** Actions and outcomes must be visible at the right grain. If important harms are systematically hidden, delayed, or deniable, then the informational system is trained on distorted targets and rewards appearances over substance.

3. **Cross-context generalization pressure:** Signals must matter in multiple future interactions, not just the current one. If agents can compartmentalize, exploit one domain, and remain trusted elsewhere, then the system never has to discover deeper structure.

4. **Costly or intervention-sensitive signaling:** Signals should be hard to fake, or at least easy to falsify through challenge, audit, and adversarial testing. Otherwise decentralized trust markets optimize for persuasive display, not actual constraint.

5. **Dense enough repetition and network propagation:** Information must circulate far enough and fast enough that local knowledge becomes socially consequential before exploitation pays off.

From this follows the hard limit: decentralized informational order breaks where **shortcut strategies remain competitively adequate**. If actors can obtain trust, access, and coordination through branding, sybil identities, selective disclosure, collusion, or manipulation of metrics, then low loss is achievable without learning or embodying the trait the system is supposed to reward. In such environments, informational infrastructure does not replace enforcement; it becomes an arena for gaming.

Its deepest limits appear in cases of severe externalities, asymmetric harm, and one-shot catastrophe. Where failures are irreversible, where victims cannot observe or report, where trust must be extended before evidence accumulates, or where strategic deception outpaces communal error correction, central enforcement remains structurally necessary. The issue is not merely insufficient information, but that the system lacks the variation, intervention, and feedback required to discriminate genuine compliance from successful imitation.

So the dividing line is: **decentralized memory can replace coercion when it induces discovery of stable behavioral invariants; it cannot when social prediction can remain accurate enough for short-term coordination while still being grounded in exploitable surface correlations.**
