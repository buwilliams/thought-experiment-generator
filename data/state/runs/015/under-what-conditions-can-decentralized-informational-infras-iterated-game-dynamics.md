## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement **only when they recreate the game-theoretic conditions of repeated interaction**: durable memory of past behavior, sufficiently public observability of actions or outcomes, credible signaling that links current conduct to future treatment, and a long enough shadow of the future that cooperation is payoff-dominant over opportunistic defection. Where these conditions weaken, decentralized enforcement does not merely become less efficient; it undergoes a structural phase change toward breakdown, capture, or fragmentation.

From an iterated-game perspective, central enforcement is not magic. It is a mechanism for solving the problems of **memory, attribution, and retaliation**. A decentralized informational system can replace it to the extent that it supplies functional equivalents: reputation ledgers instead of police records, peer verification instead of inspection, exclusion from future exchange instead of legal sanction. In repeated settings, this can stabilize cooperation without altruism. Agents cooperate because defection today predictably shrinks tomorrow’s opportunities.

What follows is a set of conditions:

1. **Persistent identity:** actors must be linkable across encounters. If identities are cheap to discard or clone, memory cannot bite.
2. **Observable defections:** harmful behavior must be visible enough that others can condition future play on it. Hidden defection destroys reciprocity.
3. **Reliable attribution:** it must be possible to distinguish bad luck from cheating, and to know who did what. Otherwise punishment becomes noisy and cooperation unravels.
4. **Low-cost information propagation:** reputational information must spread faster than opportunistic gains from one-off cheating.
5. **Future dependence:** participants must expect enough future interaction, or enough dependence on network reputation, that exclusion matters.
6. **Common reaction rules:** others must respond to signals in reasonably predictable ways; otherwise reputation has no enforceable meaning.

This illuminates both the promise and hard limits of decentralized informational order. Its promise is strongest in communities, platforms, markets, and protocols where interactions are repeated, identities persistent, and reputation portable. Its limits appear where the game becomes effectively one-shot: anonymous mass interaction, high churn, opaque harms, delayed consequences, sybil attacks, and environments where actors can defect, exit, and re-enter.

The hard limit is therefore not “lack of central authority” as such. It is **failure to sustain iterability**. When memory is lossy, observability local, signaling manipulable, or time horizons short, decentralized infrastructures cannot reliably produce cooperation from structure alone. They may even amplify failure through false accusations, reputation cartels, and information cascades. In such domains, central enforcement is not a moral supplement but a structural substitute for missing game conditions.

So the boundary is sharp: decentralized informational systems can replace centralized enforcement **where they successfully engineer repeated-game incentives**; they fail where the environment makes defection effectively anonymous, unobservable, or consequence-free.
