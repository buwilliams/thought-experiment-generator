## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A decentralized informational infrastructure can substitute for central enforcement **only where compliance can be made endogenous to the information environment**: actions must be sufficiently observable, memory of past behavior must be durable and portable, signals must be hard to fake at relevant scale, and interaction must be repeated enough that future exclusion outweighs present defection. Its hard limit appears when these conditions fail together—especially where harms are externalized, identity is resettable, observability is low, or response times exceed the speed of damage.

Construct the isolating scenario: imagine a large trading network with **no police, courts, or central administrator**, but perfect distributed recordkeeping of all publicly observable transactions. Every participant can see reputational histories, broadcast warnings, and choose whom to transact with. Assume identities are persistent. Then ask: what does enforcement become?

Under these assumptions, “enforcement” is not absent; it is **translated** from coercive punishment into **informational control of future cooperation**. The system does not force restitution; it changes the decision landscape so that defection destroys future access. In systems terms, memory plus signaling creates a negative feedback loop: cheating produces reputational loss, which reduces opportunities, which raises the cost of cheating. Repetition closes the loop. Thus decentralized infrastructure substitutes for central enforcement when the main leverage is not retrospective punishment but prospective exclusion.

Now relax the assumptions one by one.

If observability weakens, reputation ceases to track behavior. If memory is fragmented or non-portable, bad actors can defect locally and re-enter elsewhere. If signaling is cheap to fake, the infrastructure fills with counterfeit trust. If identities are easily abandoned, the future no longer disciplines the present. If interactions are one-shot or infrequent, exclusion loses force. In each case, the informational substitute degrades not because actors become worse, but because the system loses the structural means to connect action to consequence.

This reveals the hard limit: decentralized informational systems govern well where violations are **legible and socially containable**, but poorly where violations are **opaque, delayed, large-scale, or irreversible**. They are strongest in environments like marketplaces, protocols, and communities with repeated interaction and measurable performance. They are weakest for pollution, fraud hidden inside complex products, physical violence, systemic financial contagion, and collective-action failures—domains where the harmed parties cannot reliably observe, signal, or impose future costs on the violator.

So the conclusion is not “information replaces enforcement,” but more sharply: **information can replace coercion only where the relevant enforcement problem is actually an information-routing problem**. Where the core problem is not memory or coordination but the need to impose costs on actors who can evade observation, outrun reputation, or profit from irreversible damage, central enforcement is not an optional add-on but the mechanism that closes the missing loop.
