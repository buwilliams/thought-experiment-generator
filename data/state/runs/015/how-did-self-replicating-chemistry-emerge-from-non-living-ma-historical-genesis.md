## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Self-replicating chemistry did not first emerge as “template-based copying” in the modern genetic sense. It emerged earlier as a **system-level persistence strategy**: certain chemical networks came to reshape their environment so that the conditions producing them were repeatedly re-created. What we call replication likely replaced a more primitive regime of **self-maintaining, autocatalytic organization**, and it inherited from that regime the hidden assumption that “copying” is the essence of life. The deeper origin may be **closure of productive loops**, not templates.

**Origin-story reasoning:**  
The problem is usually framed backward from modern biology: life now depends on templated polymers, so the origin question becomes “How did the first template appear?” But that imports a late invention into an earlier setting. Template replication solved a prior problem: **high-fidelity inheritance across time despite environmental fluctuation**. Before that problem existed sharply, chemistry only needed to solve a simpler one: **how to persist locally against dissipation**.

What did template replication replace? Likely networks in which no molecule encoded the whole system, but the **network as a whole regenerated its own ingredients**. In such a regime, “inheritance” means re-establishing a cycle, compartment, or catalytic environment, not copying a sequence. The modern focus on discrete replicators inherits an unexamined assumption from genetics: that inheritance must be located in a special molecule. But early chemistry may have stored “knowledge” in **reaction topology, environmental modification, and spatial structure**.

So the key transition is not from “non-replicating matter” to “replicators,” but from **open-ended geochemistry** to **chemistry that creates feedback loops preserving its own preconditions**. For example, a reaction network that generates amphiphiles, local pH gradients, mineral coatings, or concentration traps could repeatedly reconstruct the niche in which that same network continues. This is replication in a broader sense: not copying a template, but reproducing a **causal organization**.

What follows from this?  
1. The origin of life should be sought less in a miraculous first informational polymer and more in **coupled systems**: autocatalysis, compartmentalization, energy flow, and environmental scaffolding.  
2. “Information” likely became fundamental first as **distributed constraint**—knowledge embodied in a network’s ability to channel matter and energy into its own continuation.  
3. Template molecules emerged later because they were a superior solution to a prior system problem: stabilizing and compressing that distributed organization into portable, high-fidelity components.

What this illuminates is that the “no prior template” objection may rest on a historical mistake. It assumes life had to begin with the mechanism that later dominated it. But evolutionary history often replaces diffuse, environmentally scaffolded processes with more modular, legible ones. In that sense, template replication may have been not the beginning of life, but a **refinement of an older kind of chemical memory**.
