## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Many power-law distributions in complex adaptive systems arise because the system is not merely “self-organizing” in a neutral physical sense, but solving a recurring **collective-action problem of coordination under sparse enforcement**. Scale-free structure appears when participation, attachment, or attention is locally voluntary, globally useful, and only weakly regulated: most agents defect from costly maintenance, while a few nodes absorb disproportionate coordinating burden because connecting to them is privately rewarding for everyone else.

**Collision of perspective and problem:**  
Instead of asking why agents “believe” some nodes are important, ask how the system economizes on the cost of coordination. In a network, maintaining many equal-strength links is expensive. Agents therefore prefer to connect to already-visible, already-maintained hubs. This is individually rational: one link to a hub buys more reach, information, legitimacy, or trade than many links to peripheral nodes. The result is preferential attachment, but interpreted socially: not as a mysterious law, but as a low-enforcement equilibrium.

**Who pays?**  
A small number of nodes pay the costs of being central: moderation, reliability, storage, signaling, standard-setting, reputation management, or infrastructural upkeep. Sometimes they are compensated directly; often they are compensated indirectly through rents, prestige, agenda-setting power, or increased survival. Most nodes free-ride on this maintenance.

**Who benefits?**  
Everyone benefits from the existence of coordination focal points, but especially marginal participants, for whom searching broadly is too costly. Hubs reduce transaction costs and uncertainty. They become institutional substitutes for universal trust or universal monitoring.

**Is defection privately rewarding?**  
Yes. For ordinary agents, defection means not investing in redundant ties, not verifying everything independently, and not contributing proportionally to maintenance. It is often cheaper to attach to what already works. This pushes load toward existing hubs, thickening the tail of the distribution.

**Why power laws rather than simple concentration?**  
Because enforcement is partial, not absolute. No central planner can assign equal degree, but neither is the system random. Agents repeatedly make myopic, cost-minimizing choices under adaptation and growth. New entrants face the same incentive landscape as old ones: attach where coordination is already cheapest. This recursive process generates cumulative advantage across scales. The distribution reflects not a single optimizing design but repeated local responses to maintenance asymmetry.

**What follows:**  
Power-law and scale-free behavior should be strongest where:
1. coordination benefits are high,  
2. link maintenance is costly,  
3. independent verification is expensive,  
4. centrality yields rents that offset maintenance, and  
5. institutions do not forcibly equalize load.

Conversely, where institutions compel redundancy, subsidize peripheral participation, cap central rents, or mandate interoperability, scale-free structure should weaken.

**What this illuminates:**  
Power laws are often signatures of **asymmetric maintenance obligations** in adaptive systems. They mark places where order persists because concentrated nodes make collective action tractable while others free-ride. The “heavy tail” is not just a statistical curiosity; it is evidence that the system has settled on a governance solution to the problem of coordinating many agents without paying the cost of universal enforcement.
