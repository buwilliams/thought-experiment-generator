## Conjecture

Conjecture: The amount of genetic information does not scale straightforwardly with organismal complexity, because genes function less like a complete blueprint and more like a contribution to a non-excludable developmental public good: a shared informational environment whose benefits are diffuse across the whole organism. Complexity therefore depends less on genome size alone than on how genetic information is organized to create, stabilize, and exploit shared developmental context.

What this perspective clarifies is why “more complex organism” need not mean “proportionally more genetic code.” If developmental outcomes are produced by many cells drawing on common signals, regulatory gradients, physical constraints, and inherited cellular machinery, then the effect of any one genetic element is broadly distributed and not locally excludable. A regulatory gene or signaling pathway can benefit many tissues at once. Once such a shared infrastructure exists, additional complexity can arise by recombining, timing, and reusing it rather than by linearly adding new instructions.

This reframes the genome-complexity relation as a collective action structure inside development. The naive view assumes each trait must be directly and separately specified, as if every part required its own dedicated informational budget. But where informational effects are diffuse, individually “local” specification is not the efficient equilibrium. Instead, evolution can build systems in which a relatively small number of genetic changes alter global developmental dynamics. Regulatory networks, gene reuse, modular signaling, and epigenetic context are all ways of funding shared informational goods that many downstream structures exploit.

From this follows an explanation of familiar puzzles like the weak correlation between genome size and complexity. Extra DNA may not increase complexity if it does not improve the shared developmental infrastructure. Conversely, substantial increases in complexity can occur through changes in regulation, control architecture, and interaction structure without large increases in raw sequence length. The key variable is not total encoded bits but leverage: how genetic information shapes the space of possible organism-wide coordination.

This lens also distinguishes true underprovision from mere ignorance. If complexity were limited only by lack of “instructions,” then adding more genes should reliably solve the problem. But if the bottleneck is the evolution of sharable developmental control systems, then complexity is constrained by the difficulty of establishing stable, reusable, organism-wide coordination mechanisms. Evolution may get stuck in local equilibria where beneficial complexity is possible in principle but not reachable by incremental changes that individually pay off.

So the relationship is indirect: genetic information encodes not just parts, but rules for generating shared contexts in which many parts can form. Organismal complexity therefore tracks the architecture of informational coordination more than the sheer quantity of DNA.
