## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law distributions and scale-free behavior when their dynamics are governed by **interaction-level feedback that reallocates growth opportunities according to current relational position**, rather than by fixed component properties. The power law is not primarily a fact about the “types” of agents, but about a system structure in which local advantages are recursively amplified across a network with no characteristic scale.

**What this claims is true:**  
In many adaptive systems, units do not grow independently. Their probability of gaining resources, links, influence, or reproduction depends on how they are already embedded in the system: who is connected to whom, which pathways concentrate flow, which nodes become coordination points, and which feedback loops reinforce visibility, trust, or utility. Once growth is conditioned on existing connectivity or accumulated success, the system creates a **rich-get-richer feedback loop**. If entry, adaptation, and recombination continue over time, this loop generates highly uneven outcomes across many scales rather than clustering around a typical size.

**Why this perspective matters:**  
From a component-centered view, power laws look puzzling: why should many different agents, with different local mechanisms, produce the same statistical form? From a systems view, this is exactly what we should expect when different systems share the same **interaction topology and feedback logic**. The common cause is not similar parts but similar generative structure: multiplicative growth, preferential attachment, threshold effects, cascading imitation, competitive exclusion, and continual inflow of novelty.

**What follows:**  
1. **Scale-free behavior indicates absence of a dominant characteristic scale in the organizing process**, not necessarily in the parts.  
2. **Heterogeneity is produced endogenously**: extreme inequality of degree, size, or influence can arise even from modestly similar agents.  
3. **Tail events are structurally normal** in such systems, not mere anomalies. Rare large hubs, crashes, cascades, or winners are expected outputs of the interaction regime.  
4. Attempts to explain the distribution solely from intrinsic fitness will systematically miss the role of path dependence and network reinforcement.

**What is illuminated:**  
Power laws are signatures of systems where adaptation occurs through **feedback-mediated selection on relations**. A node becomes important because importance attracts more interaction; a convention spreads because prevalence lowers adoption cost; a platform dominates because users attract complementary users. The resulting distribution reflects the system learning, amplifying, and freezing contingencies into structure.

So the explanatory target should shift from “What properties do the successful components have?” to “What interaction rules repeatedly convert small differences into cross-scale concentration?” On this view, power-law distributions are less a mysterious statistical regularity than a trace left by recursive network amplification in evolving systems.
