## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Stable cooperation among self-interested agents without central enforcement emerges when a social norm is embedded in a local enforcement system where (1) the private gains from defection are made contingent and reversible, (2) the costs of monitoring and punishment are distributed among beneficiaries, and (3) repeated interaction makes norm maintenance cheaper than norm breakdown.

From this perspective, cooperation is not best understood as people sincerely valuing the common good. It is a collective-action equilibrium sustained by institutions, habits, and expectations that solve the *second-order* problem: not just “why cooperate?” but “who pays to make cooperation the individually rational move?”

The key question is who bears enforcement costs. In durable cooperative orders, enforcement is rarely absent; it is decentralized. Gossip, reputation tracking, reciprocal exclusion, partner choice, and conditional aid are low-cost enforcement technologies. They let many actors each contribute a little to monitoring and sanctioning, rather than requiring a sovereign. The norm persists when those who benefit from cooperation also have sufficient incentive to punish defectors or withhold future benefits. If nobody is positioned to do this cheaply, the norm decays.

What follows is that “self-enforcement” is often a misleading term. Cooperation is maintained not by morality alone but by institutions that make defection privately unattractive. A trader who cheats may gain once, but loses future exchange opportunities. A neighbor who free-rides may save effort now, but is denied help later. Stability therefore depends on whether defection is truly profitable *after* social responses are counted. Where interactions are repeated and visible, decentralized sanctions can substitute for central authority.

This also illuminates why some norms are surprisingly sticky even when many privately disagree with them. If a norm coordinates behavior and beneficiaries are already organized to transmit and punish around it, maintenance can be unavoidable. People comply because unilateral defection is costly, not because collective endorsement is deep. Conversely, when a cooperative norm collapses, the cause may be less a moral decline than a shift in enforcement economics: anonymity rises, mobility increases, monitoring weakens, sanctions become costly, or benefits become too diffuse.

A systems view sharpens this: cooperation is a feedback structure. Compliance produces trust; trust increases the value of future interaction; that increases willingness to sanction defectors; sanctioning preserves compliance. Breakdown works the same way in reverse. The leverage points are not exhortation but visibility, repeated dealings, selective exclusion, and reducing the cost of coordinated punishment.

So the collision between this perspective and the problem suggests: stable cooperation without central enforcement is never “without enforcement” in the strong sense. It persists when social norms are backed by decentralized mechanisms that align private incentives with collective maintenance, and fails when the burden of enforcing the norm becomes too concentrated, too costly, or too avoidable.
