## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Self-replicating chemistry did not first emerge as a single molecule that somehow “contained” the secret of copying itself. It emerged as a **network-level dynamical regime** in which certain reaction loops, compartment effects, and environmental cycling jointly created a system that reproduced its own organization before any precise molecular template existed.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
The central explanatory target should shift from “Which molecule first self-replicated?” to “What interaction structure made some chemical organizations persist, amplify, and reconstitute themselves?” In prebiotic chemistry, the crucial novelty was likely not a part but a **closed-enough web of mutually reinforcing transformations**: catalysts producing substrates for other catalysts, surfaces concentrating reactants, wet-dry or thermal cycles driving assembly/disassembly, and compartments retaining productive chemistries. Such a system can exhibit emergent self-maintenance and iterative reproduction even when no individual component can replicate alone.

**What follows:**  
A prior template is unnecessary if the system can first achieve **organizational heredity**. What gets copied initially is not exact sequence information but a **stable pattern of relations**: reaction pathways, concentration biases, compartment composition, and catalytic feedbacks. Daughter compartments or neighboring environments need only inherit enough of this structure for the network to re-establish itself. Template-based replication can then arise later as an evolutionary refinement because systems that encode their organization more compactly and accurately outcompete looser forms of reproduction.

This reframes the origin problem. The paradox “How can replication begin without a replicator?” dissolves once replication is understood as an emergent property of a chemical system. Early “replication” may have meant repeated regeneration of a metabolic-cum-compartmental organization under recurring environmental conditions, not high-fidelity copying of a polymer. In this view, sequence templates are a late, powerful specialization of a more primitive phenomenon: **the recurrence of self-producing networks**.

**What is illuminated:**  
The origin of life becomes a systems transition driven by feedback structure. Positive feedback amplifies productive chemistries; negative feedback and resource constraints select for robustness; compartment boundaries couple local success to persistence; environmental cycles repeatedly reset and re-seed the system, functioning as an external clock. The emergence threshold is crossed when these interactions create a regime in which the whole network is more reproducible than its isolated reactions would suggest.

So the deepest conjecture is: **life began when chemistry acquired the capacity to reproduce an organization before it could reproduce a molecule exactly.** Template replication did not solve the origin of self-replication; it stabilized and sharpened an already-existing emergent capacity of chemical systems to make more systems like themselves.
