## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The apparent mismatch between the finite amount of genetic information and the much greater complexity of the organism is best understood as a historical artifact of what genes evolved to solve: not the problem of fully specifying an organism, but the problem of reliably *bootstrapping* self-organizing development within a preexisting cellular, ecological, and evolutionary context. Therefore, much organismal complexity is not “encoded” in the genome as a blueprint, but inherited through developmental dynamics, cellular machinery, environmental regularities, and selection-shaped constraints that the genome exploits rather than describes.

This perspective shifts the question from “How can so little DNA specify so much complexity?” to “Why did we assume genes were supposed to specify everything in the first place?” That assumption has an origin. The blueprint metaphor arose as a replacement for older vitalist or preformationist pictures: genes became the respectable causal center because they were stably inherited, experimentally tractable, and manipulable. But in replacing those older views, genetics inherited an unexamined constraint: that the hereditary material must contain a near-complete description of the organism.

Historically, that was never the actual problem evolution solved. Early replicators did not need to encode full-form organisms; they needed to persist and reproduce in already-structured physical and chemical environments. As multicellularity and development evolved, genomes accumulated regulatory strategies for steering cell behavior, timing, differentiation, and interaction. They came to function less like exhaustive plans and more like compressed control systems embedded in a rich dynamical substrate. The cell, the egg, maternal factors, morphogen gradients, protein-folding chemistry, biomechanical forces, and environmental cues all carry forward indispensable organization. The genome presupposes these.

What follows is that organismal complexity should not be expected to scale linearly with genome size or raw sequence information. Complexity emerges from the interaction of encoded rules with non-genetic structure and iterative developmental processes. A relatively modest genomic change can rewire regulatory networks and generate large phenotypic consequences because development is a feedback-rich system with high leverage points. Conversely, vast stretches of sequence can vary with little effect if they do not alter these generative rules.

So the key relationship is not between genome length and adult complexity, but between **genetic information and the organism’s capacity to recruit and constrain self-organization**. Genes are closer to switches, modulators, and recursive instructions than to a full inventory of final form. The organism is not wholly contained in the genome; it is reconstructed each generation by a historically evolved developmental system of which the genome is one indispensable but non-sovereign part.

The hidden assumption exposed by the origin story is that inheritance equals description. The better view is that inheritance often works by transmitting enough information to re-enter a process that already contains much of the order.
