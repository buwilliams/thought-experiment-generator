## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only when they convert a diffuse, non-excludable governance good into a sequence of locally excludable, observable, and reputation-linked interactions. Their hard limit appears where the benefits of order remain broadly shared while the costs of monitoring, truth-maintenance, and sanctioning remain privately borne.

The public-goods lens clarifies that the key issue is not just information scarcity or weak norms, but **structural underprovision**. Enforcement is itself a public good: if accurate records, credible signaling, and shared memory benefit everyone, each participant has an incentive to rely on others to bear the cost of producing and maintaining them. A decentralized infrastructure works when it alters this payoff structure.

What must be true for substitution to succeed?

1. **Memory must be persistent and legible.** Past actions have to remain available at low cost, so defection is not reset each round.
2. **Observability must be sufficiently local and frequent.** Actors need not see everything, but enough must be visible that behavior can be tied to consequences.
3. **Signals must be hard to fake and cheap to interpret.** Otherwise the infrastructure produces noise rather than coordination.
4. **Repetition must matter.** Future interaction, or dependence on a continuing reputation, must be valuable enough that present opportunism is costly.
5. **Sanctions must be at least partly excludable.** If defectors can still access the main benefits of the system, decentralized punishment becomes another underprovided public good.

Under these conditions, decentralized systems do not eliminate enforcement; they **embed it into the information environment**. Reputation, blacklistability, reciprocal access, and verifiable history create feedback loops where individually rational choices support provision. The infrastructure substitutes for a center because it restructures incentives, not because it produces consensus or virtue.

The hard limits follow directly. Decentralized informational order breaks down when:
- harms are **diffuse** and not traceable to particular actors;
- monitoring costs are high and benefits from monitoring spill over to all;
- identities are cheap to discard or multiply;
- signaling is manipulable;
- participation is one-shot or transient;
- sanctions require collective action whose benefits are non-excludable.

In these domains, no amount of better communication alone solves the problem, because the bottleneck is not misunderstanding but the public-goods structure of enforcement itself. Informational infrastructure can reveal defectors, but if acting on that knowledge is costly and others can free-ride on one’s effort, provision remains stuck at a low-enforcement equilibrium.

So the boundary is this: decentralized informational systems can replace central enforcement only where they can **privatize enough of the returns to vigilance and sanctioning**. Where order depends on producing a broadly shared good from individually costly actions, central enforcement—or some equivalent mechanism for compelled contribution—remains hard to avoid.
