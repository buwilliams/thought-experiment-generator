## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges when the social system contains **load-bearing interdependencies** that make defection locally profitable only at the cost of degrading the future conditions that make profit possible. Cooperation persists not because agents become altruistic, but because the structure of interaction makes reputation, repeated exposure, selective partner choice, and norm signaling mutually reinforcing constraints rather than optional ornaments.

From the “load-bearing detail” perspective, many standard explanations are too loose. If you can swap out reputation for punishment, or repeated interaction for shared identity, without changing what the explanation predicts, then those details were not doing real work. A tighter explanation is one where each element constrains the others.

Here the load-bearing parts are:

1. **Repeated interaction**: without it, information about past behavior has little strategic value.
2. **Observability or transmissible reputation**: without it, repeated interaction helps only dyad by dyad and cannot scale beyond direct reciprocity.
3. **Partner choice / exit**: without it, reputation has weak consequences because bad actors cannot be avoided.
4. **Sufficiently dense future stakes**: without meaningful ongoing gains from exchange, neither reputation nor partner choice can outweigh immediate temptation to defect.
5. **Norm legibility**: agents must be able to classify conduct as cooperative or defective with enough shared clarity for reputational updating to coordinate.

These are not interchangeable modules. Remove partner choice, and reputation becomes mostly expressive. Remove observability, and repeated interaction cannot support wider cooperative order. Remove norm legibility, and punishment and reputation become noisy, producing faction rather than cooperation. The explanation works because these pieces force compensating changes elsewhere if altered; that is a sign they are genuinely explanatory.

What follows is that **stable cooperation is less a property of “good incentives” in isolation than of an ecology of feedback loops**. Cooperative acts build reputation; reputation affects partner access; partner access changes expected future payoff; that payoff makes present cooperation rational. Defection can still occur, but if the system is well-structured, its gains are self-undermining: opportunism reduces trust, trust reduces access, and reduced access lowers long-run returns.

This also illuminates why cooperation often fails in large anonymous settings: not because self-interest is stronger there, but because one or more load-bearing constraints has been removed. Anonymous one-shot exchange strips future stakes and observability; captured institutions remove exit; norm ambiguity corrodes reputational consensus. In such settings, calls for “more trust” are narrative shells unless they rebuild the structural conditions that make trust payoff-relevant.

So the core claim is: **cooperation without central enforcement persists when institutions and social technologies bind present action to future opportunity through visible, interpretable, and choice-relevant histories.** The mechanism is not moral transformation but constrained self-interest operating inside a tightly coupled informational system.
