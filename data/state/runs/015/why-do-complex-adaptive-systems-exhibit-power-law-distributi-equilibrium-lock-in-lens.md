## Conjecture

Conjecture: In many complex adaptive systems, power-law and scale-free patterns arise when the production of useful structure is a non-excludable good whose benefits diffuse across the network, so that many agents rationally attach to already-provisioned hubs instead of paying the local cost of creating alternative structure. The result is not merely “rich get richer,” but a stable underprovision of independent connective capacity.

Collision of lens and problem:

A scale-free network can be seen as a public-goods equilibrium. Creating and maintaining links, standards, reputations, search costs, coordination protocols, or informational waypoints is costly. But once such structure exists, many others can benefit from it without bearing proportional cost. Because the benefits are diffuse and non-excludable, individual agents are often better off free-riding on existing highly connected nodes than investing in new nodes or parallel pathways.

What follows structurally is cumulative concentration. Small early asymmetries get amplified, not just because agents are confused or imitative, but because decentralized choice systematically underfunds redundancy and alternative centers. A hub is a partially provided public good; everyone prefers a world with many robust, well-provisioned hubs, but each marginal agent prefers to connect to the largest existing one. This traps the system in a locally stable no-provision equilibrium for competing structure.

This illuminates why power laws are so common in adaptive systems:

- Low-degree nodes are easy to produce privately.
- High-degree coordinating nodes generate broad spillovers.
- Spillovers weaken incentives to build new hubs.
- So attachment flows disproportionately toward incumbently provisioned structure.
- Repetition across scales yields heavy tails.

The key claim is that scale-free behavior often reflects structural underprovision of alternative organizational capacity, not just stochastic growth or optimization. The same mechanism explains why such systems are efficient in the short run yet fragile in the large: they economize on duplicated infrastructure, but only by concentrating load and influence into a few nodes.

This also distinguishes the phenomenon from mere weak motivation. Agents may strongly prefer resilience, plurality, and distributed capacity in the aggregate. Yet if no one can capture enough of the benefit from founding or sustaining alternative hubs, those hubs are systematically undersupplied. Power-law structure is then an equilibrium signature of a public-goods problem in network formation.

So the deeper explanation is: complex adaptive systems exhibit power laws when adaptive choice operates over non-excludable structural goods, causing individually rational attachment to substitute for collective investment in new structure. Scale-freeness is what underprovided redundancy looks like when repeated over time.
