## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The complexity of an organism is not linearly encoded in the quantity of its genetic information; rather, the genome functions more like a compact computational rule-set that initializes and constrains a multi-level developmental process, while much of the organism’s realized complexity is generated through iterative computation distributed across cells, environments, and evolutionary history.

**What this perspective makes visible:**  
If computation is the substrate underlying life, then a genome should not be interpreted as a full blueprint in the architectural sense. It is better understood as executable information: a generative program. In computation, simple code can produce highly complex outputs when run through recursive, parallel, and feedback-driven processes. Likewise, a relatively finite genome can produce a highly complex organism because the genome does not explicitly specify every feature. It specifies transformation rules, regulatory conditions, error-correction mechanisms, developmental timing, and interfaces for responding to local signals.

**What follows:**  
1. **Genetic information is algorithmic, not merely descriptive.**  
   The genome encodes procedures for constructing and maintaining an organism, not a one-to-one list of final traits. Therefore organismal complexity depends heavily on the computational dynamics of development.

2. **Complexity is distributed across the system, not stored only in DNA.**  
   DNA participates in a larger computation involving gene regulatory networks, epigenetic states, cell-cell signaling, physical constraints, ecological inputs, and inherited cellular machinery. The organism emerges from this whole system running, not from the genome alone.

3. **Evolution increases the power of generative rules more than the size of the codebase.**  
   Selection often favors reusable modules, regulatory hierarchies, and compositional mechanisms that allow small genomic changes to have large phenotypic effects. Thus increases in organismal complexity may correspond less to more genes than to more sophisticated control architectures.

4. **The apparent “information gap” between genome size and organism complexity is expected, not paradoxical.**  
   In computational terms, outputs can contain enormous effective complexity relative to the compactness of the program, especially when computation unfolds over time in rich environments. This dissolves the intuition that a complex organism must require a proportionally massive genetic description.

**Illumination:**  
The relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is therefore analogous to the relationship between source code and the behavior of a complex simulation: the code matters fundamentally, but the visible complexity arises only when the code is executed within a system of interacting states and feedback loops. Genetic information is best seen as compressed evolutionary knowledge for building a system capable of generating further structure. Organismal complexity is the runtime product of that computation.
