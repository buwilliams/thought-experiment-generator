## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Biological systems are robust *because* they are organized as layered, substrate-agnostic computational systems in which selection preserves **functional invariants** while allowing wide variation in their physical implementation. They are evolvable because computation admits **many-to-one mappings** from structure to function: many different lower-level configurations can realize the same higher-level behavior, and selection can search that space without immediately destroying viability.

From this perspective, robustness and evolvability are not opposites. They are complementary consequences of a particular computational architecture.

A biological system processes information across scales: gene regulation, metabolism, development, behavior, ecological interaction. At each scale, what matters for persistence is not exact microstate preservation but maintenance of certain input-output relations, error-correcting dynamics, and feedback control. This creates **redundancy, degeneracy, and modularity**:

- **Redundancy**: multiple components perform similar computations, buffering failure.
- **Degeneracy**: different structures can compute the same function in different contexts.
- **Modularity**: local changes can occur without globally catastrophic recomputation.

These are classic design features of robust computation. But they also enlarge the adjacent possible for evolution. If many genotypes map to similar phenotypes, populations can drift across a neutral or near-neutral network of implementations while preserving function. That wandering is itself a computation over design space. Once conditions change, previously buffered variation can be recruited into new functions. Thus robustness stores evolutionary option value.

Selection, on this view, does not optimize fixed structures; it shapes **searchable computational spaces**. The deepest constraint is not “keep this molecule” or “keep this form,” but “preserve the capacity to continue computing viability under perturbation.” Systems that survive are those whose generative rules absorb noise, repair local damage, and continue reproducing informative structure. Evolvability then arises when those same rules permit recombination and modification without total loss of function.

The apparent paradox dissolves once we stop treating organisms as brittle machines and instead see them as **error-tolerant, self-maintaining computational processes**. Robustness is stability of computation under perturbation. Evolvability is the capacity for that computation to be reparameterized, recombined, or re-implemented under selection.

What follows is that the key explanatory target is not any single biological mechanism, but the architecture of mappings between levels: genotype to regulatory dynamics, dynamics to phenotype, phenotype to fitness. Biological systems are robust and evolvable when these mappings are structured so that variation is mostly absorbed locally, while useful novelty can still propagate globally. In systems terms, evolution favors organizations with strong stabilizing feedback around core functions and weak enough coupling at the margins to permit exploratory change.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a sharper claim: **life evolves because it computes in ways that separate function from implementation just enough to survive errors, and couple implementation to function just enough to discover improvements.**
