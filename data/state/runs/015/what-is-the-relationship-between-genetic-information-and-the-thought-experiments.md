## Conjecture

Conjecture:

Assume a hypothetical world in which the key variable is isolated: hold chemistry, physics, and developmental machinery fixed, and vary only the amount and structure of genetic information available to build an organism. Treat “genetic information” not as raw genome length, but as the set of heritable instructions that can reliably constrain development across generations.

In that world, the relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is not linear and not direct. More genetic information does not simply “encode” more complex organisms in the way a longer blueprint yields a larger building. Instead, genetic information sets up a generative system: regulatory rules, timing switches, interaction protocols, and error-correction mechanisms that unfold through development. Complexity in the organism is therefore produced by the execution of these rules within a structured dynamical system, not by one-to-one specification.

What follows is that organismal complexity depends more strongly on the organization of information than on its quantity. A relatively modest genome, if arranged to control layered feedback loops, modular reuse, and context-sensitive developmental decisions, can generate high phenotypic complexity. Conversely, a large genome with much repetitive or weakly functional content need not yield a complex organism. The decisive variable is explanatory power per bit: how much developmental structure a given informational arrangement can generate.

This suggests the right analogy is not a parts list but a compressed program running in a rich interpreter. The genome is closer to rule-set than finished description. Development, cellular environment, and evolutionary history function as the interpreter that expands those rules into form. So the complexity of the organism is partly in the genome, partly in the developmental system the genome presupposes, and partly in the environment that stabilizes the process. “Encoding” is therefore distributed, not localized.

Strictly followed, this also implies there is no universal expectation that more complex organisms must have proportionally larger or more information-dense genomes. If complexity arises from recursive reuse, regulatory hierarchies, and interaction with conserved cellular machinery, then major increases in organismal complexity can come from changes in control architecture rather than large increases in total genetic content.

So the conjecture is: genetic information relates to organismal complexity primarily as a generative control structure relates to the behavior of a computationally irreducible system. The genome constrains the space of possible developmental trajectories, but the organism’s final complexity emerges from iterative execution of those constraints through feedback-rich processes. Therefore, the deepest relationship is not “more genes → more complex organism,” but “more powerful developmental organization of information → greater capacity for emergent complexity.”
