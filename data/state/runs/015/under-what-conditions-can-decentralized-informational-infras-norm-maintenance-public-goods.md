## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only when the target norm is mainly a **coordination-and-reputation problem** rather than a **coercion-and-adjudication problem**. They work when violations are cheaply observable, records are durable and widely legible, actors expect repeated interaction, and exclusion from future exchange is a credible penalty. Their hard limit appears where norm maintenance requires costly investigation, forced compliance, or judgment under ambiguity.

From the collective-action perspective, a norm survives not because people sincerely endorse it, but because some system pays to **detect**, **remember**, **broadcast**, and **respond to** defections. Decentralized infrastructure substitutes for a center when it lowers those four costs enough that many local actors can enforce the norm through ordinary participation. Public ledgers, shared blacklists, reputation graphs, audit trails, and community signaling systems convert private observations into common knowledge. That changes the payoff structure: defection may still be immediately profitable, but now carries future losses through refusal, higher prices, downgraded trust, or exclusion.

So the key conditions are structural:

1. **High observability:** The relevant action leaves a legible trace.  
2. **Low ambiguity:** Communities can classify violation without expensive interpretation.  
3. **Persistent memory:** Information about past behavior is hard to erase.  
4. **Cheap transmission:** Others can learn that history at low cost.  
5. **Repeat play:** Future interactions matter enough to discipline present behavior.  
6. **Distributed sanction capacity:** Many actors can individually refuse, downgrade, or route around defectors.  
7. **Low identity fluidity:** Defectors cannot cheaply reset identity and escape memory.

When those conditions hold, decentralized systems can replace much of what a central enforcer normally does. Not by compelling obedience directly, but by making cooperation the best long-run strategy. In systems terms, the infrastructure creates reinforcing feedback between observability, reputation, and compliance.

Their hard limits come from cases where one or more conditions break. If harms are hidden, delayed, technically complex, or strategically deniable, decentralized observers cannot agree on what happened. If punishment requires seizing assets, imprisoning actors, or compelling testimony, informational systems alone cannot do it. If interactions are one-shot, parties are anonymous, or identity is cheap to relaunch, memory loses force. If false accusations are easy and verification is expensive, the infrastructure itself becomes a site of manipulation. And when norms impose concentrated costs for diffuse benefits, private actors underinvest in enforcement even if the information exists.

So decentralized informational order is strongest for **screening and exclusion**, weakest for **adjudication and compulsion**. It can often substitute for central enforcement in marketplaces, professional communities, open networks, and repeated collaborations. But it cannot fully replace institutions where maintaining the norm requires authoritative fact-finding, coordinated punishment, or enforcement against actors who can profit once and disappear. Informational infrastructure can distribute enforcement; it cannot abolish the underlying collective-action costs.
