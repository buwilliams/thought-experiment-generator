## Conjecture

Let \(G\) denote the organism’s genetic information, not merely as genome length, but as a structured program: a set of symbols plus regulatory relations, reuse rules, and context-dependent switches. Let \(C\) denote organismal complexity, understood not as one scalar but as a vector of properties: number of differentiated cell types, depth of developmental hierarchy, network modularity, behavioral repertoire, and robustness under perturbation.

The key mathematical move is to reject any simple function \(C=f(|G|)\), where \(|G|\) is raw genome size. That mapping is empirically and structurally wrong. Instead, complexity is better modeled as

\[
C = F(G, D, E, H),
\]

where \(D\) is developmental dynamics, \(E\) is environmental input, and \(H\) is historical/evolutionary constraint. The genome does not directly “contain” the organism in a one-to-one descriptive sense; it constrains and parametrizes a generative process.

So the relevant invariant is not genomic quantity but generative capacity. A small rule set can generate high-complexity outcomes if it is recursively applied through developmental time. This is familiar mathematically from dynamical systems, formal grammars, and cellular automata: output complexity can greatly exceed apparent input description length when the system iterates structured rules.

That suggests a conjecture:

**Conjecture:** Organismal complexity scales primarily with the architecture of genetic regulation and developmental recursion, not with the amount of genetic sequence alone. More precisely, complexity is bounded less by genome size than by the combinatorial expressiveness of the genotype-to-phenotype mapping.

What follows?

1. **Genome size is a weak proxy for complexity.** If two organisms have genomes of similar length, the one with richer regulatory topology — more conditional expression, feedback, modular reuse, and temporal staging — can realize much greater organismal complexity.

2. **Regulatory networks are leverage points.** The mathematically decisive variables are interaction matrices, not symbol counts: which genes regulate which, under what thresholds, in what sequence, with what feedback.

3. **Development amplifies information.** Genetic information is transformed through repeated local rules into large-scale form. Thus the phenotype is not “stored” in the genome in explicit detail; it is computed from it.

4. **Complexity is relational, not intrinsic to DNA alone.** Since \(E\) and developmental dynamics enter the mapping, the same genome can yield different phenotypic outcomes across contexts. Therefore complexity belongs to the whole generative system, not to the sequence in isolation.

In short: the relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is analogous to that between a compact algorithm and the complex structure it generates. The decisive mathematical object is the rule architecture that governs expansion, differentiation, and feedback. Raw sequence length varies; generative organization does the real explanatory work.
