## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only when the system’s informational details are *load-bearing* in a very specific way: memory, observability, signaling, and repeated interaction must be mutually constraining enough that defection predictably changes future opportunities. Where any one of these can be altered without forcing major compensating redesign elsewhere, the infrastructure is not doing enforcement work but merely decorating trust.

More concretely, decentralized order is possible when:

1. **Actions leave durable, interpretable traces**  
   Memory must not be mere storage; it must be linked to identity and legible to others. If records can be erased, forked, or cheaply dissociated from actors, then “memory” is not load-bearing. Real enforcement-by-information requires that past behavior constrains future exchange.

2. **Observability is sufficiently public or transmissible**  
   It is not enough that someone knows; relevant others must be able to know at decision time. If observability can be reduced to private local knowledge without collapsing cooperation, then it was not central to the explanation. Decentralized enforcement works when visibility propagates so that misconduct becomes a system-level input, not an isolated fact.

3. **Signals are costly to fake or embedded in performance**  
   Signaling substitutes for coercion only when the signal is tethered to underlying behavior. If badges, reputations, attestations, or credentials can be swapped out for cheap talk without changing outcomes, they are narrative shells. Load-bearing signals either arise from actual conduct or are backed by structures that make dishonesty self-defeating.

4. **Interactions are repeated with future stakes**  
   Repetition matters not as a generic condition but because it couples present action to future payoff. If agents can defect and exit, or identities can be cheaply reset, then repetition loses force. Thus persistence of counterparties or of reputational identity is essential.

This yields the hard limit: decentralized informational systems fail where enforcement requires **noninformational intervention** against actors for whom future relational consequences do not bind. Examples include one-shot predation, severe power asymmetries, externalities imposed on nonparticipants, identity fluidity, and domains where harms are irreversible before information can propagate. In such cases, central enforcement is not replaceable because the missing element is not “better information” but the capacity to impose costs independent of voluntary uptake.

The key illumination is that memory, observability, signaling, and repetition are not additive ingredients. They form a tightly coupled system. Change one detail—make identity portable, observability delayed, signals cheap, or exit easy—and the others must become stronger to compensate. That interdependence is the mark of a genuine explanation. So the boundary is sharp: decentralized informational infrastructures can govern where information can be made consequential through structure; they reach their limit where consequences require a coercive backstop not endogenous to the informational network itself.
