## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Power-law distributions and scale-free behavior in complex adaptive systems are often not “natural laws of complexity” but historical residues of how systems solved an earlier problem: how to grow, coordinate, and allocate attention or resources under severe limits of local information, control, and search cost. Scale-freeness emerges when mechanisms built to cheaply extend an existing system—reuse what is already known, connect through visible hubs, reinforce successful pathways—outcompete mechanisms that would produce more even distributions.

Ask where such systems came from. Before a network is scale-free, it is usually sparse, locally organized, and uncertain about where value lies. The prior problem is not “how do we make a mathematically elegant distribution?” but “how do agents find reliable partners, pathways, or signals without global knowledge?” The cheap solution is path dependence: attach to what is already connected, copy what already works, amplify what has already been amplified. This replaces costly global optimization with local heuristics.

What does this heuristic inherit without examination? It inherits **visibility as a proxy for value**, **survival as a proxy for fitness**, and **past success as evidence of future usefulness**. Once these assumptions are embedded, positive feedback loops form: nodes with more links become easier to find, easier to trust, and easier to build around. The distribution then stretches into a power law not because the system “prefers inequality,” but because history plus local adaptation repeatedly channels flow into already-legible structures.

From a systems view, the key is not the distribution itself but the generative rule: cumulative advantage under constrained search. Power laws are signatures of systems whose growth rule is recursive and memory-bearing. They remember the past structurally. “Rich get richer” is too shallow; more precisely, **the system economizes on discovery by routing through prior discoveries**.

This also explains why scale-free behavior appears across domains that look superficially unrelated: citation networks, cities, firms, metabolic pathways, language use. The shared origin is not a shared material substrate but a shared problem of adaptive coordination under bounded knowledge. Different systems inherit the same expedient rule: extend from the already-established because exploration is expensive and reliability matters.

What follows is a criticism of reification. We should not treat power laws as deep equilibrium truths unless we have identified the historical selection pressure that made their generative rule advantageous. In some cases, scale-free structure may be an outdated solution that persists because institutions, protocols, or cognitive habits continue rewarding attachment to incumbents. The “law” may really be frozen history.

So the illuminating claim is: **complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law and scale-free behavior when they are built by iterated local solutions to the ancestral problem of coordination under ignorance, and those solutions encode unexamined biases toward reuse, visibility, and reinforcement.** The distribution is less a final explanation than a clue about what problem the system once had to solve.
