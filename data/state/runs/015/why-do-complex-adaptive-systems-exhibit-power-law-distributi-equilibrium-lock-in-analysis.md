## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Many power-law and scale-free patterns in complex adaptive systems arise not primarily because agents optimize for hubs or because “rich get richer” is always intrinsically efficient, but because systems become **equilibrium-locked into local attachment rules** that no individual agent can profitably deviate from, even when the resulting concentration creates fragility or collective inefficiency.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
In a growing adaptive system, agents face uncertainty, bounded information, and coordination constraints. Under those conditions, attaching to already-visible, already-connected, or already-legible nodes is often the safest unilateral strategy. It minimizes search cost, reputational risk, interoperability failure, and prediction error. If others are also using popularity, visibility, or existing connectivity as a proxy for reliability, then any one agent who switches to obscure or weakly connected alternatives bears a private cost while capturing little of the systemic benefit of diversification.

This creates an **equilibrium lock-in**:  
- Existing hubs attract new links because they are already hubs.  
- Their dominance improves legibility and coordination, which further justifies choosing them.  
- The resulting concentration is self-reinforcing, not necessarily because it is globally optimal, but because **no single actor can improve their outcome by defecting from the convention**.

**What follows:**  
Power-law distributions can be understood as the macroscopic trace of repeated local decisions made inside this strategic trap. A scale-free structure is then not just a statistical curiosity or a neutral outcome of stochastic growth; it is evidence that the system’s micro-rules reward **conforming to established concentration**. Preferential attachment is thus reinterpreted as a coordination equilibrium: agents use degree, visibility, or incumbency because these variables summarize where others are likely to connect, transact, trust, or route through.

This perspective also distinguishes two cases often blurred together:

1. **True equilibrium constraints:** concentration persists because unilateral diversification is punished.  
2. **Contingent failures:** concentration persists only because of bad beliefs, poor information, or removable frictions.

The distinction matters. If the observed power law comes from equilibrium lock-in, then better exhortation, smarter agents, or more awareness will not dissolve it. Structural changes are required: lowering switching costs, subsidizing exploration, enforcing interoperability, capping hub advantages, or creating collective mechanisms that let many agents diversify simultaneously.

**What is illuminated:**  
Scale-free behavior is not merely about growth plus randomness; it is often about **strategic dependence under coordination pressure**. The “heavy tail” marks where adaptive choice has frozen into a path-dependent equilibrium. Complex systems resist reform not because agents fail to see concentration, but because the individually rational move is to reproduce it. On this view, power laws are signatures of a deeper structural fact: **distributed agents can collectively generate extreme inequality in connectivity as the stable outcome of locally rational, non-cooperative adaptation.**
