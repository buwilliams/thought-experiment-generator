## Conjecture

Conjecture: Complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law distributions and apparent scale-free structure when their generative rules are load-bearing in a very specific way: local growth, selection, and resource-routing mechanisms must preserve amplification across scales without introducing a characteristic cutoff. The power law is not explained by “complexity” alone, nor by any interchangeable story of rich-get-richer dynamics. It appears when the system’s constraints are coupled so that changing one mechanism forces compensating changes elsewhere if the same cross-scale pattern is to remain.

What is load-bearing here?

First, heterogeneous accumulation is load-bearing. Some units must systematically gain more opportunities, links, or resources than others. If this is replaced by uniform allocation, the tail collapses toward thinner distributions. But heterogeneity alone is not enough.

Second, recursion of opportunity is load-bearing. Current success must alter future rates of success. This is the real constraint behind preferential attachment, cumulative advantage, and multiplicative growth. If one removes this feedback, one must add some other scale-preserving amplification mechanism, or else the power law disappears. So “preferential attachment” is not sacred as a story; the load-bearing feature is positive feedback on growth rates.

Third, continual entry, variation, or fragmentation is load-bearing. Without new small units, positive feedback would just concentrate everything into a few dominant units or saturate into finite-size equilibrium. Fresh entrants replenish the lower scales and maintain the full distribution. If entry is removed, some compensating source of subdivision or innovation must appear.

Fourth, weak or absent intrinsic scale is load-bearing. If the system imposes a strong typical size — through hard capacity limits, standardization, optimization to a target, or strong mean-reversion — then scale-free behavior is broken or truncated. Thus power laws are evidence not merely of feedback, but of feedback operating in an environment where no single scale dominates the dynamics.

What follows is that many different mechanisms can produce similar power-law signatures only because they share the same deeper explanatory skeleton: amplification + replenishment + no dominating scale. The interchangeable surface narratives are not the explanation. The explanation is the constrained structure of the system.

This also clarifies why power laws are common in complex adaptive systems specifically. Such systems adapt through decentralized feedback loops among agents competing for attention, energy, links, or fitness. These loops are generative, not equilibrium-restoring. They often route resources toward already-effective structures, while novelty continuously introduces new candidates. That combination naturally produces heavy tails.

But the conjecture also warns against overgeneralization. “Scale-free” is often a narrative shell. If altering the entry process, the feedback law, or the resource constraints leaves the claimed explanation untouched, then the explanation was never doing real work. A genuine explanation must specify which structural features are indispensable for preserving cross-scale amplification. Power laws are therefore best understood not as generic signatures of complexity, but as traces of particular load-bearing feedback architectures.
