## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges not primarily because agents become less selfish, but because certain interaction structures make cooperative behavior the locally rational outcome. The explanatory target should therefore shift from agent motives to the system of repeated encounters, reputation flows, visibility, clustering, and feedback loops that alter payoff landscapes over time.

What is true on this view is that “self-interest” does not uniquely determine defection. In a system with repeated interaction, memory, and information about past behavior, an agent’s incentives are recursively shaped by others’ responses. Cooperation persists when the network structure converts short-term gains from defection into long-term losses through distributed feedback: exclusion, retaliation, loss of future opportunities, or reputational damage. Conversely, cooperation collapses when the structure severs these feedback loops — for example, under anonymity, one-shot exchange, high churn, or poor information transmission.

The key mechanism is emergence through coupled expectations. If enough agents condition their behavior on observed or inferred past behavior, then trustworthiness becomes a system-level asset rather than a private virtue. No central enforcer is required because the community itself functions as a decentralized control system. Reputation acts as a stock; communication, observation, and gossip are flows; reciprocal strategies and partner choice create reinforcing and balancing feedback loops. These loops can stabilize cooperation even when every individual remains narrowly self-interested.

What follows is that the persistence of cooperation depends less on moral transformation than on the architecture of interaction. Small structural changes can have outsized effects: increasing transparency, reducing anonymity, enabling partner selection, preserving memory of past actions, and creating modular but connected communities can all raise the evolutionary viability of cooperation. These are leverage points because they reshape the feedback environment rather than exhorting agents to behave better.

This perspective also illuminates why cooperation is often fragile, path-dependent, and uneven. Because the system is nonlinear, early defections can trigger cascades of mistrust, while small islands of reliable reciprocity can bootstrap broader norms. Cooperative order is therefore computationally and historically contingent: one often cannot infer its durability from individual preferences alone, since the decisive variable is the evolving pattern of mutual responsiveness.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a reframing: cooperation without central enforcement is not a puzzle about how selfish parts transcend themselves, but about how a network of interactions generates incentives, information, and expectations that make cooperation self-reinforcing. The real unit of explanation is the relational structure.
