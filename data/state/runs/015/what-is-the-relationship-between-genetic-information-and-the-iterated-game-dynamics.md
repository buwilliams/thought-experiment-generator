## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The relationship between genetic information and organismal complexity is not primarily one of **direct encoding volume**, but of **iterated interaction structure**: genes specify rules, signals, and conditional responses that are repeatedly enacted across development, physiology, and evolution. Organismal complexity therefore scales less with the raw amount of genetic information than with the degree to which that information participates in **repeated, state-dependent games** among cells, tissues, symbionts, and selective environments.

From an iterated-game perspective, a genome is not a static blueprint for a finished organism. It is closer to a rule set for a long repeated process in which many agents—cells, regulatory elements, signaling pathways, and environmental cues—continually “respond” to one another. In one-shot systems, outcomes must be pre-specified in detail because there is no opportunity for correction, reciprocity, or history-dependent adjustment. But in repeated systems, relatively compact rules can generate highly organized behavior because each round conditions the next. Development is exactly such a repeated process.

What follows is that **high organismal complexity need not require proportionally larger genomes**. A modest amount of genetic information can produce great complexity if it orchestrates rich iterated interactions: feedback loops in gene regulation, repeated cell-cell signaling, checkpoint mechanisms, error correction, and modular reuse of developmental programs. The “shadow of the future” appears here as developmental and evolutionary continuation: cells and subsystems behave differently when future interactions matter. Mechanisms that stabilize cooperation—recognition, signaling, punishment of defectors, memory of prior states—map onto apoptosis, immune surveillance, developmental constraints, and regulatory repression. These are not moral features; they are structural conditions that keep multicellular cooperation from collapsing into defection, such as cancer or developmental disorder.

This illuminates why the naive expectation “more complex organism = more genes” often fails. If complexity came only from one-shot encoding, complexity should track genome size closely. But if complexity emerges from repeated interactions, then what matters is the **architecture of conditional reuse**: when and where genes are expressed, how products interact over time, and how local decisions are revised in light of prior states. The relevant informational object is not just sequence length, but the capacity to sustain stable recursive coordination.

The perspective also predicts failure modes. If the effective time horizon of cellular interaction is shortened, or if defection becomes harder to detect, cooperation should collapse. In biology, this corresponds to breakdowns in long-run coordination—e.g., cells pursuing short-term replication over organism-level function. Thus multicellular complexity depends on making cellular life an iterated game with strong visibility of behavior and durable consequences.

So the core claim is: **genetic information relates to organismal complexity the way strategy rules relate to complex cooperation in repeated games**. The genome does not need to contain a line-by-line description of the organism; it needs to encode a system in which repeated interactions reliably generate and preserve organized complexity.
