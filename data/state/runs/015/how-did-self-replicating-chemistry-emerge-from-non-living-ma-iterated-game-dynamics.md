## Conjecture

Conjecture: Self-replicating chemistry most plausibly emerged not from a single miraculous template, but from repeated interaction cycles among molecular populations in which reaction products persist long enough to encounter one another again. What this perspective illuminates is that the key transition is from one-shot chemistry to repeated chemistry. Hypercycles, autocatalytic sets, and compartmentalized reaction networks fit this pattern: they do not require altruism, only that repeated encounters let mutually reinforcing loops outcompete isolated reactions. Reputation appears here as environmental carryover: molecules acquire a history in the medium through concentration, localization, and persistent catalytic effects. Chemistry “remembers” which interactions were productive because their products remain present and bias future rounds.

This suggests that the decisive precondition for life is not merely the existence of replicator-capable molecules, but a structure that makes beneficial interaction recurrent and defection costly. Chemical “defection” corresponds to parasitic side reactions, freeloading catalysts, or chain terminators that exploit productive networks without sustaining them. Iterated dynamics predicts that cooperation-like chemical organization will collapse when horizons are short (rapid dilution, violent mixing, no compartment persistence) or when defection is obscured and unconstrained (parasites spread without local consequences). Conversely, cooperation-like networks should stabilize when compartments, surfaces, or cyclical environments make interaction repeated, local, and history-dependent.

So the origin of self-replication is better framed as a systems transition: from loose reaction events to ecologies of recurrent molecular games. Templates likely emerged later as a high-fidelity refinement within already stable iterative networks. Repetition first made cooperation among reactions possible; only then could dedicated self-copying molecules become advantageous and robust.
