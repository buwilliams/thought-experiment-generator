## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Biological robustness and evolvability coexist because living systems are organized like durable collective-action regimes: most components are structured to absorb local defection or failure cheaply, while variation is permitted in ways that do not immediately collapse system-level coordination. Robustness is the enforcement layer; evolvability is the tolerated experimentation layer.

From the norm-as-collective-action perspective, the key question is not “how can systems be both stable and changeable?” but “how is costly coordination maintained while allowing enough local deviation to discover better arrangements?” In biology, perturbations are analogous to defections, noise, or noncompliance. If every perturbation propagated freely, the organism would fail. So systems evolve enforcement mechanisms: redundancy, modularity, error correction, buffering, repair, chaperones, feedback control, compartmentalization, and developmental canalization. These are institutions that make maintenance unavoidable. They impose costs, but they prevent any one component’s failure from extracting large private gains at collective expense.

Who pays? Usually the organism pays in energy, extra structure, slower growth, and duplicated machinery. Who benefits? The whole lineage benefits because robust coordination preserves viability across noisy environments and unreliable molecular actors. Why not enforce perfectly? Because strict enforcement suppresses exploration and becomes brittle when conditions change. A regime that punishes every deviation also blocks adaptive novelty.

So evolvability appears where enforcement is selective rather than total. Biological systems often permit “sandboxed” variation: mutations in redundant genes, changes inside modules, neutral networks in genotype space, cryptic variation hidden by buffering, recombination that rearranges parts without destroying all function, and regulatory architectures that alter outputs without rewriting core metabolism. These are institutional designs that lower the cost of experimentation. Defection is often privately “rewarding” at the level of genes, cells, or variants, but higher-level control systems channel or quarantine it so the system can sample novelty without systemic ruin.

What follows is that robustness and evolvability are not opposites. They are complementary solutions to a multilevel collective-action problem. Robustness prevents destructive cascades; evolvability preserves a supply of low-cost deviations that can be promoted if conditions shift. The deepest compatibility comes from structure: modular boundaries, hierarchical control, and degeneracy let one part vary while others continue enforcing viability.

This perspective also illuminates why evolvability is often increased by features that look wasteful from a narrow efficiency view. Redundancy, excess capacity, promiscuous interactions, and layered regulation are not mere slack; they are the budget that pays for both norm enforcement and exploratory freedom. In systems terms, robustness is maintained by fast negative feedback around core functions, while evolvability comes from looser constraints at the margins where new coordination patterns can accumulate.

So the collision yields this: life remains evolvable because it does **not** treat every perturbation as intolerable. It builds institutions that make collapse rare, while leaving enough unpoliced or weakly policed space for adaptive alternatives to arise.
