## Conjecture

Conjecture:

Take a hypothetical complex adaptive system in which the only special variable is adaptive reinforcement under growth: new units enter over time, they must attach, copy, or allocate attention/resources using locally available information, and success increases future visibility or capacity. Assume everything else is stripped away — no central designer, no preferred scale, no global optimization, no characteristic size imposed from above.

Follow the logic.

If agents must act under bounded knowledge, they cannot optimize globally; they use heuristics based on what is already salient. Salience is not passive: whatever has accumulated more links, resources, attention, or influence becomes easier to find, easier to copy, and harder to ignore. That creates a positive feedback loop: accumulation increases the rate of further accumulation. Growth plus reinforcement means history is continually amplified.

Now remove any fixed characteristic scale. If there is no built-in “normal” node degree, firm size, cascade size, or city influence, then the reinforcement process operates similarly across magnitudes. Small differences, once reinforced over many adaptive steps, become large differences. Because new entrants constantly interact with an already unequal landscape, they disproportionately couple to what prior dynamics have made prominent. The system therefore does not converge toward a typical size; it reproduces heterogeneity across scales.

What follows is not merely inequality but a specific statistical form. When proportional growth governs change — when gains are roughly relative to current size rather than additive in absolute terms — multiplicative dynamics dominate. Multiplicative dynamics with ongoing entry and selective retention naturally generate heavy tails. And when the same reinforcement rule applies recursively at many scales, the tail tends toward power-law structure: the probability of very large events declines slowly because there is no strong mechanism cutting off accumulation at intermediate scales.

This illuminates power laws not as mysterious signatures of “complexity” in general, but as the visible residue of a particular system structure:

1. continual growth or turnover,
2. local adaptive choice under limited knowledge,
3. positive feedback on what has already accumulated,
4. absence of a strong characteristic scale,
5. weak enough constraints that reinforcement outruns equalization.

So the conjecture is: complex adaptive systems exhibit power-law and scale-free behavior when adaptation is mediated by scale-insensitive reinforcement loops acting on a growing population, such that history-dependent multiplicative advantage becomes the main generator of structure. Power laws are therefore not equilibrium distributions but records of path-dependent amplification.

The key implication is explanatory, not merely descriptive: to understand or alter scale-free behavior, do not ask first why large hubs or giant events exist; ask what structural feedbacks preserve salience across scales and prevent the system from imposing a typical size. Where equalizing feedbacks become strong, the power law should break.
