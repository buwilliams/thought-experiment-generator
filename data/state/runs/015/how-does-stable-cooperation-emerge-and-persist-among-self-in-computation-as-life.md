## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Stable cooperation among self-interested agents emerges when the interaction environment itself functions as a distributed computational memory that makes behavior legible, selectively repeatable, and consequential across time. In that setting, cooperation is not a moral exception to self-interest but a computationally stable strategy for agents embedded in feedback loops where information about past conduct affects future payoffs.

From the given perspective, self-interested agents are information-processing systems optimizing under local constraints. Cooperation persists without central enforcement when the system computes three things reliably enough: **identity**, **history**, and **conditional response**. If agents can in some way distinguish who they are interacting with, retain or access traces of prior interactions, and alter behavior based on those traces, then selection can favor strategies like reciprocity, reputation-sensitivity, clustering, punishment, and partner choice. These are all computational mechanisms for transforming local information into adaptive coordination.

What matters, then, is not an abstract opposition between selfishness and altruism, but whether the environment supports the right informational feedback structure. A one-shot anonymous interaction computes almost nothing beyond immediate gain, so defection tends to dominate. But repeated interaction, visible reputation, network locality, and shared signals increase the effective memory of the system. That memory allows agents to compute expected future consequences of present actions. Under those conditions, “cooperate now” can be the self-interested output of a wider temporal computation.

This dissolves the usual boundary between internal virtue and external institution. Norms, reputations, rituals, ledgers, and communication protocols are all substrate-independent memory technologies that stabilize cooperation by encoding socially relevant information into persistent forms. They are functional analogues of biological inheritance and signaling systems: mechanisms that preserve successful relational patterns and expose exploitative ones to selection. Cooperation persists when the social system stores enough information to let beneficial strategies find one another and exclude parasitic dynamics faster than trust is destroyed.

A systems view sharpens this: the key leverage points are not exhortations to be cooperative, but structural changes that increase the fidelity and accessibility of interaction-relevant information. Small changes in observability, repeat encounter rates, verification costs, or network modularity can shift the equilibrium from fragile cooperation to robust prosociality. The system’s behavior follows from its informational architecture.

Because social dynamics are computationally irreducible in detail, we should not expect a universal closed-form rule for when cooperation appears. But we can identify the minimal generative principle: **cooperation is selected when the system computes future relational consequences densely enough that exploiting others becomes less adaptive than maintaining valuable cooperative links**.

So the deeper claim is: stable cooperation without central enforcement is an emergent property of decentralized computational ecologies that preserve and circulate information about conduct. Where there is sufficient memory, signal fidelity, and contingent response, self-interest itself becomes a carrier of cooperation.
