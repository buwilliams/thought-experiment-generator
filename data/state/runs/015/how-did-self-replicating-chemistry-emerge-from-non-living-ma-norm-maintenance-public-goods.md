What follows is that “self-replication” likely began as distributed reproduction of an organized process before it became replication of a specific template. The first heritable entity was probably a self-maintaining chemical regime: a package of constraints plus reactions that reproduced the whole arrangement imperfectly across space and time. Only later, within such stabilized regimes, could high-fidelity template molecules emerge and become individually selectable.

This illuminates why origin-of-life transitions are hard: without enforcement, chemistry defects. The deep problem is not merely synthesizing complex molecules, but creating a structure in which enough components are mutually dependent that persistence outcompetes dissipation. So the decisive step from non-life to life was likely institutional before it was informational in the narrow genetic sense: the emergence of environmental and chemical constraints that made recursive production of the same organization unavoidable enough for selection to ratchet in finer-grained templates.
