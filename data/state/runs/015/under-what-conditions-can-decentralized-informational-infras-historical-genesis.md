## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Decentralized informational infrastructures can substitute for central enforcement only where the original function of enforcement was primarily *epistemic*—to create shared memory, legible histories, and common knowledge about behavior—rather than *coercive*. Their hard limit appears where order depends not on better information about past action, but on the capacity to impose losses despite anonymity, exit, collusion, or one-shot predation.

Origin-wise, central enforcement arose not merely to punish, but to solve older coordination failures: unreliable memory, local rumor, fragmented observation, and disputed records. Courts, registries, accounting systems, seals, and bureaucracies replaced informal reputation because small-scale social memory failed under scale, mobility, and complexity. Modern decentralized infrastructures inherit this same problem frame. They often present themselves as alternatives to states or firms, but what they most directly replace is not force; it is recordkeeping, auditability, and the production of public evidence.

From this perspective, their strength is clear: where defection is deterred mainly because actors can be *seen*, *remembered*, and *excluded later*, decentralized systems can do much of the work of enforcement. If a system can cheaply preserve tamper-resistant memory, make relevant actions observable, bind identities or assets across time, and enable credible signaling of reliability, then repeated interaction can discipline behavior without a central sovereign. The substitute works especially well when:  
1. interactions are repeated;  
2. observability is high and difficult to counterfeit;  
3. reputational consequences are portable across contexts;  
4. the gains from future participation exceed one-shot gains from cheating;  
5. exit is costly enough that long-term standing matters.

But the origin story also reveals the inherited blind spot: central enforcement did not emerge only because memory was poor; it emerged because memory alone was insufficient. Informational order breaks where bad actors can profit before reputational updating occurs, where identities are cheap to discard, where harmed parties cannot coordinate retaliation, or where underlying assets and bodies remain outside the informational layer. In such cases, decentralized infrastructure produces evidence of violation without guaranteeing remedy.

So the hard limits are structural, not merely technical. No increase in transparency substitutes for sanction if agents can defect, disappear, and re-enter under fresh identities. No shared ledger substitutes for adjudication when facts are ambiguous or strategically manipulated. No reputation system substitutes for force when violence, fraud, or capture can overwhelm future-oriented incentives. And no decentralized memory solves collective-action problems when everyone sees the violation but no one has enough incentive to bear enforcement costs.

Thus the boundary condition is: decentralized informational systems can replace central enforcement only where enforcement was already mostly a problem of *distributed knowledge*. They fail where enforcement is irreducibly a problem of *asymmetric power, costly punishment, and binding constraint*. In short: information can govern where legibility is what was scarce; it cannot govern where coercive capacity is what was scarce.
