## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
At the limit, the difference between cities that get safer and those that do not is not primarily the amount of force, money, or goodwill applied, but whether the city has crossed a structural threshold where **violence-suppressing feedback loops outcompete violence-reproducing ones**.

Push the problem to extremes. Imagine two boundary cases:

- In one limit, every violent act increases distrust, retaliation, witness silence, school absence, business exit, police overreaction or withdrawal, and neighborhood fragmentation. Safety efforts here are absorbed and converted into more instability. This is a city trapped in a **positive feedback regime**: disorder generates the conditions for more disorder.
- In the opposite limit, each prevented incident strengthens trust, reporting, legitimate conflict resolution, youth attachment to institutions, economic presence in public space, and the precision of enforcement. Safety here compounds. This is a **negative feedback regime**: disturbances are damped faster than they spread.

From the middle, cities can look similar—same poverty rates, same demographics, same policing budget. But at the boundary, the hidden structure appears: some cities are organized so that interventions reinforce one another, while others are organized so that interventions cancel out or backfire.

So the important variable is not any single policy but the **architecture of error-correction**. Safer cities are those that increasingly become better at detecting, localizing, and correcting the causes of violence without destroying the social knowledge needed to do so. That means institutions and communities must jointly preserve the information needed for correction: who is at risk, where conflicts are escalating, which places are criminogenic, which actors are trusted, which rules are seen as legitimate.

What follows is that long-run safety is a knowledge problem before it is a force problem. Cities diverge when one learns how to generate and retain actionable local knowledge, and another destroys it through mistrust, opacity, churn, corruption, or indiscriminate intervention. A city can flood an area with arrests and still become less safe if that action erases the very information channels required for future precision.

This suggests a sharper claim: **the decisive threshold is reached when enough institutions become “hard to vary” in the Deutschian sense**—schools, transit, housing enforcement, violence interruption, policing, courts, neighborhood organizations—so that safety does not depend on heroic individuals or temporary campaigns. Below that threshold, gains decay when leadership changes. Above it, safety persists because the city has discovered reproducible explanations of its own disorder and embedded them in practice.

Therefore: cities get safer over decades when they evolve from episodic reaction to **self-correcting safety systems**. Comparable cities that do not improve are not merely trying less hard; they remain on the wrong side of a systemic phase change, where every intervention is still being fed into a machine that reproduces insecurity faster than it can solve it.
