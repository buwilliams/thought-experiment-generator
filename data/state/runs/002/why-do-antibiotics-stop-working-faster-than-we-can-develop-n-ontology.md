## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Antibiotic failure outpaces antibiotic invention because the relevant fundamental “stuff” is not mainly molecules but knowledge: bacteria are embedded in a system that generates, stores, and transmits anti-antibiotic information faster than our institutions generate, test, and deploy counter-knowledge.

From the information-first perspective, resistance is not just a biochemical event. It is the spread of actionable knowledge in living systems: which mutations, gene combinations, plasmids, and regulatory changes preserve replication under antibiotic attack. Bacterial populations explore this space in massively parallel fashion, under relentless selection, with horizontal gene transfer acting as a communication channel. Antibiotic use increases the payoff for resistance knowledge, so the system accelerates its discovery and diffusion.

By contrast, human antibiotic development is a slower knowledge-production system. It depends on hypothesis generation, laboratory validation, safety testing, regulation, manufacturing, stewardship constraints, and weak market incentives. Each stage is a filter on error, but also a bottleneck on variation. So the asymmetry is structural: bacteria run a fast, decentralized error-correcting search over survival strategies; we run a slow, centralized error-correcting search over acceptable therapies.

What follows is that “overuse of antibiotics” is not the deepest explanation. It is one mechanism by which the environment is made highly informative for bacteria. Every dose teaches the microbial system something about which variants survive. Hospitals, farms, wastewater, and global travel networks become media through which resistance knowledge is selected and propagated. Meanwhile, stewardship rightly reduces antibiotic use, but also reduces the revenue and experimental throughput that might sustain discovery. The system therefore couples fast enemy learning to slow defender learning.

This illuminates why the problem persists even when we discover new drugs: a new antibiotic is often a new message sent into a highly capable adversarial learning environment. Unless the surrounding information ecology changes, bacteria will decode and answer it.

So the leverage point is not only “make more antibiotics,” but redesign the knowledge dynamics. That means suppressing bacterial opportunities to generate and share resistance information while increasing our own rate of explanatory progress: narrower-spectrum therapies, rapid diagnostics, adaptive treatment strategies, anti-plasmid or anti-evolution interventions, infection prevention, surveillance as real-time epistemology, and incentive structures that reward preserving effectiveness rather than maximizing sales.

In short: antibiotics stop working faster than we can replace them because bacterial populations are part of a superior local knowledge-creating system for this domain. The core problem is an imbalance in rates of information generation and transmission. Any durable solution must alter that imbalance.
