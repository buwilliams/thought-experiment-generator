## Conjecture

Conjecture: long-run urban safety is not primarily a function of “more enforcement” or “better people,” but of whether a city discovers and stabilizes self-reinforcing feedback loops that make violence, disorder, and non-cooperation harder to reproduce. What is load-bearing is not any single policy, but a system architecture that keeps generating better local error-correction.

Vary the assumptions.

Remove the assumption that safety comes from adding police capacity. Then some cities should still get safer. Many do. That suggests police are not the sole cause but one component in a wider control system. If safety improves without proportional enforcement growth, something else must be narrowing the “search space” of harmful behavior: street design, housing stability, legitimacy of institutions, informal guardianship, lead reduction, fewer high-risk youth concentrations, better emergency medicine, less alcohol disorder, stronger routines of cooperation.

Invert the assumption that comparable cities differ because of static traits like culture or wealth. If those were load-bearing, trajectories would be mostly fixed. But cities with similar starting conditions diverge over decades. So the crucial variable is dynamic: whether institutions learn. Safer cities likely have more effective criticism-and-correction loops between residents, police, schools, transit, hospitals, landlords, and courts. They detect emerging disorder, test responses, discard what fails, and retain what works. Knowledge creation here is civic, not just technological.

Replace the assumption “crime falls when you suppress bad actors” with its opposite: “crime falls when the system makes bad adaptations unviable.” Then safety becomes a systems property. Violence clusters where retaliation, anonymity, vacant space, weak legitimacy, and slow dispute resolution interact. A safer city is one that breaks these complementarities. It does not need to eliminate every cause; it needs to prevent causes from amplifying one another.

What follows is that comparable cities diverge when one crosses a threshold in feedback quality. Above that threshold, small gains compound: residents cooperate more, witnesses speak, public space fills, businesses stay, disorder is corrected early, institutional trust rises, and enforcement becomes more precise. Below it, the opposite spiral holds. This explains why safety changes can look slow for years and then durable: the underlying stock being built is not arrests but shared problem-solving capacity.

So the load-bearing factor is not “toughness” or “resources” alone, but a city’s ability to generate, retain, and coordinate local knowledge about what prevents violence in its own context. Policies matter, but mostly insofar as they improve or degrade those feedback loops.

Therefore: some cities get safer over decades because they build institutions that learn faster than criminal and disorder-producing systems adapt. Comparable cities that do not get safer are not merely missing the right policy; they are missing the error-correcting structure that would let right policies be found, criticized, and continuously revised.
