## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Concepts and meaning arise when a syntactic-statistical system acquires **internally load-bearing distinctions**: patterns become meaningful not merely by recurring, but by entering a network of constraints where altering one representation changes what the system can infer, predict, compress, and do elsewhere. Meaning is not added on top of syntax; it appears when syntax is organized so that its parts are no longer interchangeable without cost.

From this perspective, bare syntax and statistics are insufficient if they function only as a narrative shell — a bag of correlations whose internal tokens can be swapped with little consequence. A system has concepts when some of its learned distinctions are **structurally necessary** to preserve broad success across many contexts. If changing “dog” to “cat” in its internal organization requires compensating changes in perception, expectation, language use, and action policy, then the distinction is concept-like. If no such compensations are needed, the “concept” is only superficial labeling over statistical regularity.

This suggests a criterion: **meaning is load-bearing inferential role**. A representation means something insofar as it constrains downstream and lateral relations non-arbitrarily. It must help explain why certain generalizations hold, why some substitutions fail, and why the system remains competent across variation. On this view, concepts emerge from pattern recognition when patterns are compressed into reusable structures that support counterfactual stability: the system can handle novel instances because its internal distinctions track dependencies that matter across tasks.

What follows is that semantics is not a mysterious extra ingredient, nor reducible to frequency alone. Statistics supplies candidate regularities; syntax supplies combinatorial structure; pattern recognition supplies learned discriminations. But concepts arise only when these are integrated into a **tightly coupled model** whose parts mutually constrain one another. The explanatory burden shifts from “How do symbols get attached to meanings?” to “When do internal structures become indispensable for maintaining coherent competence?” Meaning is what survives criticism by substitution.

This also illuminates why richer concepts often require world-involvement, action, or multimodal feedback. These are not magical sources of meaning; they increase constraint. A representation tied to perception, intervention, error correction, and cross-context use has more ways to fail, and thus more ways to become genuinely load-bearing. The more a distinction must coordinate across systems, the less arbitrary it is.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a sharp answer: **concepts and meaning arise from syntax, statistics, and pattern recognition when the learned structures become globally constraint-bearing within a system of inference and action.** Meaning is not mere correlation, but non-interchangeability under explanatory and practical pressure.
