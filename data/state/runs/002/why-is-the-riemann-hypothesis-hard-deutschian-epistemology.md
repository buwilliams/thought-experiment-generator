## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The Riemann Hypothesis is hard not because its statement is complicated, but because any true explanation must create a *new hard-to-vary bridge* between domains that are already richly explored yet only partially connected: primes, complex analysis, and abstract structure. Its difficulty is explanatory, not merely computational.

From the conjecture-and-criticism perspective, progress does not come from searching huge formal spaces until a proof appears. The relevant issue is not that the search space is large in a combinatorial sense, but that almost all candidate arguments are *bad explanations*: they are easy to vary, local, and do not reveal why the zeros should line up on the critical line. A proof of RH, if it exists in a worthwhile form, likely has to explain this alignment as a necessary consequence of a deeper structure, not as a lucky fact extracted from calculation.

This illuminates why RH has resisted attack despite massive mathematical knowledge. We already possess many truths *around* it: equivalent formulations, numerical confirmations, partial theorems, analogies with function fields, random matrix heuristics, spectral ideas. But these do not yet amount to the right explanation. They constrain the problem without collapsing it. In Deutschian terms, they are fragments of knowledge awaiting an integrating conjecture that survives criticism.

So the hardness of RH may come from this: the problem sits at a point where existing explanatory traditions are mutually suggestive but not yet unified. Complex analysis describes the zeta function globally; arithmetic describes primes discretely; spectral and geometric analogies hint that the zeros are eigenvalue-like. But no current account makes the critical line unavoidable in a hard-to-vary way. Until such an account is found, attempts resemble guided search through a narrowed but still explanation-poor space.

What follows is a practical research attitude: do not mainly ask, “How can we prove RH from known tools?” Ask, “What deeper object or principle would make RH the natural shadow of its structure?” The fruitful conjectures are likely ontological in mathematics: perhaps the zeros are not merely roots to be bounded or counted, but manifestations of some abstract object whose symmetries force critical-line behavior. If so, RH is hard because that object has not yet been clearly conceived.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a sharper claim: **RH is hard because mathematics lacks the right explanation, not because it lacks enough computation, data, or derivations.** The eventual breakthrough will probably look less like exhaustive search and more like the discovery of a new explanatory framework in which the hypothesis becomes almost inevitable.
