## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Antibiotic failure outpaces antibiotic invention because the load-bearing parts of the two systems are asymmetrically constrained: bacterial evolution has many locally viable variation paths that preserve function, while successful antibiotic discovery depends on a tightly coupled chain of error-sensitive requirements where changing almost any central part breaks clinical usefulness.

What is load-bearing here is not “evolution is fast” or “drug discovery is slow” in isolation, but the dense constraint structure linking mutation, selection, transmission, prescribing, incentives, regulation, and ecological spillover.

A variation test exposes this. Try changing one central part while preserving the observed phenomenon:

- Remove overuse in humans: resistance still emerges through agriculture, hospitals, environmental exposure, and global transmission.
- Keep discovery science strong but remove economic weakness: firms still face the scientific bottleneck that compounds must kill bacteria, avoid toxicity, evade rapid resistance, penetrate tissues, survive manufacturing, and clear trials.
- Improve stewardship without infection control: resistant strains still spread.
- Increase new-drug output without changing incentives: firms still underinvest because good stewardship suppresses sales of the very drugs society wants preserved.
- Target one pathogen: horizontal gene transfer and ecological selection reintroduce the problem elsewhere.

These parts resist independent variation. That is evidence they are load-bearing.

So the best explanation is structural: bacteria search adaptation space through massively parallel reproduction under immediate selection, whereas humans search therapeutic space through serial, high-cost, regulation-bound criticism. Bacteria only need “good enough to survive now.” New antibiotics must satisfy a dense bundle of constraints simultaneously. Each candidate drug is therefore brittle with respect to many criticisms; each bacterial lineage is robust because countless variants can preserve viability. The result is a systematic speed advantage for resistance.

What follows is that the main leverage is not simply “discover faster.” That attacks a downstream variable while leaving the constraint asymmetry intact. More effective interventions alter the structure that makes resistance easy and durable:

1. **Reduce selection pressure**: stewardship, diagnostics, vaccination, sanitation.
2. **Reduce transmission and reservoirs**: infection control, wastewater management, agricultural reform.
3. **Change incentives**: pay for availability and preservation, not sales volume.
4. **Diversify attack surfaces**: combinations, narrow-spectrum drugs, anti-virulence approaches, phages, immune-mediated strategies.
5. **Shorten criticism loops in discovery**: better models, faster pathogen surveillance, adaptive trials.

This perspective also illuminates why “just use AI/LLMs to design new antibiotics” is unlikely to solve the core problem by itself. Even if AI improves search over candidate molecules, that changes only one segment of the human discovery chain. Unless it also helps reconfigure the larger error-sensitive system—selection pressures, transmission, incentives, and deployment—resistance will continue to outrun invention. The load-bearing explanation is therefore ecological and institutional as much as molecular.
