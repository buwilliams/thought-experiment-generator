## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The problem of how intentionality “arises” from physical processes is partly a historical artifact of a replacement: modern philosophy inherited the idea that matter is fundamentally non-about, then treated intentionality as something that must be added back in. The real explanatory task is not to bridge two alien substances — brute physical stuff and mysterious aboutness — but to explain how certain physical systems came to embody *error-correcting representations* because that solved prior problems of control, prediction, and coordination.

Origin story first. The modern problem inherits a post-Cartesian and mechanistic picture: physics won authority by describing nature without purposes, meanings, or intrinsic directedness. This replaced Aristotelian accounts in which teleology was built into nature. But in stripping purpose from physics, philosophy may also have smuggled in an unexamined constraint: that genuine aboutness cannot be part of the natural world unless it is somehow reducible to motion plus law. So “intentionality” became a residue left over by the success of mechanism.

What prior problem did intentionality-language solve? It marked a crucial distinction among internal states: some states do not merely occur; they function in guiding action *as if* the world were a certain way, and can therefore succeed or fail. A belief that there is water nearby is not just a brain event; it is a state whose role is to coordinate behavior with a distal condition, and which is corrigible when wrong. The concept of aboutness was introduced to track this action-guiding, error-possible relation.

From this perspective, intentionality arises when physical processes are organized into systems that must solve the problem of acting under uncertainty. Evolution selected for internal variables that stand in for features of the environment because this outperformed mere reflex. What matters is not resemblance but *use in a feedback loop*: the state is about X insofar as it is produced, maintained, and corrected by interactions in which X is the variable whose tracking improves the system’s viability or goals.

So the hidden assumption to reject is that aboutness must be found in physics at the level of elementary particles. That demand is inherited from the idea that only the most basic ontology is real. But many real properties are organizational. Intentionality is likely one of them: a higher-level feature of knowledge-bearing systems whose internal states participate in conjecture-and-correction about the world.

What follows is that intentionality is neither spooky nor merely metaphorical. It is the emergent property of physical systems that have crossed a threshold: they contain representations that can be *wrong*, and mechanisms that can in principle detect and revise that wrongness. Aboutness, then, is not added to physics from outside. It appears where physical processes instantiate epistemic structure — where a system does not merely react, but carries revisable content about something beyond itself.
