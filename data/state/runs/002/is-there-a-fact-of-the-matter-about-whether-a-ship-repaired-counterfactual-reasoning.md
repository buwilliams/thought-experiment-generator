Conjecture: The “fact of the matter” in the Ship of Theseus problem disappears once you vary the hidden assumption that identity is a single, load-bearing relation waiting to be discovered.

Remove that assumption and replace it with its opposite: a ship can be tracked by multiple non-equivalent continuity relations, each tied to a different explanatory purpose. Then the puzzle changes shape. “Same ship” no longer names one metaphysical fact but compresses several rival criteria: material continuity, structural continuity, historical continuity, functional continuity, legal continuity, and cultural continuity. A plank-by-plank repair preserves some of these while breaking others gradually; rebuilding from discarded planks preserves others. The apparent paradox comes from forcing these different tracking systems into one binary slot.

What breaks when we invert the assumption of unitary identity? Not practice. Shipowners, insurers, navies, museums, and storytellers already use different criteria depending on what problem they are solving. What survives is robust: we can still explain why one repaired vessel counts as “the same” for insurance, while another reconstructed from original planks counts as “the same” for heritage display. So the demand for one final answer looks incidental, not load-bearing.

Now vary a second assumption: that identity must be exact and timeless. Replace it with degree-like historical continuity. Then the repaired ship is not forced into yes/no metaphysics. Instead, it has high causal-historical continuity with the earlier ship despite low material overlap. This mirrors how we already handle persons, institutions, languages, and biological organisms. The world does not seem to supply sharp identity joints wherever language asks for them.

What follows is not that “identity is meaningless,” but that there is a fact of the matter only relative to a chosen invariance principle. If the principle is “same matter,” the answer is no after enough replacement. If it is “same organized vessel in continuous service,” the answer is yes. If it is “same artifact assembled from original parts,” the rebuilt ship from saved planks has the stronger claim. These are not arbitrary opinions; they are objectively criticizable choices about what continuity relation matters in context.

So the illuminating collision is this: the problem seems metaphysical, but its force comes from an overloaded representation. The load-bearing issue is not numerical identity itself but criterion selection. The real question is: same in what respect, for what explanatory task? Once that is made explicit, the paradox dissolves into a competition between abstractions rather than a missing deep fact.

Therefore: there is no context-free fact of the matter about whether the repaired ship is “the same ship.” There are only facts about which continuity structure is preserved, and those facts are what do the real explanatory work.
