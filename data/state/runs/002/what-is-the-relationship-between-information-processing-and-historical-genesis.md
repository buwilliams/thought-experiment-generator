## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The modern problem “what is the relationship between information processing and phenomenal consciousness?” is partly a historical artifact: it arose when **mind was redescribed in computational terms to solve problems about behavior, cognition, and mechanism**, while **phenomenal consciousness remained as the residue of what that redescription was never designed to explain**. So the apparent explanatory gap may reflect not just a metaphysical divide, but a **mismatch between two conceptual inheritances with different origins and success criteria**.

Here is the origin story.

“Information processing” became central because it replaced earlier vocabularies — vital forces, introspective psychologies, and vague faculty-talk — with a framework that could explain **discrimination, control, memory, learning, and flexible behavior**. It was selected because it was fruitful for building mechanistic, predictive theories. In other words, it solved the problem: *How can a physical system produce intelligent-seeming performance?*

“Phenomenal consciousness,” by contrast, is what remained salient from older first-person traditions once behavior and cognition were increasingly explained without invoking experience. It names the problem: *Why is there something it is like at all?* But that formulation inherits assumptions from the very history that produced it: that experience is a private inner datum, that explanation must proceed from third-person function to first-person feel, and that these are separable domains.

What follows from this collision is not that consciousness is reducible to information processing, nor that it floats free of it. Rather: **“information processing” is a concept optimized to track publicly characterizable transformations; “phenomenal consciousness” is a concept optimized to protect what is not captured by that optimization.** Their tension is therefore built in from the start.

This illuminates a hidden constraint: we often ask information processing to explain consciousness after having defined information in ways that abstract away from subjectivity. Then we treat the failure as profound. But perhaps the deeper question is historical and structural: **why did we formulate “information” and “consciousness” as if one were essentially objective process and the other essentially subjective remainder?**

So the conjecture is:

1. The hard divide between information processing and phenomenal consciousness is partly inherited, not discovered.
2. Information-processing theories succeed where the original computational turn was meant to succeed: modeling functional organization.
3. The “hard problem” intensifies when we mistake that framework’s original abstraction for an exhaustive ontology.
4. Progress may require reframing consciousness not as an extra ingredient added to processing, but as evidence that our current concept of information was historically shaped to omit the features now in question.

On this view, the key leverage is genealogical: **before asking how processing yields experience, ask what historical choices made those appear as separate kinds of thing in the first place.**
