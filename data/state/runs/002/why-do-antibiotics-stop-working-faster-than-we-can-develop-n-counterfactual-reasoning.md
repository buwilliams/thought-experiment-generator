## Conjecture

Conjecture: the load-bearing assumption in “antibiotics stop working faster than we can develop new ones” is that treatment is aimed at killing bacteria within an evolutionary arena we continually replenish. If you remove or invert that assumption, the problem changes from a race of invention to a systems problem of selection pressure and transmission structure.

Vary the assumptions.

First, remove the assumption that the bottleneck is discovery. Suppose we could invent new antibiotics much faster. What breaks? Resistance still appears, because the core generator of failure is not primarily lack of candidate drugs but the feedback loop created by widespread use: antibiotics kill susceptible bacteria, resistant variants survive, those variants spread, and each successful drug creates the conditions for its own erosion. Faster invention helps, but inside the same structure it mainly accelerates turnover. So “slow R&D” is important, but not the deepest cause.

Second, invert the assumption that more use means more health. In the short term, yes. At the system level, more exposure often means more selection for resistance. This reveals a classic stock-flow structure: the stock of effective antibiotics is depleted by use, misuse, agricultural application, incomplete courses, and environmental leakage, while replenishment through discovery is slow, expensive, and increasingly difficult. The key asymmetry is that evolution runs continuously and decentralized, while drug development is episodic, centralized, and highly regulated.

Third, replace the assumption that antibiotics are ordinary products with its opposite: treat them as exhaustible infrastructure. What follows? Then overuse is not just inefficient consumption but degradation of a shared resource. The market fails structurally: firms are rewarded for sales volume, but society needs conservation; clinicians are rewarded for immediate patient benefit, but society bears long-term resistance costs; farmers gain productivity from use, while resistance costs are externalized globally. That mismatch is load-bearing.

So the problem survives even if LLMs, medicinal chemistry, or biotech improve discovery dramatically. What breaks it is changing the selection environment and incentive structure: better diagnostics before prescribing, infection prevention, stewardship, restricted agricultural use, surveillance, reimbursement models that decouple profit from volume, and therapies that reduce selection pressure rather than maximize bacterial killing alone.

Illumination: antibiotics “stop working” faster than we replace them because bacteria are not merely targets; they are participants in an adaptive system. We have organized medicine, agriculture, and markets so that every success in antibiotic deployment trains the system against us. The fundamental scarcity is not molecules but time under effectiveness. Therefore the best explanation is: resistance outpaces replacement because evolution is built into the use-pattern, while our innovation and incentive systems are built as if effectiveness were a renewable commodity rather than a depleting commons.
