## Conjecture

Assume a hypothetical world in which we can perfectly isolate one variable: **the object of thought is identical in both cases**, but **the system-level role of that object differs**. A person has lost someone dear. In both grief and depression they think about the loss, feel pain, withdraw, sleep poorly, and struggle to act. Hold the symptoms constant. Now ask: what changes structurally?

Conjecture: **Grief and depression are distinguished not primarily by intensity, duration, or sadness, but by whether suffering remains organized around a specific reality-linked absence or has generalized into a self-maintaining negative mode of world-modeling.**

In grief, the loss is the center of the system. The person’s pain is about *this person is gone*, *this future is gone*, *this relationship cannot continue as before*. The thoughts, even when painful or repetitive, are attempts to reconfigure reality around a real change. The system is trying to update. The feedback loops are harsh, but they are tethered to a particular explanatory problem: how can the world be the world if this person is no longer in it? Grief therefore tends to preserve islands of specificity. The mourner may still say: “I am shattered because she died,” not “everything is meaningless because I am worthless.”

In depression, by contrast, the loss may be the trigger, but it no longer remains the organizing center. The negative state recruits everything into itself. The explanatory frame expands: from *I lost her* to *nothing matters*, *I ruin everything*, *the future is closed*, *I am the kind of being for whom no good world is possible*. The system ceases to be mainly an update to a loss and becomes a generator of global pessimistic explanations. Its feedback loops become self-validating: low energy impairs action, impaired action “confirms” hopelessness, hopelessness narrows attention, narrowed attention finds only confirming evidence.

What this illuminates is that grief is often **world-specific pain**, while depression is **world-general collapse**. Grief says: *this terrible thing is true*. Depression says: *terribleness is the truth of everything*. That is why grief can coexist with love, gratitude, humor, reverence, and even moments of joy without contradiction; these are still compatible with reality being painful in one central place. Depression tends to metabolize such exceptions as errors, frauds, or temporary illusions.

So the practical distinction is not “normal sadness” versus “pathology,” nor “short” versus “long.” It is whether the person’s suffering is still doing the work of **reorganizing around a loss**, or whether it has become a more autonomous system that **reinterprets self, world, and future through a generalized negative explanatory lens**.

If this conjecture is right, then the leverage point is different: grief needs help bearing and integrating reality; depression needs interruption of a runaway explanatory-feedback structure.
