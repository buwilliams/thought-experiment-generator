## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The distinction between a load-bearing explanation and a freely swappable one is historical before it is formal: load-bearing explanations descend from attempts to solve a specific contradiction, while swappable accounts descend from systems optimized to *reproduce acceptable discourse*. Therefore, a useful structural test is not just “hard to vary” in isolation, but “which inherited problem does each part constrain?”

This problem exists because “explanation quality” is being asked to do work previously done by human scientific communities. In science, an explanation was not originally a text artifact to be scored; it was a proposed resolution to an existing problem situation. Its parts became load-bearing because they were shaped against recalcitrant constraints: Maxwell’s equations, simultaneity, free fall, etc. By contrast, LLMs inherit a different lineage. They replace systems for fluent recombination of language, and so they inherit an unexamined assumption: that explanatory structure can be detected in output form alone, rather than in relation to the problem history that made the explanation necessary.

So the hidden constraint in the current framing is that search is being defined over *word combinations* or even *concept combinations*, when the more relevant search space is over **problem transformations**: what contradiction was preserved, sharpened, or dissolved by each move? Einstein’s words were not load-bearing because they were rare or because the final text was compact. They were load-bearing because each element answered a prior impossibility: frozen light waves, observer disagreement, equivalence of acceleration and gravity. Swap out the train, lightning, or freely falling observer, and the explanation survives only if the replacement preserves the same constraint network.

What follows is a sharper criterion:

- In a **swappable account**, parts are connected mainly by semantic compatibility or stylistic coherence. You can replace examples, metaphors, or intermediate claims without reintroducing the original problem.
- In a **load-bearing explanation**, parts are connected by necessity imposed by a prior contradiction. Remove or alter one part, and either:
  1. the original problem returns,
  2. another part loses its reason for being there, or
  3. the explanation no longer excludes rival accounts.

So the structural property to test is **counterfactual dependency on the originating problem**. For each component, ask:
- What earlier problem does this piece solve?
- If removed, what unresolved contradiction reappears?
- What downstream claims lose support?

This suggests a different architecture for knowledge creation. Don’t search primarily for candidate texts. Search for chains of constrained problem-replacements. A candidate explanation is promising when most of its parts can be traced to inherited unsolved tensions and when substitutions fail unless they preserve those tensions.

Thus the key illumination is: “hard to vary” is not fundamentally about textual rigidity. It is about **historical constraint density**. An explanation’s parts are load-bearing when they are remnants of a path through prior problems, not merely well-fitting tokens in a generated account.
