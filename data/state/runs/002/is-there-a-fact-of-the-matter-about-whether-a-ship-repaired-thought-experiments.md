**Conjecture:** There is no hidden fact of the matter about whether the plank-by-plank repaired ship is “the same ship” *independently of an explanatory framework*. One is a navy registry, tracking legal continuity: if repairs are continuous and authorized, it is the same ship. The other is a museum of original materials: if enough original planks are gone, it is not the same ship. Both encounter the same physical sequence of plank replacements. Follow that strictly. If there were a fact of the matter independent of criteria, then in principle there should be some additional structure in reality that selects one answer. But the usual candidates fail. Material sameness fails, because ordinary persistence tolerates material turnover. Spatiotemporal continuity fails, because reconstructed originals elsewhere may preserve material but not continuity. Functional continuity fails, because a perfect replica could match function. Causal history fails, because both the repaired ship and the rebuilt-original-planks ship can have strong causal claims. Every proposed criterion captures something real, but none uniquely governs all uses of “same ship.”

So what is illuminated? The problem is not primarily about ships. It is a stress test for whether identity is a single universal relation in all contexts. The collision suggests it is not. What exists are objects, histories, functions, legal conventions, memories, and explanatory interests. “Same ship” is a compression over these, useful when the criteria align, indeterminate when they do not.

That does not mean “anything goes.” It means answers can be **objective relative to a specified criterion**. Under continuity-of-service, yes. Under preservation-of-original-matter, no. Under historical-authenticity-for-display, perhaps the rebuilt original planks has the stronger claim. The mistake is to demand one answer after stripping away the purpose that gives the identity question content.

So the conjecture is: **there is no criterion-free fact of the matter here; the appearance of a deep metaphysical problem comes from treating a purpose-dependent identity concept as if it must correspond to a unique underlying essence.** The real work is not to discover which ship is truly the same, but to state which notion of sameness the question is trying to preserve.
