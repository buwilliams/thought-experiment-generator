## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Cities become safer over decades when they improve their *error-correcting institutions* for dealing with violence, disorder, and conflict — not merely when they add more force, money, or surveillance. The decisive difference is whether a city becomes better at generating, testing, and retaining explanations of local causes of harm.

From the stated perspective, safety is not primarily a stock of police, wealth, or laws. It is a *body of knowledge* embodied in institutions: which conflicts are predictable, which places generate repeat victimization, which interventions displace crime versus reduce it, which norms make retaliation less attractive, which agencies actually learn from failure. Comparable cities diverge when one builds mechanisms for conjecture and criticism and the other remains trapped in imitation, ideology, or political churn.

What follows is that long-run safety should correlate less with any single policy and more with the presence of structures that permit rapid detection and correction of error. These include: reliable incident measurement, cross-agency information flow, protected criticism from frontline workers and residents, pilotable interventions, and institutional memory that survives mayoral turnover. A city gets safer when it can say, in effect: *our previous explanation of why violence happens here was wrong; here is a better one; here is how we know; here is what changes.* That is knowledge growth.

This also illuminates why “same policy, different outcome” is common. Policies are not the core unit; explanations are. “More policing,” “violence interruption,” “housing first,” or “youth programs” can succeed or fail depending on whether they are attached to true local explanations or to fashionable but shallow descriptions. A safe city is one whose institutions are harder to fool by bad explanations. It does not merely act; it learns.

The systems view sharpens this: crime and safety are outputs of interacting feedback loops — retaliation, trust, clearance rates, school exclusion, illicit market opportunities, neighborhood legitimacy, trauma, and migration. But feedback loops are only governable through knowledge. If institutions misidentify the structure, they push on the wrong leverage points and often amplify harm. So the real leverage point is epistemic: improving the city’s capacity to form and criticize explanations about its own dynamics.

Thus the strongest hypothesis is: **cities that get safer are those that institutionalize local, cumulative problem-solving about harm, while cities that stagnate rely on static doctrines, fragmented agencies, and uncorrected error.** Safety is not just order. It is the outcome of a civic knowledge-creation process.
