## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
At the limit, antibiotic failure is not mainly a race between human innovation and bacterial mutation; it is a structural asymmetry between a **nearly unbounded, massively parallel evolutionary search** and a **slow, centralized, high-cost design-and-deployment pipeline**. Pushed to the boundary, resistance tends toward inevitability unless we change the system so that adaptation itself becomes harder, costlier, or less globally transferable for bacteria.

Here is what the boundary reveals:

- Let bacterial search go toward **infinity**: trillions of organisms, short generations, horizontal gene transfer, selection in every partially treated infection, farm, wastewater stream, and hospital. Then “finding resistance” is not an exceptional event but the default output of the system.
- Let the drug pipeline go toward **zero velocity** relative to that search: decade-long development, narrow incentives, stewardship pressure to use new drugs sparingly, and regulatory friction. Then even good inventions arrive into a world already pre-adapted to defeat them.

So the hidden structure is this: **we are not losing because bacteria are merely “faster,” but because the system gives them more shots on goal while rationing ours.**

From a systems view, antibiotics create a peculiar feedback loop:

1. A new antibiotic increases survival and therefore use.
2. Use increases selection pressure.
3. Selection pressure increases resistance.
4. Resistance erodes the drug’s value.
5. Lower long-term value weakens incentives to develop replacements.

That is a reinforcing loop on the bacterial side and a weakening loop on the human side.

At the extreme, a “perfect” antibiotic used everywhere still fails, because universal success produces universal selection. Conversely, even mediocre countermeasures can last if they sharply reduce selection opportunities, transmission, or the portability of resistance genes. So the key variable is not just drug potency but **the topology of selection** across the whole system.

What follows is a stronger claim:  
**Sustainable control will come less from discovering ever more antibiotics and more from redesigning the environment in which resistance evolves.** That includes combinations that require multiple simultaneous adaptations, localized or rapidly degrading treatments, diagnostics that reduce unnecessary exposure, infection prevention, wastewater and agricultural controls, anti-plasmid or anti-gene-transfer strategies, and economic models that delink profit from volume sold.

In Deutschian terms, the bad explanation is “bacteria evolve, so resistance happens.” True but shallow. The better explanation is that we have built a civilization-scale apparatus that continuously performs resistance discovery on bacteria’s behalf.

So the limiting insight is:  
**Antibiotics stop working faster than we can replace them because resistance is being computed by the biosphere as a distributed search process, while new drugs are being computed by a bottlenecked human institution.** If that is right, the decisive leverage is architectural, not merely pharmaceutical.
