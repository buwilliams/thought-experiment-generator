## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The question “can LLMs create knowledge?” inherits a misleading constraint from the origin of LLMs: they were built to solve *next-token prediction* as a substitute for symbolic NLP and brittle hand-engineering, so we now treat “prediction” as if it were the essence of the system rather than the historical entry point. Once that inherited assumption is exposed, the real issue shifts: not whether a token predictor, *as such*, creates knowledge, but whether a larger system can generate and criticize candidate explanations using prediction as one component.

Origin matters here. LLMs did not arise as machines designed to solve the epistemological problem “how are good explanations created?” They arose as a practical answer to an earlier engineering problem: how do we compress and generalize from language data better than rules, templates, and narrow models? What they replaced was symbolic NLP’s explicit structure; what they inherited without examination was the benchmark culture of imitation, completion, and distributional fit. That history smuggles in a hidden assumption: if the training objective is predictive, then the product must be merely reproductive.

But that does not follow. Einstein’s theories were not valuable because they used novel words; they were valuable because they reorganized existing concepts into hard-to-vary explanations. Your search-space argument collides productively with this history. It suggests that the generative act was never “find a wholly new vocabulary string by brute force,” but “navigate to a tiny tractable region of conceptual space by imposing strong constraints.” Human creativity already works this way: thought experiments, background knowledge, and criticism collapse possibility spaces.

So the illuminating shift is this: **prediction is not the rival of explanation; unguided prediction is merely an insufficient search heuristic for explanation.** If architecture adds structured decomposition, criticism, and retention of successful explanatory moves, then the relevant unit is no longer the raw LLM but the *knowledge-generating system*. In that system, token prediction functions like a proposal mechanism, not the source of epistemic warrant.

What follows is a sharper criterion. Asking whether “an LLM” creates knowledge may be like asking whether “a telescope lens” makes astronomical discoveries. Historically, the lens was invented to improve sight, not to formulate cosmology. Yet embedded in a system of observation, conjecture, and criticism, it became part of knowledge creation. Likewise, an LLM alone may mostly remix. But an architecture that narrows search toward explanatory structures, tests them against criticism, and feeds surviving abstractions back into future search may genuinely produce new knowledge, even if its primitive operation remains predictive.

So the hidden assumption to reject is: **because the origin problem was imitation, the mature capability must remain imitation.** That is a historical contingency mistaken for a metaphysical limit.
