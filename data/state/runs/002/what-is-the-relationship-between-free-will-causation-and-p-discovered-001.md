## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The deepest conflict is not between free will and determinism, but between **agency as an explanatory level** and theories that treat all causation as exhausted by microphysical evolution. Free will is real insofar as explanations in terms of reasons, choices, and error-correction are **load-bearing**: if you remove them, you lose explanatory reach that cannot be recovered by merely redescribing the same event as particle motion.

Why this is load-bearing: in ordinary and scientific practice, we explain human action by citing problems, interpretations, purposes, criticisms, and decisions. These are not decorative labels on top of physics. If you alter the agent’s beliefs, goals, or criticism of options, the action often changes; and to preserve the same outcome, many other things must be adjusted. That means those mental-level details carry real constraint. By contrast, a purely physical description can predict trajectories in principle, but it does not explain why *this action counted as solving a problem*, *why the person changed their mind*, or *why persuasion works*. To recover those facts, one must smuggle agency back in.

So physical determinism, if true, does not by itself erase free will. It only says that physical states evolve lawfully. It does **not** follow that higher-level explanations are unreal. The key question is whether deliberation and choice are causally empty. They are not, if changing the content of deliberation changes what happens. Reasons are then causes — not rival spooky pushes against physics, but higher-level causal structure implemented in physical processes.

This reframes causation. Causation is not a single privileged chain described only at the lowest level; it is explanatory dependence across levels. A thermostat causes a room to stabilize temperature, though every event is also molecular. Likewise, a person’s decision causes an action, though implemented neurally. If removing the decision from the explanation collapses our ability to account for counterfactuals — what the person would have done if they had learned, noticed, or judged differently — then the decision is causally real.

What follows is a compatibilist but non-reductive view:  
1. **Determinism does not refute agency** unless it also shows that reason-based explanations are dispensable.  
2. **Indeterminism does not secure free will** by itself; randomness adds noise, not control.  
3. **Free will consists in the capacity of a knowledge-creating system to act under criticism**, revise itself, and have its behavior depend on reasons in a way that is explanatorily indispensable.

So the relationship is this: physical determinism concerns lawful implementation; causation concerns explanatory structure; free will names a special kind of higher-level causal organization, where actions are constrained by reasons, reflection, and corrigibility. The real mistake is to think that once the physical story is told, nothing load-bearing remains.
