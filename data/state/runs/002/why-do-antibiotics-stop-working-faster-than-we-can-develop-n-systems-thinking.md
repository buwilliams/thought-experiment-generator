## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Antibiotic failure outpaces antibiotic discovery because the system is structurally optimized to *produce resistance* faster than it is optimized to *produce durable treatment options*. The key asymmetry is not mainly scientific difficulty versus bacterial ingenuity, but the arrangement of stocks, flows, and feedback loops across medicine, agriculture, regulation, and markets.

On the resistance side, the stock is the global reservoir of resistance genes and resistant organisms. The inflows are enormous and continuous: every antibiotic exposure in humans, animals, and the environment creates selection pressure; transmission spreads successful variants; horizontal gene transfer lets bacteria share solutions. This is a reinforcing loop: more antibiotic use selects more resistance, which causes treatment failure, which often triggers broader-spectrum or prolonged antibiotic use, which selects still more resistance.

On the innovation side, the stock is the pipeline of effective new antibiotics. Its inflows are slow, costly, and fragile: discovery is hard, clinical trials are expensive, stewardship rightly discourages high-volume use, and revenues are weak because the best new drugs are held in reserve. This creates a balancing loop that suppresses investment: the more responsibly a new antibiotic is used, the less profitable it is, so fewer such drugs are developed.

So the system contains a structural mismatch: resistance evolves through decentralized, massively parallel experimentation across billions of organisms, while drug development depends on centralized, sequential, capital-constrained institutions. Bacteria are embedded in a system with fast learning loops; we are embedded in one with slow approval, weak incentives, and poor coordination.

What follows is that “develop more antibiotics faster” is a low-leverage intervention if the resistance-generating structure stays intact. The system will simply consume new drugs the way it consumed old ones. Higher leverage lies in changing feedbacks and incentives: reducing unnecessary exposure, improving infection prevention, limiting agricultural misuse, tightening diagnostics so treatment is precise, separating company revenue from sales volume, and treating effective antibiotics as a maintained commons rather than ordinary products.

This perspective also illuminates why the problem feels like a race we keep losing. It is not a race between two isolated agents, scientists and microbes. It is one system with two coupled learning loops, where one loop is fast, distributed, and automatically rewarded, and the other is slow, bottlenecked, and often punished for success. Until that structure changes, faster innovation alone will not solve the problem; it will only feed the same system that makes antibiotics stop working.
