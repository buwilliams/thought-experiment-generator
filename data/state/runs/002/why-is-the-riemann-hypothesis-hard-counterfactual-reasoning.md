## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The Riemann Hypothesis is hard not mainly because its search space is vast, but because most of the usual assumptions that make search productive fail or become non-diagnostic in this domain. What is load-bearing is not combinatorial generation of candidate explanations, but access to a representation in which criticism strongly constrains error.

Vary the assumptions.

**Remove the assumption that more candidate generation helps.**  
In many problems, a larger search over recombinations can surface useful novelty because bad candidates can be filtered by strong tests. For RH, this breaks: there are infinitely many superficially adjacent reformulations, heuristics, analogies, and numerical patterns, but almost all are weakly informative. The issue is not lack of possible conjectural paths; it is lack of decisive criticism. Numerical verification of zeros, random-matrix analogies, prime-distribution evidence, and equivalent statements all constrain something, but not enough to kill the central error modes. So brute-force or guided search does not collapse the space of explanations fast enough.

**Invert the assumption that evidence accumulates toward proof.**  
Suppose checking more zeros or deriving more consequences should make RH steadily easier. What follows? It should begin to resemble an engineering optimization problem: enough corroboration would corner the truth. But that has not happened. This suggests RH is not bottlenecked by confidence; it is bottlenecked by explanatory structure. Existing evidence supports RH, but support is cheap here. What is missing is a theory that explains *why* zeros must lie on the critical line in a way that is hard to vary.

**Replace the assumption “the right language already exists” with its opposite.**  
If the current analytic-number-theoretic framing were sufficient, then centuries of reformulation would likely have exposed a narrowing route. Instead, many equivalent forms preserve the difficulty. That survival under translation is revealing: the hardness may be invariant across existing representations. This suggests the problem is load-bearingly about ontology and structure — perhaps we lack the right object, symmetry, or dynamical picture in which RH becomes natural. In Deutschian terms: we may have many statements, but no good explanation.

**Remove the assumption that local improvements compose globally.**  
For many domains, better estimates, stronger bounds, and sharper techniques eventually bridge the gap. For RH, these gains often stall short of the decisive threshold. What breaks is the expectation that incremental technical progress linearly approaches solution. The system has a structural barrier: many techniques operate on consequences of the zeta function rather than the deep reason for its zero distribution.

So what survives all these variations?  
RH appears hard because it is a problem where **search without explanatory leverage is sterile**. Its difficulty comes from a criticism bottleneck, not merely a generation bottleneck. Therefore the best bet is not wider exploration alone, but finding a new representation that makes wrong explanations easy to refute and the right one hard to vary — perhaps via a presently missing bridge between analytic continuation, spectral structure, and prime regularities.
