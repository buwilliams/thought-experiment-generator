## Conjecture

Conjecture: The load-bearing issue in the free will / causation / determinism debate is not whether events are caused, but whether agents can be real causal structures within the world. Physical determinism threatens free will only if we assume that causation must run exclusively “bottom-up” from microphysics and that higher-level descriptions are merely decorative. If that assumption is removed, much of the conflict dissolves.

Vary the assumptions:

1. Remove determinism.
If physical indeterminism is introduced, free will does not automatically appear. Randomness is not authorship. An undetermined choice is not thereby a willed choice. So determinism is not the main load-bearing obstacle.

2. Invert causation.
Assume causation is not only microphysical pushing, but can also be understood at the level of systems: an organism, with memories, goals, error-correction, and representations, is a causal entity. Then “my deciding” is not a ghostly interruption of physics; it is a higher-level description of a physically instantiated control system. What survives this inversion is the intuition that deliberation matters. What breaks is the idea that only particle-level descriptions are genuinely causal.

3. Replace the assumption that freedom requires being uncaused with its opposite: freedom requires being caused in the right way.
On this view, a free action is one caused by the agent’s own reasoning, values, and criticism-sensitive processes, rather than by coercion, compulsion, or bypass. This makes free will compatible with causal order and possibly with determinism. What matters is not metaphysical exemption from cause, but whether the cause runs through the person as a knowledge-bearing system.

What this illuminates:
The traditional debate often treats “caused” and “free” as rivals. But that opposition appears incidental. The deeper contradiction is between two ontologies of causation:
- one in which only fundamental physical events really cause anything,
- and one in which explanatory levels containing information, goals, and error-correction can also be real causal organizations.

If the first ontology is right, free will is hard to make sense of except as illusion or shorthand. If the second is right, determinism need not eliminate agency any more than the determinism of a computer eliminates the reality of software. The software does not escape the hardware; it is a real pattern instantiated in it, and explanations at that level are indispensable.

So the conjecture is: free will is best understood not as a break in causation, nor as a residue left over after determinism fails, but as the causal power of an agent conceived as a higher-level, knowledge-using system. Physical determinism matters only insofar as it is taken to exclude the causal reality of such systems. The real fault line is not determinism versus freedom, but reductionism versus the reality of emergent agency.
