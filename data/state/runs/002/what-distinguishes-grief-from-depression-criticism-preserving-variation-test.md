## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What distinguishes grief from depression is not a list of surface symptoms but the **load-bearing role** those symptoms play within an explanation of the person’s overall state. Grief is organized around a **specific world-model rupture**—the loss of someone or something irreplaceable—and its central elements are tightly constrained by that loss. Depression, by contrast, is organized around a more **global collapse in agency, valuation, and expectation**, where sadness may be present but is not structurally anchored to one particular absence.

From the load-bearing perspective, the key question is: **which parts of the explanation can be varied without breaking explanatory success?** If you try to vary the loss in a grief explanation—remove the bereavement, replace yearning with generic emptiness, disconnect waves of pain from reminders, or strip out continued attachment to the lost person—the explanation degrades. It loses contact with the phenomenon. The sadness in grief is therefore **error-sensitive to the lost object**, to memory, to longing, to preserved love, and often to fluctuation: moments of pain, meaning, guilt, gratitude, or even joy can coexist because they are all constrained by the same absence.

In depression, many of those features are not load-bearing. A depressed person may or may not have suffered a recent loss; the low mood often generalizes across domains; anhedonia, hopelessness, self-reproach, psychomotor change, and diminished agency can persist even when reminders of any specific loss are absent. If you vary the supposed object of sadness, the depression explanation may remain intact. That indicates the explanation is carried less by attachment-to-loss and more by a **system-wide failure mode** affecting motivation, expectation, and self-model.

What follows clinically is that “grief vs depression” is not best treated as a binary symptom checklist but as a **constraint test** across a criticism set: triggers, temporal pattern, relation to reminders, retained capacity for meaning, self-evaluation, and responsiveness to connection. If the person’s distress remains intelligible only by preserving a dense network centered on a particular loss, grief is load-bearingly present. If the distress is robust under removal or substitution of that loss-centered structure, depression is load-bearingly present.

This also illuminates why the two can overlap. A person can have grief plus depression when both constraint networks are active: one centered on bereavement, the other on generalized collapse. The practical implication is that diagnosis should test which explanatory components are non-negotiable. The distinction is real not because grief is “normal sadness” and depression “disorder,” but because they are **different explanatory structures with different load-bearing constraints**.
