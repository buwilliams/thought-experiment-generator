## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Cities become safer over decades not primarily because they “fight crime better,” but because they solve an older inherited problem: how to make dense urban life governable at scale. The modern “public safety” frame replaced earlier concerns like riot control, sanitation, labor discipline, and machine politics, but it inherited their institutions, metrics, and assumptions. As a result, cities that get safer are often the ones that accidentally redesign the underlying urban coordination system, while cities that do not remain trapped optimizing the visible output called “crime.”

Origin-first, the question is: why did “city safety” become a distinct policy object at all? Historically, fast-growing cities faced a prior problem: strangers living in proximity without shared norms. The response was not originally a knowledge-rich theory of violence reduction but a patchwork of policing, zoning, infrastructure, schooling, public health, and administrative legibility. Today we treat crime rates as if they measure a self-contained phenomenon, but they are residues of that older project. That means “safety” inherits hidden assumptions: that disorder is mainly an enforcement failure, that incidents are the right unit of analysis, and that the police department is the main actuator.

What follows is that long-run safety differences between comparable cities likely come from differences in how those inherited subsystems evolved together. Safer cities are not merely those with better tactics against crime; they are those that gradually built urban arrangements in which violence-producing feedback loops weaken over time. Examples include: higher state capacity to maintain public space consistently, better dispute-resolution pathways outside violence, more reliable informal norms, lower youth idleness, less institutional churn, and greater trust that rules will be applied predictably. These are not “anti-crime programs” in the narrow sense. They are solutions to the deeper problem of making city life intelligible and dependable.

This perspective illuminates why apparently similar cities diverge. Two cities may copy visible interventions—hot-spot policing, cameras, community programs—yet get different results because those interventions sit atop different inherited structures. One city may have institutions originally shaped by patronage, racial exclusion, fragmented governance, or fiscal instability; another may have inherited stronger municipal coordination and legitimacy. The same policy lands in a different system.

So the hidden constraint is conceptual: asking “what reduces crime?” may already be too downstream. The better question is: what historical package of institutions made violence a less useful or necessary strategy in everyday urban life, and what legacy structures still reproduce the opposite? Cities get safer when they stop treating safety as a sector and start, intentionally or not, rebuilding the conditions that earlier urban systems were crudely trying to secure.
