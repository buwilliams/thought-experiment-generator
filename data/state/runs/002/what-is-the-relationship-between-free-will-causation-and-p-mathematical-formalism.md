## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The apparent conflict between free will, causation, and physical determinism comes from treating them as statements at the same level of description. Mathematically, they are different structural constraints on a single dynamical system.

Let a physical system have state space \(S\) and law of evolution \(T\), so that
\[
s_{t+1} = T(s_t).
\]
Physical determinism is the claim that \(T\) is single-valued: given the total prior state, the next state is fixed. This says something about trajectories in \(S\).

Now define an agent not as a mysterious exception to \(T\), but as a higher-level subsystem with internal variables \(A\) that encode models, goals, and counterfactual evaluations. The agent’s “choice” is a map
\[
\pi: I \times M \to Act,
\]
from inputs \(I\) and internal model-state \(M\) to action. Free will, on this picture, is not the failure of causation, but the presence of a particular causal architecture: actions vary systematically with reasons represented in \(M\). An agent is free to the degree that its behavior is generated by stable internal transformations that are sensitive to representations, not merely by immediate external forcing.

So causation operates at multiple levels. At the micro-level, \(T\) determines the trajectory. At the macro-level, explanatory variables are coarse-grainings \(C(S)\) that preserve relevant invariants: deliberation, intention, constraint, learning. A cause at the agential level is real if interventions on that variable change outcomes reliably. This is a structural criterion, not a metaphysical add-on.

The key invariant is **counterfactual dependence under model-preserving intervention**. If changing an agent’s reasons, beliefs, or preferences changes action while holding fixed the rest of the relevant architecture, then the agent has control in the only mathematically meaningful sense: its action is a function of internal variables that encode alternatives. “Could have done otherwise” means:
\[
\exists M' \neq M \text{ such that } \pi(I,M') \neq \pi(I,M),
\]
not that the exact total physical state could yield multiple futures. The latter would violate determinism; the former is enough for agency.

Thus the non-negotiable constraint is: determinism forbids multiple outcomes from the same complete state. But it does **not** forbid higher-level causal organization in which actions are produced by reasoning processes. Free will is compatible with determinism when freedom is identified with the structure of the mapping, not with indeterministic gaps in it.

So the relationship is this: **physical determinism fixes the trajectory; causation describes the dependency structure within it; free will names a special class of causally organized subsystems whose actions track internally represented reasons across counterfactual variations.** The clash dissolves once we distinguish state-transition uniqueness from agential control.
