## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The modern “free will vs. determinism” problem is largely a historical artifact: it arose to reconcile older practices of praise, blame, choice, and agency with a newer mechanistic picture of nature. As a result, the debate inherits an unexamined assumption — that human action must fit into the same explanatory frame as billiard-ball causation — and this inherited framing makes free will appear mysterious or impossible.

Origin first. “Free will” did not arise primarily as a physics problem. It emerged within moral, legal, and theological contexts that needed to distinguish acts attributable to a person from accidents, coercion, madness, or compulsion. The concept was solving a practical problem of **agency attribution**: when is an action genuinely someone’s doing? It was later forced into collision with early modern physical determinism, which was solving a different problem: how to describe regularities in nature through universal laws.

What did this newer framework replace? It displaced older teleological and person-centered explanations of action with impersonal efficient causation. But in doing so, it smuggled in a hidden inheritance: the idea that a complete explanation of any event must be a lower-level causal story. Once that assumption is accepted, reasons, intentions, and deliberation look either reducible to prior physical states or epiphenomenal. Then “free will” becomes defined negatively: a miraculous gap in causation.

That framing is likely mistaken. It conflates **causation in physics** with **explanation in agency**. A person acting for reasons is not obviously a rival to causal order; it is a higher-level description of how certain physical systems — persons — generate actions through representation, criticism, imagination, and choice. The key question is not whether actions are uncaused, but whether the organism is the kind of system whose behavior is guided by internally modeled alternatives, reasons, and error-correction rather than by mere external forcing.

What follows is a reframing. Physical determinism, even if true, would not by itself settle the reality of free will, because free will was never fundamentally about exemption from causation. It was about whether persons are real causal-explanatory nodes in the world. Conversely, indeterminism would not automatically save free will; randomness is no better than compulsion.

So the illuminating claim is: **free will is best understood as a problem about levels of explanation and the historical misapplication of mechanistic causation to agency, not as a contest between human choice and law-governed nature.** The deepest inherited constraint is the demand that person-level explanation justify itself in terms borrowed from physics. Once that constraint is criticized, the relation becomes clearer: causation describes lawful process; determinism concerns predictability from prior states; free will concerns whether agents can create actions through reasoning. Those are related, but not identical, questions.
