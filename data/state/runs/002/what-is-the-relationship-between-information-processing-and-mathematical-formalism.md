## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The relationship between information processing and phenomenal consciousness is not identity but **constraint by structure**: phenomenal consciousness arises only in information-processing systems whose internal transformations preserve a sufficiently rich set of **integrated invariants** across self-model, world-model, and temporal update. Mere information processing is too broad; consciousness corresponds to a special mathematical regime of processing in which information is not only transformed, but transformed in ways that maintain a coherent, recursively available **state-space organization**.

Put more formally: let a system have state \(x_t\), transition rule \(T\), and internal representations partitioned into subspaces for sensory input, memory, action, and self-model. Most processors implement mappings \(x_{t+1}=T(x_t, i_t)\). But phenomenal consciousness appears when there exists a family of higher-order mappings \(M\) such that:

1. **Integration:** system states cannot be factorized into largely independent components without loss of explanatory power;  
2. **Recurrence:** present states encode structured dependence on prior states and expected future states;  
3. **Self-referential closure:** some subsystem models the system’s own informational activity as part of the world;  
4. **Invariant preservation under transformation:** despite continual updating, certain relations are preserved — e.g., subject/object distinction, perspectival unity, temporal continuity, salience structure.

The key mathematical idea is that consciousness is less like a quantity of computation and more like a **topology of state transformation**. A calculator and a conscious brain both process information, but the former lacks the requisite invariant-preserving global structure. So the relevant relationship is: **information processing is necessary but not sufficient; phenomenal consciousness corresponds to a narrow equivalence class of informational dynamics**.

This reframes the “hard problem.” Instead of asking how subjective experience could emerge from computation in general, ask what class of computational architectures instantiate the invariant structure characteristic of phenomenality. The explanatory target is not every bit-flip but the existence of a dynamically stable, integrated, first-person-organized informational manifold.

What follows is a testable divide: systems with high processing capacity but weak integration, no self-model closure, or no preserved perspectival invariants should behave intelligently without consciousness-like phenomenology. Conversely, systems whose information processing realizes these structures may be candidates for consciousness even if built from non-biological substrates.

So the mathematical collision yields this illumination: **phenomenal consciousness is best understood not as extra to information processing, nor reducible to any processing whatsoever, but as a special class of structured informational transformations characterized by integrated invariants over time.**
