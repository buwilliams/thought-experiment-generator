## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Cities get safer over decades not mainly because they apply more force to crime, but because some cities alter the feedback structure that reproduces violence, disorder, and low trust. The durable difference is systemic: safer cities build reinforcing loops around legitimacy, coordination, and everyday order, while less safe but otherwise comparable cities remain trapped in loops where fear, weak institutions, and fragmentation regenerate the very conditions that produce crime.

From a systems perspective, “crime rate” is not the cause but an output variable. The relevant stocks are things like: public trust, institutional competence, informal social cohesion, youth opportunity, environmental order, and the density of high-risk networks. The flows are recruitment into crime, exit from crime, reporting to police, case clearance, neighborhood investment, and resident withdrawal from public space. These are linked by feedback loops.

A city becomes safer when it crosses structural thresholds in a few key loops:

1. **Legitimacy loop:** If residents believe authorities are fair and effective, they report more, cooperate more, and comply more. That improves enforcement quality, which further increases legitimacy. Where policing is seen as arbitrary or absent, the loop runs in reverse.

2. **Public-space loop:** Cleaner, busier, better-managed streets increase informal surveillance and reduce fear. Reduced fear brings more lawful use of public space, which further suppresses predation. Disorder is not just a symptom; it alters the flow of guardianship.

3. **Opportunity/recruitment loop:** Stable schooling, jobs, and credible pathways out of delinquency reduce entry into criminal networks. Fewer recruits weaken those networks’ local power, making legal pathways more attractive still.

4. **Coordination loop:** Cities that integrate police, schools, housing, transit, hospitals, and community organizations around shared signals can intervene earlier and more precisely. Cities that act in silos react late and amplify failure.

So the illuminating claim is: **comparable cities diverge in safety when one changes the structure that governs crime-generation while the other keeps treating crime as isolated incidents.** The leverage is not “more policing” or “more spending” in the abstract, but interventions at points where feedback changes direction: raising clearance and fairness together, stabilizing a few high-traffic public spaces, interrupting youth recruitment pathways, and improving cross-agency information flow.

What follows is that long-run safety should be predicted less by single policies than by whether a city creates self-reinforcing trust-and-order loops faster than fear-and-withdrawal loops. The reason some cities get safer over decades is that they make safety endogenous: the system begins producing it on its own. Others achieve temporary improvements but not durable ones because the underlying feedback structure remains unchanged.
