## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Antibiotics fail “faster than we can replace them” not mainly because bacterial evolution is intrinsically too powerful, but because the knowledge-creation system on our side is structurally weak: bacterial populations run millions of parallel conjecture-and-criticism cycles in real time, while human antibiotic discovery is organized as a slow, centralized, high-cost filter that suppresses exploratory variation. The asymmetry is epistemic before it is merely biological.

From this perspective, resistance is not just “selection pressure.” It is a distributed error-correction process. Random variation generates candidate survival strategies; the environment criticizes them; successful explanations of how to survive the drug get embodied in genomes and spread. Bacteria do not “justify” resistance; they generate it through relentless conjecture and elimination. Horizontal gene transfer accelerates this by allowing successful solutions to move between lineages. So the bacterial world is a massively parallel knowledge-creating system.

By contrast, our institutions for antibiotic development are poor at generating and testing explanatory novelty. They optimize for safety, profitability, and incremental modification of known chemical classes. That narrows search too early around familiar ideas. In Deutschian terms, we are often not producing good explanations of *how to make resistance difficult*, but only new instances of old guesses. The result is predictable: bacteria criticize our drugs faster than we criticize our own design assumptions.

What follows is that the core problem is not simply “insufficient R&D” or “overuse of antibiotics,” though both matter. The deeper issue is a mismatch in the structure of search. Bacteria search locally, continuously, and in enormous numbers. We search episodically, expensively, and through institutions that reward low-variance bets. Their system is set up for rapid discovery; ours is set up for cautious exploitation of existing knowledge.

This illuminates why many interventions disappoint. New antibiotics alone fail because they enter the same evolutionary game board. Stewardship alone fails because it slows one flow without redesigning the underlying system. Faster discovery pipelines alone may still fail if they keep rediscovering vulnerable targets.

So the promising direction is not merely to speed up drug discovery, but to redesign the human knowledge system to become more like a better error-correcting search architecture: diversified parallel exploration, rapid criticism, real-time ecological surveillance, adaptive combination therapies, and platform-based design aimed at mechanisms that are hard to vary. The goal is not “winning” against evolution once, but building a system that generates better explanations faster than microbes can refute them.

So the conjecture is: **antibiotic resistance outpaces antibiotic invention because bacterial evolution is already a superior knowledge-generating system for this domain; unless we restructure our own discovery institutions around faster conjecture, criticism, and adaptation, antibiotics will keep failing on evolutionary timescales faster than we can replace them.**
