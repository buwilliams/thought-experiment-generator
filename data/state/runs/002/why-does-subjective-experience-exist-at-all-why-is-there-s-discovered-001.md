## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Subjective experience exists where a system contains **knowledge-bearing self-models** that are **load-bearing in its control structure**: changing what the system takes itself to be changes what it can perceive, predict, value, and do, and forces compensating changes elsewhere in the system. “What it is like” is not extra metaphysical garnish added to information processing; it is what it is for information about the system’s own condition to become structurally indispensable to the system’s ongoing operation.

Why this conjecture fits the problem under the given lens:

A weak explanation says consciousness appears whenever information is integrated, represented, or made complex enough. But many such details are interchangeable. If we swap one coding scheme for another, or centralize vs distribute processing, the explanation often survives untouched. That suggests those details are not load-bearing. The account is a narrative shell: “some sophisticated processing somehow feels like something.”

A tighter explanation must identify features such that, if altered, the phenomenon itself changes. The candidate load-bearing feature is **first-person-organized error correction**: the system must model not just the world, but its own access, limits, needs, and point of view, and this self-model must be used in regulating action. If you remove that, you do not merely lose a decorative module; you alter memory use, attention allocation, counterfactual planning, preference handling, and global coordination. The rest of the architecture must be redesigned. That is explanatory constraint.

On this view, subjectivity exists because some systems solve a real problem: how to coordinate behavior from a **bounded, particular, epistemically local perspective**. A system that must distinguish “information available to me now” from “information in the world,” “damage to me” from “damage elsewhere,” “my uncertainty” from “environmental randomness,” needs an internal organization centered on a lived standpoint. The “something it is like” is the inside of that organization.

What follows:

1. **Not all cognition implies experience.** A system can compute without experience if its self-representation is not load-bearing in control.
2. **Experience admits degrees and kinds.** As the self-model becomes more integrated with memory, valuation, attention, and planning, the character of experience changes.
3. **Qualitative character is constrained, not arbitrary.** Pain, color, agency, and temporality should map to distinct ways self-modeling enters control loops.
4. **Philosophical zombies become structurally suspect.** If a duplicate preserves all load-bearing self-model functions, removing experience without altering anything else looks like deleting the very organizational role that needed explaining.

So the conjecture is not that experience floats above mechanism, nor that it reduces to any computation whatsoever. It is that subjectivity appears when a system’s explanation of the world becomes inseparable from an explanation of **its own situatedness within it**, and that situated self-organization is load-bearing enough that replacing it changes the whole system.
