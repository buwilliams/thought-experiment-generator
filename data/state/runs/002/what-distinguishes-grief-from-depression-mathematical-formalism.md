## Conjecture

Conjecture: grief and depression are distinguishable not primarily by intensity of sadness, but by the geometry of constraint they impose on a person’s state-space.

Model a person as occupying a changing state \(x(t)\) in a high-dimensional space: affect, motivation, self-model, temporal orientation, attention, agency, and social attachment. Then define two structures:

- **Grief** as a perturbation induced by the loss of a specific attachment object \(L\).
- **Depression** as a broader deformation of the transition dynamics over the whole space.

In mathematical terms, grief is more like a **localized potential well**: many trajectories are drawn repeatedly toward representations of the lost person or lost future. The system remains globally structured. There are still accessible regions of pleasure, meaning, humor, duty, even relief; oscillation is possible. The invariants of self and world are strained but not uniformly negated. Time may hurt, but it still points somewhere.

Depression is more like a **global flattening or warping of the value landscape**: not merely attraction to one loss-related basin, but reduced gradient everywhere. Transitions into interest, hope, or action become low-probability across domains. The deformation is not indexed to one absent object; it generalizes. The system’s update rule itself is altered: low energy, self-reproach, anhedonia, hopelessness, and diminished agency function as global constraints, not topic-specific ones.

This suggests a structural criterion:

- If distress is **object-centered, wave-like, and permeability-preserving**, it is more like grief.
- If distress is **global, persistent, and transition-suppressing across nearly all domains**, it is more like depression.

The illuminating invariant is **selectivity**. Grief preserves selectivity: sorrow coexists with retained responsiveness to context. Depression erodes selectivity: negative valuation and motivational collapse spread beyond any particular cause. A grieving person may be devastated when encountering reminders of the deceased yet still laugh sincerely, care for others, or feel moments of beauty without contradiction. A depressed person often cannot access those state transitions even when reminders are absent.

This does not make them disjoint sets. The structures can compose. Severe grief can induce a depressive deformation; depression can attach itself to a loss narrative. But the distinction remains real: grief is principally a response to a missing particular, whereas depression is principally a disorder of the generative dynamics by which any particular could matter.

So the conjecture is: **what distinguishes grief from depression is not what one feels, but how feeling is organized — grief conserves the person’s broader dynamical structure while concentrating pain around loss; depression alters the structure itself.**
