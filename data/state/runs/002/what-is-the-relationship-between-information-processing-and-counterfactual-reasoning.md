## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The tight link many theories posit between information processing and phenomenal consciousness depends on a hidden load-bearing assumption: that *functional organization is sufficient for appearance*. If we systematically vary that assumption, what survives is a weaker but more durable claim: information processing is likely **necessary for the control, reportability, and integration** associated with consciousness, but not obviously sufficient for **there being something it is like**.

Start with the standard assumption: if a system processes information in the right integrated, recursive, or globally available way, phenomenal consciousness follows. Now invert it: suppose identical information processing could occur without phenomenality. What breaks? Theories that infer experience directly from function lose their bridge principle. A zombie system becomes conceivable: behavior, reports, self-modeling, and flexible cognition all remain intact. This does not prove zombies are real, but it shows that the move from “does the right informational work” to “feels like something” is not licensed by information-processing descriptions alone.

Now remove a different assumption: that consciousness must be explainable wholly in third-person structural terms. What follows is not mysticism, but a sharper division of explanatory labor. Information processing still explains discrimination, memory binding, attention, error correction, self-reference, and the causal role of conscious states in a cognitive economy. But the phenomenal character of those states remains an additional fact to be explained, not reducible by redescription in informational language. What survives here is the practical indispensability of information processing to any creature like us; what fails is the identity claim.

Replace the core assumption with its opposite: phenomenal consciousness is primary, and information processing is one of its expressions or constraints. Then information-processing architectures become conditions under which experience is stabilized, differentiated, or made reportable, rather than generators of experience ex nihilo. This alternative preserves much of cognitive science while denying that computation alone closes the gap.

So the collision reveals a useful distinction: **information processing may be structurally necessary for the organization of conscious minds without being metaphysically sufficient for phenomenal consciousness itself**. The load-bearing issue is not whether experience correlates with informational complexity—it plainly does—but whether correlation plus function yields identity. Varying assumptions suggests it does not.

Thus the conjecture is: **the relationship is one of constraint and articulation, not straightforward reduction**. Information processing shapes the form, accessibility, and causal role of conscious states; phenomenal consciousness is whatever additional reality makes those informational states be experienced rather than merely enacted. What survives assumption-variation is dependence. What breaks is sufficiency.
