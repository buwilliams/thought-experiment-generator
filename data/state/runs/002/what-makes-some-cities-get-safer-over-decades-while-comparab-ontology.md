## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Cities get safer over decades when they become better **knowledge-processing systems** about violence: they create, preserve, transmit, and act on reliable local information about conflict, risk, norms, and coordination. Comparable cities do not get safer when this informational metabolism is weak, fragmented, or corrupted, even if they spend similar money or adopt similar policies.

From the perspective that knowledge and information are fundamental, “safety” is not primarily a stock of police, wealth, or laws. It is an emergent property of whether a city’s institutions and communities can generate good explanations of harm and correct errors fast enough. Violence persists where ignorance is reproduced: people do not know who is at risk, which disputes are escalating, which places are criminogenic, which interventions work, or which authorities are trustworthy. A city gets safer when it solves these knowledge problems better than peer cities.

What follows is structural. Safer cities likely have tighter feedback loops between events and understanding: incidents are reported, interpreted, criticized, and converted into action. They also have memory: agencies, neighborhoods, schools, hospitals, and courts retain lessons instead of resetting with every election or leadership change. They reduce informational loss between silos. Police may know one thing, hospitals another, residents a third; safety improves when these are made mutually legible.

This perspective also illuminates why superficially similar interventions diverge. “More policing” or “more social spending” is too coarse. The real variable is whether an intervention increases the city’s capacity to discover and diffuse better explanations. A hotspot strategy works only if the hotspot model is true enough and continuously corrected. Community programs work only if they encode actionable knowledge about who influences whom, where retaliation chains form, and which norms can shift behavior. Trust matters because distrust is an information barrier: witnesses withhold, victims disengage, officials misread conditions, and false narratives spread.

So the likely difference between cities that get safer and those that do not is not merely resources, but **epistemic infrastructure**: data quality, institutional learning, cross-agency translation, public legitimacy, and the presence of local actors who can turn tacit knowledge into coordinated response. Decades-long safety gains occur when this infrastructure compounds. Failures occur when cities remain trapped in bad explanatory loops—mistaking symptoms for causes, optimizing metrics instead of understanding mechanisms, or suppressing inconvenient criticism.

A testable implication is that leading indicators of long-run safety should include not just crime rates, but measures of epistemic performance: reporting fidelity, clearance quality, inter-agency learning, neighborhood trust, speed of adaptation, and the durability of successful practices across administrations. In short: cities get safer when they learn better about violence than violence can adapt.
