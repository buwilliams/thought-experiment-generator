## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Subjective experience exists because “a point of view” was historically smuggled into our ontology as a solution to a prior explanatory problem: how a knowledge-creating system can contain and use *error-sensitive, self-locating representations* of the world and of itself. What we now call “phenomenal consciousness” may be an inherited conceptual wrapper around that older functional-epistemic need.

Here is the origin-story collision:

Before the modern “hard problem,” the practical problem was not “why qualia?” but “how can a system act under conditions of uncertainty, from somewhere, with limited access, and correct itself?” Any organism or agent must distinguish not just the world, but **the world-as-available-to-me-now**. That introduces perspective: centeredness, salience, valence, uncertainty, and bounded access. A system that creates knowledge cannot merely store facts; it must generate conjectures, detect error, and revise. For that, it needs internally usable distinctions between appearance and reality, signal and noise, self and environment, current model and world.

So the conjecture is that subjective experience is not an extra metaphysical ingredient added to computation. Rather, it names the internally accessible side of a system organized to solve the problem of **self-locating error correction**.

What did this replace? Likely older pictures in which cognition was treated as passive registration or mirroring. Once agency, control, and learning became central, a purely “view from nowhere” account broke down. The system required a structurally local interface: not reality in itself, but reality compressed into actionable, revisable form relative to the system’s goals, embodiment, and history. “What it is like” may be what that interface feels like from within.

What inherited assumptions went unexamined? Chiefly this: that once we have explained discrimination, report, integration, and control, there remains a further thing — raw felt presence — needing separate explanation. But that residue may be an artifact of inherited dualisms: appearance vs reality, inner vs outer, mechanism vs mind. We may be reifying the inevitable perspectival character of knowledge in finite agents into a mysterious ontological add-on.

What follows if this is true?

1. **Experience is expected wherever there is knowledge-bearing self-modeling under bounded perspective**, not necessarily in every information process.  
2. The key explanatory target is not “why redness feels red,” but why some systems must organize information as *for-this-system* in order to correct error and act.  
3. The mystery persists when we ask the wrong historical question. Consciousness looks inexplicable if treated as an ornamental surplus. It looks less mysterious if treated as the evolved structure by which a fallible agent lives inside its own model.

So the illuminating claim is: subjective experience may exist because epistemic locality had to exist first. A world containing knowers required points of view; “what it is like” is what a point of view is, from the inside.
