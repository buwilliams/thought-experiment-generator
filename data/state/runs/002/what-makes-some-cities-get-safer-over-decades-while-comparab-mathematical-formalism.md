## Conjecture

Conjecture: long-run safety is governed less by the level of anti-crime inputs than by the city’s position in a dynamical state space defined by a few coupled variables: trust/legitimacy, informal norm enforcement, youth opportunity, spatial concentration of disorder, and institutional learning capacity. Cities that get safer are those that cross one or more structural thresholds where the dominant feedback loops change sign—from reinforcing violence to reinforcing order.

Mathematically, think of urban safety as a trajectory \(x_{t+1}=F(x_t,u_t,\epsilon_t)\), where \(x_t\) is a vector of latent stocks, not just crime rates. Crime itself is an output variable, not the state. Comparable cities can begin with similar observed crime but differ in hidden coordinates: one may have higher civic trust or lower gang-network connectivity. Then the same policy input \(u_t\) produces different phase transitions.

The key invariants are not particular programs but structural constraints:

- violence propagates through contact networks;
- legitimacy affects whether information flows to institutions;
- concentrated disorder raises the local reproduction rate of crime;
- institutional memory determines whether successful interventions persist.

A city gets safer over decades when it reduces the effective reproduction number of serious violence below 1 across enough sub-networks for long enough that the system reconfigures. This can happen via multiple transformations: improving clearance rates, redesigning public space, stabilizing housing, increasing youth employment, or raising police legitimacy. These are different coordinates, but mathematically they are equivalent if they weaken reinforcing loops and strengthen balancing loops.

So the illuminating claim is: “safer cities” are not best explained by single causes but by basin shifts. There exist attractors—high-violence and low-violence regimes—and decade-scale divergence between comparable cities occurs when one city crosses a threshold that makes safety self-stabilizing while the other remains in a regime where shocks regenerate disorder.

This perspective also implies why copying policies often fails. A policy is not a universal cause; it is a transformation on a specific state. If two cities have different network topology or legitimacy levels, the same intervention maps one into a safer basin and leaves the other unchanged.

What follows is a testable program: measure not just crime counts but the latent variables and feedback strengths; identify early-warning indicators of regime change (declining retaliation chains, rising witness cooperation, reduced spatial clustering, persistence of gains after shocks). The deepest leverage is any intervention that changes the Jacobian of the system—altering how variables affect each other—rather than merely suppressing incidents temporarily.

So my conjecture is: cities become safer over decades when they discover and stabilize a structural configuration in which cooperation, legitimacy, and opportunity damp violence faster than violence can reproduce itself. The decisive difference is not effort but system topology.
