**Conjecture:**  
If we push *physical determinism* to its limit, free will does not disappear; instead, it changes category. Here is the collision at the edge:

- **At the deterministic extreme:** every particle trajectory is entailed by prior states and laws. If free will means “uncaused choice” or “a break in the causal order,” it goes to zero. There is no room for that kind of freedom.
- **At the opposite extreme:** if actions were purely random, produced by indeterministic noise, free will also goes to zero. Randomness is not agency; it is merely unpredictability.

So both limits kill the same mistaken picture: free will is neither miraculous independence from cause nor roulette-wheel spontaneity. The boundary reveals that the usual debate is misframed.

What remains between these failed extremes is **agency as explanatory causation**. A person is not just a physical object being pushed around; a person is a system containing models, counterfactuals, preferences, criticisms, and the ability to act for reasons. Those informational structures are physically instantiated, but they are not explanatorily reducible to microphysics in the sense relevant to choice. To explain why someone raised their hand, “because certain neurons fired” is causally true but structurally shallow. “Because they wanted to ask a question” captures the controlling variable in the system.

Thus the relationship is this:

1. **Physical determinism constrains** what can happen.
2. **Causation is layered:** microphysical causes implement higher-level causes.
3. **Free will is the capacity of a knowledge-creating system to let reasons, criticism, and anticipations govern action.**

Under this view, determinism does not negate free will unless one assumes that only bottom-level causes are real causes. But that assumption fails at the boundary, because it would erase all higher-level explanation — including biology, computation, and thought. If software can causally organize hardware behavior without violating physics, then reasons can causally organize bodily behavior without violating determinism.

So the edge case teaches this: **the true opponent of free will is not determinism but bypass** — any condition in which a person’s representational and critical capacities do not control action. Coercion, compulsion, manipulation, and ignorance reduce freedom because they sever the link between agency and action. Determinism alone does not.

Therefore: **free will is compatible with physical determinism when freedom is understood as rational self-causation within, not outside, the causal order.** The hidden structure revealed at the limit is that the debate turns on ontology: whether persons and reasons are admitted as real causal patterns, or dismissed in favor of an impoverished physics-only description.
