## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The Riemann hypothesis is hard because its key variable is not a missing computation but a missing *compression principle* that collapses an enormous space of arithmetical patterns into one necessity-generating explanation.

Construct the scenario this way: suppose RH is true, and suppose we already possess every local fact we could want — billions of verified zeros on the critical line, deep results about primes, random-matrix analogies, explicit formulas, trace formulas, equivalent statements. Assume the data is real and abundant. What remains missing? Not evidence. Not language. Not even candidate tools in the ordinary sense. What remains missing is the specific explanatory structure that makes the critical line unavoidable.

Follow that logic. If RH were easy in the way a search problem is easy, then accumulating constraints, reformulations, and computational confirmations should steadily force the solution. But that has not happened. So the obstacle is structural: each known perspective narrows the space, yet not enough to make the decisive step inevitable. The system has many feedback loops — analytic number theory, spectral theory, algebraic geometry analogies, probability heuristics — but they do not yet couple into a single rigid framework. We have many partial compressions, no final one.

This illuminates why brute-force or LLM-style generation is unlikely to solve RH merely by traversing possibility space. The difficulty is not that the space of proofs is astronomically large, though it is. The deeper problem is that most candidate explanations are too *soft*: they can mimic surface regularities without explaining why zeros must lie exactly where they do. A true solution must be “hard to vary” in Deutsch’s sense: alter one piece and the whole architecture should fail. Current approaches often produce plausibility, analogy, or local control, but not inevitability.

So RH is hard for the same reason fundamental theory-building is hard: the target is a hidden invariant of the whole system. The primes and zeta zeros form a tightly coupled informational structure, but we do not yet know the right ontology in which that structure becomes simple. In one ontology RH looks like an analytic inequality; in another, a spectral statement; in another, a statement about error terms in prime distribution. Its hardness may consist precisely in the fact that these are shadows of one object we do not yet conceptually possess.

What follows is a practical implication: progress is less likely to come from deeper search inside existing formulations than from discovering a representation in which the critical line is not a remarkable accident but a structural fixed point. RH is hard because we are still searching among descriptions, not yet operating from the right one.
