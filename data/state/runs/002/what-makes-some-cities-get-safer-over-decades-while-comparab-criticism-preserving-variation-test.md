By contrast, the load-bearing parts are those whose variation causes contradiction or loss of reach across the criticism set: e.g., if enforcement rises but legitimacy collapses, crime reporting falls and disorder relocates; if public order improves but case clearance remains weak, deterrence decays; if violence interruption exists without feedback from actual incident patterns, the intervention loses contact with the phenomenon.

These are mutually constraining achievements. Remove one central element and the rest lose force.

Operationally, this perspective suggests a stronger research program than correlating inputs with outcomes. Take a candidate explanation for a city’s safety improvement and **systematically vary each central part** while asking whether it still explains the observed durability, broad-based decline, neighborhood spread, and resilience to shocks. If the explanation survives replacing “legitimate policing” with “aggressive policing,” or “high clearance” with “high arrest volume,” or “community trust” with “public compliance,” then those parts were not load-bearingly specified. But if such substitutions break explanatory success, those are genuine structural constraints.

The main illumination is this: long-run urban safety is best explained as a **hard-to-vary institutional equilibrium**, not a policy checklist. Cities differ not just in what they do, but in whether their safety-producing parts are bound together tightly enough that error is exposed quickly and corrected before disorder compounds. That is why superficially similar cities diverge over decades.
