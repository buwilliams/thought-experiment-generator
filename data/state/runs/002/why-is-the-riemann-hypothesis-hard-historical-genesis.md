## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The Riemann Hypothesis is hard partly because it is a *survivor problem*: it was not created as a direct attempt to explain primes, but inherited from earlier analytic machinery designed for extending functions and controlling sums. As a result, the modern statement packages a deep problem about prime structure inside a framework whose assumptions came from something else. We are trying to extract an explanation from a tool-shaped object.

Origin matters here. The zeta function did not begin as “the thing whose zeros explain primes.” Euler used related series to encode arithmetic multiplicatively; Riemann then extended this into complex analysis, where continuation and functional equations became natural moves because analysis already had those moves available. The hypothesis arose in that inherited setting: once zeta had been analytically continued, its zeros became salient. But that salience may reflect the strengths of the analytic framework, not necessarily the most explanatory ontology for primes.

So what did this replace? It replaced more direct but cruder arithmetic confrontation with primes by an indirect representation: instead of asking how primes are distributed in their own terms, we study singularities and zeros of a complex function. This was enormously fruitful. But it may also have hidden a constraint: we now treat “understand primes” as “understand where zeros lie.” That equivalence is technically powerful, yet it may be explanatorily lopsided.

What was inherited without examination? At least this: that the right kind of understanding is analytic localization in the complex plane. The critical line becomes the arena because the inherited machinery makes it the arena. But the real mystery may be structural: why do multiplicative facts about integers organize themselves so rigidly under this transform? In other words, RH is hard not just because the zeros are difficult to control, but because the problem’s current form is downstream of a historical translation whose success obscures its contingency.

What follows from this perspective is not that analysis is mistaken, but that hardness may come from a mismatch between *representation* and *explanation*. We possess superb techniques for manipulating the zeta function, yet perhaps no comparably good explanation of why primes should induce exactly this spectral-looking order. The problem resists proof because we are still largely operating inside the inherited language of the translation, improving bounds and analogies, while lacking the deeper account of what zeta is *for* in the ontology of arithmetic.

So the illuminating claim is: **RH is hard because it sits at the intersection of two explanatory regimes—arithmetic and analysis—and was historically formulated in the language of the latter before the bridge itself was understood.** A proof may require not more force within existing tools, but a new explanation of why this analytic encoding is so unreasonably exact. That would change the problem from “where are the zeros?” to “why must arithmetic generate this zero-geometry at all?”
