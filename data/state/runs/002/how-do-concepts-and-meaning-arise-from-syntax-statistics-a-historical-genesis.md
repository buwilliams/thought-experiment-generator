If we ask how this problem came to exist, we see a sequence of replacements. Formal logic treated thought as rule-governed syntax; this solved the problem of rigor, but not the problem of relevance or aboutness. Statistical learning replaced brittle hand-coded rules by solving adaptability and scale; it showed that useful linguistic behavior can emerge from pattern sensitivity. Pattern-recognition systems then inherited the prestige of both: from logic, compositional structure; from statistics, empirical success. But they also inherited an unexamined constraint: that meaning is something to be extracted from correlations, as if concept-formation were a passive summary of regularities.

That historical path suggests a different conjecture: **concepts do not arise from syntax and statistics alone; rather, syntax and statistics become meaningful only within a prior problem-solving system that already treats some distinctions as worth preserving, correcting, and using.** Meaning is not what appears when enough patterns accumulate. It appears when a system can *use representations to guide error-correction about something beyond the representation itself*.

What follows is that pattern recognition is necessary but not sufficient. Statistics can compress regularities; syntax can stabilize transformations over those regularities. But concepts require a further layer: a structure of criticism, counterfactual sensitivity, and selective retention. A concept is not merely a cluster in data. It is a conjectural explanatory handle that survives because it helps solve problems across varying contexts.

This reframes “arise from” into “become embedded within.” Syntax and statistics supply the raw materials for representation; they do not by themselves generate aboutness. Aboutness emerges when patterns are recruited into a system organized around predictions, interventions, and correction of mistakes. In systems terms, meaning is not a stock of associations but a feedback achievement: representations are iteratively reshaped by whether they continue to coordinate successful engagement with the world or with other thinkers.

So the hidden assumption exposed by the origin story is reductionism: the idea that meaning must be assembled from meaningless units. The alternative conjecture is that meaning enters when a representational system becomes part of an error-correcting, problem-solving loop. Concepts are therefore less like statistical residues and more like evolved tools — inherited, revisable, and meaningful insofar as they participate in the ongoing correction of error.
