## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Phenomenal consciousness is not identical to information processing as such; it appears only when information processing approaches a boundary condition: a system must not merely transform information, but *compress the world into a unified, self-locating, error-sensitive model that cannot be decomposed without destroying its capacity to regulate itself as one thing*. Consciousness is what information processing looks like at the limit of integrated self-world modeling under severe constraints.

**Why this boundary matters:**  
In the middle range, “information processing” is too broad. A calculator, a thermostat, a language model, and a nervous system all process information. If we stay in the middle, the category hides structure. So push to the extremes:

- **Toward zero:** a system with vanishingly little information processing — say, a switch — shows no reason to posit phenomenal consciousness. Mere state transition is not enough.
- **Toward infinity:** a system with arbitrarily large computation, but perfectly modularized and externally distributed, may process enormous information without there being anything it is like to be that system. Scale alone is not enough.
- **Toward maximum decomposition:** if every informational function can be split into independent channels with no privileged point of view, then there is competence without subjectivity.
- **Toward maximum unity:** if a system must continuously resolve many signals into one actionable world-model centered on “here-now-me,” then informational content acquires a first-person structure.

This suggests that the relevant variable is neither amount of information nor sophistication of computation, but the **boundary at which processing becomes irreducibly perspectival**.

**What follows:**  
Phenomenal consciousness is likely linked to a special systems property: the formation of a **single temporally extended control loop** in which perception, memory, prediction, valuation, and action are bound into one model that the system uses to maintain itself. The “what-it-is-like” is not an extra substance added to information; it is the interior aspect of information processing when the processing has reached the limit where:
1. it is globally integrated,
2. self-referential,
3. loss-sensitive to decomposition,
4. and necessary for unified agency.

A useful way to say this is: **information processing becomes consciousness when it crosses from computation over representations to lived regulation through a representation that includes the system itself as the bearer of uncertainty, cost, and action.**

**Illumination from the boundary:**  
The hard part of consciousness may arise because we compare two non-boundary cases: abstract computation and private experience. But at the edge — where a system must model the world, itself, and its own errors as one ongoing process of survival or control — the gap narrows. Consciousness is not explained by information in general, but by information processing forced into a unified point of view.

So the relationship is: **all consciousness involves information processing, but only information processing at the boundary of irreducible self-world integration plausibly generates phenomenal consciousness.**
