## Conjecture

Conjecture: Subjective experience appears wherever a system contains an internal boundary at which information is not merely processed, but becomes inescapably self-relevant under conditions of finite access and finite control. At the limit, “what it is like” is what reality looks like from inside a knowledge-creating bottleneck.

Push the problem to the edge. At one extreme, imagine a system with no interior point of view at all: perfect transparency, perfect access to all causes, no distinction between self and world, no uncertainty, no need to model anything because nothing is hidden. Such a system would not seem to need experience. There would be no appearance, only total structure. “What it is like” vanishes at the limit of complete openness.

At the other extreme, imagine pure chaos: a system with no persistent self-model, no memory, no stable boundary, no controlled action, no compression of inputs into usable knowledge. Again, experience seems to vanish — not because there is too much access, but because there is no organized subject for anything to be like. There is no enduring locus of relevance.

So subjective experience does not arise at either limit: neither at total omniscience nor total disintegration. It appears in the middle region defined by a very specific structure: a bounded system that must act despite ignorance, using internally generated models that matter to its continued existence.

What follows is that experience is not an extra ingredient added to computation or matter. It is the inside of a certain epistemic situation. A system has subjective experience when:
1. it maintains a self/world distinction,
2. it cannot access its own total implementation directly,
3. it must compress reality into usable internal models,
4. those models feed back into action and error-correction,
5. some states are differentially consequential for the persistence of the system.

At that boundary, information is no longer neutral. It is valenced, perspectival, felt. Pain, for example, is not just damage-signaling; it is information rendered urgent from within a control architecture that cannot stand outside itself. Experience is what knowledge feels like when it is locally embodied, incomplete, and action-guiding.

This suggests an illuminating inversion of the usual question. Instead of asking, “How does matter produce experience?” ask: “What structural condition makes information appear from a point of view?” The answer may be: whenever a system is forced to know the world through a narrow, self-protective interface, there is something it is like to be that system.

So the conjecture is: consciousness is not a mysterious surplus over function, but the phenomenological boundary condition of finite, model-based agency. The hidden structure revealed at the edge is that subjectivity is what reality becomes when it is filtered through a system that must survive without ever seeing the whole.
