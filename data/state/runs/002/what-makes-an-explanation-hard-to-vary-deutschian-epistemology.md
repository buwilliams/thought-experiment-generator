**Conjecture:**  
An explanation has “all load-bearing parts” not because its words are rare or because the search space is large, but because it embodies a tightly constrained network of *counterfactual dependencies* among abstract objects. What distinguishes it from a freely swappable account is not surface compression but *error sensitivity*: if you vary one component, the explanatory reach or coherence breaks.

From the Deutschian perspective, the key unit is not the sentence but the *explanatory structure*. Einstein’s train-and-lightning example is not powerful because those particular narrative details were chosen from an astronomically large word space. Its power comes from the fact that the objects and relations instantiate a hard-to-vary abstract pattern: observers in relative motion, equal light speed, simultaneity judgments, and the resulting revision of time. Swap “train” for “spaceship,” and little changes. Swap “same speed of light for all observers” for “light speed depends on source motion,” and the explanation collapses. So the load-bearing property resides at the level of abstract constraints, not lexical realization.

This suggests a structural criterion:

1. **Necessity of components:** Each major element participates in deriving the conclusion, not merely illustrating it.  
2. **Counterfactual fragility:** Small conceptual alterations produce contradiction, loss of reach, or reversion to rival theories.  
3. **Mutual constraint:** Parts do not merely accumulate; they narrow one another.  
4. **Generativity:** The explanation solves more than the immediate problem and connects to further phenomena.  
5. **Hard-to-vary core with easy-to-vary surface:** The representation can change, but the underlying dependency graph cannot change much without damage.

So the relevant search problem for LLMs is not over word sequences but over candidate *dependency structures*. A bad explanation has many detachable ornaments: examples, metaphors, and assertions that can be swapped without consequence. A good one has a high ratio of necessity to decoration. Its parts are load-bearing when they are indispensable nodes in a criticism-resistant abstract structure.

What follows is that an architecture for knowledge creation should not primarily reward fluency or even local coherence. It should search for structures that survive variation tests: remove a part, replace a relation, alter an assumption, and see whether the account still explains what it claimed to explain. The more an explanation’s success depends on a non-arbitrary set of interlocking constraints, the closer it is to genuine explanatory knowledge.

So the illuminating collision here is this: the real boundary between “mere remix” and “knowledge creation” is whether the system can generate and criticize candidate abstract structures for *load-bearing necessity*. If it can, then new knowledge is in principle possible even when all surface elements are old.
