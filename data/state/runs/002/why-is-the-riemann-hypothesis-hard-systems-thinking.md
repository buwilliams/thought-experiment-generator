The hardness comes from the feedback structure: progress in one subsystem generates suggestive signals in another, but not decisive constraints. Computations reinforce belief without yielding mechanism. Random matrix analogies predict behavior without explaining why the zeta function must obey it. Explicit formulas connect zeros and primes, but in a way that transmits complexity both directions rather than dissipating it.

So the system exhibits **high interdependence without a dominant control point**. That is a hallmark of hard problems. Many local advances are possible, yet the whole remains stuck because no available representation serves as a strong enough coordinating structure to collapse the space of possible explanations.

1. the critical line is not an observed regularity but a system invariant, and

In systems terms, a proof would probably come from **reparameterizing the system** so that the current mystery becomes a conservation law, symmetry consequence, or fixed-point property.

So: RH is hard because our current mathematical system around it is rich in evidence but poor in closure. We have many reinforcing hints, but no architecture that converts them into an explanatory attractor. The problem is not merely finding one more fact; it is finding the representation that reorganizes the entire feedback structure.
