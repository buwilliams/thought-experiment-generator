## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A genuine explanation is historically and structurally connected to a prior problem; a sophisticated redescription is not. Redescription preserves the phenomenon while changing the vocabulary. Explanation shows *why this phenomenon, rather than some nearby alternative, had to arise given a problem situation and a generative structure*.

Applied through the lens of origins, the key question becomes: **what problem was the purported explanation invented to solve?** Many things we call explanations were introduced to repair dissatisfaction with older descriptions — to compress data, unify cases, satisfy aesthetic standards, or protect a theory from criticism. Those inheritances often go unexamined. As a result, an account can look deep because it is conceptually elaborate while merely restating what needed explaining in terms inherited from earlier frameworks.

This suggests a test. A genuine explanation does at least three things:

1. **Recovers the problem background.** It identifies the prior contrast class: what alternatives were live, what obstacle demanded explanation, and what earlier accounts failed to do.
2. **Exhibits a generative mechanism or constraint structure.** It does not just rename the regularity; it shows the system of relations from which the regularity follows.
3. **Supports counterfactual criticism.** It tells us what would be different if the underlying structure were different, and therefore what observations or cases would challenge it.

By contrast, redescription typically arises when a concept has detached from the problem that gave it meaning. It inherits assumptions from an earlier explanatory project — often hidden metaphors, taxonomies, or boundary choices — and reuses them as if they were discoveries about reality. For example, saying a trait exists “because it is adaptive” can be explanatory only if it situates the trait in a historical selection problem with specific constraints and rival outcomes. Otherwise it is just a polished restatement: the trait persists because it is the sort of trait that persists.

What this perspective illuminates is that the distinction is not mainly about complexity, formality, or predictive success in isolation. It is about **whether the account increases knowledge by solving the problem that produced the explanatory demand**, rather than merely stabilizing language around the phenomenon. Explanation is genealogical and structural: it links the phenomenon to the conditions of its production. Redescription is classificatory and retrospective: it fits the phenomenon into a conceptual scheme.

So the hidden assumption exposed by origin-analysis is this: we often treat explanation as if it were a property of sentences. But explanation is better understood as a move in the history of problem-solving. A statement counts as explanatory when it *replaces* an inadequate predecessor by revealing a deeper structure and surviving criticism. Without that lineage, sophistication can masquerade as understanding.
