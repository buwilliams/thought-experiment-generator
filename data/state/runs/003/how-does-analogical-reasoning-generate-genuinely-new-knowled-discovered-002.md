## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it is not treated as a passive transfer of content from one domain to another, but as a **person-level act of model construction and criticism** in which an agent uses one structure to reorganize another. The novelty does not lie in “copying” known relations across a gap; it lies in the agent’s creation of a new explanatory frame that can be tested, revised, and made to solve problems the original domains did not already solve.

From the free-will-as-framing lens, the key mistake is to force analogical reasoning into a mechanistic model of causation: as if one idea simply triggers another by associative law, and therefore nothing genuinely new appears. That treats thought like external pushing. But analogical reasoning, at the level relevant to knowledge, is better understood as **agency attribution**: a system internally represents candidate similarities, evaluates them against purposes, notices mismatches, and corrects them. The important question is not whether the process is lawful—of course it is—but whether the system is acting through **internal modeling, deliberation, and error-correction**.

On this view, analogy produces new knowledge because it proposes a **higher-order correspondence** that was not previously explicit in either source or target. When Maxwell used mechanical analogies in thinking about electromagnetism, or when Darwin drew on artificial selection to formulate natural selection, the result was not mere importation. The analogy highlighted structural relations, but the thinker had to decide which relations were deep and which were superficial, where the analogy broke, and what new explanatory object should replace the borrowed scaffold. The output is a new conjecture about reality, not a relocated old claim.

What follows is that novelty in analogy comes from **selective reinterpretation under criticism**. An analogy says, in effect: “Treat this target as if it were organized like that source.” This reframing changes what counts as a problem, what variables matter, and what predictions become thinkable. In systems terms, analogy is a leverage point on the conceptual structure of inquiry: it alters feedback loops of attention and error-detection. Once the target is reorganized, previously invisible regularities can be seen and new tests can be designed.

So the illuminated distinction is this:  
- **Mechanistic transfer picture:** analogy moves preexisting content; no real novelty.  
- **Agency-centered picture:** analogy is an active epistemic intervention that builds a new model, exposes it to criticism, and thereby creates knowledge not contained in either domain alone.

Therefore, analogical reasoning is knowledge-generating not because it escapes causation, but because lawful cognitive systems can still be genuine agents: they can propose explanatory restructurings, detect failure, and refine them. The newness lies in the **constructed mapping and its consequences**, not in any mysterious break with law.
