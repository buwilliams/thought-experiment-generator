## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it is pushed to the limit where the source and target domains cease to be mere “similar cases” and instead reveal a shared **abstract constraint structure**. What is new is not the borrowed content, but the discovery of a higher-order explanatory object that neither domain made explicit on its own.

At the ordinary middle, analogy looks like transfer: “this is like that, so perhaps what holds there holds here.” That can seem epistemically thin. But push to the boundary cases.

At one extreme, if two domains are **identical in all relevant structure**, analogy adds nothing; it is just redescription. At the other extreme, if they share **no structure at all**, analogy is empty metaphor. So the productive region cannot lie in mere resemblance. It lies at the edge: where two domains are different enough that transfer is nontrivial, yet similar enough that a mapping can survive criticism.

What survives there? Not surface features, but a relational pattern: conservation law, feedback loop, symmetry, optimization pressure, information bottleneck, self-reference, etc. The analogy works only if it conjectures that this pattern is doing explanatory work in both domains. That conjecture is new knowledge if it exposes a generative mechanism or constraint that was previously unformulated.

So analogy is not fundamentally a pipeline carrying knowledge from source to target. It is a **compression operation** that strips away domain-specific detail until a common abstract object becomes visible. Once that object is identified, it can generate consequences in both directions, including predictions that were present in neither domain’s prior explicit knowledge. The novelty comes from ascending to the shared structure, not descending from one case to another.

Push further: in the limiting case, the best analogies abolish the privileged status of “source” and “target” altogether. Both become instances of a more general theory. Then what seemed like transfer is revealed as theory creation. Maxwell’s field analogies, Darwinian analogies to selection processes, computation analogies in cognition—these were fruitful not because they copied facts, but because they uncovered deeper structural invariants.

From a systems view, analogy creates knowledge by reorganizing the space of possible explanations. It introduces a new feedback loop between domains: anomalies in one criticize assumptions in the other; successful mappings stabilize a more general description. The leverage is high because a single structural insight can propagate across many cases.

Therefore: **analogical reasoning is knowledge-creating precisely when it is risky**—when it proposes a shared abstract mechanism that could fail in either domain. Its epistemic productivity comes from conjecturing hidden invariants at the boundary between similarity and difference. The new knowledge is the invariant itself, and the widened problem-space it makes thinkable.
