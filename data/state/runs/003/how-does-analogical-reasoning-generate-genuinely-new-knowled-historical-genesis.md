## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge not by “carrying over” content from one case to another, but by exposing a previously unnoticed *generative structure* shared between problems; what is new is the identification of a deeper explanatory pattern that neither domain had been formulated to reveal on its own.

From the origin-story perspective, the key question is: *what problem was analogy historically solving?* It arose because rule-based deduction and direct induction are too brittle. Humans repeatedly faced novel situations where no explicit law was available, yet some response was needed. Analogy replaced pure case-by-case memory with a way of reusing *problem-structure*. But it inherited an unexamined assumption from this practical origin: that analogy is mainly a psychological shortcut for transfer.

That inheritance distorts the question. If analogy is just transfer, then of course it cannot produce novelty; it would merely relocate old knowledge. But that framing mistakes the function analogy evolved to serve. Its real role is not transport but *re-description*. It lets us conjecture that two superficially different domains are governed by similar relations, constraints, or feedback loops. Once that conjecture is criticized and developed, we often discover implications that were not explicitly known in either source domain.

So the new knowledge does not come from the source domain “containing” the answer in advance. It comes from constructing an abstract object — a candidate mapping of relations — and then interrogating it. The analogy “heat flows like water” did not merely transfer knowledge about rivers into thermodynamics; it helped isolate variables, conservation relations, and flow concepts that could then be mathematized and corrected. The analogy was false in detail yet fruitful because it pointed to a hidden structure worth formulating.

What follows is that the creativity of analogy depends less on similarity than on **selective mismatch**. If the mapping were perfect, nothing new would appear. If it were arbitrary, nothing would survive criticism. New knowledge arises in the tension: enough correspondence to generate explanatory conjectures, enough disanalogy to force revision. The breakdown points are especially productive, because they reveal which inherited assumptions from the source domain do *not* belong in the target.

Systemically, analogy is a mechanism for changing the representational structure of a problem. It introduces new variables, possible causal links, and new tests. Its leverage is not at the level of conclusions but at the level of *what can be thought and criticized*.

So the hidden assumption to discard is: “analogy is valuable when it preserves knowledge.” Better: **analogy is valuable when it creates a new space of explanations by conjecturing deeper structure than either domain had explicitly captured before.** That is how it can genuinely generate new knowledge.
