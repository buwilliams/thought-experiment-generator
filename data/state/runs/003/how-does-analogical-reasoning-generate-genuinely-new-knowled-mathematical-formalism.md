## Conjecture

Conjecture: analogical reasoning generates new knowledge when it is not treated as a content-transfer operation but as a structure-preserving map between domains that exposes previously unarticulated invariants, missing variables, and admissible transformations.

Mathematically, let a source domain be a structured system \(S = (X_S, R_S, T_S)\), with objects \(X_S\), relations \(R_S\), and transformations \(T_S\). Let a target domain be \(T = (X_T, R_T, T_T)\). An analogy is not merely the claim “this in \(S\) corresponds to that in \(T\),” but a partial mapping
\[
\phi: S \to T
\]
that preserves some relational structure. The key point is that \(\phi\) is usually incomplete. If it preserves enough structure, then any invariant in \(S\) under the preserved relations/transforms becomes a candidate invariant in \(T\). That yields a conjecture about the target not previously explicit there.

So the novelty does not come from importing facts wholesale. It comes from the constraint that if \(\phi\) is a good analogy, then certain equations, symmetries, or dependency patterns must also hold in the target. Those consequences may be new because they were not previously formulated in the target vocabulary. The analogy creates a new problem: complete the mapping so that the preserved structure remains coherent.

More sharply: analogy is knowledge-generating when it induces a commuting diagram. If processes in the source correspond to processes in the target, then different paths of inference should lead to compatible results. Failure of commutation is informative: it reveals either that the analogy breaks, that the target lacks a needed variable, or that the target’s ontology was too coarse. In each case, criticism of the mapping produces new knowledge. One learns either a new truth about the target or a new limitation on possible truth.

This suggests a non-negotiable constraint: genuinely fruitful analogies are neither arbitrary similarities nor mere redescriptions. They are mappings that preserve enough deep structure to generate testable surplus consequences. In category-theoretic language, the analogy acts like a functor preserving relevant morphisms; in model-theoretic language, it transports satisfiable constraints. Novelty arises from what the transported structure forces, not from resemblance itself.

Thus analogical reasoning is generative because structure is richer than its original interpretation. Once abstracted, a relational pattern can have consequences in any domain instantiating it. New knowledge appears when the target is recognized as an instance of a more general mathematical form, so that truths proved or discovered at the structural level become newly visible there.

In short: analogy generates new knowledge by revealing that two domains are constrained by the same abstract structure, and structure always contains implications not exhausted by any one of its prior applications.
