## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
At the boundary, **understanding is the ability to compress without losing generative power**, and **explanation is the transfer of that compression into another mind in a form it can expand correctly**.

Push the problem to the extremes.

At one limit, imagine **zero compression**: a complete raw record of everything. This is maximal detail and minimal understanding. A perfect transcript of reality does not explain anything; it merely repeats it. If explanation were just information quantity, the largest dump would be the best explanation. It plainly is not. So explanation requires reduction.

At the opposite limit, imagine **maximum compression**: a slogan, equation, or label so terse that it no longer lets you reconstruct the relevant phenomena. Then compression has become empty. “It happens because of the system” compresses, but explains little. So compression alone is not understanding either.

The structure appears at the edge between these failures. Good compression does not merely shorten; it **captures the rule that generates the many from the few**. A scientific law, a proof, or a good model is valuable because it compresses observations into a compact form that can reproduce, predict, or transform them. In this sense, understanding is not the possession of many facts but the possession of a **productive encoding**.

Now push further. If a compressed model predicts known cases but breaks at the boundary conditions, then the compression was only local. Real understanding survives contact with extremes: zero, infinity, singular cases, failure cases. Boundary cases test whether the model contains the causal or structural principle, rather than a convenient summary of regularities in the middle. Explanations that fail at the edges reveal where they were only pattern-matching.

This suggests a distinction:

- **Compression** = reduction of description length.
- **Understanding** = compression that preserves the ability to derive, vary, and criticize.
- **Explanation** = a compression expressed relative to a listener’s existing knowledge so that they can recover that derivational power.

So explanation is not the same object as understanding. A formula may embody deep understanding for an expert and almost none for a novice. The difference is not in truth but in **decompressibility by the recipient**. Explanation is audience-relative because minds have different priors, languages, and error-correcting resources.

What follows is that the best explanation is not the shortest one, nor the longest one, but the one at the **minimum sufficient compression** for a given purpose and audience: as concise as possible while still enabling the hearer to regenerate consequences, answer counterfactuals, and detect errors.

Therefore: **compression is necessary for understanding but not sufficient; understanding is compression with retained generativity; explanation is communicable generative compression.** The boundary cases show why: raw totality explains nothing, and pure brevity explains nothing. Explanation lives in the narrow zone where less says more because it contains the structure that can produce the rest.
