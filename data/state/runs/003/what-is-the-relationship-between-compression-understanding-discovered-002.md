## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Compression, understanding, and explanation are related but non-identical achievements, and they come apart precisely when we confuse a **mechanistic summary of behavior** with a **person-level account of why something happens**.

Compression is the ability to encode many observations in fewer bits. Explanation is a theory about the **generative structure** that produces those observations. Understanding is the capacity of an agent to **use** such explanatory structure for criticism, transfer, error-correction, and novel problem-solving. So compression can be evidence of explanation, but it is not explanation itself; and explanation can exist without maximal compression if it captures the right causal or rational structure.

From the free-will framing lens, the key mistake is to treat all successful representation as if it were the same kind of achievement. A physical law compresses trajectories. But when we ask why a person acted, believed, or changed their mind, a mere compressed behavioral regularity is often the wrong format. Person-level agency involves internal modeling, deliberation, reasons, and self-correction. A highly compressed predictor of human actions may omit exactly the features that make the action intelligible as an *action* rather than a motion.

Thus there are at least two kinds of explanatory gain:

1. **Lawful compression**: finding compact regularities in externally observed data.
2. **Agential understanding**: identifying the internal error-correcting models and reason-responsive processes by which a system produces its outputs.

These can overlap, but neither reduces cleanly to the other. A thermostat is explainable largely by control structure. A scientist is not understood merely by compressing her published outputs; one must explain how she forms conjectures, criticizes errors, and updates models. The latter may even require *more* representational richness, not less, because understanding agency means tracking counterfactual sensitivities to reasons, not just input-output regularities.

So the relationship is hierarchical but not reductive: **compression is a test of regularity; explanation is a claim about structure; understanding is an agent’s possession of usable explanatory structure**. Good explanations often compress because reality has discoverable structure. But the deepest explanatory progress may come not from shorter descriptions alone, but from discovering the right *level* of description. When the subject matter is agency, rationality, or choice, the right level may be irreducibly person-level.

What follows is that “the best explanation is the shortest one” is false in general. The best explanation is the one that captures the relevant generating structure at the appropriate ontological level. Compression without the right level gives opaque prediction. Explanation at the right level gives intelligibility. Understanding is what a system has when it can participate in that intelligibility—using it to anticipate, revise, and create.
