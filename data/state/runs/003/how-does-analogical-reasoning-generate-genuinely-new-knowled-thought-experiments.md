## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when the shared structure it posits between two domains implies a consequence in the target domain that was not previously formulated there, and that consequence survives criticism. What is transferred is not knowledge itself but a **generative constraint**.

Construct the hypothetical scenario as cleanly as possible. Suppose we have two domains, A and B. In A, we already know a network of relations: not just isolated facts, but a structure with explanatory power. In B, we know many local facts but lack an integrating theory. Now assume there is a real structural correspondence between A and B: certain relations in A map onto relations in B. Follow this strictly.

If the analogy were merely a relabeling, it would add nothing. But if the structure in A is richer than what has yet been articulated in B, then mapping it onto B generates **candidate consequences** in B that no one had previously noticed. These consequences were not stored explicitly in prior knowledge of B; they become thinkable only because the analogy reorganizes B into a new problem-situation.

The key variable, then, is not similarity in appearance but **structure-preserving mapping with unexploited implications**.

What follows? First, analogical reasoning is productive because explanation is productive. A good explanation contains more than the observations that prompted it; it constrains what else could be true. If B is newly seen through the explanatory structure of A, then B inherits not old facts but a new space of conjectures. Some of these will be false. That is expected. The analogy creates knowledge only when criticism eliminates bad transfers and retains a surviving explanatory relation.

Second, the novelty is real even though no “new information” was conjured from nowhere. Knowledge growth never requires creation ex nihilo; it requires a new conjecture that was not previously available in the problem-space. Analogy does exactly this by exposing hidden possibilities. It changes what can be asked, predicted, and tested.

Third, the limit becomes clear. Analogies fail when they transfer superficial resemblance instead of deep constraint. Then they produce decorative redescriptions, not knowledge. So the generative power of analogy depends on whether the mapped structure is causally or explanatorily load-bearing.

What is illuminated is that analogical reasoning is neither deduction nor mere association. It is a **conjecture engine**. It proposes that the same abstract organization may govern two different domains, and from that proposal derive novel consequences. Those consequences are genuinely new knowledge only after criticism, because the analogy itself does not justify them; it merely creates them as candidates.

So: analogy generates new knowledge by importing **structure that has implications**, not by copying content. Its epistemic product is a newly constructed explanatory pathway in the target domain.
