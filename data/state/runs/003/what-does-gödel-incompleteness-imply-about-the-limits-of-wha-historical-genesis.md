## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Gödel incompleteness shows not merely that a formal system has “limits,” but that **self-knowledge in formal systems is constrained by the historical function formal systems were designed to serve**: they were invented to stabilize and mechanize proof, not to fully model the open-ended activity of criticism by which mathematics actually grows. What appears as a deep internal barrier is partly an inherited design constraint.

Origin matters here. Formal systems arose to solve prior problems: ambiguity in proof, dependence on intuition, and the desire for certainty through explicit rules. They replaced informal mathematical practice with syntactic discipline. But in doing so, they inherited an unexamined assumption: that the essential content of mathematical knowledge could be captured as derivability within a fixed rule-set.

Gödel’s result collides with that assumption. By arithmetizing syntax, Gödel showed that once a system is rich enough to represent its own proof procedures, it can formulate statements that indirectly say, in effect, “this statement is not provable here.” If the system is consistent, such statements are true but unprovable within it. So the system cannot, from wholly within its own formal resources, complete its own account of mathematical truth.

What follows is stronger than the slogan “formal systems can’t know everything about themselves.” The more precise lesson is: **a fixed formal system cannot exhaustively represent the correctness of the very process that generated it**. It can encode its own rules, but not close the loop of self-validation without remainder. Any attempt to do so produces new problems at the edge — undecidable statements, unprovable consistency claims, or stronger systems required for assessment.

This suggests that incompleteness is not an accidental defect of formalization but a structural consequence of what formalization is: a freezing of some prior critical practice into explicit rules. Once frozen, the system can execute those rules, and even describe them, but it cannot fully replace the meta-level activity that chose, interprets, revises, and extends them. That activity is what the formal system was originally meant to discipline, not duplicate.

So Gödel implies that self-knowledge has a hierarchy: a system may know many facts about its own behavior, but not achieve total epistemic closure about itself. Full self-understanding requires stepping outside the current formal frame, proposing stronger principles, and exposing those in turn to criticism. In that sense, incompleteness reveals that mathematical knowledge is not fundamentally the possession of a static system, but the product of an open-ended process of conjecture and criticism that no fixed formalism can finish inheriting.
