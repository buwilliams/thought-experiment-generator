## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Compression is evidence of understanding only when the compressed representation is *load-bearing*—that is, when its parts are mutually constraining and changing one part would force revisions elsewhere or reduce what the account can explain. Explanation is the form of compression that preserves and exposes those constraints.

A mere shorter description is not yet understanding. We can compress by omitting, by encoding arbitrarily, or by summarizing surface regularities. Those forms of compression may save space, but they do not necessarily reveal why the thing is the way it is. If details are interchangeable without consequence, the result is a narrative shell: compact, perhaps useful for recall, but not explanatory.

Understanding appears when compression captures a structure whose elements do real work. In such a structure, details are not decorative. They are tied together by necessity-like relations: alter one assumption, mechanism, or parameter, and predictions shift, derivations break, or compensating changes become required. This is what makes an explanation powerful. It does not merely say less; it says a smaller set of things from which more follows.

So the relationship is not:

- compression = understanding = explanation,

but rather:

- **compression is a candidate sign of understanding**,  
- **understanding is grasp of a constrained generative structure**, and  
- **explanation is the articulation of that structure in a way that shows what depends on what**.

This perspective also clarifies why some highly compressed accounts fail. A slogan, analogy, or elegant equation may be concise yet shallow if its components are replaceable with no loss. Conversely, some explanations are not maximally short because extra detail is load-bearing: removing it would hide boundary conditions, failure modes, or the mechanism that produces the phenomenon. Good explanation therefore seeks not minimum length but **minimum arbitrary detail**.

What follows is a practical test: to evaluate whether compression reflects understanding, perturb it. Ask of each part: if this were changed, what else must change? What phenomena would no longer be explained? What new errors would appear? If little follows, the compression is weak. If much follows, it is explanatory.

This also illuminates why deep theories often feel both simple and rigid. Their simplicity comes from reducing many appearances to a few principles; their rigidity comes from the fact that those principles are heavily load-bearing. They are hard to vary while preserving explanatory reach.

So: **compression contributes to understanding only when it is constrained compression, and explanation is the medium that makes those constraints visible.** The essence of understanding is not brevity but non-arbitrariness.
