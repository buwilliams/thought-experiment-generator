## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Mathematics seems “unreasonably effective” in physics because both are ways of tracking the same thing at different limits: **stable, compressible structure**. At the boundary, where structure disappears or where total arbitrariness is allowed, mathematics stops being effective. Its success is therefore not miraculous but selective.

Push the problem to extremes.

At one limit, imagine a universe with **maximal irregularity**: no repeatable symmetries, no conserved quantities, no scale separation, no stable objects, no reusable regularities. In such a world, neither physics nor mathematics would get traction. There would be no laws to formulate, only brute happenings. Mathematics would not “fail” there; rather, **description itself would fail**, because prediction and explanation require compressible patterns.

At the opposite limit, imagine a universe of **pure regularity**: perfect symmetry, exact invariance, total repeatability. Here mathematics would not merely describe reality well — reality would almost collapse into mathematical structure. Physical law would look inevitable because everything observable would be generated by a small set of transformations and constraints. The more reality approaches this limit, the more mathematical description appears astonishingly powerful.

Our actual universe lies between these extremes, but crucially, **closer to the structured side in the domains where knowledge can grow**. Physics is possible only where there are stable feedback loops: persistence of objects, reproducible experiments, conservation laws, locality, and symmetries. Mathematics is precisely the discipline of exploring the consequences of such constraints abstractly. So when physics finds that reality contains symmetry groups, differential structure, Hilbert spaces, variational principles, or topology, mathematics already has a language for them — because these are not arbitrary inventions but forms of compressible organization.

The hidden middle becomes visible at the edge: mathematics is effective **not for all realities**, but for realities capable of supporting knowers. A universe that permits measurement, memory, and experiment must have enough regularity to stabilize information-bearing systems. That same regularity is what mathematics latches onto. The effectiveness is therefore conditioned by an anthropic-epistemic filter: we can only notice being in a world where abstract structure is deep enough to be discovered and reused.

What follows is a sharper claim: mathematics is most effective **where physical systems are governed by invariants under transformation**. Where invariants weaken — turbulence, strongly nonlinear many-body behavior, biological contingency, historical path dependence — mathematical description becomes harder, more statistical, more approximate. This is exactly what the conjecture predicts.

So the phrase “unreasonable effectiveness” mislocates the mystery. The real question is: **why does reality contain so much stable, lawlike, reusable structure?** Mathematics is effective because it is the study of what survives variation. Physics succeeds where nature also has things that survive variation. Their fit is not accidental; it is a convergence at the boundary between chaos and order.
