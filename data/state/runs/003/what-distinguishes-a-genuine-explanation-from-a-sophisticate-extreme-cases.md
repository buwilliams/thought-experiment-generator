## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A genuine explanation is distinguished from a sophisticated redescription by what happens at the boundaries: when pushed toward extreme cases, counterfactual variations, or recursive compression, the explanation continues to generate structure, while the redescription collapses into restatement.

Here is the logic of the collision.

In ordinary cases, explanation and redescription can look similar because both may fit the phenomenon closely. So push the problem to the limit. Imagine a “perfect” redescription: it captures every observed detail, names every pattern, embeds the phenomenon in elegant language, perhaps even formal language. At the limit, this becomes isomorphic to the phenomenon itself — a mirror of it. But a mirror does not explain what it reflects. If the account approaches one-to-one correspondence with the thing described, then whatever explanatory power it seemed to have vanishes into duplication.

Now push the other side. Take a genuine explanation and test it under variation: alter initial conditions, remove components, scale the phenomenon up or down, ask what would happen if a key variable were absent, inverted, or driven to an extreme. A genuine explanation survives this stress not by preserving every surface feature, but by identifying a generative structure that determines which features are robust, which are accidental, and how the phenomenon would differ under changed circumstances.

So the boundary test reveals a difference in kind:

- **Redescription tracks actuality.**
- **Explanation tracks possibility space.**

A redescription answers: *What happened, in richer or more abstract language?*  
An explanation answers: *Why this happened rather than nearby alternatives, and what would happen if the conditions were different?*

This suggests a sharper criterion: explanation is not primarily about compression, elegance, or depth of vocabulary, but about **counterfactual reach**. If an account cannot discriminate between the actual phenomenon and closely related non-actual phenomena, it is only redescribing. It may be sophisticated, mathematically dense, or statistically accurate, but it has not yet exposed the mechanism, principle, or constraint that produces the phenomenon.

At the limit of zero variation — when we look only at the single observed case — redescription can masquerade as explanation. At the limit of infinite variation — across many interventions, scales, and hypothetical perturbations — the masquerade fails. The hidden structure becomes visible only at the edge.

So the conjecture is: **the distinction between explanation and redescription is not linguistic but structural. Explanation is an account that remains informative under systematic deformation of the phenomenon; redescription loses content as soon as one departs from the actual case.**

This also implies a practical test: to evaluate an explanation, do not ask only whether it fits. Ask what breaks it. If changing the case merely changes the wording, it was redescription. If changing the case reveals a principled map of dependencies, constraints, and outcomes, it was explanation.
