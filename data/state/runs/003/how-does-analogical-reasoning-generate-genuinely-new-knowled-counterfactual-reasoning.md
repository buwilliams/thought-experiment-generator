## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge not by “moving content” from a source to a target, but by **exposing a shared generative structure that was not previously explicit in either description**. What is new is the *abstract problem-structure* made visible by the analogy, plus the novel criticisms, predictions, and redesigns that follow from treating that structure as real.

Apply the variation test:

1. **Remove the assumption that analogy is transfer.**  
If analogy were merely transfer, then nothing genuinely new could appear: the target would only inherit what was already known in the source. But in practice, strong analogies often produce surprises. Why? Because the act of mapping is not passive copying; it is a conjecture that *these relations matter more than the local details*. That conjecture can reveal unnoticed constraints in the target, or unnoticed generality in the source.

2. **Invert the assumption: suppose analogy works by preserving differences rather than similarities.**  
Then analogy becomes useless as a guide. But what actually happens is subtler: fruitful analogy depends on a disciplined selectivity about which differences are irrelevant. New knowledge emerges when that selectivity is criticized and refined. So the load-bearing element is not resemblance as such, but a **criticism-worthy claim of structural invariance**.

3. **Replace the assumption that knowledge comes from observation with its opposite: knowledge comes from explanatory structure.**  
Then analogy’s creative role becomes clearer. An analogy proposes that two domains instantiate the same abstract explanatory pattern. Once that pattern is conjectured, one can derive consequences in the target that were never observed there before. This is not importation of facts; it is the creation of a new explanatory object at a higher level of abstraction.

What breaks under variation is the view that novelty must be either empirical discovery or logical deduction from prior premises. Analogy is neither. It is a method for **constructing candidate abstractions**. What survives every variation is that analogy is productive only when it yields new error-correcting opportunities: predictions, re-descriptions, incompatibilities, or interventions.

So the key distinction is:

- **Bad analogy:** “A is like B, therefore what is true of B is true of A.”
- **Knowledge-creating analogy:** “If A and B share this deep structure, then consequences C, D, E should appear; if not, the analogy fails.”

In systems terms, analogy does not transfer surface properties between nodes; it identifies a common feedback structure across systems. That can reveal leverage points in the target that were invisible before. In epistemic terms, analogy creates new knowledge when it compresses two previously separate domains under a single explanatory schema that then survives criticism better than rival descriptions.

So the conjecture is: **Analogical reasoning generates new knowledge when it functions as a conjecture about abstract structure, whose testable consequences were not contained as explicit knowledge in either domain before the analogy was made.** The novelty lies in the new abstraction and in the criticism it enables.
