## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The question “why does subjective experience exist at all?” is partly a historical artifact of inheriting a split between **objective description** and **first-person appearance** that earlier theories created to solve other problems. Subjective experience did not arise, in our conceptual scheme, as a phenomenon in need of independent explanation; it became a “hard problem” only after we adopted explanatory frameworks that defined reality in third-person, structural, and public terms, and then treated what those frameworks left out as mysterious.

Origin story first: what problem was the modern concept of “subjective experience” solving? It helped distinguish **the world as represented** from **the world as it is**, especially in response to error, illusion, and epistemic limitation. Descartes and successors needed some secure starting point for knowledge; “what it is like” became the indubitable residue. Later, mechanistic science solved the problem of predicting and controlling physical systems by stripping away qualities, purposes, and appearances in favor of measurable structure. What was replaced was an older, less sharply divided picture in which experience, value, and world were not conceptually split in the same way. What was inherited without examination was the assumption that a complete explanation must be exhausted by third-person functional/physical description.

From that origin, the hard problem follows almost automatically. If a theory is built to explain only relational structure, causal role, and external behavior, then first-person presence will appear as an extra fact. But this may reveal less about consciousness than about the explanatory **filters** we chose. The hidden assumption is: *reality is fundamentally describable without perspective, and perspective must therefore be added back in.* That assumption was useful for physics; it does not follow that it is metaphysically complete.

So the conjecture is not merely that experience exists, but that **“experience as an ontological surplus” is a problem manufactured by a historically specific abstraction regime**. There is something it is like to be a system when that system contains knowledge-bearing models that distinguish self from world, error from success, and appearance from reality. Not because “qualia” get added to mechanism, but because the very conceptual machinery that makes representation possible also creates an internal point of view. A system that can be wrong about the world must, from within, have a way the world seems.

What follows: the deepest explanatory target may not be “how matter produces qualia,” but **how certain systems come to instantiate perspective-bearing knowledge**. On this view, subjective experience is not an accidental byproduct appended to objective reality; it is what reality looks like from the inside of a system organized around fallible world-modeling. The real task is then genealogical and structural: identify what forms of representation, self-maintenance, and error-correction make “seeming” unavoidable. The mystery shrinks once we stop treating third-person description as the unquestioned baseline of all existence.
