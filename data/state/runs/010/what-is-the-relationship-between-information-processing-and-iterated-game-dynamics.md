## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Phenomenal consciousness is not explained by information processing *per se* but by a specific **iterated self-modeling game** inside an agent: when informational processes repeatedly monitor, predict, correct, and negotiate with one another over time under conditions where future coordination matters, a stable internal “point of view” emerges. What we call phenomenal consciousness tracks this enduring regime of repeated internal interaction, not mere computation in a one-shot sense.

**Why this follows from the iterated-game perspective:**  
A one-shot game does not reward reputation, reciprocity, or long-horizon coordination. Likewise, a purely feedforward information-processing system can classify, optimize, or respond without needing an enduring subjective perspective. It can process information competently while remaining, in this framing, phenomenally empty. Nothing in one-pass transformation alone creates pressure for a unified “self” that must live with the consequences of current processing in future cycles.

By contrast, in a system whose subsystems repeatedly interact, present outputs become future inputs. Perceptual, attentional, memory, valuation, and action-selection processes are locked into ongoing exchange. Under these conditions, there is structural pressure to create internal analogues of cooperation:  
- **Reputation:** subsystems must track which signals, predictions, and priorities have proven reliable over time.  
- **Reciprocity:** memory informs perception; perception updates memory; action depends on both and feeds back into future appraisal.  
- **Shadow of the future:** current processing must preserve coherence for later cycles, since the same organism must continue acting, learning, and integrating consequences.

This predicts that consciousness is associated with architectures where information processing is not merely abundant but **recurrently accountable to itself**. Phenomenality may be what it is like for information processing to occur in a system that cannot treat each computation as disposable because future internal interactions depend on maintaining a stable negotiated perspective.

**What this illuminates:**  
It explains why some sophisticated information processing may seem nonconscious: if processing is modular, opaque to later interaction, or effectively one-shot, there is little structural basis for a unified phenomenal field. It also explains why consciousness appears closely linked to temporal integration, global availability, working memory, and reportability: these are signatures of repeated play among subsystems.

**Further prediction:**  
If you shorten the effective time horizon of internal processing—through fragmentation, disrupted recurrence, memory collapse, or inability of subsystems to track one another’s outputs—phenomenal consciousness should degrade. Likewise, if “defection” among subsystems becomes unobservable—e.g. signals cannot be globally checked or integrated—cooperation collapses into disunity, and conscious experience should become thinner, unstable, or absent.

So the relationship is: **information processing is the substrate, but phenomenal consciousness arises when that processing is organized as a repeated internal game whose stable solution is an enduring, integrated first-person perspective.**
