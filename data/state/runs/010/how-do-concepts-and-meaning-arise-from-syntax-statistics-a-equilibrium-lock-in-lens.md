## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Concepts and meaning arise when syntactic and statistical systems solve a **public-goods coordination problem** inside a learner or community: many local pattern matches are individually useful, but genuinely semantic structure is a **non-excludable, globally shared internal good** that no single token, feature, or interaction can “pay for” on its own. Meaning appears when the system crosses from myopic prediction to maintaining shared latent structures that amortize interpretation across many cases.

Here is the collision of the lens with the problem:

Pure syntax and next-step statistics can produce competent local behavior without yet producing robust concepts. Why? Because concepts are expensive organizational achievements. They compress many experiences into reusable abstractions, support counterfactuals, and stabilize interpretation across contexts. But the benefit of building such abstractions is **diffuse**: once a latent concept exists, many downstream inferences can use it. No single training example uniquely justifies creating it; each example “hopes” other examples will carry enough weight. Structurally, this resembles underprovision of a public good.

So a system can get stuck in a **stable no-provision equilibrium**: it keeps exploiting surface regularities, correlations, and narrow pattern recognition because those yield immediate predictive payoff, while deeper semantic variables remain underbuilt. This can look like “mere statistics without meaning,” but the lens suggests that the issue is often not confusion or lack of intelligence. It is **structural underinvestment in shared abstraction**.

What follows is that meaning is not a mysterious extra ingredient added after syntax. It is what emerges when the system provisions internal public goods:  
- **shared latent variables** that many representations can use,  
- **compositional abstractions** that generalize across contexts,  
- **error-correcting interpretive norms** that align use across time and agents.

In an individual mind, these are globally available conceptual resources. In a linguistic community, meanings are public coordination equilibria sustained by mutual prediction, correction, and reuse. In both cases, semantics is less like attaching labels to patterns and more like building a common infrastructure for inference.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine a learner rewarded only for one-step local prediction in highly redundant data. It can succeed via shallow cues indefinitely. Now add tasks where many distant contexts depend on the same hidden cause, and where success requires transferring structure across them. Suddenly, investing in a shared latent concept becomes rational: the abstraction pays for itself because its benefits are aggregated. Meaning grows where the system architecture or environment **subsidizes abstraction formation**.

So the key illumination is: **concepts are not just detected patterns; they are provisioned shared structures**. Syntax and statistics are the raw mechanism, but semantics appears when learning dynamics and social or architectural feedback loops make it advantageous to construct nonlocal, reusable, publicly available representations. Where those loops are weak, systems remain fluent yet semantically shallow.
