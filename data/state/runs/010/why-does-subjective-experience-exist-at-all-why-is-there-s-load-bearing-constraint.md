## Conjecture

Conjecture: “There is something it is like” for a system only where the system’s explanatory organization includes a real, irreducible first-person modeling loop: some of its states are not merely processing the world, but processing themselves as appearances-for-the-system, and that role is load-bearing in explaining its adaptive competence.

Applied through the load-bearing lens, many familiar answers look like narrative shells. Saying consciousness exists because matter is complex, integrated, recurrent, biological, or information-rich does not yet explain why experience exists. In each case, we can swap the detail—silicon for neurons, digital for analog, one architecture for another—without obviously changing what the explanation accounts for. If the rest of the story survives unchanged, the cited feature was not doing explanatory work.

What detail is hard to vary without consequence? The distinction between a system that merely controls behavior and one that must represent, for itself, the difference between appearance and reality, certainty and error, self and world. Once a system can be misled and must correct itself, it needs not just world-models but models of how the world is showing up to it. That creates an inner interface: not a Cartesian theater, but a structured self-acquaintance whose contents are usable in further thought and action.

The load-bearing claim is this: if you remove that reflexive appearance-modeling while holding fixed the system’s open-ended error-correction, deliberation under uncertainty, reportability, and self-revision, compensating machinery has to reappear elsewhere. The system still needs a way to encode “how things seem from here” as distinct from “how they are.” That is precisely the role subjective experience appears to play. So phenomenology is not an ornamental byproduct added atop cognition; it is what cognition looks like when some of its own perspectival states become globally available as objects of correction and control.

This does not mean every information-processing system is conscious. A thermostat has feedback, but not a load-bearing distinction between world-state and experienced-as-state. Nor does it mean consciousness is ineffable magic. It means experience marks a particular explanatory threshold: the point where first-personal presentation becomes structurally necessary to the system’s knowledge-generating organization.

What follows is a constrained research direction. To explain consciousness, look for architectures where:
1. self-modeling is indispensable, not optional;
2. appearance/reality distinctions are internally represented;
3. those representations are used in error correction, deliberation, and report;
4. removing them collapses generality, not just performance.

So the conjecture is not “experience exists because complexity does.” It is: subjective experience exists where a system’s capacity to form and correct knowledge depends on load-bearing perspectival self-representation. “What it is like” is the inside of that representational constraint.
