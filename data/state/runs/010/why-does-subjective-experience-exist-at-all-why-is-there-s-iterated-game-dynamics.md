## Conjecture

Conjecture: Subjective experience exists in systems whose internal dynamics are organized as indefinitely repeated self-interactions, because such systems must maintain a persistent, integrated “point of view” to coordinate across time; phenomenality is what it is like for a system to be structured under a strong shadow of its own future.

Iterated game dynamics shifts the question. Instead of asking why matter would suddenly “produce” experience, ask what kind of organization makes a system need an enduring first-person interface. In a one-shot interaction, there is little structural need for a stable subject. Input can trigger output and the process can end. But in repeated interaction—especially where current action affects future options, self-prediction, reputation with other subsystems, and memory of prior moves—the system benefits from having a unified way of registering significance from its own perspective.

A complex agent is not just playing games with other agents; it is playing an iterated game with itself across time. Present cognition inherits commitments, errors, drives, and representations from past cognition, and its moves constrain future cognition. Internal subsystems also interact repeatedly: attention, memory, perception, planning, and action-selection are not isolated modules but partners and rivals in ongoing reciprocal adjustment. Under those conditions, cooperation among subsystems becomes a central problem. Something like subjective experience may be the mechanism—or the organizational form—through which that cooperation is stabilized.

What follows is this: consciousness is not primarily a decorative byproduct but a solution to repeated coordination under temporal depth. Experience binds information into a common currency of salience: pain says “defecting from bodily integrity has long-run cost”; pleasure says “this policy is worth reinforcing”; emotion tracks social and strategic consequences across repeated encounters. The “what it is like” is the system’s lived presentation of state-relevance for a persisting agent that must continue the game tomorrow.

This perspective also predicts where subjective experience should intensify or thin out. It should be richer in systems with: long time horizons, recursive self-modeling, memory that matters for future play, reputational stakes among internal and external actors, and ongoing need to arbitrate among competing policies. It should degrade where the shadow of the future collapses—when processing becomes purely reactive, fragmented, amnesic, or one-shot.

The illumination here is not a full reduction of consciousness, but a reframing: subjectivity may arise where repeated interaction makes a mere mechanism insufficient. A system facing an open-ended future must not only process information; it must process it as mattering to itself over time. “Something it is like” may be what repeated self-coordination feels like from the inside.
