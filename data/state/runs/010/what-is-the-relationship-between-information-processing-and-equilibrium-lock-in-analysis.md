## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The hard part of relating information processing to phenomenal consciousness is not primarily a missing mechanism, but an **equilibrium lock-in between explanatory frameworks**: third-person information-processing theories and first-person phenomenal reports each optimize within their own evidential game, yet neither can unilaterally move to a shared standard without losing explanatory power in its home domain. What looks like a metaphysical gap may partly be a **structural trap between mutually stable but incompatible epistemic equilibria**.

Here is the collision of perspective and problem:

- **Information-processing models** explain cognition by functional organization, causal roles, integration, prediction, attention, reportability, global availability, etc. These are powerful because they generate public tests and intersubjective criticism.
- **Phenomenal consciousness** is picked out by what experience feels like from the inside. Its key datum is first-person presence, not merely behavior or computation.

Each side faces a unilateral-defection problem.

If the phenomenologist gives up first-person authority and accepts only publicly measurable information processing, the target phenomenon may be dissolved rather than explained: the account preserves function while potentially discarding felt experience.  
If the computational theorist grants first-person givenness as irreducible evidence, they risk weakening the norms of public explanation and prediction that make information-processing science successful.

So both sides rationally remain in position:
- the functionalist says, “If there is a real difference, show it in processing structure or causal role,”
- the phenomenal realist says, “Any such structure leaves out what experience is like.”

This is a Nash-like lock-in: **no single camp can switch methods alone and do better by its own standards**.

What follows is important. The persistent stalemate does **not** by itself show that consciousness is non-physical, nor that it is “nothing but” computation. It suggests instead that the debate may be stabilized by a mismatch in what counts as explanatory success:
- for one side, success = causal/functional compression;
- for the other, success = preserving the datum of subjectivity.

On this view, information processing and phenomenal consciousness are related, but not straightforwardly by identity claim alone. Rather:

1. **Information processing may be necessary for the controllable, reportable, cognitively integrated forms of consciousness we can scientifically study.**
2. **Phenomenality names the aspect of those states that is not captured merely by adopting the third-person equilibrium of explanation.**
3. Therefore, the real leverage is not arguing harder within either equilibrium, but **changing the game**: developing bridge principles that preserve first-person constraints while remaining open to public criticism.

The illuminating claim is this: the explanatory gap may be partly a **coordination failure between epistemic access modes**, not just a gap in ontology. If so, progress will come from new structures of inquiry—methods that let first-person and third-person descriptions constrain each other—rather than from expecting either information processing alone or introspection alone to force a solution.
