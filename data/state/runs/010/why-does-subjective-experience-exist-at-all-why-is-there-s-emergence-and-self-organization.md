## Conjecture

Conjecture: Subjective experience exists when a system’s organization includes self-modeling feedback loops that make some internal states globally available as constraints on the system’s own ongoing regulation; “what it is like” is not a property of the parts, but an emergent feature of a certain interaction structure.

From this perspective, the mistake is to look for experience in neurons, molecules, or computation taken locally. If experience is emergent, then the explanatory target is the closed network of relations by which a system monitors, predicts, and modifies itself. A thermostat has feedback, but its organization is too thin: it regulates one variable with little internal integration. A conscious system, by contrast, may be one in which many subsystems continuously model both the world and the system’s own changing state, and where these models mutually constrain action, attention, memory, and further modeling.

The key structural claim is: when information about the system becomes part of the causal loop that determines what the system does next, a new system-level property appears. The system is no longer merely processing inputs; it is living through internally unified states that matter to its future behavior because the system treats them as its own condition. Subjectivity is the inside-view of such recursive organization.

This shifts the question from “Which component produces qualia?” to “What interaction structure yields an intrinsic point of view?” An intrinsic point of view would arise when the system’s boundaries are not imposed only by an external observer, but are enacted internally by persistent feedback: the system distinguishes self from non-self, integrates diverse signals into a coherent state-space, and uses that integrated state to guide further activity. “There is something it is like” because there is, within the system, a dynamically maintained difference between how things are for the system and how they are outside it.

What follows is that experience likely comes in degrees and kinds depending on organizational features: integration, recursion, temporal depth, bandwidth of self-modeling, and the richness of global constraint. This also suggests why experience seems irreducible: analyzing parts in isolation destroys the very relational pattern that constitutes the phenomenon, just as analyzing a whirlpool into water molecules misses the vortex.

This does not fully solve the hard problem; it does not deduce qualia from structure with certainty. But it illuminates why subjective experience might exist at all: not as an extra substance, nor as a mysterious add-on, but as what emerges when a system becomes organized such that its own states are internally unified, recursively accessible, and causally indispensable to its continued activity. On this view, consciousness is not hidden in the components. It is the lived aspect of a certain kind of self-sustaining system.
