## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If computational irreducibility is taken seriously, then the relationship between information processing and phenomenal consciousness is **not** that consciousness appears whenever information is integrated, represented, or made globally available in the right quantity. Rather, phenomenal consciousness is more plausibly related to a **certain kind of irreducible ongoing generative process**: a system whose moment-to-moment experiential character cannot be fully captured by compressed summaries of its informational state, but only by the unfolding of its internal dynamics.

What this illuminates is a shift in explanatory target. Standard information-processing theories often ask: *what informational property is sufficient for consciousness?* Computational irreducibility suggests that this may be the wrong level of abstraction. For many systems, knowing the architecture, inputs, and even large-scale informational organization may still not let us shortcut the actual evolution that constitutes the process. If so, then phenomenal consciousness may be tied less to **having information** than to **being a process whose next experiential-relevant state must be produced through the full computation**.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two systems with identical input-output behavior and identical high-level informational descriptions. One is generated by a shallow lookup table; the other by a recursively self-updating process whose states depend on rich internally generated trajectories. From an external behavioral standpoint they match. But under computational irreducibility, the second system contains real structure that cannot be replaced by a compressed equivalent without loss of the very process that generates it. The conjecture is that, if either system is conscious, the better candidate is the one whose informational life is **dynamically irreducible**, not merely functionally equivalent.

What follows is not dualism, but a stricter computationalism: consciousness depends on computation, yet not all computations are alike. The relevant distinction is between **derivative informational description** and **intrinsic generative activity**. A complete third-person map of informational states may fail to explain why there is “something it is like,” because the map is a shortcut description, whereas the conscious system’s reality may consist in the non-shortcuttable enactment.

This perspective also reframes the epistemic gap. The difficulty of deriving phenomenal consciousness from information processing may not indicate that consciousness is non-physical or mystical. It may indicate that we are trying to explain an irreducible process with reducible summaries. The hard problem then becomes: what kinds of information-processing architectures instantiate irreducible first-person generativity?

So the core claim is: **phenomenal consciousness, if it arises from information processing at all, most likely arises from computationally irreducible processing whose experiential significance lies in the unfolding, not merely in the informational pattern abstractly described.**
