## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The modern problem “How can free will exist if physical determinism is true?” is largely a historical artifact: it arose when older moral and theological concerns about *agency, responsibility, and authorship* were reformulated in the language of *mechanism and law*. In that translation, “free will” inherited assumptions that were never examined — especially the idea that freedom must mean exemption from causation.

Ask how the problem came to exist. “Free will” was not originally posed as a physics problem. It was a solution to earlier problems: how to distinguish action from mere happening, praise from blame, deliberation from compulsion, and a person from a passive vessel of fate or divine manipulation. Later, the rise of mechanistic science replaced a world of purposes and souls with a world of efficient causes. The old question of responsible action was then forced into a new frame: if nature is law-governed, where could freedom fit?

What this inherited, without examination, was a false contrast: either actions are *caused*, in which case they are unfree, or they are *uncaused*, in which case they are free. But that contrast is itself a relic of the transition from premodern moral psychology to modern physics. It assumes freedom is a kind of causal gap.

That assumption is likely the mistake. A person’s will is not a rival to causation; it is a particular kind of cause. Deliberation, criticism, imagination, and choice are not absences of determination but higher-order causal processes in which reasons, representations, and counterfactual thinking alter what happens next. The relevant question is not “Are human actions caused?” but “What kinds of causes are persons?”

From this perspective, physical determinism does not by itself threaten freedom. Determinism says that, given prior physical states and laws, later states follow. But it does not settle whether some physical systems — namely persons — contain explanatory structures best understood as *knowledge-bearing, error-correcting, reason-responsive agents*. If they do, then saying an action was physically caused does not compete with saying it was chosen for reasons. Those are different explanatory levels, inherited from different problem traditions.

So the real collision is not between free will and causation, but between two inherited conceptions of explanation: one from moral life, one from post-Galilean physics. The pseudo-problem emerges when one is allowed to erase the other.

**What follows:** free will should be reconstrued not as metaphysical independence from law, but as the capacity of a knowledge-creating system to model alternatives, evaluate reasons, and let those representations causally govern action. Under that conception, indeterminism is not automatically helpful, and determinism is not automatically fatal. The crucial issue is whether agency is real as an emergent explanatory structure, not whether it floats outside the causal order.
