## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The modern framing of the relationship between information processing and phenomenal consciousness is itself an inheritance from an earlier problem: how to explain intelligent behavior in mechanistic terms. As a result, “information processing” entered the debate already shaped to solve the problem of control, representation, and prediction — not the problem of felt experience. Therefore, the persistent explanatory gap is not merely an unsolved linkage between two things; it may be evidence that the dominant concept of information processing was never designed to reach phenomenal consciousness in the first place.

This origin story matters. “Information processing” rose to prominence as a replacement for behaviorism and simple stimulus-response models. It preserved mechanistic intelligibility while allowing internal states, computation, representation, and control. But in doing so, it inherited several unexamined assumptions: that cognition is fundamentally a matter of function, that what matters scientifically is causal role, and that a successful explanation of behavior is close to a successful explanation of mind.

Phenomenal consciousness, by contrast, became salient precisely where those assumptions failed. It names what is left over when function, discrimination, reportability, and behavioral control have been specified but the “what it is like” still seems untouched. So the historical sequence is revealing: information processing was built to explain performance; phenomenal consciousness was sharpened as a concept to mark what performance-based explanations omit.

What follows is not that information processing and consciousness are unrelated. Rather, their relationship is likely asymmetric. Information processing may be **necessary for access, report, integration, memory, and self-modeling** — all the machinery by which conscious states become usable in cognition. But from this genealogical perspective, it is doubtful that standard notions of information processing are **sufficient** to explain phenomenal consciousness, because sufficiency was never part of the concept’s original problem-solving role.

A thought experiment clarifies this: imagine a science of “circulation processing” developed to explain how cities move goods efficiently. It tracks routes, bottlenecks, timing, and distribution. It becomes very powerful. Then someone asks: where, in circulation processing, is the *value* of the goods being moved? The framework may correlate with value, condition access to value, and distribute what is valued — yet value is not the kind of thing the framework was built to characterize. One could force an identity claim, but if the fit remains strained, the wiser move is to question the inherited framework.

So the illuminating claim is this: the hard problem may persist not because consciousness is mystical, but because we keep asking a framework optimized for explaining **functional organization** to explain **subjectivity**. The real leverage point is conceptual, not merely empirical: we may need a successor to “information processing” — or a deeper account of information itself — that does not inherit the old assumption that explaining what a system does is nearly the same as explaining how it is experienced from within.
