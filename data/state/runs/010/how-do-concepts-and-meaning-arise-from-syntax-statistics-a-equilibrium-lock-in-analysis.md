## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Concepts and meaning arise not when syntax or statistics become “rich enough” in isolation, but when a pattern-recognizing system becomes locked into a stable coordination regime between internal representations, prediction, and successful action/interpretation. Meaning is what exists at the equilibrium between symbol patterns and the broader system of use in which those patterns reliably constrain expectations.

From an **Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis** perspective, the key issue is not “How does syntax magically turn into semantics?” but “What structural trap prevents purely syntactic or statistical systems from becoming meaningful on their own?” The trap is this: syntax can only optimize formal consistency; statistics can only optimize compression and prediction over observed distributions; pattern recognition can only optimize discrimination among inputs. None of these, acting alone, can unilaterally generate meaning, because meaning depends on coordinated alignment across multiple agents or subsystems: signs, interpreters, world-models, and practical consequences.

A thought experiment sharpens this. Imagine a system that perfectly predicts token sequences but has no coupling to any enduring use, correction, or action loop. It can continue improving next-token performance indefinitely. But no internal step lets it decide whether “tree” refers to trees, green things, useful climbing objects, or merely the statistical environments in which “tree” appears. Any one mapping is exchangeable so long as prediction remains preserved. This is an equilibrium lock-in: the system cannot escape underdetermination by better syntax or better statistics alone, because each local improvement is neutral with respect to reference.

What breaks the lock-in is not a mysterious semantic ingredient added from outside, but a **new coordination structure**: stable feedback loops linking representations to error correction across contexts. Meaning emerges when a representation participates in a regime where misalignment is punishable and alignment is self-reinforcing — for example, through action failure, communicative breakdown, perceptual updating, or social correction. At that point, concepts are not just clusters in data; they are equilibrium-stabilized invariants that survive criticism across many uses.

So the conjecture is: **concepts are equilibrium objects**. They are the relatively stable attractors formed when syntactic operations, statistical regularities, and pattern-recognition capacities become embedded in feedback structures that make some interpretations more corrigible, compressive, and coordinatively useful than others. Meaning is therefore not reducible to syntax, but neither is it separate from it. It is what syntax becomes when locked into a system where interpretation has consequences and can be iteratively corrected.

This illuminates why debates between “symbolic,” “statistical,” and “grounded” accounts often stalemate. Each describes part of the system, but the decisive explanatory step is structural: semantics appears when the system crosses from free-floating formal optimization into a constrained equilibrium of shared, corrigible use.
