## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The free-will dispute is partly a mistake of game horizon. When agency is modeled not as a one-shot event (“Was this act fully caused?”) but as an iterated process (“How does a chooser update across repeated decisions under anticipated future consequences?”), free will appears as a real strategic capacity that can exist even in a deterministic world. Physical determinism fixes the state-transition rules; it does not eliminate the agent-level dynamics created by repeated self-reference, reputation, and policy selection over time.

From the perspective of iterated game dynamics, a human agent is not merely selecting isolated acts but playing an extended game against environments, other agents, and future versions of itself. In repeated play, what matters is not whether an act has a cause—everything may be caused—but whether the system contains feedback loops by which current deliberation affects future behavior, expectations, and responses. “Free will” then names a structurally important capacity: the ability to act on policies that are sensitive to long-run consequences, including consequences for one’s own future dispositions and for others’ expectations.

This reframes causation. Causation is not the enemy of freedom; it is the mechanism that makes strategic agency possible. If choices did not causally shape later states—habits, trust, deterrence, self-command, reputation—then repeated-game cooperation with oneself or others could not emerge. A deterministic world can still contain higher-level causal regularities in which deliberation matters because deliberation is one of the causes. The relevant contrast is not “caused vs free,” but “one-shot impulse vs policy-bearing participation in an iterated system.”

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two worlds equally deterministic. In World A, agents make choices once, with no memory, no future interaction, and no reputational effects. In World B, agents remember, anticipate, punish, reward, and become more or less trustworthy over time. In A, “free will” is thin because no stable policy can matter beyond the instant. In B, a rich form of agency emerges: agents can sustain commitments, shape incentives, and govern present action by reference to future rounds. The difference is not metaphysical indeterminism but the presence of repeated interaction and informational feedback.

What follows is that many intuitions about freedom track the visibility of these iterative structures. We experience ourselves as more free when the shadow of the future is long, actions are legible, and self-binding policies can be maintained. We experience less freedom under myopia, obscured consequences, addiction, coercion, or fragmented memory—conditions that collapse intertemporal cooperation. So physical determinism does not by itself refute free will; what threatens free will is a breakdown in the iterative architecture that lets deliberation function as strategy across time.

So the collision yields this illumination: free will is best understood less as exemption from causation and more as a stable game-theoretic achievement within causation.
