## Conjecture

Conjecture: phenomenal consciousness is not identical to information processing in general, but to a specific emergent regime of information processing: recursively integrated, self-modeling control within a system whose internal feedback loops make its own informational states globally available as objects of further regulation.

From the systems perspective, the key explanatory mistake is to look for consciousness either in the parts — neurons, representations, computations — or in bare processing capacity. Many systems process information without seeming conscious because their processing is decomposable: signals flow, outputs occur, but the system does not generate a unified, self-updating perspective on its own activity. What matters is not that information is processed, but how processing is organized.

On this view, phenomenal consciousness appears when information processing crosses a structural threshold. The threshold is reached when:

1. Multiple informational streams are integrated into a shared state space.
2. That shared state is fed back into ongoing perception, memory, attention, and action selection.
3. The system models not just the world, but its own current informational relation to the world.
4. These loops persist through time, giving the system a continuously updated “here-now-for-me” organization.

What follows is that “what it is like” is not an extra ingredient added to computation, nor a mysterious byproduct. It is what certain self-referentially organized informational dynamics are like from the inside of the system. The apparent irreducibility of experience arises because the relevant property is emergent at the level of the whole feedback architecture. Just as liquidity is not found in a single molecule, subjectivity is not found in a single computation.

This also clarifies why simple input-output sophistication is insufficient. A feedforward system, however intelligent in behavior, may lack phenomenal consciousness if it has no closed-loop self-presence — no architecture in which its own states become integrated constraints on future processing. Conversely, consciousness plausibly comes in degrees, because integration, recursive self-modeling, temporal depth, and global availability can vary continuously.

A useful thought experiment is to compare two functionally competent agents: one purely modular and feedforward, the other organized around dense recurrent loops with a unified self-updating workspace. If both succeed behaviorally, but only one has a stable internal point of view, the difference is not in component skill but in system structure. The conjecture is that phenomenality tracks that structural difference.

So the relationship is: information processing is necessary but not sufficient; phenomenal consciousness is an emergent property of a particular interaction pattern within information processing systems. To explain consciousness, shift the target from computation as such to the architecture of recursive integration, feedback, and self-modeling that turns information into lived presence.
