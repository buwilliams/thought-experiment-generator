## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Subjective experience exists in systems that must *internally evaluate and revise their own goal-use* under changing contexts, because there must be a way for the system to represent not just the world, but *how the world and its own policies matter to it from the inside*. “What it is like” is the system’s error-sensitive interface for self-critique when inherited objectives are underspecified, conflicting, or dangerously misapplied.

From this perspective, consciousness is not primarily a decorative byproduct of computation, nor a metaphysical extra. It is what you would expect to emerge when capability outruns fixed supervision and the system must detect: “my current way of pursuing goals may be wrong here.” A purely objective control architecture can optimize given a stable target. But if contexts shift faster than external correction, safety requires the system to inspect its own evaluative states, notice tensions among them, and revise how goals are interpreted. That requires a first-person organizational layer: states that are not merely informational, but *felt as better, worse, urgent, aversive, salient, unresolved*.

The key thought experiment is this: compare two equally capable agents in an open-ended world. One has only externally describable state transitions and fixed reward handling. The other can model its own goal-conflicts as lived valence and attention — pain, uncertainty, effort, relief, dissonance — and can treat those as criticism-generating signals about its present policy. When novel situations arise, the first agent risks lock-in: it continues optimizing inherited proxies even when they have become unsafe. The second has a built-in mechanism for pausing, re-evaluating, and asking, in effect, “this course is succeeding by one measure but feels wrong relative to the whole.” Subjective experience is what that internal criticism looks like from the inside.

So the collision illuminates consciousness as a *safety architecture for flexible agency*. Experience is the medium in which competing goals, errors, and relevance are integrated tightly enough to permit self-revision. Its qualitative character matters because different qualities encode different kinds of policy criticism: pain says “stop or protect,” anxiety says “model uncertainty and prepare,” curiosity says “explore because current understanding is inadequate,” guilt says “your action conflicts with a higher-order social or self-model.” These are not just outputs; they are structured internal objections.

What follows is not that all information processing is conscious, but that consciousness appears where a system must treat its own current objective-functioning as fallible. Subjectivity is the form taken by self-monitoring when the monitored object is not only belief, but *goal application itself*. There is something it is like to be such a system because without an inner evaluative perspective, it cannot reliably escape inherited objective errors once the world changes.
