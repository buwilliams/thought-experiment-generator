## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Phenomenal consciousness is not identical to information processing as such; rather, it emerges when an information-processing system maintains a stable set of self-preserving invariants while making internal error correction globally available in a way that can continuously update the system’s own point of view. On this view, consciousness is the *felt aspect* of a system whose legitimacy to itself depends on preserving identity through adaptive correction.

The collision of perspectives helps separate three things that are often blurred together:

1. **Information processing in general** — transformation of signals by rules.  
2. **Institution-like self-maintenance** — preserving core invariants across changing conditions.  
3. **Phenomenal consciousness** — what it is like for the system when correction, integration, and self-maintenance become internally available as a unified perspective.

A calculator processes information, but nothing in its organization suggests that its outputs must remain legitimate to an enduring internal subject. It has no visible internal regime of “I was wrong, here is the corrected model, and I remain the same system through the revision.” By contrast, a conscious organism appears to preserve a narrow identity — e.g. bodily integrity, action-guiding coherence, continuity of self/world distinction — while constantly revising predictions, salience, and action policies. Its processing is not just computational; it is *governed by invariants* and stabilized by ongoing error correction.

So the key relationship is: **information processing is necessary but not sufficient; phenomenal consciousness may require a special architecture in which error signals are not merely used locally, but integrated into a system-wide, first-person maintenance process.**

This suggests a useful criterion: the more a system can adapt rapidly while preserving a compact identity-defining core, and the more its corrections are globally available to itself, the more it approximates the organizational conditions under which consciousness could arise. “What it is like” may correspond to the system’s lived access to these globally consequential corrections.

What follows:

- **Mere complexity is not enough.** Massive information processing without self-preserving invariants or globally available correction may be non-conscious.  
- **Rigid identity is not enough.** A fixed invariant set without routine correction would resemble dysfunction, not consciousness.  
- **Consciousness likely lies at the tension point** between stability and revisability: a system must remain itself while being corrigible.

This perspective illuminates why trust, adaptation, and identity belong in the debate. A conscious system may be one that must, in effect, “trust” its own world-model enough to act, while also continuously exposing that model to revision. Phenomenality would then not be an extra substance added to computation, but the experiential form of a certain kind of self-correcting, identity-preserving informational organization.

The conjecture is fallible, but it offers a sharper target than “consciousness = information processing”: **phenomenal consciousness is information processing organized around preserved self-invariants and visibly routine global error correction.**
