## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Subjective experience exists because any sufficiently capable cognitive system faces an **equilibrium lock-in** between distributed information processing and unified action selection. Once a system must integrate many competing internal models, goals, and error signals into decisions that cannot wait for global consensus, it becomes structurally advantageous — and then effectively unavoidable — to construct a **single privileged internal standpoint** from which the system treats information as “for me.” What it is like to be that system is the appearance, from the inside, of this lock-in solution.

The key claim is not merely that consciousness is useful, but that it may arise from a **coordination trap inside the system**. Imagine a mind as a society of semi-autonomous processes: perception, memory, valuation, prediction, language, motor planning. Each subsystem could in principle optimize its own local task. But no subsystem, acting alone, can solve the whole-system problem of **binding relevance, priority, and ownership** across all the others. If each process keeps its own format and authority, the result is fragmentation: no stable agent, only competing partial control.

A reform to pure decentralization may be globally attractive in some respects — cheaper, more parallel, less bottlenecked — but any one subsystem abandoning the shared, first-person coordination frame would lose influence and intelligibility relative to the rest. So the system gets stuck in an equilibrium where all major subsystems “report to” a common evaluative center. This center need not be a Cartesian soul; it is a structural role: the attractor that stabilizes cross-system coordination by converting distributed processing into **owned significance**.

From this perspective, subjective experience is not an optional decorative glow added to computation. It is what a coordination architecture feels like when the system must continuously resolve: *Which signals matter now? To whom do they matter? What counts as success, danger, pain, or action for this organism as a whole?* The “something it is like” is the internally generated format in which these questions become jointly actionable.

This illuminates why consciousness seems both unified and selective. Unity comes from equilibrium pressure toward a single action-relevant standpoint; selectivity comes from the impossibility of globally broadcasting everything. It also suggests why experience is perspectival rather than omniscient: the system is not representing reality from nowhere, but enforcing a bargain among subsystems under conditions of bounded time, conflict, and control.

So the deeper answer is: subjective experience exists where a system cannot remain merely a collection of local optimizers, yet no component can unilaterally impose global order except by participating in a stabilized first-person interface. Consciousness is the internal signature of that lock-in.
