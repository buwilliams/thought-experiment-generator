## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The apparent conflict between free will, causation, and physical determinism is partly a category error caused by explaining agency at the wrong level. If we shift from components to interaction structure, then free will is not the absence of causation, nor a miraculous break in physical law, but an **emergent system property** of organisms whose internal feedback loops model alternatives, evaluate consequences, and regulate action across time. Physical determinism, if true, constrains the substrate; it does not eliminate the higher-level causal organization that constitutes willing.

What matters is not whether neurons obey laws, but whether the whole system has the structure of **self-governance**. A thermostat is causal but not free because its control structure is too thin. A person, by contrast, is a layered control system: perception, memory, counterfactual modeling, social learning, norm representation, self-monitoring, and recursive revision of goals. In such a system, behavior is caused not merely by local pushes from prior microstates, but by the organized interaction of many subsystems in feedback with an environment. The relevant explanatory target is therefore not particle motion but the **agent-architecture**.

This reframes causation. Causation is not exhausted by bottom-up efficient pushes; in complex systems there is also causation by **constraint, control, and informational organization**. A plan can be causally real even if physically implemented, just as software is real despite running on hardware. Saying “the particles made me do it” is like saying “the silicon, not the program, caused the computation.” It misses the level at which the behavior is intelligible.

Under this view, determinism does not automatically negate free will. If an agent’s action flows from its own integrated evaluative structure—its reasons, learned models, commitments, and capacity to revise itself in light of criticism—then the action can be both determined and free in the sense that matters. The opposite of free will is not determinism but **heteronomy**: behavior bypassing or destabilizing the system’s self-regulating architecture, as in coercion, compulsion, manipulation, or severe fragmentation.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two physically deterministic systems: a falling rock and a reflective person choosing whether to apologize. Both are law-governed. But only one contains internal loops that represent possible futures, simulate social consequences, compare them to norms, and alter behavior accordingly. The difference is not in exemption from causation but in the presence of a causally potent emergent organization. That organization is what we call agency.

So the relationship is this: **physical determinism describes the lawful evolution of the substrate; causation links states across levels; free will names a special emergent causal regime in which a system can use representations and feedback to direct itself.** The paradox weakens once we stop asking whether agency escapes the system and ask instead what kind of system agency is.
