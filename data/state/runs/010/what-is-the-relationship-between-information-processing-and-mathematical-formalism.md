## Conjecture

Conjecture: phenomenal consciousness corresponds not to information processing in general, but to a special class of information-processing structures that maintain a unified, self-updating model of possible world-states under strict integration constraints.

To make the problem mathematical, define a system \(S\) with state space \(X\), input stream \(I_t\), internal update rule \(F\), and output rule \(G\). Any information-processing system computes transitions

x_{t+1} = F(x_t, i_t).

This by itself is too weak: thermostats, compilers, and cortical networks all satisfy it. So the relevant question is: what additional structural properties distinguish systems that are conscious?

A plausible invariant is not computation alone, but integrated model-maintenance. Let \(M_t\) be the system’s internal model of latent causes of its inputs, including a distinguished variable corresponding to itself as an embodied locus of interaction. Then consciousness arises when three conditions hold together:

1. **High internal integration**: the system’s state cannot be decomposed into nearly independent subsystems without major loss of predictive and control power. Formally, partitioning \(X\) into \(X_1 \times X_2\) produces a large drop in relevant mutual information or causal efficacy.

2. **Temporal coherence**: \(M_t\) is not a momentary encoding but a dynamically stable trajectory preserving information across time, so present content depends on counterfactual relations among past, present, and anticipated states.

3. **Self-locating closure**: the model includes the system as a variable within its own inferred world, allowing distinctions like “this state is happening here, to me, now.”

The conjecture is that phenomenal consciousness is the first-person aspect of such a structure. On this view, raw information processing is related to consciousness as arbitrary dynamics are related to geometry: necessary as a substrate, insufficient as an explanation. What matters is the organization of transformations on information, especially whether they generate a globally constrained state-space with a privileged point of view.

This reframes familiar debates. If two systems are functionally equivalent only at the input-output level but differ in internal integration topology, they may process the same information without sharing phenomenal character. Conversely, if consciousness supervenes on these structural invariants, substrate matters only insofar as it preserves them.

What is illuminated is a constraint: any adequate theory of consciousness must identify a mathematically specifiable property of information-processing architecture that is preserved across implementations and strong enough to distinguish conscious from nonconscious computation. If no such invariant exists, “information processing” is too coarse a category to explain phenomenology. If one does exist, the relationship is structural, not merely correlational: consciousness is what certain recursively integrated informational geometries are like from the inside.
