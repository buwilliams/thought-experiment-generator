A useful conjecture is therefore this: information processing relates to phenomenal consciousness only when it forms a tightly constrained self-referential model-selection system. In such a system, information is not merely transformed; it is organized so that:

1. the system constructs a temporally stable world-model,
2. locates events relative to a persistent point of view,
3. binds multiple modalities into a single action-guiding scene,

5. uses those representations in downstream control and report.

Each clause is load-bearing. Remove temporal stability, and experience loses continuity. Remove perspectival organization, and there is information without a “for whom.” Remove multimodal binding, and unified experience fragments. Remove self-representation, and there may be discrimination without seeming. Remove downstream use, and “experience” becomes explanatorily idle.

What follows is a sharper relationship: information processing is necessary but not sufficient for phenomenal consciousness. The sufficiency claim applies only to information processing with a specific internal dependency structure. Phenomenality is linked not to the amount of information, nor merely to complexity, but to whether the processing realizes an integrated explanatory package in which changing one feature alters the rest.

This also illuminates why simpler computational analogies often fail. A calculator processes information, but its details are not organized around a unified perspective-bearing self-model. Likewise, many machine learning systems classify richly, yet if their internal organization can be radically altered while preserving input-output behavior, that suggests the alleged basis of consciousness was not load-bearing. By contrast, if an architecture’s perspectival unity, self-modeling, and global coordination are mutually constraining, then the explanation becomes tighter and more candidate-worthy.

So the relationship is not “information processing produces consciousness” in the abstract. It is: phenomenal consciousness is whatever information-processing organization has enough non-interchangeable internal structure to make the characteristic features of experience come out together. Consciousness is where informational description stops being a loose summary and becomes a tightly interlocked explanatory system.
