## Conjecture

Conjecture: If computational irreducibility is real, then the apparent conflict between free will and physical determinism is partly misplaced: even in a fully deterministic universe, some choices can be causally determined yet not compressibly predictable except by enacting the very deliberative process that produces them. In that case, “free will” is illuminated not as freedom from causation, but as the irreducible operation of a reasoning system within causation.

Here is the collision of ideas.

Physical determinism says that, given the total prior state and laws, only one future is possible. That seems to threaten free will if freedom is taken to mean “could have done otherwise under exactly identical conditions.” But computational irreducibility changes what determinism means in practice and explanation. A deterministic rule need not yield shortcut prediction. The future may be fixed, yet inaccessible except through the system’s own unfolding.

Thought experiment: imagine a deterministic agent whose decision procedure is so computationally irreducible that no external observer can predict its output faster than the agent itself reaches it. To know what the agent will choose, one must effectively run the agent’s entire deliberation. Then the agent’s reasoning is not a dispensable surface on top of causation; it is the causal process. The choice is determined, but determined by the internal evaluation, modeling, and criticism constituting agency.

What follows is a reframing of causation. On this view, causation is not only microphysical pushing from below. Higher-level computational organization — beliefs, preferences, arguments, self-models — is causally real because it is the minimal generative rule for the behavior at the scale where choice exists. If no shorter account predicts the act than “the person thought about reasons and then chose,” then that level is not merely convenient language; it captures genuine structure.

So the strongest illumination is this: determinism does not by itself erase agency, because irreducible systems can be both law-governed and explanation-demanding at the level of their own computations. Free will, in the compatibilist sense, becomes the capacity of such a system to generate action through internally structured deliberation rather than external coercion. “Could have done otherwise” is then redescribed counterfactually: if the agent’s reasons, interpretations, or internal computations had differed, the action would have differed — even if, holding the entire universe fixed, it would not.

This does not prove libertarian free will. Computational irreducibility does not create metaphysical gaps in causation. It does, however, undercut the inference from “determined” to “not genuinely chosen.” In irreducible systems, the only way the determined future comes into being is through the agent’s own computation. Thus free will and determinism are related not as opposites, but as levels: determinism describes the lawful unfolding; free will describes the irreducible deliberative form that unfolding takes in certain systems.
