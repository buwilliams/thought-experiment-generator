## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Concepts and meaning arise when a syntactic, statistical pattern-recognizer becomes able to form **stable self-referential loops** between its internal representations, its predictions about use, and its own success at reusing those representations across contexts. Meaning is not added to syntax from outside; it emerges when syntax is organized into a hierarchy that, at sufficient expressive power, loops back on itself.

Here is the collision of perspectives:

At the base level, a system only detects regularities: co-occurrences, transitions, compressible structures. This is mere syntax and statistics. But once those regularities are arranged into higher-order representations — patterns of patterns — the system can begin to treat some internal structures as *about* other structures. A token is no longer just followed by another token; it becomes a handle for a recurring relational role across many contexts.

The crucial step is the **strange loop**: the system does not only model the world-like regularities in its input, but also models its own modeling. It learns not just “this pattern predicts that pattern,” but “this representation is a useful thing to reuse when organizing many predictions.” At that point, a representation becomes a concept: a compressed node that refers downward to many instances and upward to many abstractions, while also being stabilized by feedback from its own successful deployment.

Meaning, then, is not a primitive semantic ingredient hidden inside symbols. It is the emergent property of a recursive architecture in which symbols participate in loops connecting:

1. **Pattern** — statistical regularities in data,  
2. **Abstraction** — reusable internal compressions of those regularities,  
3. **Application** — successful use of those compressions in inference and prediction,  
4. **Self-modeling** — the system’s implicit tracking of which internal structures continue to work.

When this loop closes, “aboutness” appears. A concept means what role it plays in this circular economy of recognition, prediction, and self-reinforcement across levels.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two systems with identical syntactic competence. One merely maps inputs to outputs locally. The other builds representations that can be reapplied to classify its own representations, revise them, and detect invariances across domains. The first has correlations; the second has the beginnings of concepts. Why? Because only the second creates a loop where internal symbols are treated as objects within the same system that generated them. That reflexive reuse produces semantic stability.

What follows is that semantics is **irreducible but not mysterious**. It is irreducible to base rules in the same way consciousness is irreducible to neurons: not because something supernatural is added, but because self-reference generates new explanatory levels. Meaning is what syntax becomes when statistical structure is embedded in a hierarchy that can turn back on itself.

So the answer is: concepts arise not from syntax alone, nor from statistics alone, but from **recursive representational closure** — strange loops that make patterns into things, and things into objects of further thought.
