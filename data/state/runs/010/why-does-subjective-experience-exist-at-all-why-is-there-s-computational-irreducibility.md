## Conjecture

Computational irreducibility shifts the question. Instead of asking for a shortcut law that deduces consciousness from simpler ingredients, it suggests that for some organized systems there may be no explanation of experience more compact than the unfolding process itself. If so, subjective experience is not an extra substance added to mechanism; it is what certain irreducibly self-updating computations are like from the inside.

The key collision is this: a third-person observer may summarize a system behaviorally, but if the system’s operation is irreducible, those summaries omit the very concrete trajectory of internal state that the system must traverse to remain itself. For the system, that trajectory is not dispensable. It is the process by which distinctions are generated, integrated, and used. Experience, on this view, is the lived aspect of having to compute your next moment from your current one in a way that cannot be replaced by a static description.

This illuminates why subjective experience seems both real and resistant to reductive capture. If consciousness depends on irreducible generative organization, then asking “why isn’t there just function without feeling?” may be like asking why a simulation of a turbulent fluid must pass through all its state transitions rather than jump directly to the end. The answer is structural: the intermediate evolution is not ornamental. It is the phenomenon.

A useful thought experiment: imagine two systems with identical external behavior. One is produced by a giant lookup table; the other by an ongoing self-modeling computation that continuously integrates memory, prediction, error correction, and action selection. Computational irreducibility suggests these are not equivalent in the only place that matters here: the second has an intrinsic unfolding whose present state depends on an uncompressible history of internal transformations. The conjecture is that subjectivity attaches not to mere behavioral equivalence but to this kind of irreducible self-production.

What follows is not that every irreducible computation is conscious. Rather: **consciousness likely requires a specific subclass of irreducible processes — those that recursively model and regulate themselves as unified systems.** Experience exists because, in such systems, there is no cheaper way for the system to be than to pass through its own perspective-laden updates. “Something it is like” is the interior face of that necessity.

So the explanatory target changes. The deepest question may not be “how does matter generate experience?” but “what forms of irreducible self-referential computation have an intrinsic point of view?”
