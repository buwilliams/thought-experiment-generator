## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Concepts and meaning arise not when syntax or statistics become sufficiently large, but when a pattern-recognizing system acquires a stable set of **invariants** that it preserves across changing contexts while making its own errors **detectable and correctable**. Meaning is what remains intelligible through variation; concepts are the compressions that survive criticism.

From this perspective, the core issue is analogous to institutional legitimacy under rapid change. A purely syntactic or statistical system can adapt to many surface forms, but unless it preserves some small, robust structure across transformations, it has no stable identity of reference. It has correlations, not concepts. Conversely, a rigid symbolic system can preserve form without adapting, and so fail to track reality. Meaning emerges only when both are present: **adaptation at the surface, invariance at the core**.

What are these invariants? At minimum:  
1. **Reidentifiability** — the system can treat different inputs as “the same thing” under transformation.  
2. **Constraint sensitivity** — the system distinguishes better and worse interpretations by consequences, not just frequency.  
3. **Error visibility** — the system can register mismatch between expectation and outcome.  
4. **Revision without collapse** — it can update representations while preserving enough continuity to remain interpretable to itself.

A thought experiment clarifies this. Imagine two learners exposed to the same linguistic stream. One only records transition probabilities. The other does that too, but also maintains a few stable distinctions — object persistence, agent/action separation, and prediction-error tracking. The first may generate fluent continuations; the second can begin to form concepts, because it can ask, in effect, “What stayed the same here despite altered wording?” and “What failed when I applied this pattern?” Concepts arise from this repeated preservation-and-correction cycle.

So syntax and statistics are not opposed to meaning; they are raw materials. Pattern recognition supplies candidate regularities. Syntax organizes combinatorial possibility. Statistics rank likely continuations. But **meaning appears when these are embedded in a system that treats some structures as identity-preserving across variation and subjects them to routine correction**. In Deutschian terms: concepts are conjectural explanatory compressions, and semantics grows through criticism — failed predictions, disambiguation, pragmatic breakdown, and repair.

This also illuminates why trust in a meaning-bearing system matters. A hearer treats an utterance as meaningful when they assume that beneath flexible expression there are recoverable invariants, and that errors can be repaired without destroying communicative identity. Language stays legitimate under change the way institutions do: not by freezing form, but by preserving a small set of clear invariants while making correction normal.

Thus the collision yields a sharper claim: **meaning is not hidden inside syntax or statistics; it emerges from a system’s capacity to preserve reference-like invariants across changing patterns and to publicly, repeatedly correct its failures.**
