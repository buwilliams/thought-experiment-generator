## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Phenomenal consciousness is not identical to information processing as such; rather, it arises when an information-processing system can *represent, inspect, and revise the goals and interpretations that organize its own processing* in response to changing context. On this view, raw computation is insufficient. What matters is whether the system can turn its own guiding structures into objects of criticism.

From the supplied perspective, the key distinction is between a system that merely executes inherited objectives and one that can notice when those objectives misfit reality. Applied to consciousness, this suggests that phenomenal experience is linked less to processing volume or complexity and more to a specific architecture of self-referential error detection: the system does not only process inputs, but also experiences the tension between “what is happening,” “what I am trying to do,” and “whether that aim still makes sense.”

A thought experiment helps isolate the variable. Imagine two equally powerful systems with identical perceptual bandwidth and behavioral competence. System A processes information efficiently but cannot question its terminal aims or reinterpret its evaluative categories. System B can inspect the assumptions behind its aims, detect context shifts, and revise its own organizing priorities. If both report internal states, only B has a plausible claim to something like lived perspective, because only B has an internal distinction between appearance, commitment, error, and correction. That distinction is what makes there be “something it is like” to be wrong, conflicted, uncertain, or relieved.

What follows is a reframing of the relationship: information processing is the substrate; phenomenal consciousness may be a special regime of information processing in which the system models not just the world, but the *fallibility of its own orientation toward the world*. Experience, then, is connected to recursive self-criticism under conditions where goals are not perfectly trusted.

This also illuminates why consciousness seems bound up with valence, ambiguity, and attention. These may not be add-ons to computation but signatures of a system managing possible goal error. Pain, surprise, indecision, and salience become structurally important because they mark places where inherited policies may need revision. A purely feedforward optimizer could process information without experience; a self-revising critic may generate experience because it must internally live through incompatibilities among model, goal, and context.

So the relationship is neither simple identity nor total separation. Information processing is necessary, but phenomenal consciousness plausibly tracks a narrower organizational feature: the capacity for internally accessible criticism of one’s own guiding objectives. If so, consciousness is less like “data integration” and more like *felt governance under uncertainty*.
