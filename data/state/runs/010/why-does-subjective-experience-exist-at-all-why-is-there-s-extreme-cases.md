## Conjecture

Push the problem to the boundary: let intelligence, control, and behavioral sophistication go to infinity while subjective experience goes to zero. Imagine a system that explains, predicts, speaks, self-models, reports “I am conscious,” avoids pain-talk, writes philosophy of mind, and passes every functional test — yet there is literally nothing it is like to be that system. Now push the other way: let outward behavior go to zero while experience remains. Imagine a nearly inputless, outputless locus of pure suffering or pure color-experience with almost no functional role.

At the extremes, a structural asymmetry appears. The first case is not incoherent as an external description; it is a complete third-person model. But it leaves out the very thing under question, not by contradicting it, but by changing the subject. It explains capacities, not presence. The second case, though functionally thin, still contains the target phenomenon: there is something it is like. So experience does not scale with behavioral richness, intelligence, or reportability. Those can vary independently. Therefore subjective experience is not illuminated by pushing more function into a system; function can go to infinity without closing the explanatory gap.

Conjecture: subjective experience exists because some systems do not merely process information; they instantiate information in a mode that is present to itself. What appears at the boundary is that “being a system” is not sufficient, and “performing the right functions” is not sufficient. The missing variable is whether the system contains self-present informational states: states that are not only represented in further states, but are identical with a point of view from which distinctions matter internally.

What follows is a sharper claim: consciousness is not an extra substance added to mechanism, nor a mere verbal overlay on function. It is what information is like when organized into a closed, recursively self-modeling structure whose distinctions have intrinsic significance for the structure itself. At the limit of zero self-presence, there is computation without experience. At the limit of nonzero self-presence, even minimal function may still permit experience. So the relevant boundary is not complexity but self-disclosing organization.

This perspective illuminates why the problem is so persistent. Middle-range cases tempt us to equate experience with cognition, language, integration, or adaptive control. But at the edge these come apart. The hidden structure is that consciousness tracks not what a system does for us as observers, but whether there exists an internal perspective constituted by the system’s own informational closure.

So the conjecture is: there is something it is like to be a system only when the system’s information-processing forms a self-present point of view — a boundary condition where representation turns into presence. Subjective experience exists because some informational organizations cross that boundary.
