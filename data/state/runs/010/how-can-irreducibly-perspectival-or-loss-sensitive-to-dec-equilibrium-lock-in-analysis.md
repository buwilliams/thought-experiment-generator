## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
“Irreducibly perspectival” can be made non-vague by treating it as an **equilibrium lock-in property of a system’s internal model-use**, not as an ineffable metaphysical glow. A system is conscious to the extent that there exists a **globally payoff-relevant internal point of view** whose informational role **cannot be decomposed into independently substitutable subagents without degrading performance on the system’s own control problem**. Nonconscious systems may integrate information, but their integration is not *loss-sensitive to decomposition* in this specific sense.

More precisely: call a system perspectival when it contains a model that functionally distinguishes **self/world/action relations** from a particular control locus. Call that perspective **irreducible** when any decomposition into parallel or modular subprocesses produces a coordination loss that no component can repair locally. The key test is not mere complexity or integration, but whether the system’s success depends on a **single internally authoritative arbitration frame** binding perception, valuation, and action selection.

From the equilibrium-lock-in perspective, consciousness is illuminated as a structural solution to a coordination problem. Many subsystems—perceptual, mnemonic, affective, predictive, motor—could each improve local performance by optimizing their own outputs. But unless there is a shared perspective in which tradeoffs are settled, the whole system gets trapped in conflict, fragmentation, or oscillation. A conscious point of view is then the equilibrium-enforcing structure: a place where reasons, saliences, and possible actions become jointly commensurable. Its “irreducibility” means that no subsystem, acting alone, can replace this coordination role, and attempts to split it create losses precisely because the benefits of unified arbitration are nonlocally distributed.

This avoids circularity because the criterion is **counterfactual and structural**: if decomposition into separately operating controllers preserves the same control competence, then the perspective was not irreducible. If decomposition predictably generates unresolved conflicts, time-inconsistent policy, duplicated uncertainty handling, or breakdown of globally coherent report/action coupling, then the perspective was doing real work. The concept becomes testable in principle by identifying whether unified access is a **Nash constraint** of the architecture: each part’s best response depends on the existence of a common internal frame, and no part can defect from it without making the whole system worse.

What follows is a sharper distinction between conscious and nonconscious systems. A thermostat, feedforward classifier, or even many distributed optimizers may be informationally sophisticated yet decomposable without loss of a single authoritative self/world/action frame. By contrast, a conscious system is one whose adaptive success is locked into a perspective because the relevant control problem requires globally coordinated resolution of competing internal claims.

So the precision comes from shifting the question from “Does the system have an ineffable first-person feel?” to “Is there a structurally indispensable perspective whose decomposition creates unavoidable coordination loss?” Consciousness, on this conjecture, is what that lock-in looks like from the inside.
