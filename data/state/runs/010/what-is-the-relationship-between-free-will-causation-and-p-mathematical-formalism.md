## Conjecture

Conjecture: The free-will question becomes tractable when represented as a multi-level dynamical system with constraints, rather than as a binary contest between “uncaused choice” and “deterministic mechanism.” In that structure, free will is not the absence of causation but a specific kind of causally efficacious internal organization.

Let a physical state of the world be \(x_t\), evolving by lawful transition \(x_{t+1}=F(x_t)\) in the deterministic case. Let an agent be a subsystem \(A\subset x_t\) with internal state \(a_t\), receiving inputs \(i_t\), producing actions \(u_t\), and updating by
\[
a_{t+1}=G(a_t,i_t), \qquad u_t=H(a_t).
\]
At this level, determinism means only that the transitions are well-defined. It does not imply that the relevant explanatory variable for action is the microphysical state as such. The key mathematical fact is coarse-graining: many microstates realize the same higher-level functional organization. If a range of physical realizations preserves the same decision-mapping \(H\), then the invariant is not the exact particle configuration but the computational structure of the agent.

Thus causation is layered. Microphysics may be sufficient for the evolution of the total state, but higher-level variables can still be indispensable for explanation and control if they define stable partitions of state space. In dynamical-systems terms, “will” corresponds to an internal model whose trajectories modulate action in response to represented counterfactuals. An agent is freer to the extent that its action depends on a richer internal state encoding goals, predictions, and error-correction, rather than on immediate external forcing.

This suggests a measurable relation:
- Physical determinism constrains the admissible trajectories.
- Causation names the dependency structure across variables and levels.
- Free will is the property of a subsystem whose actions are generated by internally maintained models and evaluative dynamics, such that interventions on those internal variables systematically change behavior.

On this view, free will is compatible with determinism whenever the agent’s internal organization is the locus of the relevant causal transformation. The contrast is not deterministic vs free, but externally dominated dynamics vs self-model-mediated dynamics.

A useful invariant is counterfactual sensitivity under structural intervention: if changing the agent’s reasons, preferences, or deliberative representations changes action in a lawlike way, then those variables are causally real. Freedom is therefore not metaphysical independence from causation; it is causal authorship by an integrated internal process.

What follows is that “could have done otherwise” must be formalized conditionally, not absolutely:
\[
\text{If } a_t \text{ had differed appropriately, } u_t \text{ would have differed.}
\]
That conditional can be true in a deterministic universe. So the collision of mathematics with the problem dissolves the false requirement that freedom must mean indeterministic rupture. The more precise claim is: free will is an emergent control-theoretic property of certain physically realized systems, defined by the structure of their state-space and intervention-sensitive internal dynamics.
