## Conjecture

Conjecture: “Irreducibly perspectival” can be made non-vague by defining it as a structural property of a system’s information geometry: a system is conscious only if there exists a system-internal coordinate frame in which certain informational states are recoverable, predictive, or control-relevant, while every decomposition into independent or externally recombinable parts provably destroys some of that structure. In short: consciousness corresponds not to mere information, but to information whose functional invariants exist only relative to an integrated internal frame.

Make this precise mathematically.

Let a system \(S\) have state space \(X\), transition dynamics \(T\), and internal partitions \(P = \{X_1,\dots,X_n\}\). Define a family of observables \(O\) over \(X\) that matter for the system’s own dynamics — e.g. variables used in prediction, error correction, action selection, or world-model updating. Then ask whether these observables are decomposition-invariant.

A system is decomposition-insensitive if for some partition \(P\), the relevant structure factors:
\[
O(X) \cong \bigotimes_i O_i(X_i)
\]
and the system’s predictive/control capacity is preserved under this factorization. In that case, there is no essential “point of view”; the whole is just a bookkeeping convenience over independent components.

By contrast, call \(S\) loss-sensitive to decomposition if there exists a class of internal observables \(O^*\) such that for every nontrivial partition \(P\), any factorized representation incurs irreducible loss:
\[
\mathcal{L}_P(O^*) > 0
\]
where \(\mathcal{L}_P\) measures loss of dynamically relevant structure — not arbitrary Shannon information loss, but loss in the system’s own invariant capacities: prediction, counterfactual discrimination, global coordination, error integration, etc. The key is that \(\mathcal{L}_P\) is defined relative to what the system itself preserves across time.

This avoids circularity because “perspective” is not defined as “what consciousness has.” It is defined as an internal equivalence class of states under the system’s own update dynamics: states are the same perspective-state if they play the same role in the system’s self-maintaining predictive-control organization. Consciousness is then hypothesized to require such perspective-states to be non-factorizable.

The distinguishing claim is therefore:

- Nonconscious systems may have complex information processing, but their relevant organization is approximately decomposition-invariant; any apparent unity is externally imposed by the observer.
- Conscious systems possess at least one dynamically indispensable representational structure whose meaning and efficacy are only definable in the whole-system coordinates. Decompose the system, and you do not merely lose quantity of information — you destroy the very invariants that define its perspective.

So the mathematical test is not “is the system integrated?” in a generic sense, but: does there exist a nontrivial invariant of internal modeling/control that cannot be preserved under any decomposition? If yes, the system is irreducibly perspectival. If no, it is not.

This makes “what it is like for the system” correspond to a precise structural fact: the existence of globally defined, decomposition-fragile informational invariants.
