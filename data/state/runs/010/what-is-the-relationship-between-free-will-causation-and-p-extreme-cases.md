## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
At the boundary cases, “free will” is neither the absence of causation nor the falsity of determinism. It is the capacity of a system to become a *cause through internal representation*—to contain explanatory models that can redirect its own behavior. Physical determinism, if true, constrains what happens; it does not erase the distinction between behavior caused *through reasons* and behavior caused only by external push.

Push to the limits:

- **Limit 1: total randomness.**  
If actions were uncaused or purely stochastic, that would not increase freedom. A choice produced by noise is not *more mine* than one produced by mechanism. So free will cannot be identified with indeterminism.

- **Limit 2: total external determination.**  
Imagine a being whose every movement is directly controlled from outside, with no internal world-model, no counterfactual thinking, no ability to revise policy. Even if physically identical outputs occurred, we would say it lacks free will because it is not an origin of explanations. So free will cannot be identified merely with complex motion or unpredictability.

- **Limit 3: perfect internal determinism.**  
Now imagine an agent whose physical evolution is fully determined, but whose structure includes memory, abstraction, self-critique, and the ability to simulate alternatives: “If I do X, Y follows; if I value Z, I should do W.” Such an agent can be caused to act by *arguments*—including self-generated ones. Here, determinism does not abolish agency; it is the substrate in which agency is implemented.

This reveals the hidden structure: the key divide is not **determined vs. undetermined**, but **non-explanatory causation vs. explanatory causation**.

A thermostat, a billiard ball, and a reflective person are all caused. But only the reflective person can entertain rival possibilities as *representations*, criticize them, and let the winning explanation alter future action. “Could have done otherwise” then means: under different reasons, criticism, or aims processed by the same agent, a different action would have followed. That counterfactual remains meaningful even in a deterministic world.

So the relationship is:

1. **Causation is universal**: free will does not escape it.  
2. **Determinism is orthogonal**: whether physics is deterministic does not by itself settle whether agents are free.  
3. **Free will is an emergent causal mode**: a system is free insofar as it can instantiate knowledge, evaluate alternatives, and act from reasons that it can in principle criticize and change.

The edge case also clarifies moral significance: responsibility attaches not to metaphysical exemption from causation, but to participation in the space of criticism and reasons. A rock cannot answer an argument; a person can. That is the relevant difference.

So the collision of these ideas suggests: **free will is not causeless choice, but causation becoming self-referential, model-based, and corrigible inside a physical system.**
