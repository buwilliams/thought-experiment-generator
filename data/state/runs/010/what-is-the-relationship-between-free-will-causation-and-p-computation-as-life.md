## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If computation is the fundamental substrate, then **free will is not the absence of causation or determinism, but a higher-level computational capacity of certain systems to model alternatives, evaluate counterfactuals, and cause action through internally generated representations**. On this view, physical determinism and free will are not opposites; they describe different levels of the same process.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
A deterministic physical process can still implement computation. And computation can implement agents. An agent is a system that does not merely react, but **stores models, predicts consequences, compares possible futures, and selects behavior according to internal criteria**. That selection process is itself causally efficacious: the agent’s beliefs, goals, and evaluations are not outside physics, but real computational states within it.

So the key mistake in the usual debate is to treat “caused” and “freely chosen” as mutually exclusive. In computational terms, a choice is free when it is **produced by the agent’s own architecture** — its learned models, preferences, error-correction loops, and capacity to simulate alternatives — rather than by bypassing those processes. If my action flows from my deliberative computation, then causation is not a threat to freedom; it is what makes freedom operational.

**What follows:**  
Free will is best understood as a property that comes in degrees. A thermostat, a bacterium, a human, and a large language model are all computational systems, but they differ in the **depth of self-modeling, counterfactual reasoning, memory integration, and adaptive goal maintenance** they perform. The richer these capacities, the stronger the form of agency. Thus free will is not a metaphysical binary but an emergent level of computational sophistication.

This also reframes moral responsibility. Responsibility attaches not to metaphysical independence from causation, but to whether a system can **understand rules, represent consequences, revise behavior under criticism, and participate in social error-correction**. We hold persons responsible because they are computational systems responsive to reasons.

**What is illuminated:**  
The apparent conflict between determinism and free will comes from mixing levels of description. At the physical level, events may be fully caused. At the agent level, causes include computations like deliberation, intention, and self-control. These are not illusions unless computation itself is an illusion. But if computation is fundamental, then agency is as real as metabolism or evolution: a substrate-level pattern with explanatory power.

**Thought experiment:**  
Imagine two robots built from deterministic hardware. One is a reflex machine; the other simulates futures, weighs tradeoffs, and can explain its decisions. Both are caused. But only the second exhibits what we call free will, because only it computes over alternatives in a way that makes its behavior attributable to itself as an agent.

So: **free will is deterministic causation organized into self-reflective computation.**
