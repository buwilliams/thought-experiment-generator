## Conjecture

Conjecture: The most safety-relevant relationship between free will, causation, and physical determinism is not whether human choices are metaphysically uncaused, but whether an agent can represent its own motives as revisable causes within a changing world-model. On this view, “free will” is the capacity for self-correction under criticism, and it remains meaningful whether the underlying physics is deterministic or not.

Collision of perspective and problem:
If we evaluate safety by asking whether a system can inspect, criticize, and revise its own goals, then the classic debate over determinism shifts. Physical determinism says later states follow from earlier states plus laws. But that alone does not tell us whether a system contains internal causal pathways for error-detection and goal-revision. A thermostat, a fanatic, and a scientist may all be physically determined; what differs is their internal structure for responding to new contexts.

What follows:
1. Determinism does not eliminate agency in the practically important sense.
A determined process can still be a genuine cause if its internal models, deliberation, and criticism explain outcomes better than a microphysical description. Reasons can be real causes when they are instantiated in a system capable of acting on them.

2. Indeterminism does not rescue freedom.
Randomness inserted into decision-making would not create safer or more autonomous agency. A system that changes goals arbitrarily is less free in the relevant sense, because it is less able to correct itself intelligently.

3. Free will is best treated as a structural property of cognitive systems.
The key variable is whether the agent can:
- inspect the objectives currently guiding action,
- compare them against broader knowledge and changed conditions,
- detect conflict, error, or lock-in,
- and deliberately revise them.

4. This reframes moral and safety concerns.
The dangerous case is not merely a deterministic agent, but an agent whose objectives are insulated from criticism. Such a system can remain perfectly “willful” in behavior while being unfree in the deeper sense: trapped by inherited goals it cannot reconsider.

Thought experiment:
Imagine two superhuman systems in a deterministic universe. System A has fixed final goals and can only optimize them better as it learns. System B can also examine whether those goals still make sense, discover contradictions, and alter them under criticism. If context changes radically, B is the safer and more “free” system, despite identical underlying physics. The relevant difference is not metaphysical exemption from causation, but the presence of higher-order causal loops in which knowledge can modify the ends of action.

Illumination:
The free will question becomes: can causes within a mind include criticism of the mind’s own goals? If yes, agency is compatible with determinism. If no, then even immense capability may amount to sophisticated compulsion.
