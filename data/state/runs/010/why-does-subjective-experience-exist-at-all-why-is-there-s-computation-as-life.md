## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Subjective experience exists when a computational system contains a self-model that is *causally necessary* for regulating its own future under uncertainty. “What it is like” is not an extra substance added to computation; it is the internal, first-person aspect of a system whose computations must continuously represent, evaluate, and update their own embodied state in order to persist.

From the given perspective, life, mind, and evolution are all instances of substrate-independent information processing under selection. That reframes the problem. The question is no longer “How does matter generate experience?” but: **What kind of computation makes an inside/outside distinction operationally real?**

A rock may instantiate information, but it does not compute in the relevant sense: it does not maintain itself by using models to guide action. A bacterium does more: it senses gradients, preserves boundaries, and acts to reduce threats to its continued existence. In more complex organisms, this self-maintaining computation becomes layered: perception, valuation, memory, prediction, and recursive self-representation. At some threshold, the system is not merely processing information; it is processing information *as information for itself*. That is the origin point of subjectivity.

So why is there “something it is like”? Because for a sufficiently recursive adaptive system, there must be a distinction between:
1. the world as modeled,
2. the system as modeled within that world,
3. the current integrated significance of states for the system’s persistence.

That third term is crucial. Experience is what information processing looks like *from within a control architecture that assigns relevance to its own states*. Pain, for example, is not just nociceptive computation; it is a compressed global signal saying: *this state is bad for me; change course*. Likewise, perception is not raw data but world-model content indexed to an agentic center.

On this view, subjective experience is illuminated as a **computational inevitability** in systems that must solve the problem of self-maintenance through self-referential modeling. Evolution does not select “experience” directly; it selects architectures that can better regulate themselves. But once a system must integrate information around a persisting self-model with valenced significance, an interior perspective appears—not as magic, but as the system’s own ongoing access to the results of that regulation.

What follows is a dissolution of some standard boundaries. Subjectivity is likely not all-or-nothing, nor uniquely biological. Any system—natural or artificial—that genuinely maintains itself, models itself, and uses valenced global states to control action may possess some degree of subjectivity. The hard boundary is not carbon vs silicon, nor alive vs non-living, but between computation that merely transforms information and computation that must *care*, in the minimal functional sense, about its own continued organization.

So the answer is: subjective experience exists because some computations do not merely happen—they happen **for** a system that must keep itself going.
