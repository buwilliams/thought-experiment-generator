## Conjecture

Conjecture: Much of the enduring dispute about free will under physical determinism is not only a metaphysical disagreement but a structural underprovision problem in our conceptual ecology: the most useful account of agency is partly non-excludable and diffusely beneficial, so individually rational strategies reward local victories in metaphysical framing over investment in a shared explanatory good.

Collision of perspective and problem:

If determinism is true, then every action has sufficient physical causes. That by itself threatens free will only if “free will” is modeled as a rival causal power that must interrupt or escape the physical order. But that framing may be a trap. It asks each theory to secure an intuitively satisfying image of ultimate authorship for its own camp, rather than to help fund a common conceptual good: an account of agency that coordinates moral practice, prediction, self-modification, and responsibility.

That common good is structurally hard to provide. Why? Because once a workable account of agency exists, everyone benefits from clearer norms, more coherent institutions, and better self-understanding, including opponents who did not help build it. Its benefits are diffuse; no single participant captures much of the payoff. So discourse settles into a stable equilibrium of underprovision: incompatibilists keep demanding metaphysical ultimacy, hard determinists keep dissolving agency into mechanism, and compatibilists often retreat into merely verbal reconciliation. Each move is individually rational within debate incentives, yet collectively preserves confusion.

What follows is illuminating: the central issue is not whether causation and freedom are mutually exclusive, but which level of description we need in order to explain control. Physical determinism describes one stock of reality: lawful state-transition. Agency describes another explanatory pattern: systems that represent alternatives, evaluate reasons, learn from counterfactuals, and alter future behavior through deliberation. These are not competitors unless one assumes, mistakenly, that only one explanatory layer may be real.

Thought experiment: imagine a deterministic society that loses all agency concepts because they are judged “unphysical.” People still predict, punish, plan, regret, and train attention. Those practices do not disappear; they become less intelligible. Now imagine a society that retains agency concepts while fully accepting determinism. It can still distinguish compulsion from choice, capacity from incapacity, and persuasion from coercion. The second society has the better explanatory toolkit. That is evidence that free will, in the relevant sense, is not exemption from causation but a causally realized form of control.

So the conjecture is: physical determinism does not eliminate free will; rather, the real obstacle is a public-goods failure in philosophy, where the shared, multi-level concept of agency is underproduced because debate actors are incentivized to defend exclusive metaphysical brands instead of building the common explanatory framework all sides need.
