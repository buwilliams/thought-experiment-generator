## Conjecture

Conjecture: The strongest explanations of free will are those in which agency is a load-bearing causal level, not an ornamental gloss on physics. Physical determinism matters only insofar as changing it changes what the theory can explain about deliberation, responsibility, error-correction, and choice.

Apply the load-bearing test:

If “free will” is removed from an explanation and nothing else has to change except vocabulary, then it was never doing explanatory work. That is the weakness of views where all behavior is fully explained by microphysical state transitions and “choice” is just a convenient summary. On that picture, replacing “she decided for a reason” with “particles evolved lawfully” does not alter the predicted outcome. Agency is not load-bearing there; it is a narrative shell.

But the same test cuts the other way. If we alter the explanation by removing reasons, criticism, or deliberation, we do lose explanatory power. We can no longer explain why argument changes behavior, why people can act on abstract knowledge, why they distinguish coercion from consent, or why institutions built on accountability work differently from those built on force. These phenomena are not captured merely by citing prior physical states. To explain them, one needs a level at which persons consider alternatives, represent counterfactuals, and let reasons causally constrain action.

So the key collision is this: determinism by itself does not eliminate freedom unless it makes the agential level explanatorily redundant. And it does not obviously do that. A deterministic chess computer still searches, evaluates, and selects moves; describing transistor evolution does not replace the algorithmic explanation. Likewise, a person could be physically determined while still being an agent if their action depends in a load-bearing way on internal processes of criticism, reflection, and reason-sensitive control. In that case, causation is layered: physical causation instantiates the process, while agential causation explains the form of the process.

What follows is a compatibilist conjecture, but not a cheap one. Free will is not “uncaused choice,” since making choices random would not increase agency. Nor is it merely “whatever happens inside a person,” since bypassed or manipulated processes would then count as free. Rather, free will consists in a system whose behavior is causally organized by its own reason-responsive, counterfactual, self-correcting structures. Those structures are load-bearing because if you change them, you change what the person does and what norms of responsibility can explain.

Thus the real contrast is not free will versus causation, nor free will versus determinism. It is between shallow descriptions in which agency is dispensable and deeper ones in which agency is a real explanatory constraint. Freedom is compatible with physical determinism if deliberation, reasons, and self-modification remain indispensable parts of the causal story.
