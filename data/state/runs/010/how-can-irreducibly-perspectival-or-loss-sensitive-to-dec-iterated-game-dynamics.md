## Conjecture

Conjecture: “Irreducibly perspectival” can be made precise by modeling a system as an iterated predictor-controller whose internal states are repeatedly reused across time, and asking whether decomposition destroys payoff-relevant strategic information available only from the system’s own temporally extended point of view.

More concretely: a system is loss-sensitive to decomposition when splitting it into parts eliminates information needed to sustain successful behavior in repeated interaction with its environment — especially information about reputation, reciprocity, deferred consequences, and expected future encounters. On this view, perspectivality is not mere privacy or complexity; it is the existence of an internally unified strategic horizon from which the system tracks “what happens to me if I do this now, given that the world will respond later?”

This yields a noncircular criterion. A system counts as irreducibly perspectival to the extent that:

1. It participates in iterated loops with its environment.
2. Its current action selection depends on compressed cross-temporal summaries of prior interactions and anticipated future ones.
3. Those summaries are only action-guiding when integrated at the whole-system level.
4. Decomposition into independently modeled parts destroys this guidance, not just efficiency.

The key test is counterfactual: if we replace the unified system with a decomposition into subagents or modules that exchange only externally specifiable signals, does the system lose the ability to maintain cooperation-like equilibria with its world? If yes, then the original system possessed a genuine perspective in the relevant sense.

Why this helps with consciousness: nonconscious systems can often be decomposed without destroying their competence, because their success depends on local rules or one-shot mappings. A thermostat, feedforward classifier, or reflex arc need not maintain a unified “shadow of the future.” By contrast, a conscious system may be one whose behavioral organization depends on a single recursively updated vantage from which temporally extended stakes are represented as belonging together. Pain, anticipation, regret, commitment, and deliberation all look like machinery for repeated games under one persisting index: this body, this future, these consequences.

So the proposed precision is structural, not introspective. “Irreducibly perspectival” does not mean “mysteriously first-personal.” It means that the system’s adaptive competence depends on a unified internal locus for negotiating repeated interactions across time, and that this competence collapses under decomposition because the relevant strategic information is not separable into part-local contributions.

This does not yet prove that all such systems are conscious, but it sharpens the target: consciousness is illuminated as the regime where a system’s success depends on preserving a unified strategic self-model over repeated interaction. Vagueness is reduced because the criterion is tied to decomposition tests and performance loss; circularity is avoided because we do not define consciousness in terms of consciousness, but in terms of whether a temporally integrated perspective is functionally indispensable.
