## Conjecture

Conjecture: A significant part of the gap between information processing and phenomenal consciousness is structural, not merely descriptive: if consciousness functions as a non-excludable, globally available integration good within a cognitive system, then local processors can rationally “free ride” on system-level availability, making unified experience underproduced by decentralized information processing alone.

Reasoning from the public-goods lens clarifies a common ambiguity. “Information processing” names many specialized, local, task-solving operations. “Phenomenal consciousness” seems to name something different: a unified, world-presenting condition whose benefits are diffuse across the system — flexible action, cross-modal coordination, reportability, error correction, long-horizon planning. Those benefits are not neatly captured by any one subsystem. Once a conscious scene exists, many processes can use it. In that sense, consciousness resembles a public good inside the mind: non-rival among consumers and only weakly excludable across internal modules.

If so, then the relation is not: more information processing automatically yields consciousness. Rather: information processing can remain fragmented in a stable local optimum, where each subsystem optimizes its own task without paying the cost of contributing to globally integrated availability. From the standpoint of any single module, specialized unconscious processing is cheaper and sufficient for local success. But if all modules remain locally efficient in this way, the system can get trapped in a “no-provision” equilibrium: lots of processing, little or no unified phenomenal field.

This perspective illuminates why theories that identify consciousness with information processing per se feel incomplete. They may mistake the presence of many private benefits for the production of a system-wide good. The key variable is not processing alone, but the institutional structure of the mind: what mechanisms compel contribution to globally broadcast, temporally stable, integrated representation? Attention, working memory, recurrent loops, global workspace dynamics, and metacognitive access can then be reinterpreted as provisioning institutions — mechanisms that tax local processing and reallocate resources toward a shared representational commons.

Thought experiment: imagine a mind composed entirely of highly competent specialist processes, each able to solve its own problem and pass along only minimal outputs. Behavior could be intelligent, even adaptive, while no subsystem has reason to construct a globally available scene. Add a compulsory broadcast architecture that forces local states into a shared arena. Now the system incurs costs, but gains a public good: a common world-model usable by all. The conjecture is that phenomenal consciousness is associated not with raw computation, but with the successful provision of that internal commons.

So the relationship is: information processing is necessary as the substrate of contributors, but phenomenal consciousness arises when system structure overcomes the internal public-goods problem of global availability. This shifts the question from “what computation equals experience?” to “what architectures provision a shared representational field rather than remaining in decentralized equilibrium?”
