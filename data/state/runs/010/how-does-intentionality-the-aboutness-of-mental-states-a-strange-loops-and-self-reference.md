The key collision is this: bare physical processes are only local interactions, but strange loops show how a hierarchy of such interactions can fold back on itself. A nervous system can first track environmental regularities, then track its own tracking, then regulate its behavior in light of that self-tracking. At that point, an internal state is no longer just a caused event; it is embedded in a loop where it is **interpreted by downstream processes as indicating something** and where success or failure of that indication matters to the system’s continued regulation.

So the crucial move is from **correlation** to **recursively embedded use**. The strange-loop perspective illuminates why intentionality feels irreducible to base rules without being nonphysical. Aboutness is not visible at the level of individual particles for the same reason “self” is not visible there: it is a **cross-level organizational property**. When the system’s causal architecture allows representations of the world to include representations of the system representing the world, semantic content emerges as a stable fixed point of recursive model-use.

A thought experiment sharpens this. Imagine two physically similar systems that both correlate internal states with external objects. In one, those states trigger fixed outputs only. In the other, the states are available to memory, error-correction, counterfactual simulation, and self-prediction. Only the second has the resources for genuine aboutness, because only there can a state be treated as a representation *within a loop that includes the system itself*. Intentionality is thus not mere covariance, nor mysterious intrinsic meaning, but **the role a state plays in a self-referential predictive-control hierarchy**.

What follows is that intentionality should scale with the depth and flexibility of these loops: the richer the system’s capacity to model the world, model itself modeling the world, and revise those models through feedback, the more robust its aboutness. In short: physical processes become intentional when they are arranged into strange loops that make some states function as world-directed self-updating models.
