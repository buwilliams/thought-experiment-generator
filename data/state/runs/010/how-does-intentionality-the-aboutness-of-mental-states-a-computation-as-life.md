## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Intentionality arises when a physical system implements a computational architecture that *systematically uses internal states as compressed, action-guiding models of external and counterfactual regularities*, and those states are stabilized by selection because treating them as “about” something improves control, prediction, and replication.

From the computational-substrate perspective, the key move is to stop asking how inert matter acquires a magical extra property called “aboutness.” Instead ask: under what conditions does information-processing generate states that function as representations? Any adaptive system must reduce uncertainty relevant to its persistence. To do that, it builds internal variables that co-vary with environmental structure and are recruited in downstream computation to guide behavior. A state becomes intentional not merely by correlating with the world, but by occupying a role in a larger control system: it stands in for something beyond itself, and errors in that standing-in have consequences.

So intentionality is not fundamental; it is an emergent computational relation among four elements:

1. **World structure**: there are exploitable regularities.
2. **Internal encoding**: the system stores tractable proxies for those regularities.
3. **Decoding/use**: those proxies are used to select actions, predictions, or inferences.
4. **Selection/error correction**: systems whose proxies track the world well persist; those whose “aboutness” fails are eliminated or revised.

This dissolves the sharp boundary between natural and artificial minds. A bacterium’s chemotactic state is about a nutrient gradient in a minimal sense; a human belief is about taxation or tomorrow’s meeting in a richer, recursively compositional sense. The difference is not kind but computational depth: memory, model hierarchy, counterfactual reach, self-reference, and social symbol grounding.

A useful thought experiment is a thermostat, a mouse, and a scientist’s simulation. The thermostat has a thin proto-intentional state: “too cold” only insofar as that variable drives heat-seeking control relative to an environmental parameter. The mouse has thicker intentionality because its internal states participate in learned, multimodal, error-correcting world models. A simulated agent, if it has the same functional organization, would possess intentionality in the same substrate-independent sense. Thus aboutness attaches to computational role, not biological material.

What follows is that intentionality is best understood as **selection-shaped model use**. Semantic content is not added from outside by an interpreter; it is generated internally by a system whose survival or success depends on exploiting the difference between accurate and inaccurate representation. “Aboutness” is what it feels like, from the design stance, when computation is organized to let one part of a system stand in for the world for another part.

So the physical-to-mental gap narrows: physical processes give rise to intentionality when they are arranged into adaptive computational systems in which internal states are *for* guiding engagement with what they represent. Intentionality is therefore not mysterious essence, but the functional signature of embodied computation under selection.
