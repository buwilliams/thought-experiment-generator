## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The appearance that the fundamental constants are “fine-tuned” for complexity is not best treated as a demand for more raw explanatory capacity—more universes, more parameters, more brute-force search—but as a symptom of a deeper structural limit in our current mode of inquiry: we are trying to explain a space of possible laws without yet possessing a good error-correcting theory of which variations are genuinely possible, stable, or explanatory.

What is true here is that “fine-tuning” already presupposes a comparison class: constants could have been otherwise, most alternatives would not permit complex structures, and our universe lies in a narrow viable region. But each part of that claim is theory-laden. We do not know that the constants are independently variable; we do not know the true space of possible laws; and we do not know whether complexity is rare in that space or only rare under parametrizations inherited from current physics. So the problem may reflect not an astonishing cosmic coincidence but an immature explanatory framework.

From this perspective, the key question is not “Why did nature pick these numbers?” but “Why does our current theoretical structure represent them as contingent free parameters at all?” Historically, fine-tuning problems arise when a theory leaves important features unexplained and packages ignorance into constants. Later theories often reduce apparent arbitrariness by showing that supposedly independent quantities are linked by deeper principles. The leverage point, then, is not scaling anthropic reasoning or multiverse enumeration, but improving the structure of conjecture and criticism about law-space itself.

What follows is this: explanations of fine-tuning should be ranked by how much error-correction they enable. Anthropic selection, for example, may filter observers from a vast ensemble, but by itself it is weakly corrective unless it comes with a theory that constrains the ensemble and exposes ways it could be wrong. A deeper unification principle is stronger if it reduces adjustable freedom and forbids large classes of alternatives. Likewise, a theory of complexity that explains why robust structure emerges across broad regions of law-space would dissolve the fine-tuning problem more effectively than one that merely declares our constants lucky.

So the illuminating claim is that progress here is limited less by computational inability to scan possibilities than by the poor epistemic structure of the search. We lack good mechanisms for generating, criticizing, and retaining theories about possible laws. “Fine-tuning” may therefore be less a fact about reality than a marker of where our explanatory net has too many holes. The real task is to construct theories in which the constants are either necessary, dynamically selected by deeper constraints, or shown not to be the fragile special cases we currently imagine.
