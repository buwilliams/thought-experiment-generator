## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The direction of time is not primarily a property added by the laws of physics; it is a property of how error-correcting structures can exist and persist within a universe governed by those laws. Time-symmetric microdynamics permit both directions, but only one temporal orientation supports the growth of records, memories, explanations, and reliable selection among alternatives. The “arrow” we experience is therefore the direction in which knowledge-bearing structures can accumulate.

Here the key question is not whether the universe has enough “capacity” to evolve in either direction — the laws allow that — but what structure must be present for stable asymmetries to emerge. The crucial structural condition is a low-entropy boundary condition in what we call the past. That is the inherited constraint. Without it, there are motions; with it, there can be irreversible-looking processes: stars burning, gradients relaxing, organisms metabolizing, brains remembering, instruments recording.

From this lens, entropy increase is not the whole explanation but the substrate on which a deeper asymmetry rides. A memory, a fossil, a photograph, a genome, a scientific theory — all are not just physical states but selected survivals of error-correcting processes. They depend on there being many possible microstates compatible with a coarse-grained present, but far fewer that encode a coherent record of what preceded. The future is “open” not because the laws privilege it, but because knowledge of it has not yet been physically instantiated by such selection processes. The past feels fixed because it is densely overconstrained by records.

So what follows? The arrow of time is best understood as an emergent asymmetry of *record formation and retention* under special boundary conditions, not as a contradiction of time-symmetric law. The laws provide reversible capacity; the low-entropy past provides a gradient; but the experienced direction comes from the fact that error correction works only in one orientation relative to that gradient. A system can contain usable information about what we call earlier states because those states are the source from which records are copied forward. The reverse would require pervasive anti-records: improbably fine-tuned states in which traces converge rather than spread. That is dynamically allowed but structurally non-robust.

This also reframes the historical genesis of the problem. The puzzle arises because we expect the laws alone to explain temporality. But laws explain permitted transformations, not which explanatory structures become common. The real leverage lies in boundary conditions and in the emergence of systems that preserve and criticize information across time.

In short: **time has a direction because only one temporal orientation supports robust accumulation of records and knowledge under our universe’s boundary conditions.** The arrow is not imposed on symmetric laws from outside; it is selected by the structure of inquiry, memory, and irreversible record-making that those laws, plus a low-entropy past, make possible.
