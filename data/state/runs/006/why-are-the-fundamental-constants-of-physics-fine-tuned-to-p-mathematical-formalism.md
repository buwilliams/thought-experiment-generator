## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The “fine-tuning” problem is best understood not as a mystery about particular numerical values, but as a **constraint-satisfaction problem on a parameter space**. Let the fundamental constants be coordinates \(c \in \mathcal{P}\), where \(\mathcal{P}\) is the space of physically possible laws-parameter combinations. Let \(S(c)\) denote the set of stable composite structures permitted by those constants: nuclei, long-lived stars, chemistry, information-bearing matter, etc. Then the relevant question is not “Why these exact numbers?” but:

**What is the geometry of the subset**
\[
\mathcal{L} = \{c \in \mathcal{P} : S(c) \text{ contains hierarchically stable, complexity-supporting structures}\}?
\]

The mathematical perspective suggests this claim:

### Fine-tuning is the statement that \(\mathcal{L}\) is a narrow but structured region defined by multiple coupled inequalities, not isolated exact equalities.

Complex structure requires simultaneous satisfaction of several invariants across scales:

- **Boundedness:** matter must form stable bound states.
- **Hierarchy:** there must be separations of scale (nuclear \(\ll\) atomic \(\ll\) stellar \(\ll\) cosmological).
- **Metastability:** stars and nuclei must persist long enough to drive nonequilibrium processes.
- **Diversity:** chemistry must admit many accessible configurations.
- **Controllability:** interactions must be neither so strong as to collapse all structure nor so weak as to prevent assembly.

These are not arbitrary desiderata; they are mathematical constraints on ratios of constants. The key objects are therefore **dimensionless combinations**—for example, relative strengths of gravity, electromagnetism, and nuclear forces, and particle mass ratios. Complexity depends mostly on these ratios because rescalings that preserve them often preserve the qualitative structure of the world.

So the conjecture is:

> **Complexity-permitting universes form an allowed phase in law-space, bounded by bifurcation surfaces where one or more organizing hierarchies disappear.** Fine-tuning reflects proximity to such phase boundaries, not miraculous exactness.

Cross a boundary and some structural invariant fails: no stable hydrogen, no carbon production, no long-lived stars, no chemical complexity, no gravitationally organized astrophysics. Inside the allowed region, many micro-variations may still preserve complexity because what matters is not precise values but **membership in the same structural phase**.

This reframes explanation. If the constants appear fine-tuned, there are two broad mathematical possibilities:

1. **The viable region \(\mathcal{L}\) has very small measure** in a natural parameterization of \(\mathcal{P}\), so complexity is genuinely rare.
2. **Our parameterization is misleading**, and in a deeper theory the constants are not independent coordinates but images of a lower-dimensional structure, making the observed values generic under hidden constraints.

Thus the problem becomes one of **mapping the topology and measure of \(\mathcal{L}\)** and identifying whether apparent fine-tuning is due to improbability or to an incomplete choice of variables. The mathematical structure illuminates that “fine-tuning” is fundamentally about **robustness of emergent complexity under perturbation in law-space**.
