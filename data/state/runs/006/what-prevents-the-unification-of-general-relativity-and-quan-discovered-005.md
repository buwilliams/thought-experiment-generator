## Conjecture

The failure to unify them suggests that neither is self-interpreting.

Likewise, an equation has physical meaning only within an interpreter system: assumptions about observables, time, measurement, locality, subsystems, and what counts as a physical state. GR and QM come with different interpreter systems. In GR, spacetime geometry is itself the stage and dynamical object; in QM, dynamics presuppose structures like Hilbert space, state decomposition, and often an external time parameter. Trying to “merge the blueprints” fails because the control layers that make them meaningful are mutually misaligned.

Historically, each theory solved a different prior problem. QM solved the stability and discreteness problems of matter and radiation by revising what physical alternatives and probabilities mean. GR solved the problem of gravity by absorbing it into spacetime structure and invariance. Each inherited unexamined assumptions from the problem it replaced: QM often retained a background framework for evolution and observation; GR retained continuity and geometric universality. At ordinary scales these inherited assumptions coexist. At black holes, the early universe, and Planckian regimes, they collide.

What a unified theory must explain, then, is not merely “quantum gravity” as quantized curvature. It must explain the developmental relation by which spacetime geometry, quantum state structure, causality, and observability co-emerge from a deeper informational organization. In particular, it must account for:

1. **Why both GR and QM are so accurate in their domains** — as effective regulatory layers, not fundamental blueprints.  
2. **How the interpreters arise** — where time, measurement, locality, and geometry come from.  
3. **How consistency is maintained across scales** — analogous to developmental canalization, where robust macroscopic order emerges despite microscopic variability.  
4. **Why information has the constraints it does** — e.g. black hole entropy, holographic bounds, entanglement structure, and the apparent finiteness of physically accessible information in spacetime regions.

So the main obstacle is likely not missing mathematics alone, but missing ontology: we lack the deeper system in which “quantum” and “gravitational” descriptions are readable as coordinated outputs. A successful unified theory would therefore look less like stapling two complete formalisms together, and more like discovering the regulatory architecture that makes both of them intelligible as historically evolved, limited, but remarkably powerful layers of description.
