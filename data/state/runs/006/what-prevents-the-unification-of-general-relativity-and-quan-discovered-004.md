## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What blocks unification is not primarily insufficient mathematical power or experimental reach, but a defective *error-correction structure* in how the problem is posed. General relativity and quantum mechanics were built to correct different classes of errors: GR repaired failures in our account of gravitation, inertia, and spacetime structure; QM repaired failures in our account of discreteness, interference, and measurement outcomes. We keep trying to merge their formalisms before identifying the deeper explanatory substrate that made both successful in their own domains.

From this perspective, “unification” fails because the field is still organized around inherited assumptions that may be mutually parochial: that spacetime is fundamental, that quantization is a universal recipe, that continuity/discreteness is the right dividing line, or that the task is to mathematically combine two finished theories rather than replace both with a better one. This is a historical-genetic problem: each theory solved local problems and carried forward background commitments that became invisible. Their conflict may therefore signal not a missing bridge, but a mistaken ontology.

So the main constraint is not raw compute. More simulation power can explore candidate models, but it cannot by itself generate the right conjectures or criticism. The leverage lies in improving the *selection environment* for theories: clearer criteria for what must be explained, sharper identification of inherited assumptions, and better mechanisms for preserving and criticizing partial explanatory advances rather than rewarding only formal elegance or empirical inaccessibility.

A unified theory would need to explain, not merely encode:

1. **Why both predecessor theories worked so well where they do.**  
   It must recover GR and QM as limiting cases and explain their domains of validity.

2. **What is fundamental.**  
   Are spacetime, fields, amplitudes, information, causality, or something else basic? “Unification” requires an ontology, not just shared equations.

3. **The apparent tension between dynamical spacetime and quantum description.**  
   Not just reconcile them formally, but explain why geometry and quantum states seem to demand incompatible structures.

4. **Measurement, locality, and causation in one framework.**  
   A real successor theory must say what happens, where, and according to what invariant structure.

5. **The origin of gravitational and quantum regularities.**  
   Why gravity couples universally; why quantum probabilities, interference, and entanglement arise; why classical spacetime emerges at all.

6. **What counts as an observer-independent reality.**  
   Since both theories pressure common-sense realism in different ways, the successor must clarify what exists independent of description.

The key illumination is this: progress likely depends less on scaling calculation than on constructing a better epistemic system for generating and killing explanations. Unification will come not from “more of the same” formal synthesis, but from a new explanatory framework that makes both GR and QM look like approximate answers to a better question.
