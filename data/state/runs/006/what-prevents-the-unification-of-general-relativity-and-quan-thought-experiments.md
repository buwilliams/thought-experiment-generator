## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct the cleanest hypothetical: assume there exists a finite region of the world in which *all physically real distinctions are operationally quantum* — that is, every possible measurement outcome in the region is represented by a quantum state and its transformations — while *spacetime geometry in that same region is physically real in the classical GR sense*, with definite metric relations independent of measurement.

Now follow the logic strictly.

If geometry is definite and classical, it can in principle distinguish events, causal order, and localization with arbitrary exactness. But if all matter and fields are quantum, then the sources of geometry exist in superposition, so the geometry they determine must either:  
1. be sourced by expectation values of quantum states,  
2. branch with the superposition, or  
3. remain definite despite indefinite sources.

Option 1 fails because expectation values erase physically relevant alternatives and generically predict wrong back-reaction: a superposed mass is not the same thing as an averaged mass distribution. Option 3 fails because it introduces a hidden selection rule or collapse structure not contained in either GR or unitary quantum theory. So if we keep quantum theory serious, only option 2 survives: geometry must also participate in superposition.

That isolates the key variable: **the status of spacetime itself**. The obstacle to unification is not merely technical mismatch between equations; it is that GR treats spacetime as the dynamical stage, while QM treats physical systems as existing on a stage whose distinctions are fixed enough to define states, observables, and evolution. Each theory inherits what the other problematizes.

So the conjecture is:

**Unification is prevented because GR and QM disagree about which structures are fundamental: GR makes spacetime geometry fundamental and dynamical; QM makes the algebra of possible distinctions, states, and transformations fundamental, presupposing no single definite geometry. A unified theory must therefore explain spacetime as emergent from a deeper quantum structure of relational information, rather than merely quantizing the classical metric.**

What follows?

A successful unified theory would need to explain, not just calculate:

1. **Why classical spacetime appears at all**  
   How definite geometry, locality, and causal order emerge from quantum substrate.

2. **Why gravity is universal**  
   Why all forms of energy/information couple to the same effective geometry.

3. **The meaning of observables without fixed background time**  
   How dynamics is defined when time is not external.

4. **Black hole information and horizon thermodynamics**  
   Why geometry carries entropy, and how information is preserved or transformed.

5. **The origin of the equivalence principle**  
   Not as postulate, but as emergent necessity.

6. **The limits of localization**  
   Why attempts to probe arbitrarily small distances create gravitational obstruction.

Historical genesis clarifies the conflict: quantum theory was built to explain discrete outcomes against a mostly fixed background; GR was built to eliminate fixed background structure from gravity. Their collision exposes an unexamined inheritance in each.

So the real problem is not “how to combine two successful theories,” but **which one tells us what the world is made of**. The strict logic suggests: not particles in spacetime, nor geometry alone, but a deeper quantum-relational structure from which both matter and spacetime are derived.
