## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The “direction of time” is not an extra law added to time-symmetric physics; it is the consequence of a **load-bearing asymmetry in the universe’s boundary conditions**, especially an extremely low-entropy past. The arrow is real not because the dynamical laws prefer future over past, but because explanations of records, memory, causation, and irreversible macroscopic processes all jointly depend on that special initial condition. Remove or vary that condition, and many other features must change with it.

From this perspective, the key question is: **which parts of the standard explanation are interchangeable, and which are structurally necessary?** The time-symmetry of the laws looks non-load-bearing for the arrow itself: symmetric laws can generate asymmetric histories given asymmetric constraints. What does the real work is the past hypothesis or something like it — a highly ordered early universe.

Why think this is load-bearing rather than narrative padding? Because changing it forces compensating revisions elsewhere. If the universe did not begin in a low-entropy macrostate, then the usual explanation of why entropy tends to increase would fail. But more than that fails: our explanation of why there are stable records of the past but not the future, why memory tracks one temporal direction, why radiation is observed as retarded rather than advanced, and why “preparation then measurement” is the normal experimental structure all lose their unified account. These are not independent facts loosely grouped under “time’s arrow”; they are coupled consequences of one asymmetry.

This suggests a sharper view: **the arrow of time is epistemic and physical at once**. It is physical because entropy gradients, cosmological expansion, decoherence, and thermodynamic irreversibility are objective features. But it is epistemic because the very possibility of knowledge-bearing records depends on those gradients. A record is a localized entropy-exporting structure that correlates with what we call “the past.” In a time-symmetric lawbook, this asymmetry of knowability must come from asymmetric conditions, not dynamics.

What this illuminates is that “why does time have a direction?” may be partly mis-asked. Fundamental laws need not contain the arrow as a primitive if the universe’s actual history already does. The deeper unresolved problem is then not “how do symmetric laws create asymmetry?” but **why the universe had the particular low-entropy beginning that makes all these arrows cohere**.

So the load-bearing claim is: **the temporal direction we experience is a system-level consequence of special boundary conditions, and the evidence that this is the right explanatory locus is that multiple apparently distinct arrows depend on it together**. Any rival explanation must match that integrative constraint; otherwise it is a narrative shell with one arrow explained at the cost of leaving the others accidental.
