## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct the scenario this way: imagine a world exactly like ours except that “measurement” is not treated as a special physical event at all. The only physically real process is unitary evolution of quantum states. A detector, an observer, and an environment are just quantum systems that become correlated. Then ask: what variable remains to explain “definite outcome”? The key variable is not collapse, but **which branch contains a given observer-state after decoherence has created effectively non-interfering records**.

Follow the logic strictly. If system + apparatus + observer evolve unitarily, then after interaction the total state is a superposition of correlated terms: observer-sees-up, observer-sees-down, etc. Decoherence rapidly destroys interference between these terms relative to the environment, so each term becomes dynamically autonomous for all practical purposes. At that point, no further physical selector is needed to make a record definite *within a branch*: each observer-copy has a definite record because its local state is definite relative to that branch.

So the usual question, “what selects which outcome occurs?”, contains a hidden inheritance from pre-quantum thinking: it assumes there must be **one globally actualized result** selected by an additional mechanism. But in the hypothetical scenario, that assumption is precisely what has been removed. What occurs is not one outcome chosen from many; what occurs is **branching into multiple observer-relative definitenesses**. The “selection” problem is replaced by an **indexical problem**: why do I find myself in this branch rather than that one? Physics alone does not select a unique “I”; it only yields a structured multiplicity of successor observers.

What follows is that the measurement problem splits in two:

1. **Why are outcomes definite?**  
   Because decoherence plus entanglement produce stable quasi-classical branches in which records are internally definite.

2. **Why this outcome?**  
   There is no further physical fact selecting a single outcome globally. What remains is self-location among post-measurement observer copies, constrained statistically by branch weights.

This illuminates the role of the Born rule. It is not the law of stochastic collapse selecting one real outcome; it is the rule governing rational expectation or measure over future observer-locations in branching structure. The demand for a unique selector is therefore a misplaced demand for an extra variable.

So the conjecture is: **quantum measurement produces definite outcomes because decoherence creates effectively autonomous branches with definite internal records, and no additional physical mechanism selects a single outcome; the appearance of selection is the observer’s self-locating uncertainty within that branching structure.** If this is right, the core mistake is treating definiteness and uniqueness as the same problem. Quantum theory gives the first; the second is a classical prejudice.
