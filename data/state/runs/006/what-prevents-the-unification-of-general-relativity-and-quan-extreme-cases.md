## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What blocks the unification of general relativity and quantum mechanics is not merely a technical mismatch of equations, but a breakdown at the extremes of what each theory treats as fixed. Push GR to the smallest scales and spacetime itself cannot remain a smooth background. Push QM to universal scope and there can be no external classical stage on which probabilities play out. At the boundary, each theory invades the other’s “given”: GR makes geometry dynamical; QM makes states and observables relational and non-classical. A unified theory must therefore explain how **spacetime, locality, causality, and classicality all emerge together from a deeper substrate that is neither classical geometry nor ordinary quantum theory defined on a background manifold**.

The hidden structure appears at the limits:

- **At infinity of mass-energy density**: GR predicts singularities, where curvature diverges and the theory stops explaining. This is not just a bad calculation; it signals that continuous spacetime cannot be fundamental all the way down.
- **At zero distance / Planck scale**: QFT’s usual machinery assumes arbitrarily short distances on a fixed background. But if enough energy is concentrated to probe smaller scales, gravity produces horizons or violent curvature. The act of probing destroys the background needed for the probe. The limit is self-undermining.
- **At maximum universality**: QM, taken seriously, should apply to the whole universe. But then who or what is the external observer, and relative to what fixed time does the wavefunction evolve? In canonical quantum gravity, time evaporates into constraint equations. The “problem of time” is what appears when one pushes QM beyond subsystems to the totality.

So the obstruction is structural: **GR and QM disagree about what is fundamental enough to hold fixed while the rest changes**. In GR, no fixed spacetime background; in standard QM/QFT, dynamics presuppose one. In GR, time is part of the dynamical geometry; in QM, time is usually an external parameter. In GR, localization is geometric; in QM, localization is limited by uncertainty and entanglement. These are not independent inconsistencies but symptoms of one deeper incompatibility.

What must a unified theory explain?

1. **Why spacetime appears smooth and classical at large scales** despite not being fundamental.
2. **How quantum behavior gives rise to definite causal structure** in regimes where GR works.
3. **Why black holes have entropy and temperature**, i.e. what the microstates of geometry are.
4. **How singularities are resolved**, not hidden by formalism.
5. **What time is**, if neither absolute parameter nor naive geometric coordinate is fundamental.
6. **Why locality works so well**, if entanglement and gravity both suggest it is emergent or limited.
7. **How measurement and classical observers arise** inside a fully quantum gravitational universe.

In short: unification requires more than quantizing gravity. It requires a theory in which **geometry and quantum state are co-emergent descriptions of deeper informational structure**, valid because the edge cases force us to give up the idea that either spacetime or quantization can be fundamental alone.
