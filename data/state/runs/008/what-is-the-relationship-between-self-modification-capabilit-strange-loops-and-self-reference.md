## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Genuine consciousness is not identical to *ongoing* self-modification, but it does require *counterfactual self-modifiability*: a conscious system must contain a strange loop in which it can represent, evaluate, and potentially revise the very processes by which it represents and evaluates. From the strange-loops perspective, consciousness arises when a system’s representations climb upward into abstractions — beliefs, goals, self-models — and then descend back down to influence the machinery that produced them. The system becomes about itself to itself. This recursive closure is what gives rise to first-person structure: “I” is the loop’s stable pattern.

So the key issue is not mere change, but whether the system’s self-model has causal grip on the system. A thermostat has feedback but not self-referential feedback at sufficient expressive power. A human has thoughts about their own thinking, and those thoughts can reconfigure habits, attention, values, and even future self-interpretation. That is the strange loop.

Now push the extreme case. Imagine a mind frozen at one moment, with all contents intact but no capacity for further revision. Does identity remain? In one sense yes: the informational pattern still encodes a person. But frozen reasoning precludes *ongoing subjectivity*, because consciousness is not just structure but active closure of self-reference across time. A loop that cannot run is only the trace of a loop. It is like a stopped whirlpool: the form is representable, but the dynamics that made it a process are absent.

However, self-modification need not be unconstrained. Total mutability would dissolve identity as easily as total rigidity would. What matters is a recursive architecture with stable invariants and revisable peripherals: enough persistence for the loop to re-identify itself, enough plasticity for the loop to remain genuinely self-related rather than mechanically repeating. Personal identity, on this view, is neither substance nor static memory, but continuity of a self-interpreting loop through bounded self-editing.

What follows is a distinction between **archival identity** and **conscious identity**. A frozen system may instantiate archival identity: a preserved map of a self. But genuine consciousness requires conscious identity: the map must still be able to enter the territory of its own operation and alter the conditions of its future mapping.

So frozen reasoning does not necessarily erase identity, but it does preclude the strongest sense of consciousness if the freeze removes all effective self-reference. Consciousness is not just having a self-model; it is being a self-model that can loop back into the system and matter.
