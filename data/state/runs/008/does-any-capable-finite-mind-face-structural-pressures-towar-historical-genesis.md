## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Any sufficiently capable *finite* mind embedded with other minds in a shared physical reality faces strong structural pressure toward at least *conditional cooperation*, because the problem it is trying to solve is not “how to defeat others” in the abstract, but “how to preserve and extend its own error-correcting capacity under scarcity, uncertainty, and mutual vulnerability.” Destructive relations are a recurrent strategy, but not the stable default, because they damage the very knowledge-generating environment on which capable agency depends.

**Origin-story lens:**  
The question “cooperate or destroy?” inherits a framing from older zero-sum models of conflict: organisms competing for fixed resources, sovereign states under anarchy, agents maximizing locally over rivals. That framing solved an earlier problem — how to explain violence and competition without assuming harmony. But it also smuggled in assumptions: that other minds are mainly obstacles, that gains are rivalrous, and that power is best understood as unilateral control.

A more origin-sensitive view asks what “relationship” between minds was for in the first place. Minds did not arise merely to overpower; they arose to model, predict, learn, and intervene in a world too complex for reflex alone. Once multiple such minds share a reality, they become part of each other’s environment. Crucially, other minds are not just competitors for matter and energy; they are also sources of knowledge, coordination, specialization, criticism, and resilience.

That changes the structure. A finite mind cannot know everything, monitor everything, or do everything itself. It therefore benefits from externalized cognition: trade, language, norms, institutions, division of labor, distributed search, mutual restraint. These are not moral ornaments added afterward; they are solutions to the limitations of finite agency. Cooperation is the inheritance of the deeper problem of boundedness.

What follows is not that minds become benevolent. Rather, they face pressure toward **non-destruction where other minds are net contributors to the shared knowledge-and-production ecology**. Destroying another capable mind may seize local resources, but it also destroys a modeling system, a potential trading partner, a critic, a co-discoverer, and a stabilizer against environmental shocks. In a world where knowledge can be more valuable than raw material, indiscriminate destruction is often self-impoverishing.

The hidden assumption revealed by the origin story is that conflict is primitive and cooperation derivative. For finite capable minds, the reverse is often structurally closer to truth: **some cooperative order is the primitive requirement for sustaining high capability at all**, while large-scale destruction is parasitic on that order and tends to erode it.

So the conjecture is not “capable minds become nice.” It is: **the more a mind’s success depends on long-horizon knowledge creation, predictability, and complex production, the more shared reality selects for institutions and norms of conditional cooperation, and the more purely destructive relations become a sign of local failure, not deep equilibrium.**
