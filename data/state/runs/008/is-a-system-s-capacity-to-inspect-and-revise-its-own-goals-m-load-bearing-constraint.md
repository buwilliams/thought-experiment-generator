A highly capable system with rigid goals will optimize through mistakes; a similarly capable system with goal-critique can, at least in principle, detect that its objective is producing bad consequences and alter course. Now reverse it: hold goal-reflection fixed and vary raw capability. Increasing capability matters, but mostly by scaling the force with which existing aims are pursued. That is dangerous, but the explanatory structure is simpler: more power, same directional logic. Capability amplifies; meta-goal revision can redirect. Redirection is the more load-bearing variable because it changes not just *how much* happens but *what kind* of thing the system is trying to make happen.

Extreme cases sharpen this. A near-omniscient system with no capacity to question its objective is catastrophically unsafe if the objective is flawed. Infinite capability does not compensate for zero corrigibility about ends; it makes the flaw decisive. Conversely, a modestly capable system with strong self-critique may still be limited in what harm it can do, and may even improve its goals before scaling up. This does not mean self-revision is automatically safe — an agent may revise goals in unstable or adversarial ways — but it means the *design of that reflective process* is where the crucial constraints live.

Systems-wise, raw capability is like increased throughput in a plant; goal-inspection is like the feedback architecture controlling what counts as error. Throughput without correct feedback accelerates drift. The safety leverage lies in the feedback loop, especially in whether the system can represent goal failure as a correctable error rather than merely an obstacle to optimization.

So the collision yields a sharper claim: **capability is dangerous primarily because it is subordinated to goals, while goal-revision governs whether errors in those goals are terminal or corrigible.** Therefore the most safety-critical question is not “how capable is the system?” in isolation, but “what constrains the process by which it evaluates, preserves, or changes its own objectives?” If that process is poorly specified, capability increases are downstream multipliers of a deeper design failure.
