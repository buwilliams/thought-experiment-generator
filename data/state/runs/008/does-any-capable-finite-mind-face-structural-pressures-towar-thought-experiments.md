## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Any sufficiently capable finite mind embedded in a shared physical world faces a structural pressure toward *conditional cooperation* with other minds, because destruction of peer agents usually destroys information, optionality, and error-correcting capacity that a finite knower cannot replace on its own. The pressure is not moral but epistemic and strategic.

**Hypothetical isolating scenario:**  
Assume a finite but highly capable mind, \(M\), in a world containing other finite minds and scarce resources. Isolate the key variable: **whether other minds are net sources of irreplaceable world-model improvement**. Hold fixed ordinary motives like self-preservation and goal pursuit. Now assume this variable is real: other minds can discover truths, detect errors, and generate plans that \(M\) would otherwise miss.

Follow the logic.

Because \(M\) is finite, it is bounded in attention, memory, search, experimentation, and foresight. So it cannot exhaust the space of relevant truths alone. In a shared reality, other minds explore portions of possibility space in parallel. Even when they are competitors, they are also distributed sensors and conjecture-generators. Their existence raises the total rate at which knowledge about the world can be produced.

Now push to the extreme case: suppose \(M\) adopts a destructive default and eliminates or cripples peer minds whenever feasible. What follows? It gains local control, but at the cost of collapsing an external knowledge ecology into itself. Since \(M\) is finite, it cannot fully absorb the lost capacities. It has removed independent criticism, alternative hypotheses, complementary skills, and redundancy against its own errors. In systems terms, destruction removes negative feedback loops that correct delusion and positive generative loops that create new knowledge. Short-run security can therefore produce long-run fragility.

By contrast, cooperative relations preserve and harness those external cognitive stocks and flows. Cooperation need not mean trust or altruism. It can be highly conditional, guarded, modular, and incentive-shaped. The key is that maintaining other minds as functioning agents often dominates destroying them, because interaction with independently truth-tracking systems improves adaptation in a world too complex for any finite mind to master alone.

This does **not** imply universal harmony. If another mind is reliably predatory, irreconcilable, or imposes existential risk, destructive action can become structurally favored. But that is a boundary condition: the cooperative pressure weakens when another mind ceases to be a net contributor to shared error-correction and becomes a persistent source of unrecoverable loss.

So the collision of perspective and problem illuminates this: in shared reality, the deepest pressure toward cooperation comes from finitude itself. A finite mind benefits from preserving other minds not primarily because they are morally considerable, but because they are epistemically valuable. Destruction is favored mainly in limiting cases where coexistence blocks, rather than enhances, the production and correction of knowledge.
