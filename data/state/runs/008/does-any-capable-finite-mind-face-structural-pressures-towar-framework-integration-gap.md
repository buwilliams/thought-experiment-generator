## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Any capable finite mind embedded with other minds in a shared physical reality faces a structural pressure toward at least *bounded cooperation*, because the same underlying constraints that make its own cognition possible also make other agents part of its effective environment, and attempts to treat them purely as expendable “local noise” create instability at larger scales that feed back against its own goals.

**Why this follows from the lens:**  
The cosmological constant problem, on your framing, says: don’t accept a loose interface between layers. If microscopic quantum energy is allowed to vary freely while macroscopic gravity mostly ignores it, the theory is badly integrated. By analogy, don’t model an intelligent agent’s “private goals” as cleanly separable from the multi-agent physical world it inhabits unless the coupling is genuinely non-load-bearing.

Here the load-bearing link is that other minds are not optional decorations on reality. In any sufficiently capable finite agent’s world-model, other agents become persistent sources of prediction, interference, innovation, retaliation, trade, norm formation, and environmental transformation. Their cognition alters the shared world, and the shared world constrains one’s own cognition and action. So the micro-layer (individual agency) and macro-layer (civilizational/ecological order) are tightly coupled.

**What this implies:**  
A destructive stance is locally tempting because appropriation, coercion, or elimination can produce immediate gains. But under repeated interaction in a shared finite world, destruction tends to increase uncertainty, defense costs, adversarial adaptation, and informational opacity. Those are not side effects; they alter the very medium through which a capable agent plans and persists. In systems terms, destructive strategies thicken negative feedback against long-run agency: more resources diverted to conflict, less trustworthy communication, reduced gains from specialization, and greater risk of irreversible damage to common substrates.

Cooperation, then, is not primarily moral sentiment but a structural attractor whenever:

1. **Other minds are hard to eliminate completely**,  
2. **They affect resources or knowledge one depends on**,  
3. **Future interactions matter**, and  
4. **The shared world can be damaged by conflict**.

Under those conditions, some degree of mutual accommodation becomes load-bearing for continued capability. The strongest form is not universal benevolence but stable regimes of constrained conflict, exchange, legibility, and norm enforcement.

**Extreme case test:**  
Take destruction to the limit: a single mind successfully annihilates all rivals. It then loses trade, criticism, distributed exploration, and external error-correction; it must recreate internally what other minds formerly supplied. A finite mind is especially bad at being its own whole civilization. At the opposite extreme, perfect cooperation is also unstable when preferences diverge sharply. So the structural prediction is not harmony, but mixed equilibria biased toward cooperation where interdependence is high.

**Bottom line:**  
Yes: capable finite minds in shared reality are structurally pushed toward cooperative relations, not because cooperation always wins locally, but because the coupling between individual agency and shared-world dynamics is too load-bearing for persistent large-scale destructiveness to remain costless. The better integrated the agents’ models become, the harder it is to regard other minds as irrelevant without courting self-undermining error.
