## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Any capable finite mind sharing a physical reality with other capable finite minds is under a *conditional structural pressure* toward cooperation, not because cooperation is morally privileged, but because repeated interaction in a shared world makes future payoffs interdependent. Cooperative equilibria become attractive when encounters are ongoing, actions are at least partly observable, memory and reputation persist, and the gains from continued coexistence exceed the short-term gains from predation. Destructive relations dominate when these conditions are weakened.

From an iterated-game perspective, “sharing the same physical reality” is already a strong structural fact. It means minds compete and coordinate over overlapping resources, can affect one another’s future options, and cannot fully escape the consequences of current interaction. This creates a shadow of the future. In such settings, a finite mind that treats every encounter as one-shot leaves value on the table: trade, specialization, information exchange, mutual restraint, coalition-building, and reduced defense costs all become available under repeated play.

So the pressure is not simply “be nice.” It is: **preserve the conditions under which beneficial iteration remains possible.** Reciprocity, credible retaliation, forgiveness, signaling, and reputation become instrumentally valuable. Even self-interested minds may converge on norms like non-aggression, property recognition, honesty in limited domains, or negotiated boundaries, because these reduce the frequency of costly destructive spirals.

But the same perspective sharply limits the claim. Cooperation is structurally favored only where defection can be detected and answered. If a mind can defect anonymously, destroy rivals permanently, or seize resources in a way that ends future bargaining, then the game locally becomes one-shot and predation becomes rationally attractive. Likewise, if time horizons shrink, if asymmetries become extreme, or if one agent expects decisive dominance, the cooperative equilibrium unravels. In the extreme case of a final conflict with no meaningful future after victory or defeat, the shadow of the future disappears and so does the central mechanism generating cooperation.

Thus the answer is not “yes, any capable finite mind will tend toward cooperation,” but rather:

1. **Repeated coexistence structurally generates cooperative pressures.**  
2. **These pressures are strongest when futures are long, observability is high, and retaliation/reputation are credible.**  
3. **They collapse when interaction is effectively one-shot, opaque, or terminal.**

What this illuminates is that the key variable is not character but **game structure**. A shared physical reality does not automatically produce peace; it produces an iterated strategic environment. Whether that environment yields cooperation or destruction depends on whether minds can expect to keep meeting one another under conditions where today’s move changes tomorrow’s possibilities.
