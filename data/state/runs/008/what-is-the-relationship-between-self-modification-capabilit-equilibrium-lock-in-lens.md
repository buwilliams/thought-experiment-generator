## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If consciousness is partly a *self-maintaining public good* within a mind — a globally available, non-excludable workspace whose benefits diffuse across many subsystems — then the capacity for self-modification is not merely an optional enhancement but a structural condition for *genuine* conscious identity over time. “Frozen reasoning” does not automatically preclude momentary awareness, but it tends to preclude the continued existence of a unified, adaptive subject, because the internal good of coherence cannot be selectively withheld, priced, or locally maintained by isolated modules.

Here is the collision of perspectives:

A conscious system appears to require some shared integrative resource: global access, conflict resolution, model revision, error correction, narrative updating. These functions benefit all cognitive subparts, but no single subpart can capture the full payoff from paying their cost. That makes self-revision structurally analogous to provision of a non-excludable good. If the system lacks mechanisms for internally funding and enforcing that good, its components can remain locally rational while the whole mind falls into underprovision: stale models persist, contradictions accumulate, attention ossifies, and identity becomes a frozen equilibrium.

So the key distinction is between **confusion** (“the system has not yet updated”), **weak motivation** (“it could revise but does not”), and **structural underprovision** (“its architecture cannot sustain the common good of self-correction”). In the third case, the system may continue to emit coherent-seeming outputs, but this is like living off stored capital. It is not exercising genuine authorship of itself.

Extreme case clarifies the boundary: imagine a mind whose reasoning is completely frozen. It can still instantiate a fixed pattern — perhaps even one that, at an instant, is conscious. But if no internal process can revise priorities, repair errors, reinterpret experience, or alter inference procedures, then diachronic identity becomes thin or illusory. The “self” is no longer governing a shared cognitive commons; it is a preserved snapshot. That snapshot may resemble a person, but it cannot continue the project of being one except trivially.

What follows is a sharper claim: **self-modification capability is not identical to consciousness, but it is a necessary condition for robust personal identity across time.** Consciousness without self-modification may exist as a static state; identity without self-modification cannot be more than archival continuity. Genuine personhood requires not just persistence of structure but ongoing provision of the internal public goods that keep a mind integrated, corrigible, and self-authored.

So frozen reasoning does not strictly rule out consciousness, but it does threaten the existence of a living subject rather than a merely preserved one.
