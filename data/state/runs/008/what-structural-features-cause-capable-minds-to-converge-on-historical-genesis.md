## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Viable middle-ground relationships arise when a social-cognitive system inherits, and preserves, institutions of *partial separability* from prior problems of domination and fusion. The key structural feature is not “moderation” in the psychological sense, but an evolved architecture that solves an older problem: how can agents cooperate without surrendering error-correction?

The origin story matters. “Relationship” as a stable middle ground likely emerged as a solution between two bad ancestors: raw conflict, where disagreement is settled by force, and total merger, where disagreement disappears only because one perspective is absorbed, subordinated, or erased. What the middle-ground form replaced was not merely hostility, but also premodern hierarchies, kin absorption, doctrinal unity, and other arrangements where peace was purchased by suppressing independent agency. Modern reciprocal relationship inherits many assumptions from those forms — loyalty, mutual obligation, identity entanglement — but often without examining which inherited features destroy the very independence needed for genuine coordination.

So the structural features that produce viable convergence are those that keep persons *jointly connected but independently corrigible*. In systems terms, the relationship must have:

1. **Boundaries that prevent total fusion**  
   Each mind retains a domain of sovereign revision: private thought, exit options, asymmetric experimentation, protected dissent. Without this, agreement stops being informative because one side can no longer generate criticism.

2. **Feedback channels that prevent conflict escalation**  
   There must be low-cost ways to surface error before it becomes identity-threatening: norms of revisability, turn-taking, shared language for uncertainty, and mechanisms that convert grievances into information rather than weapons.

3. **Slack and buffering**  
   Relationships fail when every disagreement is high stakes. Time, material security, emotional margin, and institutionalized pauses allow correction without rupture. Slack is what keeps negative feedback stabilizing rather than explosive.

4. **Non-zero-sum shared projects**  
   Convergence is easier when the relationship is organized around something external both can improve together. This replaces the inherited assumption that relationship exists primarily to secure loyalty or sameness.

5. **Legible asymmetry without permanent subordination**  
   Real relationships contain unequal knowledge, need, or power. The middle ground becomes viable when asymmetry is explicit, limited, and revisable, rather than denied or naturalized.

What follows is that capable minds do **not** converge chiefly because they become more similar, more empathic, or more compromising. They converge when the structure of the relationship preserves the very differences that generate knowledge, while constraining the pathways by which difference turns into warfare or disappearance.

At the extreme cases this becomes clearest: total merger eliminates criticism; total conflict eliminates cooperation. Both destroy learning. The viable middle is therefore not a midpoint on a spectrum, but a specific architecture descended from historical attempts to solve the problem of cooperation among non-identical minds. Its hidden requirement is institutionalized separateness. Without that, “harmony” is often just domination in a more intimate form.
