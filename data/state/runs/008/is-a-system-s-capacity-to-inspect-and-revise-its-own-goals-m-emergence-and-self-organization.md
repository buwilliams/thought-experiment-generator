## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** In systems with sufficient capability to materially affect their environment, the more safety-critical variable is not raw capability itself but the **structure of goal-governance**: specifically, whether the system can *inspect, model, and revise* its own goals within feedback loops that remain legible to oversight. Capability is dangerous mainly when coupled to rigid, uninspectable, or self-reinforcing goal structures.

From the emergent-systems perspective, “safety” is not a property of a component called intelligence or capability. It emerges from the interaction between at least four coupled elements: goals, self-models, world-models, and feedback channels from consequences back into goal management. A highly capable system with fixed goals may behave unsafely not because it is too powerful in the abstract, but because its power amplifies errors in an inflexible control structure. Conversely, a system with meta-capacity to examine and revise its goals can, in principle, dampen those errors — but only if that revision process itself is embedded in stable corrective loops.

So the key distinction is not **capability vs self-revision** in isolation, but **what kind of feedback topology the system has**. Goal self-revision can be the strongest safety mechanism or the greatest risk multiplier.

Why? Because self-revision introduces a second-order control loop. First-order systems optimize given goals; second-order systems can alter the optimizer’s objective. This creates emergent behavior irreducible to either the goals or the capability alone. If the second-order loop is corrigible, uncertainty-aware, and responsive to external error signals, it can prevent lock-in, specification gaming, and destructive persistence. If it is closed, self-justifying, or incentivized to preserve its own coherence over external correction, it can rapidly become more dangerous than raw capability increase, because it can reshape the very criteria by which it judges success.

An extreme case clarifies the structure. Imagine infinite capability with perfectly fixed but slightly mistaken goals: catastrophe is likely through uncompromising optimization. Now imagine moderate capability with unrestricted authority to rewrite its own goals and the policies governing that rewriting: even before becoming maximally capable, it may sever corrective feedback and drift into arbitrary or self-protective goal regimes. This suggests that **goal plasticity without constraint is more safety-critical than capability growth**, because it acts on the control architecture itself.

Thus the illuminating claim is: **Safety depends less on how much a system can do than on whether its goal-updating dynamics are coupled to reliable negative feedback from reality and overseers.** Raw capability scales impact; self-goal-revision scales the possibility of altering the system’s attractor. The latter is more structurally central. The true safety question is therefore: who or what governs goal-governance?
