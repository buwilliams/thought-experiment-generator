### Why this follows from computational irreducibility
If other minds and the social-physical systems they participate in are computationally irreducible, then no finite agent can in general shortcut the process of forecasting their full responses. A destructive orientation — domination, permanent suppression, unilateral exploitation — depends on being able to predict consequences across many interacting agents, feedback loops, and time horizons. But in an irreducible system, those consequences can only be discovered by letting the system unfold. That makes destruction structurally brittle: local victories propagate through opaque dynamics and often generate novel adversaries, coordination against the destroyer, or damage to shared substrates the destroyer also depends on.

Cooperation, by contrast, does not require detailed prediction of everyone’s internal state. It works by installing **low-complexity generative constraints**: reciprocity, tolerance, trade, boundaries, credible commitments, error-correction, and norms against irreversible harm. These are not predictions of behavior but rules that shape interaction so that mutually beneficial patterns can emerge even when the fine-grained future is unknowable. In this sense, cooperation is a compression strategy for dealing with irreducible minds.

### What this illuminates
The key issue is not benevolence but **tractability**. A finite mind cannot cheaply compute the long-run effects of destruction in a world of other adapting minds. So the minimal viable policy is to prefer relations that preserve optionality, information flow, and shared infrastructure. Cooperation is favored because it is robust to predictive limits; destruction is disfavored because it amplifies the cost of those limits.

### Boundary cases
- **If a mind were effectively unopposed** and others were simple enough to model, destructive control might be stable.  
- **But as capability and environmental coupling increase**, other minds become more able to adapt, hide, retaliate, recombine, and alter the environment. Then irreducibility bites harder, and cooperation becomes more attractive.
- At the extreme, among many capable minds sharing deeply interdependent reality, attempts at total domination become equivalent to trying to compute and freeze an open-ended evolving system — an anti-optimization likely beyond any finite mind.

### Bottom line
So the pressure is real but not moral or universal. It is **structural**: in a shared reality populated by capable finite minds, computational irreducibility makes destructive strategies increasingly expensive, fragile, and self-undermining, while cooperative strategies function as simpler generative rules for surviving and benefiting under unavoidable uncertainty.
