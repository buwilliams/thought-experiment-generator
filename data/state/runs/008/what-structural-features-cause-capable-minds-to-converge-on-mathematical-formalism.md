## Conjecture

Let the relationship between two capable minds be modeled as a coupled dynamical system.

Each mind has:
- a state vector \(x_i\): beliefs, values, goals, habits
- a boundary operator \(B_i\): what can change without loss of identity
- an update rule \(U_i\): how it incorporates criticism, evidence, and the other mind’s influence
- a utility or viability function \(V_i\): conditions under which the mind can continue to function and endorse the relationship

The relationship itself is not just \(x_1+x_2\), but an interaction map \(R\) that governs exchange: communication rate, reversibility of commitments, conflict resolution, and division of decision rights.

We can now distinguish three attractor types:

1. **Destructive conflict**: coupling is too adversarial or too rigid. Small disagreements amplify through positive feedback, pushing one or both minds outside their viability region \(V_i>0\).

2. **Complete merger**: coupling is so strong, or boundaries so weak, that the two systems collapse into one effective decision process. Distinct error-correction is lost.

3. **Viable middle-ground**: the pair forms a stable composite system with persistent differentiation. They influence each other, but neither is absorbed nor broken.

**Conjecture:**  
Capable minds converge on viable middle-ground relationships when the interaction map preserves a specific invariant: **mutual error-correction under bounded influence**. Formally, the coupling must be strong enough to transmit useful information but weak enough to preserve independent model-updating.

This implies several structural features:

- **Semi-permeable boundaries**: each mind allows local revision but protects core identity constraints. If all variables are negotiable, merger occurs; if none are, conflict freezes.
- **Negative feedback dominating positive feedback**: disagreement triggers repair, clarification, or temporary decoupling rather than escalation. Stable middle ground requires damping.
- **Reversible low-cost experimentation**: the system can test accommodations without catastrophic lock-in. This lets the pair search the space of arrangements rather than fight over one-shot commitments.
- **Orthogonality or partial complementarity of goals**: if all goals are zero-sum, no stable middle exists; if all goals are identical, differentiation vanishes. Viable relationship space opens when some aims are shared and some are distinct.
- **Distributed sovereignty**: there exist conserved domains in which each mind has final authority. This maintains independent error-correcting capacity.
- **Meta-agreement on update rules**: even when object-level values differ, both accept procedures for criticism, renegotiation, and exit.

The key mathematical insight is that middle-ground is not a compromise point halfway between two states. It is a **stable region in phase space** where differentiation and coordination coexist. What matters is not moderation of opinion but architecture of coupling.

So the structural cause of healthy convergence is: **a relationship topology that maximizes information exchange subject to identity-preserving constraints**. Too little coupling yields alienation; too much yields absorption; badly signed feedback yields war. The viable middle is the regime where two minds remain two minds, and that very separateness improves the joint system’s capacity to detect and correct error.
