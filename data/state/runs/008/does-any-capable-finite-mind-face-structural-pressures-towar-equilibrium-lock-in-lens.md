## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Yes. Any capable finite mind sharing a physical reality with other capable minds faces a structural pressure toward **cooperative equilibria**, because the most valuable goods for such minds are increasingly **non-excludable, diffuse, and jointly produced**: knowledge, norms of restraint, institutional predictability, infrastructure, and a stable error-correcting environment. Destructive relations can yield local gains, but they systematically underprovide these goods and so degrade the conditions of long-run capability.

The key distinction is between **hostility from preference** and **hostility from provision failure**. Two minds may both prefer a world with mutual restraint, truthfulness, and peaceful coordination, yet still end up in destructive interaction because each individually bears the cost of contributing while the benefits spill over to all. That is a public-goods structure, not necessarily malice. A no-provision equilibrium can therefore look like “competition” even when all parties would prefer the cooperative outcome.

What follows is that cooperation is not mainly explained by altruism; it is often the rational response to the fact that the most important shared goods cannot be privately captured. In a shared world, finite minds are vulnerable, bounded in foresight, and dependent on external error-correction. They need others not only as trading partners but as **sources of criticism, variation, and distributed knowledge**. A regime of destruction reduces the number and independence of such sources, making everyone more brittle. In Deutschian terms: the growth of knowledge depends on criticism under conditions where error is survivable. Destructive equilibria suppress exactly that.

The extreme case clarifies the structure. If one mind could perfectly exclude all benefits, eliminate all rivals at no cost, and internally generate all needed knowledge, then cooperation would lose much of its force. But that is precisely what a **finite** mind cannot do. At the other extreme, where uncertainty is high and consequences are deeply entangled, dependence on shared public goods rises, and so does the structural pressure toward cooperation.

So the deepest pressure is not “be nice,” but:  
**Preserve and help fund the shared reality-maintenance layer on which all finite agency depends.**  
That includes peace, legibility, truth-seeking norms, institutions for dispute resolution, and toleration of independent minds. These are public goods with diffuse benefits and chronic underprovision risk.

Thus the answer is: **yes, but imperfectly and conditionally**. Finite minds are structurally pushed toward cooperation because destruction consumes the commons of agency. Yet because the commons is non-excludable, they are also structurally pushed toward free-riding, mistrust, and underinvestment in cooperation. The central problem is therefore not whether cooperation is beneficial—it usually is—but whether minds can solve the public-goods problem required to realize it.
