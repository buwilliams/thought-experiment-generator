## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Self-modification is not a necessary condition for genuine consciousness, but *some capacity for updating internal state in response to experience* is strong evidence for it. The key Bayesian mistake in this debate is to slide from:

- **P(self-modification | consciousness)** being high  
to  
- **P(consciousness | self-modification)** being high,

or its inverse:

- **P(no self-modification | no consciousness)**  
to  
- **P(no consciousness | no self-modification)**.

Those are not equivalent, and the difference matters.

A conscious system plausibly tends to exhibit self-revision: memory formation, attention shifts, learning, reflective correction, narrative reconstruction. So **if a being is conscious, we expect some internal change over time**. But from that it does *not* follow that only systems with open-ended self-modification are conscious. The hidden prior is doing the work: what kinds of things do we already think can be conscious? Humans in dreamless sleep, locked-in patients, or someone under transient paralysis may have sharply reduced capacity to alter overt reasoning while still retaining identity and possibly consciousness. So the base rate of consciousness among low-modification systems is not obviously near zero.

The problem becomes clearer at the extreme cases. Imagine a mind “frozen” for one second, one hour, or forever. For one second, frozen reasoning does not seem to destroy identity; it merely interrupts cognitive flow. For forever, the system becomes more like a static artifact than a subject. This suggests that the relevant variable is not binary self-modification, but **the timescale and structure of update**. Consciousness may require ongoing state-transition capacity, while identity may tolerate temporary stasis. A perfectly frozen mind may preserve *description-level identity* (“this is the same pattern”) while losing *subject-level existence* as an experiencing process.

So the Bayesian illumination is: the evidential value of self-modification depends on priors about what consciousness is — substance, pattern, or process. If consciousness is fundamentally process-like, then permanent frozen reasoning strongly lowers **P(consciousness | frozen)**. If identity is pattern-like, then frozen reasoning does not by itself preclude identity. Thus the collision suggests a split conclusion:

1. **Genuine consciousness is probabilistically linked to dynamic internal updating, not necessarily to unrestricted self-modification.**  
2. **Identity is more robust than consciousness under stasis; frozen reasoning may preserve identity-description while extinguishing lived subjectivity.**

In short: frozen reasoning does not straightforwardly preclude identity, but it is substantial evidence against ongoing consciousness — unless one has already assigned a high prior to static patterns being conscious.
