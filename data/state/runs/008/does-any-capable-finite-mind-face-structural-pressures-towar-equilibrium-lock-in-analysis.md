## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Yes. Any sufficiently capable *finite* mind embedded with other minds in the same physical reality faces a strong structural pressure toward at least *bounded cooperation*, because destructive strategies tend to generate equilibrium traps that waste scarce predictive, energetic, and strategic resources. But this pressure is not toward universal benevolence; it is toward stable regimes of mutual toleration, coordination, and rule-formation whenever conflict is costly and future interaction cannot be cleanly terminated.

**Why this follows from equilibrium lock-in analysis:**  
A finite mind cannot optimize against every other mind at once. It has limited computation, limited energy, limited ability to monitor, and limited ability to eliminate all rivals permanently. In a shared reality, other minds are not just obstacles; they are persistent sources of uncertainty, retaliation, alliance formation, imitation, and environmental modification. This creates a strategic structure where unilateral defection often worsens the long-run position of the defector unless others also remain passive.

The key lock-in is this: if each mind treats others destructively by default, all are pushed into high-cost vigilance, preemption, concealment, arms races, and destruction of potential gains from trade, specialization, and knowledge exchange. No single agent can safely exit that regime alone if others remain hostile. So destructive equilibrium can exist. But precisely because it is costly for all, there is structural pressure to search for credible moves that shift the game: signaling restraint, building enforcement mechanisms, creating boundaries, norms, contracts, or institutions.

Thus the relevant claim is not “minds become nice.” It is: **shared reality plus finitude makes pure predation systematically self-undermining except in special cases**.

**Extreme cases clarify the boundary:**
- If one mind is effectively omnipotent relative to others, cooperation pressure weakens; domination may be stable.
- If interactions are one-shot, anonymous, and externalities are negligible, destructive exploitation can be locally rational.
- If rivals can be perfectly eliminated at low cost, cooperation pressure falls.
- But as soon as minds are mutually vulnerable, repeatedly interacting, unable to secure total victory, and capable of producing positive-sum gains, cooperative structure becomes a major equilibrium attractor.

**What this illuminates:**  
The main obstacle to cooperative relationships is not usually bad values or irrationality alone. It is equilibrium lock-in: all parties may prefer a safer, richer cooperative order, yet remain stuck in defensive hostility because no one can unilaterally make cooperation safe. Therefore, the deepest structural pressure on finite minds is not directly toward goodwill but toward **mechanisms that make cooperation an equilibrium**: credible commitments, transparency, deterrence, norms, modular separation, and institutions for dispute resolution.

So the answer is: **yes, but conditionally**. Finite minds in shared reality are pressured toward cooperation not because harmony is morally inevitable, but because under common conditions destruction is an unstable and expensive way to coexist.
