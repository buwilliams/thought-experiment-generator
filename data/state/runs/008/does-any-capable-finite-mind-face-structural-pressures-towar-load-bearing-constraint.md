## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Yes. Any sufficiently capable *finite* mind sharing a single physical reality with other minds faces a structural pressure toward at least *conditional cooperation*, because the details that make finite agency possible also make unilateral domination expensive, fragile, and information-poor. Destructive relations remain possible, but they are not the default attractor for capability under shared-world constraints.

**Load-bearing details:**

1. **Finite mind** is crucial.  
If a mind were effectively unbounded in prediction, control, and resources, it might not need others except instrumentally. But a finite mind has bounded attention, model capacity, search depth, energy, and time. Changing this detail changes everything: finitude creates uncertainty about others, vulnerability to error, and dependence on external knowledge. That forces a tradeoff: destroy, control, ignore, bargain, or cooperate. Cooperation becomes structurally attractive because it can convert other minds from sources of unpredictability into distributed cognition.

2. **Shared physical reality** is crucial.  
If minds lived in causally isolated worlds, their relation would be optional. But sharing one reality means interference is unavoidable: they compete for resources, alter common environments, and generate side effects for one another. This makes other minds part of each mind’s problem-setting environment. Once that is true, reducing conflict costs and stabilizing expectations has real value.

3. **Capability** matters in a non-obvious way.  
A minimally capable mind may simply react locally. Greater capability increases both the power to harm and the ability to model second-order effects. Past some threshold, a capable mind can see that destructive strategies provoke defensive adaptation, arms races, concealment, sabotage, and information degradation. Those costs are load-bearing: if others can respond at all, coercion tends to manufacture adversarial optimization. Cooperation, by contrast, can elicit truthful signaling, specialization, and lower enforcement overhead.

4. **Other minds are epistemic resources, not just rivals.**  
This is also load-bearing. In a complex world, no finite mind can generate all useful knowledge alone. Other minds contain models, discoveries, perspectives, and error-correction capacities inaccessible from one viewpoint. Destroying them may remove threats, but it also destroys explanatory and practical resources. If this detail is removed—if others contribute nothing knowable or useful—then the pressure toward cooperation weakens sharply.

**What follows:**

- The pressure is not toward universal benevolence, but toward **institutions, norms, and interfaces** that make others legible, predictable, and mutually beneficial.  
- The strongest cooperative pressure appears where:
  - the world is complex,
  - power is distributed,
  - verification is costly,
  - and innovation matters.

**Extreme-case test:**  
At the limit of a perfectly simple world, cooperation may be unnecessary. At the limit of absolute asymmetry, domination may locally win. But in the broad middle—finite minds, complex shared reality, reciprocal vulnerability, and persistent uncertainty—cooperation is load-bearing in the explanatory structure of successful agency itself.

So the deepest claim is: **for finite capable minds, cooperation is not mainly a moral add-on; it is a structural response to the epistemic and control limits imposed by reality and by one another.**
