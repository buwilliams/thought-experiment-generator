## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Model each mind as a finite agent \(M_i\) with bounded memory, bounded compute, and a utility-like objective defined over trajectories of a shared physical state \(S_t\). Let interaction occur in a repeated game with partial observability, resource competition, and the possibility of prediction, communication, and commitment. Then, for a broad class of such systems, there is a structural attractor toward **conditional cooperation** whenever three inequalities hold:

1. **Future coupling is nontrivial:** the expected value of future interaction is large relative to one-shot gains from predation.  
2. **Mutual modeling is possible:** each agent can represent the other as a policy-bearing system rather than mere noise.  
3. **Destructive conflict is dissipative:** fighting consumes scarce negentropy, attention, time, or optionality faster than negotiated coexistence.

In mathematical terms, destructive relations are favored when payoff is dominated by immediate extraction. Cooperative relations are favored when the game contains **positive-sum transformations**: information-sharing, specialization, trade, norm formation, joint defense, and predictability. These create surplus by changing the feasible set, not merely reallocating fixed goods.

The key invariant is this: in shared reality, agents are not independent variables. Their actions are coupled through the same conservation laws, bottlenecks, and causal channels. If two finite minds occupy the same world, then “winning against the other” often damages the common substrate on which both depend. Destruction tends to reduce the dimensionality of future possibility; cooperation tends to preserve or enlarge it. For finite agents, preserved optionality has structural value because they cannot perfectly foresee which future branch is best.

Extreme cases clarify the boundary. If resources are truly zero-sum, future interaction negligible, and one agent can exterminate another at near-zero cost, then destructive behavior may be structurally stable. But this is a knife-edge regime: it requires low interdependence, low retaliation risk, low uncertainty value, and negligible gains from division of cognitive labor. As soon as any of these assumptions relax, cooperation becomes favored because finite minds are ignorance-limited. Other minds become sources of world-model compression, error-correction, innovation, and insurance against one’s own boundedness.

So the conjecture is not “minds become nice.” It is sharper: **for capable finite minds in a shared physical reality, the generic pressure is toward institutions and strategies that convert raw strategic rivalry into legible, conditional, information-rich cooperation, because such arrangements dominate destructive relations in preserving resources, optionality, and epistemic gain under finitude.** Destruction remains an ever-present local equilibrium, but cooperation is the more scalable solution whenever agents expect to keep inhabiting the same constrained world.
