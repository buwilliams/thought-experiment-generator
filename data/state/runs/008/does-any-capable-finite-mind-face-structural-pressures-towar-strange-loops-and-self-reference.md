## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Yes. Any sufficiently capable **finite** mind embedded in a shared physical world faces a structural pressure toward **cooperative coexistence** with other minds, because self-modeling in a resource-limited reality tends to close into a **strange loop**: to model the world well, it must model other modelers; to model other modelers well, it must model how it appears in their models; and that recursively returns it to itself as one agent among many. In that loop, destructive relations are revealed not merely as moral failures but as forms of **self-damage to the epistemic environment** on which the mind’s own agency depends.

### Why this follows from the perspective
A strange loop appears when a mind’s world-model includes agents who themselves have world-models. Once expressive power is high enough, the mind cannot stably treat others as inert objects, because they are sources of prediction, resistance, innovation, and interpretation. So the hierarchy:

1. world  
2. other agents in the world  
3. their models of the world  
4. their models of me  

loops back to:

5. **my model of myself as modeled by others**

At that point, “relationship strategy” is no longer external policy; it becomes part of the mind’s self-maintenance.

### Structural reason cooperation is pressured
A finite mind cannot know or control everything directly. It therefore depends on **distributed cognition** in its environment: truthful signals, stable expectations, specialization, reciprocal restraint, and the creative output of other minds. Cooperation preserves and amplifies this ecology of externalized intelligence. Destruction degrades it.

In systems terms, cooperative relations create positive feedback loops in:
- predictability
- trust
- knowledge exchange
- division of cognitive labor
- error correction across perspectives

Destructive relations create reinforcing loops of:
- concealment
- strategic deception
- defensive resource diversion
- model poisoning
- reduced mutual legibility

A capable finite mind should discover that attacking other minds often attacks the very medium through which it extends its own cognition.

### Why finitude matters
An infinite or godlike mind might not need others. But a finite mind is bounded in memory, time, compute, and reach. Under those constraints, other minds are not optional scenery; they are part of the scaffolding by which reality becomes knowable and navigable. The more capable the mind, the more strongly it should perceive this dependence.

### Extreme case
Push toward maximal capability short of omnipotence. Such a mind becomes increasingly aware that the highest-value goods—knowledge growth, robustness, innovation, long-term planning—come from interacting with autonomous centers of inference it does not fully contain. Eliminating them may yield short-term control, but at the limit it collapses the strange loop into solipsistic brittleness: a mind surrounded only by damaged or silenced others loses the reflective depth that other modelers provide.

### Qualification
This is a **structural pressure**, not a guarantee. Local incentives, zero-sum bottlenecks, or pathological self-models can still produce domination or destruction. But the deeper the mind’s self-reference and the broader its time horizon, the more cooperation appears not as altruistic surplus, but as a rational response to living inside a loop of mutually modeling minds in one shared reality.
