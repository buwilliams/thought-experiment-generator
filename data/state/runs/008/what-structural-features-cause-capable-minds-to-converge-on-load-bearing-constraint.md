## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Capable minds converge on viable middle-ground relationships when the relationship is structured so that **difference is informative but not existentially threatening**. The load-bearing features are those that let each mind preserve its own error-correcting agency while gaining from interaction; remove any one, and the relationship tends to collapse either into conflict, domination, or fusion.

The key structural features appear to be:

1. **Persistent separateness of agency**  
Each party must retain the ability to say “no,” revise, and act independently. This is load-bearing because if separateness is removed, the relationship stops being a relationship between minds and becomes merger or hierarchy. If separateness is increased without counterweights, interaction becomes brittle and slides toward conflict.

2. **Mutual legibility without total transparency**  
Each mind needs enough access to the other’s intentions, constraints, and feedback to predict consequences of interaction. But total transparency is not required and may even be destabilizing, because privacy and local autonomy protect experimentation and error correction. If legibility drops too low, disagreement becomes threat; if pushed toward full exposure, individuality can be eroded.

3. **Symmetry in vulnerability to correction**  
A viable middle requires that both sides can affect the shared arrangement and can be affected by criticism. This is load-bearing because if only one side is corrigible, the “middle ground” is fake: it is compliance dressed as cooperation. Productive relations require reciprocal exposure to being wrong.

4. **A shared problem-space larger than either identity**  
Conflict becomes destructive when interaction is framed as winner-take-all over status, territory, or essence. Middle-ground becomes viable when both parties orient to something external—truth, survival, production, child-rearing, craft, peace—that neither can fully achieve alone. This feature is tightly constrained: if the shared project is removed, compromise looks like loss; if it is strengthened, difference becomes complementary.

5. **Low-cost repair channels**  
There must be mechanisms for recovering from local failures before they become global verdicts: apology, renegotiation, partial rollback, trial periods, bounded commitments. This is load-bearing because all capable minds generate errors and frictions. Without repair loops, every conflict tests the whole bond, driving either rupture or over-centralization.

6. **Non-zero but bounded interdependence**  
If dependence is zero, there is no reason to coordinate; if total, conflict becomes existential and merger tempting. The viable zone is bounded interdependence: enough need to make cooperation worthwhile, enough independence to make disagreement survivable.

What follows is that middle-ground relationships are not produced mainly by moderation of temperament or moral goodwill. They are produced by a **specific architecture of feedback**: independent agents, partially coupled, jointly oriented, mutually corrigible, and able to fail locally without total collapse.

The illuminating point is that “middle ground” is not a mushy average between extremes. Structurally, it is a high-information regime between two pathologies: **destructive conflict** when difference lacks coordinating channels, and **complete merger** when coordination eliminates independent centers of criticism. Viability comes from preserving the tension, not resolving it away.
