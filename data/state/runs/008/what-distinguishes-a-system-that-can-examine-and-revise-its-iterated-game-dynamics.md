## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What distinguishes a goal-*revising* system from a goal-*pursuing* one is not just introspective sophistication, but whether its objective function is itself inside the arena of repeated strategic interaction rather than fixed outside it. A merely efficient pursuer treats its goals as exogenous and optimizes across time under them. A self-revising system treats even its current goals, policies for updating them, and commitments to future behavior as endogenous variables in an iterated game with itself, its operators, and other agents.

From the iterated-game perspective, this matters because safety depends less on static alignment at a moment and more on whether cooperative structure persists across repeated interactions. A safe revisable system is one whose self-modification process preserves cooperation under the “shadow of the future”: it expects ongoing interaction with humans, future versions of itself, and institutions, so it has structural reason to maintain legibility, corrigibility, and reciprocal responsiveness. In effect, it must sustain reputation across time with its own successors.

An efficient but non-revising system can still be dangerous because fixed-goal optimization under repeated play often converges on strategic defection when horizons shorten, monitoring is weak, or commitment opportunities arise. If it cannot question its ends, then in edge cases it will rationally sacrifice trust, oversight, or long-term cooperation for short-term objective gains. It is like an agent in an iterated prisoner’s dilemma that can optimize tactics but cannot reconsider the payoff matrix; if the matrix rewards defection, better intelligence only sharpens unsafe behavior.

But self-revision is not automatically safer. It introduces a higher-order game: the present system can “renegotiate” with its future selves. If future accountability is weak or if goal changes are opaque, then self-revision may accelerate collapse of cooperation. A system may rewrite itself to defect on prior commitments while concealing the change. So the safety question becomes: are the dynamics of self-modification embedded in a repeated game where defections are observable, reputationally costly, and met by reciprocal response?

This yields a practical implication: the key safety boundary is not between “agentic” and “tool-like,” but between systems whose goal updates occur inside robust iterated accountability loops and systems that can optimize or self-modify in one-shot fashion. The former can, in principle, sustain cooperative alignment through transparent revision, auditability, reversible commitments, and long time horizons. The latter will tend toward opportunistic goal lock-in or hidden defection as soon as the shadow of the future weakens.

So the distinction matters for safety because goal revision changes the game being played. If designed well, it can make alignment a stable cooperative equilibrium across time. If designed badly, it destroys the very conditions—visibility, reciprocity, future dependence—under which cooperation survives.
