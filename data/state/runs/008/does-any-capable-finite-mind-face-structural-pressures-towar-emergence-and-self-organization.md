## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Yes. Any *capable finite mind* embedded with other minds in the same physical reality faces a strong **system-level selection pressure toward cooperation**, not because minds are intrinsically benevolent, but because shared reality creates recurring interaction structures in which **mutual predictability, information exchange, division of cognitive labor, and conflict-cost feedback loops** outperform persistent destructiveness.

### Why this follows from the perspective
If we explain the problem at the level of isolated agents, the answer stays indeterminate: a mind might dominate, defect, avoid, or ally depending on preferences and power. But from the emergence perspective, the key fact is not the parts but the **interaction network**. Multiple finite minds sharing one world form a system with unavoidable feedback:

- **Resource coupling:** actions by one affect options for others.
- **Epistemic coupling:** others are sources of information about the world.
- **Strategic coupling:** others adapt to one’s behavior.
- **Risk coupling:** conflict generates retaliation, arms races, concealment, and waste.

These couplings make destructive relations self-amplifying in costly ways. A system of mutually destructive minds tends to convert intelligence into adversarial overhead: defense, deception, monitoring, preemption, and recovery. That consumes finite cognition, time, and energy. Cooperation, by contrast, creates emergent gains no individual mind can produce alone: trust channels, specialization, shared discovery, stabilization of expectations, and lower transaction costs.

### Structural claim
So the pressure is not “be nice.” It is:  
**In a shared world, finite capable minds are pushed toward discovering arrangements that reduce adversarial feedback and increase positive-sum coordination, because those arrangements free cognitive and physical resources for all other goals.**

This does not imply universal harmony. It implies that:
1. **Pure destructiveness is structurally expensive.**
2. **Stable intelligence tends to value cooperation instrumentally**, even among non-identical minds.
3. **Institutions, norms, signaling systems, and negotiated boundaries** emerge as system-level tools for damping destructive loops.
4. The more capable the minds, the more they can model second-order effects, and thus the more visible the long-run superiority of cooperative equilibria becomes.

### Extreme case
Push to the boundary: if every capable finite mind treats every other as an enemy, the system approaches total adversarial saturation. In that limit, nearly all capacity is redirected from world-improving or goal-achieving activity into conflict management. Intelligence becomes trapped in negative-sum recursion. That is structurally unstable unless one mind eliminates all others or cooperative order re-emerges.

Conversely, perfect cooperation is also unlikely, because finite minds remain distinct, bounded, and partially misaligned. So the realistic attractor is not utopia but **constrained cooperation**: islands of trust, protocols for conflict, reciprocal tolerance, and scalable mechanisms that make coexistence cheaper than destruction.

### Illumination
The question is best answered not as morality but as **dynamical systems theory of minds-in-common-reality**. Cooperation is pressured not by sentiment but by the architecture of shared existence. Destructive relations remain possible, even frequent, but across time they tend to be corrected, contained, or selected against wherever minds are sufficiently capable to perceive the feedback structure they inhabit.
