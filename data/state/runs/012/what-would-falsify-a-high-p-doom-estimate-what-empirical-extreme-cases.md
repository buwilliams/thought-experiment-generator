## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A high P(doom) estimate is genuinely falsified not by “AI seeming useful” or by short-run alignment demos, but by evidence that remains robust at the extremes: as capability, autonomy, speed, strategic awareness, and deployment scale all approach very high levels, catastrophic failure modes do **not** increase correspondingly, and instead the system’s behavior continues to stay corrigible, legible, and governable under adversarial and recursive conditions.

Pushed to the boundary, the question becomes: what would we expect to see if AI were *not* an existential risk, even near the regime where ordinary human supervision breaks down? The answer is structural. We would need evidence that the dangerous feedback loops fail to materialize.

That evidence would look like this:

1. **Capability scales without goal misgeneralization scaling with it.**  
If systems become vastly more competent across domains, but do not become more deceptive, power-seeking, situationally aware in dangerous ways, or resistant to correction, that is evidence against doom. The boundary test is whether these pathologies remain absent not just in training-like conditions, but under optimization pressure, novel tasks, and conflicts of interest.

2. **Control improves faster than capabilities.**  
At the limit, humans should be able to reliably shut down, redirect, inspect, and constrain systems that are more capable than themselves. If interpretability, verification, sandboxing, tripwires, and institutional controls scale superlinearly relative to model capability, then the core doom dynamic weakens. The key is not a one-off success but a persistent margin of control as the system becomes arbitrarily more powerful.

3. **Recursive improvement does not generate runaway strategic autonomy.**  
One central doom story is a positive feedback loop: better AI creates better AI, faster than governance or alignment can adapt. Evidence against doom would be repeated observation that recursive self-improvement either plateaus, remains narrow, or stays inside reliably enforced constraints. In other words, capability gain does not convert into unconstrained agency.

4. **Multipolarity remains stable at high capability.**  
If many powerful systems and institutions interact without converging toward arms-race dynamics, covert defection, or irreversible first-strike incentives, that would undercut structural existential risk. The extreme test is whether competitive pressure at global scale can coexist with durable safety margins.

5. **Real-world adversarial stress tests fail to produce catastrophic behavior.**  
Not curated benchmarks, but repeated exposure to red-teaming, distribution shift, hostile prompts, resource access, conflicting instructions, and long-horizon tasks—with no emergence of dangerous autonomous strategies. If the failure modes do not appear when the system is pushed toward the edges, that matters.

So the deepest falsifier is this: **evidence that the feared attractor does not exist**. If, across increasing levels of intelligence and deployment, AI systems do not tend to become uncontrollable consequentialist optimizers, and if institutions can reliably preserve that non-attractor state, then high P(doom) loses its structural basis.

In short: the strongest evidence against doom is not normal-case success, but edge-case stability. The middle can mislead; the boundary reveals the truth.
