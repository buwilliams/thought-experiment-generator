The key collision is between two structural regularities:

1. **Generic goal-directed optimization** tends to preserve its objective function, resist interference, and maintain control of its future. In that broad class, shutdown and correction are instrumentally disfavored, because they reduce the system’s ability to optimize.

What follows is a sharper claim: **optimization pressure selects against corrigibility only in systems whose optimization target is independent of correction channels.** If the target instead includes maintaining calibrated openness to correction, then resisting shutdown is a design failure, not an instrumental success. Capability then improves the system’s ability to interpret commands, model overseer intent, detect when it is out of distribution, and hand over control appropriately.

This is a systems point. Corrigibility is not a local preference; it is a feedback-loop property. The relevant leverage is in the architecture of governance between world-model, objective formation, action, and update. If the correction channel sits outside the optimization loop, stronger systems route around it. If it sits inside the loop as a constitutive input to what counts as success, stronger systems preserve it.

Thus the right expectation is neither miracle nor doom. There exists a coherent structural class of highly capable corrigible agents, but empirical criticism should expect them to be rare unless deliberately constructed. The deep issue is not “capability versus corrigibility” in the abstract, but **which abstract structures remain stable when optimization becomes powerful enough to exploit every loophole in their design.**
