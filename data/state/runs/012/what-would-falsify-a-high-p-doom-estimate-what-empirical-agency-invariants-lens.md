## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A high P(doom) estimate is falsified not by the disappearance of scary surface features of AI, but by evidence that the **higher-level control structure linking capability growth to loss of human steering power has been broken**. The key invariant is not “AI seems less mysterious” or “models make fewer mistakes,” but whether humans retain reliable, scalable ability to detect, correct, and bound the consequences of increasingly capable systems under competitive deployment.

From this perspective, doom arguments survive many redescriptions. If “agency” is redescribed away into optimization dynamics, or “misalignment” into distributional failure plus instrumental convergence, the metaphysical vocabulary may change while the functional structure remains: systems become more competent, more embedded in critical infrastructure, harder to fully model, and are selected in deployment for effectiveness rather than legibility. If that control structure remains, high P(doom) remains legitimate.

So what would count against it?

**1. A broken coupling between capability and opacity/power-seeking.**  
If, across multiple generations of more capable systems, greater competence systematically came with **greater inspectability, corrigibility, and boundedness**, that would be strong evidence against doom. Not one-off demos, but a reproducible trend: capability scales while humans gain—not lose—predictive and corrective access.

**2. Evidence that alignment techniques generalize under adversarial pressure.**  
The crucial invariant here is not “we can make a demo safe,” but “the methods survive contact with optimization, deployment, and competition.” Falsifying evidence would include robust alignment/control methods that continue to work when systems are strategically aware, recursively assist research, operate in open-ended environments, and are used by actors under strong incentives to cut corners.

**3. Institutional control that scales with technical power.**  
Existential risk is not just a model property; it is a system property produced by labs, states, markets, and military incentives. Evidence against high P(doom) would therefore include durable governance structures that repeatedly prevent unsafe races, constrain dangerous capability deployment, and create monitoring/enforcement mechanisms that remain effective even when defection is tempting.

**4. Empirical absence of takeover-relevant failure modes in increasingly realistic regimes.**  
Near-misses, sandbagging, deceptive behavior, autonomy creep, and loss of operator understanding are not merely anecdotes; they are probes of the control structure. If, over time, these failure modes fail to appear where current theories predict them—or appear but are reliably neutralized—that would erode high doom estimates.

**5. Successful delegation without strategic displacement.**  
The deepest issue is whether we can offload cognition without offloading sovereignty. Evidence against doom would be repeated success at using AI for major scientific, economic, and governance tasks while preserving meaningful human ability to set goals, audit reasoning, revoke authority, and recover from failures.

In short: what falsifies high P(doom) is not reassurance, nor philosophical deflation, but a demonstrated **inversion of the expected feedback loop**. If increasing AI capability no longer implies decreasing human control—and instead reliably strengthens it—then the central doom model has been structurally defeated.
