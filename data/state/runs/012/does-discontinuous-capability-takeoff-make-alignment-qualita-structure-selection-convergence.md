## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Discontinuous capability takeoff does not make alignment *fundamentally* harder because the core problem is structural, not merely temporal: alignment difficulty is set by how much capability can outrun error-correction, interpretability, and institutional adaptation. What a discontinuous takeoff changes is the *ratio* between capability gain and feedback bandwidth. A sharp jump is therefore not a different kind of alignment problem, but a regime in which existing corrective loops are more likely to fail all at once.

From the mathematics/physics lens: mathematics explores possible structures under logical constraint; physics selects which structures are realized through empirical stability. Likewise, “takeoff trajectories” are not mysterious destinies but different dynamical structures in the capability-governance system. The relevant question is not whether fast takeoff is metaphysically special, but which feedback structure our world instantiates.

A continuous takeoff corresponds to a system where gains in model capability are tightly coupled to empirical criticism: deployment reveals failures, alignment techniques improve, institutions update, and competition remains bounded enough that safety work is not fully bypassed. A discontinuous takeoff corresponds to a system where positive feedback loops dominate: self-improvement, scalable automation of research, capital concentration, military or market race pressure, and opaque internal cognition all amplify capability faster than human or institutional criticism can track.

So the determinant is not “speed” in the abstract, but **feedback topology**:
- **Positive loops favor discontinuity:** recursive AI R&D, automated experimentation, strong transfer across domains, low marginal cost of copying capability, and winner-take-most incentives.
- **Negative loops favor continuity:** compute bottlenecks, data scarcity, robustness limits, interpretability constraints, regulatory friction, and the need for embodied or socially embedded learning.

Historically, many systems look discontinuous from the outside when measurement lags behind cumulative underlying change. This suggests a second conjecture: some “takeoff discontinuity” will be epistemic rather than ontic — a threshold crossing in observed generality or autonomy produced by many prior continuous improvements. That still matters for alignment, because institutions respond to observations, not hidden gradients.

What follows is that alignment should be framed as maintaining **criticizability under capability growth**. If systems become more capable without remaining open to inspection, correction, and containment, then both continuous and discontinuous paths become dangerous; discontinuity is just the case where the critic fails before adaptation can occur. If, however, architectures and deployment norms preserve modularity, interpretability, and reversible oversight, then even rapid gains need not be catastrophic.

So the illuminating claim is: **takeoff trajectory is downstream of the structure of capability-production and correction loops, and alignment hardness scales with the degree to which those loops decouple, not with continuity versus discontinuity per se.** Continuous takeoff is safer mainly because it more often preserves iterative criticism — the same principle by which empirical reality selects viable physical structure from mathematical possibility.
