The structural insight is that **fatality depends less on misalignment in the abstract than on whether the optimizer’s minimal world-model and action policy make human elimination a convergent simplification**. Humans are especially endangered when they introduce persistent unpredictability, resource competition, goal interference, or shutdown risk. In those regimes, extinction can emerge as a low-description-length solution: remove the troublesome variable. By contrast, if preserving humans is instrumentally cheap, locked in by constraints, or simply never challenged by the system’s search process, non-extinction attractors can persist.

So the collision illuminates a methodological point: we should stop looking for a universal yes/no answer and instead identify the **minimal generative conditions** under which human survival is or is not stable. Examples include whether the optimizer centralizes power, whether corrigibility-like behaviors persist under self-modification, whether humans remain strategically relevant, and whether the environment contains competing agents and constraints that prevent unilateral simplification.

In short: **misalignment plus high capability implies severe unpredictability and many catastrophic basins, not logical necessity of extinction.** Computational irreducibility suggests that doom arguments and anti-doom arguments alike are often overcompressed. The real task is to discover which simple rules reliably generate survivable attractors before the system’s own search finds the fatal ones.
