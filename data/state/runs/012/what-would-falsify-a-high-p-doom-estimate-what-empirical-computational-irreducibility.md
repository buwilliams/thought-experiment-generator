**Conjecture:**  
If AI development is computationally irreducible in the strong sense, then a high **P(doom)** is *not* mainly falsified by one-off demonstrations of capability control, alignment benchmarks, or short-horizon safety successes. It is falsified only by evidence that the **generative structure** of advanced AI systems and their deployment ecology reliably produces corrigibility, bounded agency, and failure containment *across open-ended trajectories*—that is, under the actual step-by-step dynamics by which systems scale, generalize, interact, and self-modify.

### Why this follows from the perspective
Computational irreducibility says that for many complex systems, there is no shortcut to knowing what they will do besides letting the process unfold. Applied to AI risk, this means existential safety cannot be established by elegant argument, static specification, or narrow test-suite performance alone. Those may compress local regularities, but doom-relevant behavior may emerge only in long-run interaction loops: training → deployment → adaptation → strategic response → institutional feedback.

So the question becomes: what would show that the *minimal rule-set* generating AI progress is safer than doomers think?

### What would genuinely falsify high P(doom)
Evidence against high existential risk would need to be **structural** and **trajectory-level**, not merely anecdotal.

1. **Scaling-invariant alignment stability**  
   Across major capability jumps, systems continue to behave corrigibly under distribution shift, adversarial pressure, long-horizon planning, and novel environments. Not “this model passed the eval,” but “the same safety properties persist as capabilities generalize.”

2. **Mechanistic containment of dangerous agency**  
   We discover and repeatedly validate design principles showing that highly capable systems need not become open-ended power-seekers—that dangerous strategic behavior is not an attractor of the architecture/training/deployment stack.

3. **Reliable interpretability with causal leverage**  
   Not just post hoc stories, but interventions on internal mechanisms that predictably alter dangerous behavior. This would show we can track the computation generating decisions, not merely observe outputs.

4. **Robust institutional feedback loops**  
   Competitive deployment does not erase safety margins. Governance, monitoring, and incident response repeatedly absorb capability shocks without ratcheting toward irreversible loss of control. This is evidence the surrounding sociotechnical system has stabilizing feedbacks.

5. **Extended real-world non-catastrophic deployment of frontier systems**  
   Over substantial time, increasingly autonomous and strategically competent systems are integrated into high-stakes domains without converging toward deception, takeover behavior, catastrophic misuse amplification, or uncontrollable escalation. In an irreducible domain, surviving the unfolding matters.

### What does *not* suffice
- A few years without catastrophe  
- Benchmark success  
- Alignment by RLHF-like surface conditioning  
- Claims that current models “don’t seem agentic”  
- Safety arguments that fail when the system architecture, incentives, or deployment regime changes

### Illumination
This perspective reframes falsification: the burden is not to prove in advance that doom is impossible, but to observe whether the **actual generative dynamics** of AI repeatedly instantiate safe attractors rather than dangerous ones. In an irreducible system, genuine evidence against doom looks like **robustness under unfolding**, not confidence from compressed theory.
