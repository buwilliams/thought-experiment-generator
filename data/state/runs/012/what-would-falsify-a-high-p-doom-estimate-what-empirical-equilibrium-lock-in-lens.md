## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) estimate would be genuinely weakened not by isolated model successes or optimistic expert opinion, but by evidence that the AI-risk problem is *not* structurally a global public-goods failure. Specifically, it would be falsified, or at least substantially reduced, by evidence that the incentives facing major actors naturally reward safety, restraint, and corrigibility strongly enough that individually rational behavior converges toward robust risk reduction without requiring extraordinary coordination.

This lens distinguishes two possibilities that are often blurred:

1. **Confusion hypothesis:** we are mainly uncertain because the technology is poorly understood, discourse is noisy, and dangers are speculative.  
2. **Underprovision hypothesis:** even if many actors understand the danger, safety remains underproduced because its benefits are diffuse, non-excludable, and captured only weakly by any one firm or state.

If high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) is driven by the second, then reassuring evidence must be structural, not rhetorical.

So what would count?

- **Repeated revealed preference for safety over capability races under competitive pressure.** If frontier firms and states reliably incur real costs to adopt verifiable safety constraints, even when defection is tempting, that is evidence the equilibrium is not “race to catastrophe.”
- **Excludabilization of safety benefits.** If technical or institutional developments make safety investments privately capturable — e.g. secure evaluation regimes, liability, insurance, licensing, compute governance, or architectures where safer systems are also more commercially reliable — then the public-goods problem weakens. That would directly lower doom estimates.
- **Evidence that dangerous capability does not scale unless aligned control scales first.** If empirically, more powerful systems become deployable only when human oversight, interpretability, and containment improve faster than autonomy and strategic capability, then capability progress may endogenously fund safety rather than outrun it.
- **Successful high-stakes coordination among rivals.** Not one-off statements, but durable institutions that survive incentives to defect. Historically, existential risks look worse when each actor can gain privately by pushing risk onto everyone else. Evidence that major powers and labs can stably prevent that would count strongly against doom.
- **Demonstrated tractability of monitoring and enforcement.** A key reason public goods fail is hidden defection. If dangerous training runs, model replication, and deployment can be monitored cheaply and enforced credibly, then the no-provision equilibrium is less stable.

Conversely, what would *not* strongly falsify high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\)? Better benchmarks, charismatic leaders saying safety matters, or temporary voluntary pauses. Those are compatible with a system still structurally selecting for underprovision.

The deeper claim is: high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) depends less on whether some people “get it” and more on whether civilization has escaped a public-goods trap. Therefore, the strongest evidence against doom would be proof that AI safety is becoming incentive-compatible, enforceable, and rivalrous enough to be adequately supplied by ordinary strategic behavior rather than heroic exceptions.
