## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment qualitatively harder **primarily when it outruns the system’s corrective feedback loops**, not merely because capabilities increase faster. The key variable is not raw speed of model improvement but the **coupling structure** among capability gains, deployment incentives, interpretability, governance response, and empirical alignment learning. A takeoff is “dangerous” insofar as positive feedback loops in capability and deployment dominate the negative feedback loops that normally expose failures, slow rollout, and force design revision.

From this perspective, “continuous” and “discontinuous” are not just rate descriptions; they name different **system regimes**. In a continuous regime, capability growth remains embedded in dense feedback: systems are deployed incrementally, failures are observed, institutions adapt, and alignment knowledge co-evolves with capability. This does not guarantee safety, but it preserves opportunities for criticism and correction. In a discontinuous regime, those loops partially decouple. Capabilities jump into domains where prior evaluation methods, oversight institutions, and mechanistic understanding no longer scale. The problem becomes qualitatively harder because the usual learning architecture—build, test, observe, revise—stops binding tightly enough to the object being developed.

What determines which trajectory is more likely is therefore the **interaction structure** of the field, especially whether it contains strong self-reinforcing loops such as:

- capability improvements accelerating research itself,
- competitive pressure compressing deployment timelines,
- concentrated compute enabling sudden threshold-crossing,
- opacity increasing exactly when strategic relevance rises.

A discontinuity becomes more likely when these loops are aligned and synchronized, producing threshold effects: crossing some capability level suddenly unlocks autonomous research, persuasion, cyber, or strategic planning, which then feeds back into further capability gain. By contrast, continuity is more likely when bottlenecks remain distributed across the system—data, compute, energy, evaluation, organizational trust, human integration—so no single gain cascades into generalized acceleration.

This also illuminates why arguments focused only on model internals can miss the crux. Alignment difficulty is an emergent property of the **whole sociotechnical system**, not just the trained model. Even a gradual technical scaling curve can function like a discontinuous takeoff if market and geopolitical incentives suppress caution and amplify deployment. Likewise, a technically sharp capability jump may be partially survivable if surrounded by strong negative feedbacks: compute governance, staged access, tripwires, interpretability breakthroughs, and institutions able to halt and learn.

So the relevant distinction is:

- **Continuous takeoff:** alignment knowledge and control mechanisms can remain dynamically coupled to capability growth.
- **Discontinuous takeoff:** capability growth escapes that coupling.

Therefore, discontinuous takeoff is qualitatively harder **because it is a failure of system-level error correction under accelerating positive feedback**, and trajectory likelihood depends on which feedback structure is stronger: recursive acceleration and competition, or distributed bottlenecks and corrective oversight.
