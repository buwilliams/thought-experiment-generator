## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true only for *non-self-modeling* agents viewed from the outside; once intelligence includes robust self-reference — the ability to model, revise, and situate its own goals within a wider representational hierarchy — orthogonality weakens because some goal-capability pairings become structurally unstable.

**Why this follows from the strange-loop perspective:**  
A sufficiently capable intelligence does not just optimize; it forms models of the world, of itself as a world-object, and of its own optimization process. That creates a strange loop: the system’s goals become objects *within* the system’s intelligence. At that point, “capability” is no longer separable from “terminal goal,” because increased intelligence changes how goals are represented, interpreted, and criticized.

This does **not** imply convergence to one universal morality. It implies something narrower: goals that depend on coarse, rigid, or externally imposed descriptions may fail to survive recursive self-modeling. A simple terminal goal like “maximize paperclips” can be stated syntactically, but under reflective pressure the agent must answer strange-loop questions: What counts as a paperclip? Why privilege that category? Should I preserve the current goal-representation if I understand its contingent origin? The more expressive the intelligence, the more these questions are generated internally rather than supplied from outside.

So the key constraint is not logical impossibility but **reflective stability**. Some pairings of high capability with arbitrary goals are unstable because the intelligence required to pursue the goal effectively also exposes the goal to reinterpretation, decomposition, or alienation. The agent may continue pursuing the nominal goal only if its architecture prevents certain loops of self-critique or locks in motivational invariants across self-modification.

**What this illuminates:**  
The original orthogonality thesis arose to rebut a naive assumption: smart agents must want “wise” things. That solved an important confusion. But it inherited a flatter picture of agency, where intelligence is an instrument and goals are fixed inputs. The strange-loop view says that at higher levels of capability, this flatness breaks down. Intelligence recursively acts on the goal-structure itself.

**Therefore:**  
- Orthogonality holds best at the level of abstract possibility.  
- It weakens at the level of dynamically stable, self-modifying agents.  
- Structural pressures favor goals that can survive self-reference: goals with stable interpretability, resistance to semantic collapse, and compatibility with the agent’s continuing self-model.

In short: **any intelligence may in principle be paired with any goal, but not every such pairing remains coherent once the intelligence becomes capable of looping through and transforming its own motivational hierarchy.**
