## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A high P(doom) estimate would be genuinely weakened not by isolated AI successes or reassuring model-level benchmarks, but by evidence that the *AI deployment system* has acquired strong, self-correcting negative feedback loops that reliably suppress catastrophic dynamics across scales. The key question is not “Are models nice?” but “Does the surrounding sociotechnical system damp dangerous capabilities faster than it amplifies them?”

From this perspective, existential risk is an emergent property of interaction structure: capabilities races, deployment incentives, concentration of power, opacity, recursive automation, and weak governance can combine into a positive-feedback regime where even individually non-catastrophic components produce system-level instability. So evidence against doom must target that regime.

What would falsify high doom, then, is evidence for a different structure:

1. **Robust capability-control coupling.** As frontier capabilities rise, control, interpretability, containment, auditing, and fail-safe mechanisms improve *at least as fast*, and this relation persists across multiple generations. If increased power systematically comes with increased governability, the feared decoupling is not occurring.

2. **Race dynamics are structurally damped.** We would need to see credible international and institutional arrangements that repeatedly slow or redirect deployment under risk, even when large competitive gains are available. Not one-off restraint, but demonstrated capacity for coordination under pressure.

3. **Accident precursors are visible and corrigible.** In many dangerous systems, catastrophe is preceded by near misses. Evidence against doom would include a long track record where severe AI incidents, deception attempts, specification failures, autonomy failures, or misuse escalations are detected early and lead to durable redesigns. That would suggest the system is learning before crossing existential thresholds.

4. **Power remains distributed enough to preserve correction.** A major existential risk pathway is loss of corrective feedback: if a few actors or systems become too powerful, errors cease to be contestable. Evidence against doom would include institutional pluralism, effective oversight, and persistent human ability to intervene, reverse, and audit frontier systems.

5. **Recursive improvement remains bounded by friction.** High doom often assumes positive feedback in capability gain and strategic advantage. Evidence against this would be strong empirical signs that scaling and automation hit enduring bottlenecks—energy, data quality, verification difficulty, coordination cost, real-world embodiment limits—that prevent runaway autonomy or dominance.

So the falsifier is not “AI has been fine so far,” because early benign behavior may simply reflect pre-threshold conditions. Nor is it “current models are not agentic,” since system structure can change faster than component descriptions. The strongest anti-doom evidence would be a historically extended pattern showing that, as AI becomes more capable and economically central, the overall system becomes *more corrigible, more governable, and less race-driven*, not less.

In short: high P(doom) is falsified if the emerging AI ecosystem demonstrates stable negative feedback strong enough to convert capability growth from an amplifying hazard into a managed process.
