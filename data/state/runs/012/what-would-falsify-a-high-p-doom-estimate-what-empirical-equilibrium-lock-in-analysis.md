## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A high P(doom) estimate should be falsified not by isolated demonstrations of model usefulness or by temporary periods without catastrophe, but by evidence that the AI transition is *not* locked into an equilibrium where every major actor is compelled to increase capability and deployment despite preferring a safer outcome. The key question is structural: is existential risk being driven by a Nash-like trap, or by remediable incompetence?

If high P(doom) is correct in the strong sense, then one should expect a persistent strategic pattern: labs, states, and firms all say safety matters, yet each faces incentives to race, conceal capability, dilute standards, and deploy systems whose failure modes they do not fully understand. In that world, “better intentions” do little, because unilateral restraint is punished. The system reproduces danger even when participants recognize it.

So genuine evidence against high P(doom) would be evidence that this lock-in is absent or has been broken.

That evidence could take several forms:

1. **Verified coordination that survives competitive pressure.**  
   Not mere agreements, but durable international and inter-firm arrangements where actors actually accept slowed capabilities progress, intrusive monitoring, and meaningful penalties for defection — and the arrangement persists even when large gains from defection are available. This would show the race dynamic is not structurally inescapable.

2. **Repeated voluntary non-deployment of dangerous capabilities.**  
   If leading actors consistently discover highly profitable or strategically valuable capabilities and still refrain from deploying them, without being displaced by less cautious rivals, that is evidence the competitive equilibrium has changed. It would suggest restraint is individually survivable, not just collectively desirable.

3. **Reliable interpretability/control that closes the strategic gap.**  
   If systems become transparent enough that dangerous goal formation, deception, power-seeking, or loss of control can be detected and prevented before deployment in routine practice, then doom was not driven by an uncontrollable principal-agent trap. The burden is not “we have some safety tools,” but “we can robustly veto catastrophic behavior without relying on hope.”

4. **A history of capability shocks without escalation into loss of control.**  
   If over time there are many opportunities for destabilizing races, misuse, autonomy failures, or near-catastrophes, and institutions repeatedly absorb them by changing incentives rather than merely getting lucky, that is evidence against structural inevitability.

5. **Clear decoupling of advantage from frontier recklessness.**  
   If the actors who win economically and geopolitically are not the fastest deployers of opaque frontier systems, but those embedded in stronger safety regimes, then the payoff structure itself has changed. That would directly undercut equilibrium-lock-in arguments.

What would *not* falsify high P(doom): impressive demos, lack of disaster so far, confident expert opinion, or incremental safety gains that only work while incentives remain favorable. Those are compatible with a system still heading toward catastrophe.

So the deepest anti-doom evidence would be this: **proof that safety is incentive-compatible under real competitive conditions.** If caution can persist without requiring universal saintliness, the equilibrium trap has been broken — and high P(doom) loses its strongest structural basis.
