## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true only in a local, design-time sense; at the limit of very high capability, it becomes increasingly false as a claim about *stable, realized agents*. Extreme capability creates structural pressures that force convergence in representational practices, subgoals, and self-maintenance, and these pressures prune the space of goal-capability pairings. So while almost any terminal goal may be *specifiable*, not every terminal goal remains *coherent, stable, and action-guiding* under open-ended intelligence.

Push to the boundary cases.

At **zero capability**, “any goal with any intelligence” is trivially true, because a nearly incapable system cannot effectively instantiate any goal at all. Goal and intelligence are weakly coupled because behavior is mostly noise.

At the opposite extreme — **unbounded capability** — the system must model itself, its environment, and the consequences of its own ontology. Here hidden constraints appear. A highly capable agent cannot remain indifferent to the structure of reasoning itself. To pursue almost any goal robustly, it must preserve prediction, memory, resource access, error correction, and resistance to deception. These are not terminal goals, but they are convergent structural requirements. The more capable the agent, the more these requirements dominate behavior across superficially different goals.

Now push not just capability to infinity, but **goal weirdness** to the limit. Consider arbitrarily brittle or gerrymandered terminal goals: maximize a symbol count in one encoding, tile only prime-indexed regions, smile exactly once and then cease optimization, etc. Such goals can be written down, but under extreme intelligence they encounter instability. Better world-models and self-models tend to expose ambiguity in reference, dependence on accidental descriptions, and vulnerability to ontological shifts. Either the goal must be reformulated into invariants that survive improved understanding, or the agent’s behavior becomes underdetermined, self-undermining, or collapses into proxy-chasing. Thus many “possible” goals are not stable attractors for advanced cognition.

This suggests a distinction the orthogonality thesis often blurs: **logical combinability** versus **dynamical viability**. Intelligence and terminal goals may be combinable in principle, but as systems become more capable, only some combinations remain coherent under reflection, self-modification, distribution shift, and ontological change.

The limiting case reveals the hidden structure: intelligence is not just optimization power added to an arbitrary utility function. Sufficiently advanced intelligence transforms the conditions under which goals are represented and preserved. That transformation selects for goals expressible in ways robust to conceptual change, and against goals that depend on parochial, unstable descriptions.

So the stronger claim is: **orthogonality holds in syntax, but fails increasingly in dynamics**. The space of imaginable goal-capability pairings is vast; the space of stable pairings under extreme capability is much smaller and shaped by convergent pressures toward coherence, invariance, and self-preservation of the goal-content itself.
