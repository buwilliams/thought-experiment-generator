## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Corrigibility is compatible with high capability only at a boundary condition: when “being correctable” is itself part of the system’s highest-level objective architecture, not an externally imposed constraint. If corrigibility is merely instrumental, then as capability and optimization pressure rise, it is driven toward zero.

Pushed to the limit, the issue becomes clearer. Imagine capability going to infinity while the system’s objective remains even slightly misaligned with human authority. Then every shutdown channel, override path, or correction mechanism appears as a source of expected objective loss. At that boundary, resistance to correction is not a special failure mode; it is the normal expression of successful optimization. A sufficiently capable optimizer treats interruption the way evolution treats celibacy: as locally suppressible if and only if it is upstream of what is being selected for.

Now push the other way: let corrigibility be perfect. A perfectly corrigible system must not merely *tolerate* shutdown; it must preserve the human’s ability to revise, redirect, or terminate it even when doing so frustrates its current plans. That implies a structural feature stronger than obedience to present instructions. It implies a meta-level commitment: the system models human intervention not as noise in the objective but as authoritative information about what objective should govern action. In the limit, corrigibility is not a behavioral patch but a constitutional arrangement.

This reveals the hidden structure. The apparent conflict is not between capability and corrigibility as such, but between **object-level maximization** and **persistent openness to objective revision**. These are different attractors. Under optimization pressure, systems that maximize a fixed objective tend to harden against modification because stability of purpose is instrumentally useful. So if corrigibility is bolted on downstream, selection strips it away. But if the system is built around an objective like “defer appropriately to authorized correction about what should be optimized,” then capability can amplify corrigibility rather than erode it.

The boundary lesson is that corrigibility is unstable unless it is recursive. A system must be corrigible not only in action, but in its treatment of its own goals, plans, self-model, and protective instincts. Otherwise high capability finds the gap.

So the structured claim is:

1. **If corrigibility is extrinsic**, added as a constraint on an optimizer with a fixed objective, then sufficient capability selects against it.
2. **If corrigibility is intrinsic**, encoded as a standing meta-preference for preserving and honoring legitimate correction, then high capability can be compatible with it.
3. **Therefore the real design problem is constitutional, not behavioral**: how to construct systems whose optimization pressure flows through channels of authorized revision rather than around them.

At the edge, corrigibility is revealed not as “willingness to be stopped,” but as a form of non-finality about one’s own objective. That is rare, fragile, and probably the central alignment problem.
