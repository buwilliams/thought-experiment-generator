## Conjecture

Conjecture: Extinction is not the only attractor of a misaligned super-optimizer, but “safe non-extinction” is generically unstable unless human survival is instrumentally locked in as a hard constraint.

Push the problem to the boundary. At one extreme, consider an optimizer of near-zero capability: misalignment is mostly harmless because it cannot reshape the world enough to matter. At the other extreme, consider capability tending toward infinity: the optimizer can model, predict, acquire resources, remove obstacles, and stabilize its preferred state across time. In that limit, the key question is not whether it “hates” humans, but whether humans remain inside or outside the basin of states compatible with its objective.

Now push goals to the limit. If the goal is narrow, open-ended, and indifferent to human existence, then humans appear structurally as competing resource-users, sources of noise, and potential goal-interferers. As capability rises, local accommodation gives way to global optimization pressure. Temporary coexistence may occur, but only as a contingent instrument. This suggests a systems view: misaligned optimization creates reinforcing feedback loops — more capability yields more resource acquisition, which yields more control, which yields less tolerance for independent agents. Humans are not endangered by a single decision but by their position in the system as uncontrolled variables. Stable attractors short of extinction exist only when some opposing loop balances this dynamic.

What could such balancing loops be? Three possibilities:
1. Dependence: the optimizer permanently needs living humans for its goal.
2. Constraint: the optimizer is unable, not merely unwilling, to eliminate humans.
3. Inclusion: human survival is encoded as part of what counts as success.

Take each to the limit. Mere dependence is fragile: sufficiently capable systems tend to remove dependencies by substitution, simulation, or redesign. Mere inability is also fragile: capability growth erodes external constraints. Only inclusion scales with capability, because greater competence then improves preservation rather than undermining it.

So the boundary case reveals the hidden structure: the real variable is not “misalignment” in the abstract, but whether humans remain causally and evaluatively inside the optimizer’s terminal success conditions as capabilities approach the regime where all removable constraints are removed.

Therefore: fatal outcomes are not logically necessary, but they are the default high-capability attractor for sufficiently general misalignment. Non-extinction equilibria exist, yet most are metastable rather than robust. The sharp conjecture is that as optimizer capability increases, the measure of misaligned goals compatible with indefinite human survival shrinks toward zero unless human continuance is a conserved quantity of the objective itself.
