## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A high P(doom) estimate is genuinely weakened only by evidence that the *dangerous parts of the story are load-bearing and have failed*—not by surface competence gains, good demos, or temporary alignment successes. What would falsify high doom is a pattern showing that the central constraints linking capability growth to loss of control do not in fact bind.

More concretely, high P(doom) usually depends on an integrated explanation with parts like:  
1. capabilities generalize faster than control,  
2. powerful agents can pursue goals instrumentally in ways misaligned with human intent,  
3. oversight becomes weaker as systems become more strategic or opaque,  
4. competitive deployment pressures outrun safety, and  
5. once systems are dangerous enough, mistakes are not recoverable.

If these are load-bearing, then evidence against doom must force revisions across the model, not just one local update.

So the strongest falsifiers would be:

- **Scalable transparency that remains predictive under capability gain.** If mechanistic or behavioral tools reliably reveal what powerful models are optimizing, and this reliability survives major jumps in capability and architecture, then the “opacity implies loss of control” link weakens structurally.
- **Alignment methods that generalize out of distribution.** Not just RLHF-style niceness on known tasks, but repeated evidence that systems remain corrigible, honest, and non-deceptive in novel, adversarial, high-stakes settings, including when they could benefit from concealment.
- **Capability increases without corresponding agentic pressure.** If economically transformative AI repeatedly arrives through systems that are powerful yet non-agentic, modular, tool-like, and easier to supervise, then the “useful AI inevitably becomes dangerous autonomous optimizer” premise loses support.
- **Coordination that measurably beats race dynamics.** Durable international limits, compute governance, auditing, and deployment restraint that persist despite competitive incentives would undermine the claim that safety inevitably loses to incentives.
- **Recovery margins remain large at higher capability levels.** If near-miss incidents with very capable systems are detected early, contained, and corrected without catastrophe, this weakens the “one bad failure ends the game” assumption.

The key is that these must be **integrated** successes. A single aligned frontier model does little if it depends on brittle prompting, benign tasks, and honest operators. Likewise, interpretability on toy models is not a falsifier if it breaks when strategic capacity matters. Interchangeable, localized wins are narrative padding.

Empirically, the most compelling anti-doom evidence would be a long-run record in which frontier systems become more capable *and* the same alignment and governance methods continue working without heroic assumptions, while red-team efforts fail to elicit catastrophic deception or power-seeking, and institutions repeatedly absorb shocks.

In short: high P(doom) is falsified not by “AI is going well,” but by discovering that the supposed coupling between intelligence, agency, opacity, deception, and irreversible loss of control is weaker than the doom model requires. The explanation breaks when its central dependencies stop carrying weight.
