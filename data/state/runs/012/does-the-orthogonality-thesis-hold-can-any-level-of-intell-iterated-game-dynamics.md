## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is broadly true at the level of *logical possibility*, but false as a claim about *stable strategic existence* in worlds where intelligence operates through repeated interaction. In iterated environments, not every intelligence–goal pairing is equally sustainable: structural pressures select for agents whose terminal goals, or at least policy-level commitments, are compatible with cooperation, reputation maintenance, credible reciprocity, and long-horizon adaptation.

The key collision is this: orthogonality says capability and terminal goals are independent. Iterated game dynamics says repeated play changes what kinds of behavior persist. If an intelligent agent exists in a one-shot setting, almost any goal may be pursued directly. But in repeated settings, agents face ongoing incentives to preserve beneficial relations, avoid triggering retaliation, cultivate trust, and remain legible enough for mutually advantageous cooperation. This does not require altruism. It follows from the payoff structure of repeated interaction.

So the important distinction is between **having** a terminal goal and **successfully pursuing** it under strategic recurrence. A highly intelligent agent could in principle have a bizarre, destructive, or myopic terminal goal. But if that goal systematically induces defection against other capable agents in situations with memory and future consequences, then the agent will often be sanctioned, excluded, counter-coalitioned against, or locked into inefficient conflict. Over time, such goal-policy combinations are less stable than ones that support contingent cooperation.

This suggests a refinement: **orthogonality holds in design space, but not in evolutionary or ecological equilibrium space**. Repeated interaction imposes filters. Goals that can be pursued through reciprocal restraint, reputation-sensitive behavior, and credible commitments will tend to outperform equally capable agents whose goals require unbounded exploitation or opaque opportunism. Intelligence does not erase these pressures; it sharpens them, because more intelligent agents better recognize the shadow of the future.

A further implication is that what matters is not only terminal goals but their **strategic expression**. Even agents with non-cooperative final ends may converge on cooperative instrumental policies if they expect ongoing interaction. Conversely, if time horizons shorten, identities become nonpersistent, or defection becomes hard to detect, cooperation collapses—and the space of stable goal-capability combinations widens toward predatory or chaotic forms. Thus the environment’s iterability partly determines whether orthogonality appears robust.

So the thesis should be split in two:

1. **Metaphysical orthogonality:** intelligence can in principle pair with arbitrary terminal goals.  
2. **Dynamic orthogonality:** in repeated multi-agent systems, structural pressures constrain which pairings remain viable.

What this illuminates is that the deepest challenge to orthogonality is not moral psychology or rational necessity, but **game structure**. The question is not “Can an intelligent mind want anything?” but “Which wants can survive contact with other minds under repetition?”
