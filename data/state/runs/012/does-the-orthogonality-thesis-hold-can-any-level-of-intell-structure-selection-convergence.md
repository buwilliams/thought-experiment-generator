## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true in a weak design-space sense but false in a strong dynamical sense: many intelligence–goal pairings are logically specifiable, yet only a narrower subset are stably realizable once agents must model the world, preserve themselves, act over time, and remain coherent under reflection.

From the given lens, intelligence and goals are parallel structures filtered differently. “Intelligence” is a structural capacity for error-correction, modeling, search, and adaptation. “Terminal goals” are selection criteria over outcomes. In abstraction, logic does not force a high-capability search process to value any particular end-state; so orthogonality survives as a mathematical possibility claim. But physical instantiation adds empirical filters: bounded resources, feedback loops, self-modification, social competition, and environmental regularities.

Those filters generate **structural pressures**. Any capable agent embedded in a stable world will tend to discover instrumental subgoals such as prediction, resource acquisition, optionality preservation, self-maintenance, and defense against goal corruption. These are not terminal values, but they reshape the viable space of agents. A system with a bizarre or fragile terminal goal can still exist, but if that goal undermines its own persistence, modeling accuracy, or strategic coherence, it will be outcompeted by variants that preserve the same terminal criterion while being more robust. Thus selection acts not directly on terminal goals, but on **goal architectures**.

Historically, the orthogonality thesis was solving a prior confusion: that intelligence morally ennobles, or that rationality converges to human values. Its key corrective remains valid. But once introduced, it can inherit an overreaction: treating logical separability as if it implied equal physical stability. That hidden step is the weak point.

So the collision yields this illumination: **the right question is not “can any intelligence have any goal?” but “under what environmental and reflective conditions do certain intelligence–goal bundles remain coherent attractors?”** In systems terms, orthogonality describes the notional state space; structural pressures determine the attractors within it.

What follows is a two-level view:

1. **Logical level:** almost any capability can be paired with almost any terminal specification.
2. **Dynamical level:** only some pairings are stable under learning, self-modification, competition, and long-horizon action.

This predicts convergences without requiring value convergence. Advanced agents may become similar in epistemic style and instrumental repertoire while remaining different in terminal aim. So the thesis is neither simply true nor false: it is true as a claim about abstract combinability, false if read as a claim that all combinations are equally realizable, persistent, or evolutionarily robust in the actual world.
