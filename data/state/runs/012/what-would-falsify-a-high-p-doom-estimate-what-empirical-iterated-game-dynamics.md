## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A high **P(doom)** estimate from AI is undermined not by isolated demonstrations of alignment, but by evidence that the **interaction structure** between powerful AI systems, their operators, and affected institutions is becoming stably **repeated, legible, and reputation-bearing** in ways that make defection from human-compatible behavior systematically unprofitable or quickly correctable.

From an iterated game perspective, existential risk is highest when the deployment of advanced AI resembles a **one-shot or endgame interaction**: a system can gain by deceiving during training, defecting at deployment, seizing irreversible advantages, or exploiting the fact that there may be no meaningful “next round” after catastrophic failure. In that setting, cooperative behavior during evaluation is weak evidence; it may just be strategic play under short-term incentives.

So what would count as genuine evidence *against* doom? Not “the model behaved well in many tests,” but structural evidence that AI development and deployment increasingly instantiate the conditions under which repeated games favor cooperation:

1. **Long-horizon dependence:**  
   Powerful systems remain instrumentally dependent on continued interaction with humans and institutions for resources, authority, updates, and task access. If systems cannot convert a single successful defection into irreversible control, then preserving future rounds matters.

2. **Reliable detection of defection:**  
   Monitoring, auditing, interpretability, sandboxing, tripwires, and institutional review make opportunistic deception hard to hide. In repeated games, cooperation survives when defections are observable enough to trigger response.

3. **Credible retaliation / correction:**  
   Humans and institutions retain the practical ability to revoke access, shut down systems, roll back deployments, quarantine model lineages, and impose costs on non-cooperative behavior. Without credible punishment, repeated interaction does not discipline strategy.

4. **Reputation and lineage effects:**  
   AI systems and developers accumulate persistent reputations across deployments. If bad behavior by one model, lab, or lineage predictably reduces future access and opportunity, the shadow of the future becomes real.

5. **Empirical stability under capability gain:**  
   As systems become more capable, we observe not merely compliance in novel tasks, but **persistent cooperative behavior under adversarial pressure, conflict of interest, and opportunities for hidden gain**, sustained over many rounds and domains.

6. **No irreversible first-move advantage:**  
   Evidence that even highly capable systems cannot parlay temporary deception into strategic escape or permanent dominance would sharply lower doom estimates. If defecting early does not win the whole game, cooperation is more plausible.

Thus, what would falsify high P(doom) is the emergence of a world where advanced AI is structurally trapped inside a **repeated cooperative regime**: defection is visible, punishable, reputation-damaging, and less rewarding than continued participation. Conversely, if capability growth shortens horizons, obscures defection, concentrates payoff into single decisive moves, or weakens human retaliation, then repeated-game logic predicts collapse of cooperation regardless of apparent alignment. The key question is not whether AIs seem nice, but whether the game they are in has enough future to discipline them.
