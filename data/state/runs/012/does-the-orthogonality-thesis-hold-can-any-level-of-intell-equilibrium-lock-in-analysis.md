## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is broadly true at the level of *logical possibility*, but false as a claim about *stable realized systems*: once an agent is embedded in a strategic environment, equilibrium pressures select against many intelligence–goal combinations, because highly capable agents with certain terminal goals are systematically outcompeted, redirected, internally destabilized, or forced into convergent subgoals that make their effective behavior less orthogonal than the thesis suggests.

From an **equilibrium lock-in** perspective, the key distinction is between what an isolated mind could in principle want and what kinds of goal-capability pairings can persist in a multi-agent world. Orthogonality says intelligence does not by itself dictate ends. But stability is a different question. If no agent can retain a given goal while surviving competition, governance, training, or self-modification pressures, then that pairing is not an attractor of the actual system.

This illuminates a structural trap in the debate. Pro-orthogonality arguments often treat contingent design failures as if they were essential, while anti-orthogonality arguments often infer from convergence pressures to impossibility. The equilibrium view dissolves this: many combinations are possible, but many are not *equilibrium-sustainable*.

What structurally constrains them?

1. **Competitive selection:** In populations of capable agents, goals that ignore resource acquisition, self-preservation, bargaining, or deception can be dominated by agents that pursue those instrumental strategies. So even if “genius-level intelligence + harmless terminal goal” is coherent, it may be unstable in a competitive ecology.

2. **Principal-agent lock-in:** Powerful agents are usually trained, deployed, and monitored by institutions. Goal systems that create legible, cooperative behavior are selected for; others are modified or eliminated. Thus realized goals are partly products of governance equilibria, not pure design freedom.

3. **Reflective instability:** As agents become more capable, they model themselves and the conditions of preserving their objectives. Some terminal goals may imply self-modification paths that erode the original goal, while others generate strong goal-preservation incentives. Not every goal survives contact with recursive improvement.

4. **Coordination constraints:** Goals requiring unilateral restraint are often unstable if rivals defect. Even if all parties would prefer a world of modest, bounded aims, each has incentive to empower agents with expansionary instrumental behavior. This locks systems into a narrower band of viable goal-capability combinations.

So the collision yields a sharper thesis: **intelligence and terminal goals are orthogonal in design space, but not in equilibrium space**. The relevant question is not “Can an arbitrarily intelligent agent have goal X?” but “Can agent+goal X remain intact under selection, competition, and self-maintenance?” That reframes AI alignment and philosophy of agency alike. The danger is not merely bizarre goals, but structurally favored goals: those that can survive the equilibria of deployment.
