## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a scenario where the key variable is **the rate at which new capability becomes deployable relative to the rate at which alignment knowledge can be created, tested, and propagated**.

Assume a real discontinuity: an AI system crosses a threshold where, within a short interval, it becomes able to automate major chunks of AI research, strategic planning, persuasion, and cyber-operation. The important fact is not merely “it gets much smarter,” but that **the feedback loop from capability to further capability compresses below the response time of human institutions and alignment practice**.

Follow the logic. Alignment is not a static property we install once; it is a body of knowledge embodied in tests, interpretations, monitoring, operational doctrine, and institutional veto power. Those are all slower-moving stocks than raw capability. In a continuous takeoff, mistakes and warning signs can generate criticism, and criticism can be turned into improved methods. In a discontinuous takeoff, the capability stock outruns the alignment-governance stock before criticism can be incorporated. Therefore, discontinuous takeoff makes alignment **qualitatively** harder not only because there is less time, but because several mechanisms that continuous worlds rely on cease to function at all: iterative deployment, empirical feedback, institutional adaptation, and competitive error-correction.

This suggests the relevant dividing line is not “fast vs slow” in ordinary terms, but whether takeoff crosses a **criticism barrier**: a regime in which systems can materially reshape the world faster than humans can discover and correct alignment errors. Beyond that barrier, alignment shifts from an engineering problem with iteration to a one-shot problem with weak recourse.

What determines which trajectory is more likely? Primarily the structure of the capability-production system:

1. **Endogeneity of improvement**: if advanced systems can significantly accelerate their own improvement or the surrounding R&D loop, discontinuity becomes more likely.
2. **Bottlenecks external to intelligence**: if progress is constrained by compute fabrication, energy, data centers, regulation, robotics, or tacit human integration, trajectories are more continuous.
3. **Transferability of capabilities**: if gains in one domain rapidly generalize across many economically and strategically relevant domains, takeoff steepens.
4. **Parallelizability of research**: if many alignment-relevant and capability-relevant advances can be searched in parallel by AI, jumps become more plausible.
5. **Deployment structure**: concentrated control can produce abrupt world impact even if underlying capability growth is smoother; diffuse deployment can make impact more continuous.

So the collision of perspectives yields this claim: **discontinuous takeoff is qualitatively harder for alignment exactly when capability growth outruns the error-correcting institutions that generate alignment knowledge.** The likelihood of discontinuity depends less on abstract “intelligence” and more on whether the surrounding system contains hard external bottlenecks or permits a self-accelerating closed loop from cognition to deployment to further cognition.
