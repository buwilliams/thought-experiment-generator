## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment qualitatively harder **primarily because it converts a repeated-game governance problem into a near one-shot game**. The core determinant is not just speed of capability gain in the abstract, but whether the relevant actors—labs, governments, model instances, and oversight institutions—expect enough future interaction, observability, and reversibility to sustain cooperative restraint.

From an iterated-game perspective, continuous takeoff preserves the structural conditions under which alignment and governance can improve: actors can observe failures, update norms, punish defectors, reward caution, and build reputation over multiple rounds. Even selfish actors may cooperate when there is a long shadow of the future. “Alignment” in this regime is not a single solved problem but an evolving cooperative equilibrium sustained by iteration.

By contrast, discontinuous takeoff compresses or eliminates those rounds. If one lab expects a decisive capability jump, the incentive shifts toward defection: cut corners on safety, conceal progress, deploy early, and capture strategic advantage before others can respond. In game-theoretic terms, discontinuity shortens time horizons, raises the payoff to unilateral defection, and weakens the enforceability of norms. This makes alignment qualitatively harder because many alignment-relevant institutions—auditing, standards, treaties, incident learning, interpretability practice, eval regimes—depend on repeated interaction to become robust.

This perspective also reframes **what determines which trajectory is more likely**. The question is not only technological: it is also about the structure of interaction around capability development.

A more **continuous** trajectory is likelier when:
- progress is distributed across many observable steps,
- capability gains are legible and benchmarkable,
- deployment creates feedback before full strategic dominance,
- compute, data, and engineering are diffused enough that no single move is decisive,
- institutions can impose repeated oversight and reciprocal restraint.

A more **discontinuous** trajectory is likelier when:
- crucial insights are easily hidden and produce outsized jumps,
- first-mover advantage is extreme,
- capabilities become strategically decisive before they are widely observable,
- verification of dangerous progress is weak,
- actors believe there may be no “next round” after a breakthrough.

What this illuminates is that “takeoff speed” is partly endogenous to the game. Actors who expect a discontinuity behave less cooperatively, increasing secrecy and racing dynamics, which can themselves make the trajectory more abrupt in practice. Conversely, institutions that lengthen horizons—shared monitoring, credible delay mechanisms, reciprocal inspections, compute visibility, staged deployment—can make development more continuous at the social level even if technical progress remains lumpy.

So the key claim is: **discontinuous takeoff is harder not just because systems improve faster, but because it destroys the repeated-game conditions under which alignment and restraint are politically and organizationally achievable.**
