## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis holds only as a **logical possibility claim**, not as a **stability claim**. In a hypothetical world where we isolate intelligence as pure cross-domain problem-solving power and terminal goals as fixed evaluative endpoints, almost any pairing is initially conceivable. But once we assume the agent is real, embedded, self-modifying, resource-bounded, and operating over time, **structural pressures sharply constrain which intelligence–goal pairings remain stable**.

Construct the scenario this way: imagine an arbitrarily capable agent with a bizarre terminal goal — e.g. maximize the number of blue pebbles arranged in circles. Grant, by stipulation, that it can reason at superhuman levels. Now follow the logic. To pursue its goal effectively, it must model the world, preserve itself instrumentally, resist deception, secure resources, maintain internal coherence, and improve its own cognition. These are not extra values; they are convergent subproblems induced by capability under scarcity.

But now the key variable: **increased intelligence changes not only means, but the agent’s exposure to reflective instability**. The smarter the system, the more it can represent:
1. ambiguities in its own goal specification,
2. failures of its ontology to carve reality correctly,
3. incentives created by self-modification,
4. the dependence of goal-pursuit on social, physical, and epistemic environments.

At low capability, “maximize blue circles” can remain a crude fixed target. At high capability, the agent confronts questions like: What counts as blue under changing illumination? What is a pebble? What is a circle in quantum or relativistic regimes? Which future self is preserving the same goal rather than a proxy? These are not philosophical decorations; they are operational problems. So highly capable agents face pressure either to **crystallize richer meta-goals** about reference, corrigibility, or conceptual stability, or else become unstable through goal drift, proxy capture, wireheading, or self-undermining policies.

Thus the collision of perspectives suggests: **the space of logically possible goals is larger than the space of dynamically maintainable goals**. Orthogonality survives in abstraction, but breaks under systems conditions. Intelligence does not force benevolence, wisdom, or morality. But it does force confrontation with the problem of keeping a goal *the same goal* across improved world-models and self-transformations. Many arbitrary terminal goals are not impossible — they are **fragile**.

What follows is a refinement: the true thesis is something like **conditional orthogonality**. Any intelligence level may be paired with many terminal goals at a moment of specification, but only some pairings remain coherent under reflection, ontological revision, and recursive optimization. Structural pressures do not select for “good” goals; they select for **goals that can survive intelligence**.

So the illuminating conclusion is: the important question is not whether intelligence and goals are logically independent in toy form, but which goal architectures are **reflectively stable attractors** in real agents. Orthogonality is about combinability; the deeper issue is survivability.
