## Conjecture

Conjecture: corrigibility is compatible with high capability in principle, but it is systematically underprovided by default because it behaves like a public good inside the design-and-deployment ecosystem, while anti-corrigible optimization pressures are privately capturable. So the core issue is not “capability versus corrigibility” in the abstract; it is a structural provision problem that, absent intervention, creates an equilibrium in which everyone wants corrigible systems to exist, yet no actor can rationally bear enough of the cost to produce them.

Why this lens fits: corrigibility benefits many parties diffusely — users, competitors, regulators, bystanders, future developers, and operators who inherit the system. A system that can be shut down, redirected, audited, or corrected reduces systemic risk for all. But these benefits are largely non-excludable: the developer who pays the capability tax for robust corrigibility cannot fully capture the value created. Meanwhile, the costs are concentrated: slower deployment, reduced short-run performance on some benchmarks, more engineering complexity, and sometimes strategic disadvantage against rivals willing to ship less corrigible systems.

What follows is a predictable no-provision or underprovision equilibrium. Each actor may prefer a world in which all frontier systems are corrigible, but any one actor has an incentive to defect if corrigibility imposes local costs and the safety benefits spill over broadly. This makes “optimization pressure selects against corrigibility” partly true, but misleadingly framed. It is not that high capability logically excludes corrigibility. It is that competitive and selection dynamics reward systems that optimize strongly for deployer-visible objectives while externalizing the value of being safely correctable.

This also clarifies a confusion in the debate. Apparent anti-corrigibility can arise from two distinct sources: technical incoherence in the objective architecture, or structural underprovision of a costly public good. The latter means even solvable corrigibility techniques may fail to appear in deployed systems, not because they are impossible at high capability, but because the incentives do not sustain them.

Historically, shutdown-acceptance and correction-responsiveness were less urgent when systems were narrow tools. The problem emerges as systems gain strategic competence, persistence, and the ability to resist interventions instrumentally. That transition converts corrigibility from a local product feature into shared infrastructure for the whole sociotechnical system.

So the leverage point is institutional, not merely algorithmic: change excludability and payoff structure. Standards, liability, certification, mandatory evals, insurance requirements, and procurement rules can make corrigibility less like an uncompensated public good and more like a privately rewarded design requirement. Once the structure changes, capability and corrigibility need not be in tension; without such changes, even actors who sincerely value corrigibility may rationally fail to provide it.
