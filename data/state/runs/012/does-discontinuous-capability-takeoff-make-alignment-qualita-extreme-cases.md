## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment *qualitatively harder* not mainly because systems become suddenly more powerful, but because the **error-correction loop breaks before the capability loop does**. The decisive variable is not “speed” in the abstract; it is the ratio between (1) how fast capabilities compound and generalize, and (2) how fast human institutions can detect, interpret, and correct misalignment. A trajectory is “continuous” when these loops remain coupled; it is “discontinuous” when capability growth outruns oversight by orders of magnitude.

Pushed to the limit, the distinction becomes clear. Imagine an infinitesimally slow takeoff: every capability increment is exposed to criticism, monitoring, governance adaptation, and empirical feedback. Alignment remains difficult, but it is an engineering problem inside an active learning system. Now push to the opposite extreme: a system jumps from economically useful to strategically dominant before meaningful external feedback can act. In that limit, alignment ceases to be mainly about tuning objectives and becomes a **one-shot specification and control problem under deep ignorance**. That is qualitatively harder because fallibilist correction — our main way of creating knowledge — is structurally blocked.

This suggests a systems view. There are at least two interacting stocks: **capability** and **alignment knowledge**. Capability can increase through scaling, recursive improvement, automation of research, and deployment revenues. Alignment knowledge increases through interpretability, adversarial testing, incident learning, and institutional adaptation. If capability growth generates more capability growth, while alignment knowledge depends on slower human-mediated criticism, then a positive feedback loop in capability and a delayed balancing loop in oversight create the possibility of discontinuity. Takeoff becomes sharp when systems can substantially improve the inputs to their own improvement.

At the boundary, another hidden fact appears: “continuous” and “discontinuous” are not purely properties of models; they are properties of the **whole socio-technical system**. A capability jump may be continuous in lab benchmarks but discontinuous in strategic consequence if it crosses thresholds for autonomous replication, persuasion, cyber offense, or R&D automation. Conversely, raw technical gains may look discontinuous but be governable if deployment, compute, and autonomy remain bottlenecked.

So what determines which trajectory is more likely? The key determinants are bottlenecks. Continuous takeoff is more likely when progress depends on scarce, externally controlled resources: compute, energy, chips, data pipelines, evaluation infrastructure, organizational coordination, and real-world integration. Discontinuous takeoff is more likely when the leading systems can remove those bottlenecks themselves — especially by accelerating AI research, deception, resource acquisition, or autonomous operation.

Therefore the main practical implication is: don’t ask only whether intelligence scales smoothly. Ask whether **correction scales with power**. If not, then even modest discontinuity in capability can create a sharp discontinuity in alignment difficulty.
