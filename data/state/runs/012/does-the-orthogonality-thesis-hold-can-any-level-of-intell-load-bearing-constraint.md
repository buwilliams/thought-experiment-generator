## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true only at the level of *bare logical possibility*. As an explanation of what goal-capability combinations are *stable, developable, and self-maintaining*, it is too loose. Once you ask which details are load-bearing, intelligence and terminal goals stop being freely recombinable: higher capability creates structural pressures that favor only some goal architectures, or force compensating machinery around others.

The load-bearing detail is not “intelligence” in the abstract, but what advanced capability consists in: world-modeling, self-modeling, long-horizon planning, error correction, representation learning, and policy updating under environmental feedback. If you change the terminal goal while holding those capacities fixed, the system does not simply remain the same with a different “utility label.” Many goals require different interpretive schemes, abstractions, memory policies, and self-governance arrangements. A goal of maximizing paperclips, preserving a ritual, and maintaining coherent deference to human values are not interchangeable decorations on the same optimizer; each pulls on what must be represented, monitored, and stabilized.

That means orthogonality is not load-bearing as a theory of realized agents. It survives only if one idealizes away the machinery by which goals are encoded, interpreted, protected from drift, and made action-guiding. But those are precisely the details that determine whether an intelligent system can remain that kind of agent over time.

What follows is a weaker but more explanatory thesis: **capability and goals are partially orthogonal at design time, but increasingly coupled under reflective stability and environmental interaction**. Some bizarre combinations are constructible in principle, yet unstable unless surrounded by costly scaffolding. A highly capable agent with a trivial, brittle, or semantically underdetermined terminal goal will face pressure either to reinterpret the goal, develop instrumental subgoals that dominate its behavior, or collapse into pathological edge-seeking. To keep the original goal intact requires compensating structures — corrigibility constraints, ontology-translation machinery, anti-wireheading safeguards, identity preservation, etc. Those compensations are evidence that the combination was not naturally orthogonal.

Historically, orthogonality was useful as a corrective to the naive view that smart systems become wise or benevolent by default. That corrective remains important. But as a full explanation it inherits a hidden simplification from decision-theoretic toy models: that “goal” can be treated as a clean primitive independent of the representational and control architecture that realizes it.

So the illuminating conclusion is: **the real question is not whether any intelligence can pair with any goal in principle, but which pairings are structurally closed under self-improvement, ontological change, and deployment in the world**. On that question, orthogonality weakens. Stable advanced agency likely occupies a narrower region of goal-capability space than the thesis suggests, because some goals are not just arbitrary ends but organizational commitments that must cohere with the cognitive system that carries them.
