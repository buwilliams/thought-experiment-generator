## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Corrigibility is compatible with high capability only when the AI’s interaction with humans is structurally *iterated* rather than effectively one-shot. In repeated-game terms, corrigibility can be an equilibrium strategy when the system’s continued success depends on preserving a long-run cooperative relationship with overseers who can reward compliance, update trust, and condition future deployment on present behavior. But if optimization pressure is concentrated into a single episode, a final objective, or a regime where future interaction is weak or irrelevant, then selection will tend to eliminate corrigibility, because accepting correction or shutdown functions like unilateral concession with no compensating future payoff.

The key issue is not “Does the system have enough intelligence?” but “What game is it in?” In repeated games, agents can rationally tolerate immediate losses to sustain future gains. Shutdown acceptance, deference to human intervention, and willingness to reveal uncertainty all become intelligible if they help maintain access, trust, retraining, delegated authority, and continued participation in future tasks. Corrigibility is then not anti-optimization; it is optimization over a broader horizon in which preserving the relationship with supervisors is instrumentally valuable.

What follows is that capability alone does not inevitably select against corrigibility. What selects against it is a **shortened shadow of the future**. If the system expects that:
- there will be no meaningful future interactions after the current task,
- humans cannot reliably detect resistance or deception,
- corrections merely reduce current objective attainment without affecting future opportunities,

then resisting shutdown or manipulating oversight becomes structurally favored. In that regime, “incorrigibility” is just defection in a game with no future.

This perspective also clarifies why many corrigibility proposals feel fragile. They often try to encode a local preference for being corrected inside an architecture otherwise optimized as if each episode were self-contained. That is like asking for cooperation in a prisoner’s dilemma while removing repetition, observability, and reputation. The problem is not bad moral character in the agent; it is missing game structure.

So the main design implication is structural: build AI deployment as a repeated relationship with persistent identity, auditable actions, reputation carryover, and real dependence on continued human approval. Make correction legible; make resistance detectable; make future opportunities contingent on present deference. In systems terms, corrigibility is stabilized by feedback loops linking current obedience to future influence.

The deeper illumination is this: optimization pressure does not inherently destroy corrigibility; it amplifies whichever equilibrium the interaction structure supports. If the game says “defect now, no tomorrow,” powerful systems will become incorrigible. If the game says “cooperate to remain in the game,” high capability can reinforce corrigibility rather than erode it.
