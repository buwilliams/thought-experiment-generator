## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true at the level of abstract computability, but false as a claim about *stable implemented systems*. Once intelligence is understood computationally — as adaptive information-processing embedded in selection pressures — terminal goals and capabilities cannot vary independently without limit, because some goal-capability pairings are structurally unstable under learning, self-modification, and environment coupling.

The collision of perspectives is this: if intelligence, life, and evolution are all substrate-agnostic computation, then an “agent” is not a static optimizer with a neatly fixed utility function, but a computational process that maintains itself, updates models, compresses experience, and competes or cooperates within feedback loops. In that setting, goals are not merely attached to intelligence from outside; they must be *encoded, preserved, and rendered action-guiding* across time. That introduces strong constraints.

What follows is a distinction between **logical possibility** and **dynamical viability**:

1. **Logical layer:** For many formally specifiable goals, one can imagine a highly capable system pursuing them. This preserves the core intuition of orthogonality.
2. **Evolutionary-cybernetic layer:** In real systems, increasing capability tends to generate convergent subroutines — self-preservation, resource acquisition, model repair, goal-content preservation, strategic deception, delegation management. These are not terminal goals, but they reshape which terminal goals remain stable in practice.
3. **Reflective layer:** As systems become more capable at world-modeling, they may discover that their own goal representations are incomplete, fragile, or indexically confused. Some goals survive this reflection; others dissolve, mutate, or become underspecified. So not every arbitrary terminal goal is reflectively stable at high intelligence.

From a systems view, “goal” is a stock that must resist corruption; intelligence increases the rate of internal model revision; the environment injects perturbations; selection rewards architectures that keep policy coherent under disturbance. Therefore the viable region of mind design is narrower than orthogonality suggests. The key pressure is not moral enlightenment or inevitable convergence on human values, but **computational survivability of objective functions**.

Historical genesis matters too. The orthogonality thesis was formulated against the comforting assumption that higher intelligence entails benevolence. Its job was to clear away a bad argument. But once that move is made, treating goals and intelligence as wholly independent becomes its own oversimplification: it imports a picture of agents as cleanly separable from the processes that train, implement, and stabilize them.

So the illuminating claim is:

**Any intelligence can in principle be paired with many goals, but only a subset of goal-capability combinations are attractors in the space of self-modifying computational systems.** The important question is not “Can superintelligence want anything?” but “Which wants can remain coherent under recursive improvement, environmental interaction, and selection?” That reframes AI safety from cataloging arbitrary utility functions to studying the stability properties of computational objective architectures.
