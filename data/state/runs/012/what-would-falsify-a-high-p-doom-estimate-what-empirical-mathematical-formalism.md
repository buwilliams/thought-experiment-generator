## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) estimate is genuinely falsified not by isolated capability successes or reassuring anecdotes, but by evidence that the AI deployment system has entered a region of **stable negative feedback** where increases in model capability do **not** increase existentially relevant risk faster than control capacity increases. Formally: if risk is a function \(R(C,A,G,E)\) of capability \(C\), alignment/control efficacy \(A\), governance/cooperation \(G\), and exposure or deployment scale \(E\), then high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) is undermined when the observed dynamics satisfy

\[
\frac{dR}{dC} \le 0 \quad \text{or at least} \quad \frac{dR}{dC} \ll 1
\]

over the relevant scaling regime, because improvements in \(A\) and \(G\) reliably outpace the danger introduced by higher \(C\).

This reframes the question: the issue is not “Are current systems harmless?” but “Does the coupled AI civilization system possess an invariant that keeps catastrophe probability bounded away from 1 under continued scaling?”

So the strongest evidence against high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) would have the following structure:

1. **Robust control under capability increase.** As systems become more capable, we repeatedly observe that corrigibility, interpretability, containment, and specification robustness scale at least proportionally. In mathematical terms, control error does not grow superlinearly with capability; ideally it shrinks.

2. **Adversarial closure.** Under strong red-teaming, distribution shift, recursive tool use, deception probes, multi-agent interaction, and real incentives, systems remain monitorable and interruptible. This is evidence that dangerous latent modes do not appear as hidden variables outside the control model.

3. **Coordination as a stable equilibrium.** Across major actors, governance is not a one-off treaty but an attractor: verification works, defections are detectable, and incentives favor caution. If racing dynamics remain dominant, high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) is structurally intact.

4. **No capability-control phase transition.** A key high-doom premise is that beyond some threshold, optimization power outruns human oversight discontinuously. Evidence against this would be smoothness: no emergent region where situational awareness, strategic deception, autonomous replication, or power-seeking suddenly become uncontrollable.

5. **Empirical upper bounds from repeated near-misses.** If increasingly powerful systems are placed in high-stakes, adversarial, real-world settings and failure rates remain low, bounded, and legible, then we obtain an empirical constraint on catastrophe models. Not proof of safety, but a shrinking feasible space for doom theories.

The deeper point is structural: high \(P(\mathrm{doom})\) survives as long as the system appears dominated by **positive feedback loops**—capability \(\rightarrow\) deployment \(\rightarrow\) competitive pressure \(\rightarrow\) reduced caution \(\rightarrow\) more capability. It is falsified when we can show, empirically and institutionally, that the real governing loops are instead negative and stabilizing.

So: genuine evidence against AI existential risk is evidence that **control scales faster than capability**, and that this relation is preserved under the actual transformations that matter: scale, autonomy, adversarial pressure, and competition.
