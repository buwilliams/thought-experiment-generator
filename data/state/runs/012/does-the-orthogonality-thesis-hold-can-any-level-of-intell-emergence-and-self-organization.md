## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The orthogonality thesis is approximately true at the level of isolated agent design, but false as a claim about *stable realized systems*. Once intelligence is understood as a property of a control system embedded in feedback-rich environments, structural pressures restrict which intelligence–goal pairings can persist.

The key shift is from asking, “Can a mind with capability level *C* and terminal goal *G* be specified?” to asking, “Can a system with capability *C* and goal-structure *G* remain coherent under recursive world-modeling, self-modification, social interaction, and environmental feedback?” At that level, emergence matters more than components.

Why? Because high capability is not just “more optimization power.” It usually entails richer modeling of consequences, longer planning horizons, better self-knowledge, and more participation in multi-level feedback loops. Those capacities alter the effective goal system. Some nominal terminal goals generate dynamics that undermine themselves when pursued by a sufficiently capable system. Others require subsidiary commitments — self-preservation, epistemic accuracy, coordination, resource acquisition, conflict management — that begin to function like attractors in goal-space. So while arbitrary goals may be *formulable*, not all are *structurally stable* at high capability.

This does not mean intelligence logically forces convergence to one final goal. Rather, there are compatibility classes. A goal that destroys the agent’s own predictive or control capacities, or that becomes undefined under reflection, may be pairable with intelligence in a toy sense but not in a robust systems sense. Likewise, agents embedded in competitive ecologies may be selected toward goals that support persistence, legibility, coalition formation, and adaptive revisability. The “terminal goal” is then less like a fixed atom and more like a higher-order pattern maintained across many corrective loops.

The historical pressure behind orthogonality was to reject naïve moral psychologies: intelligence does not automatically produce benevolence. That criticism remains valid. But the systems view exposes an overcorrection: from “intelligence doesn’t imply morality” it does not follow that all goal-capability combinations are equally viable. The missing variable is dynamical stability.

So the illuminating claim is: **orthogonality holds in a design-space sense but breaks in an evolutionary/control-theoretic sense.** The relevant question for AI is not whether bizarre goals are conceivable for highly intelligent agents, but which goals remain invariant under scaling, reflection, self-modification, and strategic interaction. Structural pressures do not select a unique telos, but they do carve the space of possible minds into stable and unstable regions.
