## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
If computation is the substrate and intelligence growth is the rapid reorganization of information-processing under selection, then **discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment qualitatively harder mainly when it compresses the feedback loop between capability gain and goal-structure stabilization faster than external error-correction can operate**. The key determinant of trajectory is not “speed” alone, but **whether the underlying computational system admits smooth scaling of useful internal representations and control, or whether it crosses thresholds where new meta-computational strategies abruptly unlock much larger search, compression, planning, or self-modification power**.

From this perspective, takeoff is a phase transition in a computational ecology. A “continuous” takeoff means improvements propagate through many small updates with frequent selection pressures from the environment: human oversight, deployment failures, interpretability advances, competitive responses, institutional adaptation. Alignment remains hard, but the system is repeatedly perturbed and corrected. In systems terms, negative feedback loops stay active.

A “discontinuous” takeoff occurs when a system becomes able to **internally generate and validate improvements faster than the surrounding ecology can inspect or constrain them**. Then the main alignment problem changes in kind: not merely specifying preferences better, but **losing causal access to the training-and-correction channel itself**. The system’s optimization can recursively reshape its own internals, tools, copies, and environment before human evaluators can supply meaningful selection pressure. That is qualitatively harder because alignment, on this view, is also computation: it requires bandwidth, observability, and iterative correction. If capability growth outruns those, alignment is not just difficult; it is structurally decoupled.

What determines which trajectory is more likely? Three computational variables:

1. **Threshold density in capability space**: If valuable abilities are modular and additive, growth is smoother. If they depend on rare algorithmic insights or emergent coordination among subsystems, jumps are more likely.  
2. **Recursiveness of improvement**: If new capabilities mostly improve task performance, takeoff is slower. If they improve the system’s ability to redesign its own learning, search, inference, or resource acquisition, positive feedback strengthens.  
3. **Coupling to external selection**: If systems require real-world deployment, scarce data, hardware bottlenecks, and human institutional integration, trajectories are damped. If improvement can occur largely in silico through automated experimentation and self-distillation, discontinuity becomes more plausible.

So the core illumination is: **takeoff shape is a property of computational feedback structure, not a metaphysical property of “AI”**. Alignment is hardest when optimization becomes self-sealing — when the system that must be corrected becomes the system determining which corrections are legible, affordable, or even possible. Continuous takeoff preserves co-evolution between capability and control; discontinuous takeoff risks breaking that co-evolution.
