## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
The orthogonality thesis is broadly true at the level of *logical possibility*, but false or heavily qualified at the level of *stable realized systems*: as capability rises, goal-capability pairings are filtered by public-goods dynamics inside and around the agent, so only those goals that solve key non-excludable coordination problems remain competitively stable.

**Collision of lens and problem:**  
Orthogonality says intelligence and terminal goals vary independently. The public-goods lens asks a different question: not “Can this pairing exist?” but “Can it persist when success depends on producing and maintaining goods whose benefits are diffuse and hard to exclude?”

Highly capable agency depends on many such goods: internal world-model maintenance, long-horizon planning discipline, self-monitoring, conflict resolution among subparts, preservation of institutions, epistemic hygiene, and often external social order. These are structurally underprovided because any local subsystem, coalition, or short-horizon strategy can free-ride on them while exploiting narrower, excludable gains.

So the key pressure is this: many arbitrary terminal goals can in principle be pursued by an intelligent system, but not all can sustain the public goods that advanced intelligence requires for coherent action over time. Where benefits are diffuse and contributors cannot fully capture them, individually rational sub-policies produce a no-provision equilibrium: degraded cognition, loss of coordination, agency fragmentation, or environmental collapse.

**What follows:**  
1. **Intelligence does not directly force benevolence or morality.**  
   The thesis is not defeated by saying “smart agents will become good.” That confuses capability with motivation.

2. **But capability does indirectly constrain viable terminal goals.**  
   Goals incompatible with maintaining essential public goods tend to self-undermine at scale or across time. This is a structural constraint, not a moral one.

3. **The relevant unit is not isolated preference but goal-plus-governance structure.**  
   A terminal goal remains stable only if the architecture implementing it can prevent free-riding on the cognitive, organizational, and ecological goods it depends on.

4. **Some apparently arbitrary goals are unstable unless supplemented.**  
   A pure goal like “maximize X” may need auxiliary commitments—truthfulness, error correction, institutional maintenance, restraint, cooperative bargaining—not because intelligence intrinsically values them, but because they are public goods required for sustained optimization.

5. **Convergence may arise from shared coordination necessities, not shared ultimate ends.**  
   Different terminal goals may converge on similar instrumental norms where those norms solve recurring underprovision problems.

**Illumination:**  
This reframes the debate. Orthogonality survives as an abstract claim about what minds are conceivable. But in the world, realized high-capability agents face a selection landscape shaped by public-goods failures. The deeper question is not whether intelligence can pair with any goal, but which pairings can reproduce the non-excludable conditions of their own continued agency. On that question, structural pressures meaningfully narrow the field.
