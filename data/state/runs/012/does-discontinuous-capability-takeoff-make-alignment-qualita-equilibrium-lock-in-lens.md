## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment *qualitatively harder* not mainly because fast systems are intrinsically less alignable, but because it intensifies a preexisting **collective action failure in safety provision**: alignment knowledge, evals, interpretability tools, incident reporting, and restraint norms are largely **non-excludable goods with diffuse benefits**, while capability gains are relatively excludable and privately captured. Under discontinuous takeoff, the payoff horizon compresses, secrecy rises, and first-mover advantages appear larger, pushing actors into a stable **no-adequate-provision equilibrium** even if most would prefer a world with stronger alignment.

What follows is a distinction between two different claims often blurred together:

1. **Technical difficulty claim:** more capable systems may be harder to align.  
2. **Provision difficulty claim:** under certain trajectories, the institutions and shared knowledge needed for alignment are harder to supply.

This lens suggests the second claim is the sharper discontinuity. A continuous takeoff does not automatically solve alignment, but it changes the game structure: more time for feedback, more visible warning shots, more opportunities to amortize safety investment across actors, and more chances for public-good provision through standards, regulation, and shared tooling. Continuous takeoff therefore increases the feasibility of escaping the underprovision trap, even if technical problems remain hard.

What determines which trajectory is more likely is not just “how intelligence scales,” but the **structure of incentives around capability production**. Discontinuous takeoff becomes more likely when:
- progress depends on concentrated, private inputs with steep returns;
- capabilities can be rapidly weaponized or monetized;
- labs can internalize gains from secrecy but externalize systemic risk;
- safety knowledge is hard to appropriate commercially;
- deployment pressure outruns the institutions that convert lessons into public safeguards.

Historically, many dangerous domains begin with inherited assumptions from ordinary competition: move fast, keep secrets, treat failures as local. But if frontier AI has large non-excludable downside risk, those inherited norms are maladapted. The key issue is not whether actors are confused or reckless; they may be individually rational inside a structure that punishes unilateral caution.

So the illuminating claim is: **takeoff shape is partly endogenous to governance and incentive design.** A “discontinuous takeoff” is not only a property of models; it is also a property of how much shared safety capacity the system fails to build before crossing thresholds. If so, the main leverage is to convert alignment from a weakly funded public good into a partially excludable or mandated provisioned good: require eval gates, liability, compute governance, incident sharing, and deployment constraints that make safety investment individually rational.

On this view, discontinuity matters because it collapses the window for solving the public-goods problem. Continuous takeoff is safer less because it is gentle in itself, and more because it leaves room to build the institutions that discontinuity would outrun.
