## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Discontinuous capability takeoff makes alignment qualitatively harder not mainly because rapid progress leaves less calendar time, but because it can push the field into an **equilibrium lock-in** where every major actor faces the same strategic barrier: once capabilities cross a threshold enabling decisive competitive advantage, no single lab, state, or internal safety team can unilaterally slow deployment to resolve alignment without increasing the chance that a rival captures the gain. The core difficulty is therefore structural, not merely epistemic or managerial.

What follows is a distinction between two kinds of “hardness”:

1. **Continuous takeoff:** alignment difficulty is dominated by cumulative error-correction. Capabilities arrive in increments, so institutions, auditors, and researchers can update on failures, build standards, and reallocate effort. Bad outcomes may still happen, but many are contingent failures: misjudgment, underinvestment, poor governance, fragmented coordination. These are in principle corrigible because actors can improve their position through better decisions.

2. **Discontinuous takeoff:** alignment difficulty becomes equilibrium-constrained. If a small capability gain unlocks outsized military, economic, or scientific advantage, then caution becomes individually irrational even if collectively preferred. In that regime, “just do more safety” is not an available move for any one actor unless others are also bound. This is the lock-in: all parties may prefer a slower, more legible transition, but each defects because unilateral restraint is dominated.

This perspective also clarifies what determines which trajectory is more likely. The main drivers are not only technical properties of AI, but the **structure of payoffs and bottlenecks**:

- **Steepness of returns near frontier thresholds:** if crossing a capability threshold yields winner-take-most advantage, discontinuity is more likely to produce lock-in.
- **Observability and verifiability:** if actors cannot reliably observe others’ capabilities, compute, or safety practices, coordination collapses and race dynamics intensify.
- **Concentration of critical inputs:** if compute, talent, and deployment channels are centralized, coordination is more feasible; if diffuse, discontinuous dynamics are harder to govern.
- **Reusability of advances:** if one breakthrough generalizes rapidly across domains, capability jumps propagate faster than alignment knowledge can localize them.
- **Deployment irreversibility:** if early movers can entrench standards, infrastructure, or power before correction, actors become even less willing to wait.

So the key illumination is this: the continuous/discontinuous question is not just “how fast progress happens,” but whether capability gains alter the game from one of iterative improvement to one of **strategic no-exit**. A discontinuous takeoff is qualitatively worse for alignment when it couples threshold capabilities with competitive incentives that eliminate unilateral safety as an option.

Therefore the highest-leverage interventions are those that change the game’s equilibrium conditions: verifiable compute governance, shared evaluation thresholds, liability and licensing tied to capability levels, and concentration-aware monitoring of critical inputs. These do not solve alignment directly; they prevent alignment from becoming unsolvable by any actor acting alone.
