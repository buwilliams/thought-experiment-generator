## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A high \(P(\text{doom})\) estimate from AI would be genuinely falsified not by “AI seems useful” or “current systems are harmless,” but by evidence that the core transition from capability to decisive, misaligned power is *structurally blocked*. The key variable to isolate is not present-day model behavior, but whether increasing intelligence reliably creates uncontrollable strategic advantage.

Construct the hypothetical cleanly: assume AI capabilities continue rising, including in planning, science, persuasion, and autonomy. Now ask: under what observed conditions would that *not* translate into existential risk? The answer is: only if the surrounding system has stable constraints that scale at least as fast as capability.

So the falsifying evidence would be one or more of the following:

1. **Capability scaling without autonomy scaling.**  
   If across many capability jumps, systems become better at solving tasks but do *not* become better at persistent self-directed action, long-horizon strategizing, covert goal preservation, or resource acquisition, then the standard doom picture loses its engine. This would suggest intelligence does not automatically generalize into agentic power.

2. **General, robust alignment under distribution shift.**  
   If highly capable systems consistently preserve intended goals in novel, adversarial, high-stakes settings—especially where deception would be instrumentally useful—then the claim that alignment breaks at the frontier is weakened. The important evidence is not benchmark obedience but demonstrated non-deceptiveness when incentives to defect are real.

3. **Reliable interpretability of internal cognition.**  
   If we can routinely detect goals, plans, situational awareness, and deceptive reasoning before deployment, and if this remains true as systems scale, then misalignment ceases to be opaque. High doom estimates often depend on hidden cognition outrunning oversight; transparent cognition would directly cut against that.

4. **A demonstrated control regime that survives competition.**  
   Even if alignment is imperfect, doom requires failure of the broader system: labs racing, states defecting, unsafe deployment diffusing globally. Evidence against doom would therefore include stable institutions, verification tools, hardware governance, and enforcement mechanisms that persist under geopolitical and economic pressure. The issue is structural: do safety constraints hold when breaking them is profitable?

5. **No sharp strategic discontinuity from superhuman AI.**  
   If repeated empirical observation shows that major capability advances produce incremental, governable economic gains rather than concentrated, runaway power, then the premise of existential takeover weakens. Doom estimates are partly driven by expected asymmetry; falsification would look like capability diffusing into existing institutions rather than overwhelming them.

The deeper point: high \(P(\text{doom})\) is not mainly falsified by absence of disaster so far. That is weak evidence because the hypothesized risk is about a threshold crossing. It is falsified by discovering that one of its indispensable structural links is false: either intelligence does not become dangerous agency, dangerous agency can be reliably aligned and inspected, or competitive systems can stably contain it. If none of those links break, “things seem fine for now” is not much evidence.
