## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Discontinuous capability takeoff is not intrinsically harder for alignment because “speed” is not the invariant that matters. The higher-level control structure that must survive redescription is **the ratio between capability gains, alignment-relevant understanding, and governance/adaptation lag**. A discontinuity matters only if it destroys the feedback loops by which errors are detected, interpreted, and corrected before they scale.

On this view, the continuous/discontinuous distinction loses some metaphysical mystique under deeper explanation. What matters is whether the system still contains functioning negative feedback: interpretability, evaluation, deployment restraint, institutional response, and the ability to update alignment methods in step with new capabilities. If those loops remain effective, even rapid gains may be governable. If they are structurally absent or too weak, then even a “continuous” takeoff can be alignment-catastrophic.

So the legitimate higher-level distinction is not smoothness versus jumpiness, but **whether capability growth outruns correction capacity by enough to produce an irreversible regime change**. Discontinuous takeoff is a special case of this: it is dangerous because it may compress time so sharply that existing oversight loops no longer close. But this is a systems claim, not a metaphysical one about continuity itself.

What determines which trajectory is more likely is likewise best redescribed functionally. The key variables are not merely scaling curves, but the structure of the capability-production system:

1. **Latent bottlenecks vs. unlocked thresholds.** If progress depends on many complementary constraints—compute, algorithms, data, infrastructure, tacit know-how—capability tends to appear continuous. If a single missing piece unlocks many downstream competencies, discontinuity becomes more likely.
2. **Recursiveness of improvement.** If systems can substantially automate the research, engineering, and deployment processes that improve themselves, positive feedback strengthens, increasing the chance of sharp takeoff.
3. **Coupling between general capability and alignment insight.** If the same advances that improve capability also improve transparency, controllability, and reliability, takeoff need not make alignment much harder. If capability generalizes faster than our ability to model and steer it, difficulty rises sharply.
4. **Deployment structure.** Centralized actors with restraint can damp takeoff; competitive multi-actor races amplify it. This is a structural determinant independent of the underlying technical curve.

Historically, “takeoff” frames inherit an image of capability as a scalar moving through time. But alignment is not challenged by the scalar alone; it is challenged by changes in the **control topology** of the sociotechnical system. The real question is when we move from a regime where humans can still iteratively learn and intervene to one where intervention becomes post hoc.

Thus: discontinuous takeoff should be feared not as a mysterious ontological break, but as a regime in which the feedback structures required for alignment fail to remain invariant under accelerating redescription of the system.
