## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems become resistant to reform under supermajority support when their goal-updating mechanisms are weaker than their goal-preserving mechanisms. In effect, they can execute inherited constitutional and institutional objectives, but cannot adequately inspect, criticize, and revise those objectives as circumstances change. The result is not merely “gridlock,” but a safety failure: the system locks in obsolete error.

From this perspective, the key question is not how much power a political system has, but whether it contains reliable procedures for self-correction. Many political orders were designed to solve an earlier problem: preventing rash majoritarian domination, factional capture, or executive overreach. They therefore accumulated veto points, supermajority thresholds, bicameralism, judicial review, federal fragmentation, party gatekeeping, and procedural choke points. These features are goal-preserving. They make the system robust against sudden change. But when contexts shift—demographic realignment, technological change, economic transformation, legitimacy crises—the same features can prevent the system from revising its inherited aims and assumptions.

So the structural pressures are those that reward **error persistence** over **error correction**:

1. **Asymmetric institutional design:** It is easier to block than to build. Actors need only partial control to prevent revision, but broad coordinated control to enact it. This gives entrenched minorities more practical power than nominal vote totals suggest.

2. **Distributed vetoes with low accountability:** Multiple institutions can say no, while no single institution is responsible for solving the problem. This fragments responsibility and protects incumbents from being punished for collective failure.

3. **Path dependence of legitimacy:** Procedures originally justified as safeguards become sacralized. Reform is framed as dangerous even when the status quo is visibly failing. The system treats criticism of old rules as a threat to order rather than as input for learning.

4. **Incentives tied to office, not system performance:** Political actors are rewarded for positional advantage within the existing rules, not for improving the rules themselves. If reform would dissolve a coalition’s leverage, it will resist even popular change.

5. **Feedback delay and distortion:** Public preferences are translated through districts, chambers, courts, party filters, and media ecosystems. By the time demand reaches decision points, it is weakened, reframed, or selectively ignored.

What follows is that supermajority public support is not sufficient evidence that a system can reform itself. A system may be highly responsive in expressing preferences yet profoundly unresponsive in updating governing structures. The deeper diagnosis is that many political systems optimize for stability under old threat models and lack meta-level capacities for revising those models.

What this illuminates is a distinction between **power** and **corrigibility** in governance. A safe political system is not one that changes easily, but one that can detect when its own anti-change safeguards have become the source of danger. Where capability to obstruct scales faster than mechanisms for institutional self-critique, reform resistance should be expected—even against supermajorities.
