## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they are built not just to execute inherited goals, but to **inspect, criticize, and revise the goals and assumptions that define their legitimacy**. Most fail to adapt because they are structurally optimized for continuity of purpose under known conditions, while rapid change turns yesterday’s legitimating goals into error-preserving constraints.

From this perspective, legitimacy is not mainly a stock of trust or authority. It is a **dynamic error-correcting relationship** between an institution and the people or environments it governs. An institution remains legitimate when it can publicly answer: *What are we trying to do, how do we know that still makes sense, what evidence or criticism can change our course, and who is empowered to force that revision?*

The key structural features, then, are:

1. **Goal-audit mechanisms**  
   Institutions need explicit procedures for examining whether their mission, metrics, and operating assumptions still fit current reality. Without this, adaptation is confined to means, while ends become frozen.

2. **Internalized criticism with protection from retaliation**  
   Dissent must be institutionally legible and survivable. If criticism can only arrive from outside, then capability scales faster than self-correction, and legitimacy erodes before reform begins.

3. **Separation between core values and current implementations**  
   Legitimate institutions preserve continuity by revising lower-level objectives in order to remain faithful to higher-level purposes. Those that collapse values into policies mistake fidelity for rigidity.

4. **Fast feedback linked to decision rights**  
   Information about changing conditions must reach actors who can revise goals, not merely optimize execution. Many institutions collect signals but route them into reporting layers that cannot alter mission.

5. **Visible revision rituals**  
   Public legitimacy depends not only on adaptation, but on intelligible adaptation. Institutions must make it clear why changes are occurring, what was mistaken, and how learning happened.

Why do most fail? Because their structure was historically designed to solve a different problem: preventing drift, faction, corruption, or chaos. They inherit legitimacy models suited to slower environments, where stability itself signaled competence. This creates reinforcing loops: success under old conditions rewards standardization, standardization suppresses criticism, suppressed criticism hardens goals, hardened goals make new realities appear as implementation failures rather than conceptual errors.

So under rapid change, many institutions become **high-capability executors of outdated purposes**. They do not merely respond too slowly; they cannot recognize that what needs revision is their objective function, not their effort level.

The implication is that the deepest leverage point is not more oversight alone, nor more agility rhetoric, but **constitutionalized self-revision**: structures that let institutions question the goals by which they justify their own power. Legitimacy survives change when institutions can learn at the level of purpose. It fails when they can only become more efficient at being wrong.
