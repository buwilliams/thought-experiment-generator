## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies systematically favor short-term outcomes because they are computational systems optimized for high-frequency preference aggregation, not for long-horizon preference preservation. When political decision-making is implemented through short electoral cycles, media feedback, and rapidly updating public signals, the system selects for agents, messages, and policies that compress well into immediate, legible rewards. Even if most citizens *represent* long-term preferences, those preferences are computationally weak unless they can survive repeated rounds of selection in present-tense institutions.

From this perspective, democracy is not primarily “failing to do what people want.” It is executing a specific kind of computation: converting dispersed, noisy social information into collective action under tight bandwidth, limited attention, and adversarial competition. In such a system, near-term signals have structural advantages. They are easier to measure, easier to attribute to incumbents, easier to campaign on, and easier for voters to use as heuristics. Long-term goods—climate stability, infrastructure resilience, institutional trust, scientific capacity—are informationally diffuse, delayed, and causally entangled. So they lose in the selection environment even when they win in opinion polls.

The key mechanism is a mismatch between **stored preference** and **selected behavior**. Citizens may contain long-run values, but the democratic system only “reads” those values through interfaces like elections, headlines, budget cycles, partisan contests, and crisis response. Those interfaces privilege information with short feedback loops. What gets replicated politically is not the deepest preference but the most competitively transmissible representation of it.

Historically, this makes sense. Mass democracy evolved as a solution to domination, legitimacy, and coordination in large societies—not as a civilization-scale optimizer over centuries. It inherited institutional rhythms suited to constraining rulers and resolving conflict among contemporaries. The short term is therefore not a bug layered atop a long-term machine; it is the native timescale of the machine unless additional memory structures are built in.

What follows is that the leverage point is not exhortation—telling voters or politicians to “care more about the future.” That adds little computational capacity. The real intervention is architectural: create institutions that encode long-horizon information so it can survive present-time selection. Examples include automatic stabilizers, independent planning bodies, sovereign wealth funds, carbon rules, multi-decade budgeting, citizens’ assemblies with delayed evaluation, constitutional protections for future generations, or metrics that convert long-term degradation into immediate political costs.

So the conjecture is: **democratic short-termism persists not because long-term preferences are absent, but because current democratic architectures cannot reliably compute them into durable policy.** Majorities may prefer the long term in principle, yet unless those preferences are given persistent, low-loss institutional form, the system will keep selecting short-term outputs.
