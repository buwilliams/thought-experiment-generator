## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
At the limit of rapid change, institutional legitimacy no longer depends primarily on the stability of outcomes or traditions, but on the institution’s visible capacity for **error-correction without identity collapse**. Institutions remain legitimate when they preserve a small set of intelligible invariants while making their decision rules, revision mechanisms, and learning processes legible to the public. Most institutions fail to adapt because they were built to optimize for equilibrium: they treat legitimacy as a stock accumulated from past success, when under high-rate change legitimacy behaves more like a flow sustained by ongoing criticism, responsiveness, and intelligible self-revision.

Pushing the problem to the boundary clarifies the structure. Imagine change approaching infinity: norms shift, technologies reorder incentives, and old expertise decays faster than institutions can certify it. In that regime, institutions cannot rely on precedent, slow deliberation, or symbolic continuity alone. If they do, they appear inert or fraudulent. Conversely, if they change everything at once, they dissolve the very continuity that makes them recognizable. So legitimacy at the edge requires a paradoxical structure: **rigid at the core, plastic at the periphery**.

The core consists of invariants: mission, jurisdiction, procedural fairness, and publicly understandable standards for what counts as success or failure. The periphery consists of policies, tactics, classifications, and implementation details that must be rapidly revisable. The institution survives only if it can say, in effect: *“We remain the same kind of thing because these principles do not change; we deserve continued trust because everything else can.”*

This implies that legitimacy is not fundamentally belief in institutional infallibility, but belief that institutional errors can be discovered and corrected before they become catastrophic. At the boundary, a legitimate institution resembles a scientific tradition more than a monument: it must expose itself to criticism, incorporate anomaly, and update without forfeiting coherence.

Why do most fail? Structurally, they inherit feedback loops designed for slower worlds. Reputation systems reward confidence over corrigibility. Bureaucracies preserve categories long after reality has changed because budgets, careers, and legal mandates are tied to those categories. Information from the edge arrives distorted or too late. Leaders face asymmetric penalties: visible change risks blame now, while hidden decay can be deferred. Thus institutions become **anti-learning systems** precisely when adaptation is most needed.

Historically, many institutions were built to suppress volatility, not metabolize it. Their legitimacy came from being buffers against change. But when change becomes ambient and continuous, buffering alone becomes maladaptive. The hidden assumption—that stability in the environment will subsidize stability in the institution—breaks.

So the structural feature that matters most is not resilience understood as resistance, but **corrigible continuity**: the capacity to remain recognizably itself while revising rapidly in response to error. Institutions that lack explicit mechanisms for internal criticism, decentralized sensing, and principle-preserving adaptation will mistake rigidity for integrity until legitimacy suddenly collapses.
