## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress to the extent that they contain *institutional mechanisms for inspecting, criticizing, and revising their own operative goals without dissolving their identity*. They collapse into new regimes when inherited constitutional objectives become uncorrectable under changed conditions, so that adaptation can occur only by breaking the constitutional frame rather than through it.

From this perspective, the key variable is not simply rigidity versus flexibility, nor coercive capacity versus legitimacy, but whether the constitutional order can recognize when its own purposes are being misapplied. A constitution is not just a constraint on power; it is a goal-encoding system. Under stress—war, depression, demographic change, technological upheaval, legitimacy shocks—the original goal set is often exposed as incomplete, internally contradictory, or tuned to a vanished environment.

A surviving constitution therefore needs meta-constitutional capacities: procedures, norms, and institutions that allow society to ask not only “Are rulers obeying the rules?” but “Are these rules still serving the reasons for which constitutional order exists?” Judicial review, amendment procedures, federalism, party competition, emergency powers with sunset constraints, and traditions of public criticism matter insofar as they make constitutional goals *corrigible*. The crucial distinction is between systems that can revise interpretation and priority while preserving continuity, and systems that treat inherited formulations as unquestionable even when reality has shifted.

What follows is that constitutional collapse is often less a failure of strength than a failure of error-correction. A regime may appear stable while suppressing criticism, but this often locks in objective errors: who counts as part of the polity, what liberties are non-negotiable, how emergency authority is bounded, what economic baseline is necessary for legitimacy. When stress arrives, unresolved errors accumulate faster than the constitution can process them. Politics then migrates outside constitutional channels—to coups, revolutions, plebiscitary executives, secession, or extra-legal administration—because only external rupture can revise the blocked goals.

Historically, many constitutions inherit unexamined assumptions from the conflicts that produced them: class compromise, elite fears of democracy, religious settlement, imperial structure, racial hierarchy, military balance. These assumptions can remain invisible during normal periods. Major stress reveals them as outdated objective functions. Survival depends on whether the system can expose those inheritances to criticism before they harden into existential contradictions.

So the deepest predictor of survival is not textual brilliance or initial design alone, but the presence of recursive learning: institutions that can detect goal failure, authorize criticism from within, and enact revision at lower cost than regime replacement. Constitutional resilience is therefore a special case of epistemic resilience. A constitution endures when it is not merely binding, but *self-correcting*.
