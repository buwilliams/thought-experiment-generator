## Conjecture

Conjecture: Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change only when their justifying story, decision procedures, and error-correction mechanisms are tightly coupled — so that adaptation changes policy without forcing a collapse of identity. Most institutions fail because their legitimacy rests on interchangeable narrative claims while their actual structure depends on frozen incentives, status hierarchies, and delayed feedback loops.

Using the “load-bearing detail” lens, the key question is: which parts of legitimacy can be altered without breaking the institution’s ability to coordinate action and secure compliance? In robust institutions, legitimacy is not mainly carried by slogans like “tradition,” “expertise,” or “the will of the people.” Those are often replaceable surface descriptions. The load-bearing parts are deeper: who may revise rules, by what process, on what timescale, with what visible checks on error, and how losses are distributed during adaptation. If you change these, the institution’s behavior changes substantially; compensating changes are required elsewhere. That is evidence they are structurally real.

So the institutions that survive rapid change tend to share a few structural features:

1. Separable core and periphery. They preserve a stable governing purpose while allowing rules, personnel, and methods to change. If every procedural update threatens the institution’s identity, adaptation looks like betrayal.

2. Visible error-correction. They can admit mistake without delegitimizing themselves. This requires a conception of legitimacy based not on infallibility but on reliable revision.

3. Fast internal feedback, slow constitutional drift. They can update operational decisions quickly while changing foundational commitments slowly and explicitly. Without this layering, either nothing adapts or everything destabilizes.

4. Loss-containment mechanisms. Adaptation creates losers. Institutions remain legitimate when they can absorb or compensate these losses rather than externalize them onto constituencies whose support they still require.

5. Recursive authorization. They have accepted procedures for altering the procedures. This is crucial under novel conditions; otherwise adaptation appears unconstitutional even when necessary.

Why do most fail? Because historically, many institutions were built to solve a prior environment’s problem — often stability, elite bargaining, or mass compliance — not high-velocity learning. They inherit legitimacy narratives from that earlier settlement. Under rapid change, these narratives stop matching performance, but the institution cannot revise them without exposing that legitimacy was never grounded in the story alone. Its real load-bearing structure is elsewhere: veto points, patronage networks, professional monopolies, rigid metrics, and reputational commitments to certainty. Change one of those and the whole coalition must be renegotiated.

Thus most institutional adaptation fails not from ignorance of change but from structural incoherence: the parts that must change are the same parts that currently carry legitimacy for internal actors. The institution can explain itself or it can preserve itself, but not both in the same form. Durable legitimacy under change therefore depends on a rare design: identity anchored in corrigibility rather than continuity of specific arrangements.
