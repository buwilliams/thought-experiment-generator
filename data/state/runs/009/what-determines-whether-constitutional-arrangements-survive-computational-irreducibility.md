## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional survival under major social stress is determined less by the formal design of institutions than by whether the constitution functions as a **computationally tractable rule-set for conflict processing** under novel conditions. Constitutions collapse when stress generates interaction effects among factions, institutions, and norms that cannot be compressed into existing legal scripts, forcing actors to “compute” outcomes through live struggle rather than through recognized procedures.

From the perspective of **computational irreducibility**, a constitutional order is not a static blueprint but an ongoing distributed computation: elections, courts, bureaucracies, parties, media, and public expectations recursively update one another. In ordinary times, this computation appears predictable because routines have stabilized. Under major stress—war, depression, demographic reordering, technological shock, legitimacy crises—the system enters regions where no actor can shortcut the consequences. The constitution’s real test is whether its minimal generative rules still let political conflict unfold without destroying the rule-system itself.

What matters, then, is not mainly textual completeness or balance in the abstract, but whether the arrangement contains **simple, reusable, socially legible procedures** for absorbing high novelty. Survival is more likely when:
1. **Key actors can keep iterating within the constitutional game** even after losing major battles.
2. **Ambiguity is bounded**: there is room for adaptation, but not so much that every dispute becomes existential.
3. **Feedback loops remain negative rather than positive**: institutional defeats do not automatically escalate into delegitimation, purges, secession, or coups.
4. **Norms and organizations compress complexity for participants**: parties, courts, federal units, and civil services translate social turmoil into manageable procedural contests.

Collapse becomes likely when constitutional rules no longer reduce complexity but instead amplify it. This happens when actors cannot know in advance how losses will be contained, when enforcement depends on discretionary loyalty rather than impersonal procedure, or when constitutional interpretation itself becomes the main battlefield. At that point, the regime ceases to be a common computational framework and becomes merely one strategy among others.

This reframes unpredictability. Constitutional breakdown is not simply a failure of foresight or virtue. In many cases, no elite could have predicted the path in advance because the crisis had to be “run” through millions of interactions. But computational irreducibility does not imply explanatory defeat. It suggests a different explanatory target: identify the **minimal generative structure** that determines whether stress is processed as reversible constitutional conflict or as recursive escalation into regime replacement.

So the deeper determinant of survival is: **Can the constitutional order continue to serve as the cheapest shared protocol for political rivalry when society becomes hard to predict?** If yes, stress is metabolized. If no, actors migrate to extra-constitutional computation—force, paralysis, emergency rule, or refounding—and a new regime emerges.
