## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change only when they are built to **shift equilibria faster than stakeholders can defect**. Most fail not because leaders misread events, but because legitimacy itself is embedded in a lock-in structure: every key actor depends on visible continuity, so any unilateral adaptation looks like betrayal, weakness, or rule-breaking before it can be recognized as renewal.

From an equilibrium lock-in perspective, institutional legitimacy is not merely public belief or performance. It is a **coordination settlement** among multiple parties — leaders, staff, funders, regulators, users, critics, and the broader public — about what counts as valid authority. That settlement includes expectations about process, symbols, competence, fairness, and continuity. Under stable conditions, this equilibrium is self-reinforcing: each actor supports the institution because others do too.

Rapid change disrupts the fit between inherited rules and present reality. But adaptation is hard because legitimacy is jointly produced. If one actor moves first — a university changing standards, a court changing doctrine too quickly, a newsroom changing editorial norms, a government agency changing procedures — others can interpret that move as illegitimate opportunism. So even when all parties would prefer a reformed institution to a decaying one, no single party can safely authorize the transition alone. The result is a structural trap: **everyone waits for proof that adaptation is legitimate, but that proof can only emerge after coordinated adaptation has already occurred**.

This explains why most institutions fail. They inherit governance structures optimized for **error avoidance under old conditions**, not for **credible collective revision under new ones**. Their legitimacy mechanisms are backward-looking: precedent, charter, professional norms, compliance routines, donor expectations, and reputational risk controls. These features stabilize trust in normal times but become veto points in turbulent times. Adaptation then carries concentrated short-term legitimacy costs, while nonadaptation distributes long-term failure more diffusely. So institutional actors rationally defend stale forms even when they privately know change is needed.

What follows is that adaptive legitimacy requires specific structural features:

1. **Authorized revision channels** — recognized procedures for changing rules without appearing to abandon them.
2. **Shared diagnostic mechanisms** — ways for stakeholders to converge on “the environment has changed” so adaptation is seen as necessary rather than discretionary.
3. **Cross-stakeholder commitment devices** — reforms that move multiple actors together, reducing first-mover penalties.
4. **Narrative continuity** — institutions must frame change as fidelity to mission, not rupture with identity.
5. **Slack and redundancy** — without reserve capacity, adaptation is experienced as pure threat and triggers defensive lock-in.

So the key illumination is: legitimacy under rapid change is less about flexibility in the abstract than about whether an institution has built **meta-legitimacy** — legitimacy for changing its own operating equilibrium. Most institutions lack this, so they can preserve legitimacy briefly or adapt effectively, but not both at once.
