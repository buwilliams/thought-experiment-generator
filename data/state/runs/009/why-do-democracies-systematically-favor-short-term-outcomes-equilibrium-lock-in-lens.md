## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies often underprovide long-term policy for the same structural reason markets underprovide public goods: future-oriented governance is a **diffuse, non-excludable good**, while the costs of supplying it are **immediate, concentrated, and politically attributable**. This creates a stable **no-provision equilibrium** in which individually rational behavior by voters, politicians, parties, and interest groups systematically selects short-term outcomes even when most people would prefer the long-term benefits to be secured.

What is illuminated is that the problem is not mainly ignorance, hypocrisy, or weak civic virtue. It is a coordination failure generated by institutional structure. A stable climate, resilient infrastructure, debt sustainability, pandemic preparedness, basic research, and state capacity all have public-good features: no individual voter can secure them alone, no one can easily be excluded from their benefits, and each person’s contribution to achieving them is negligible. By contrast, the sacrifices required to fund or protect them — higher taxes, delayed consumption, regulatory burdens, foregone patronage, visible tradeoffs — are immediate and often concentrated.

This asymmetry generates reinforcing feedback loops:

- **Voters** rationally reward visible present benefits over uncertain future gains.
- **Politicians** maximize electoral survival by supplying targeted, legible benefits now and deferring costs.
- **Interest groups** mobilize intensely around concentrated losses, while beneficiaries of long-term policy remain latent and unorganized.
- **Parties and media** further shorten time horizons by tying accountability to near-term outcomes.

So even if a majority says it wants “the long term,” that preference is weakly actionable unless institutions convert it into credible commitment. The majority preference is real but politically non-operative, much as widespread willingness to enjoy a public good does not by itself fund it.

Historically, this helps explain why democracies repeatedly invent commitment devices: independent central banks, civil service protections, constitutional debt rules, sovereign wealth funds, automatic stabilizers, long-term budgeting, and insulated expert agencies. These are not deviations from democracy so much as attempts to solve a public-goods problem internal to democratic choice.

What follows is that exhortation alone will not fix short-termism. The leverage lies in **changing the excludability and timing structure** of political rewards and penalties. Institutions must make long-term provision more politically appropriable and short-term extraction more costly. For example: automatic funding mechanisms, cross-party binding frameworks, delayed-benefit lock-ins, citizen assemblies with future-oriented mandates, or constitutional protections for intergenerational goods.

So the key claim is: **democratic short-termism is best understood as structural underprovision of long-term public goods, not merely as confusion or lack of will.** Majorities can prefer the funded future and still reproduce a no-provision equilibrium because the political system privatizes present costs while socializing future benefits.
