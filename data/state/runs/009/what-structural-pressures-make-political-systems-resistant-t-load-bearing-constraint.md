## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform under supermajority support when the institutions that translate preference into action are built from mutually reinforcing veto points, coalition-fragmenting incentives, and incumbent-protecting feedback loops. In such systems, “majority favor” is not a load-bearing variable. What matters is whether support is organized in the specific form the system can absorb.

The explanatory test is this: if we swap out one detail, does the account still work? Some common details are not load-bearing. It does not fundamentally matter whether the issue is healthcare, housing, campaign finance, or electoral reform; resistance recurs across domains. Nor is “public ignorance” sufficient, because reform often stalls even when preferences are durable, informed, and lopsided. Those details can vary while the phenomenon remains.

What *is* load-bearing is the structural arrangement.

First, reform usually requires crossing more thresholds than preservation. Bicameralism, courts, federalism, party gatekeepers, committee systems, executive vetoes, and administrative choke points create **asymmetric friction**: doing nothing requires less coordination than changing rules. If this detail changes — if veto points are removed or aligned — reform rates rise. So this is explanatory, not decorative.

Second, broad majorities are often **shallow and cross-pressured**, while minorities that benefit from the status quo are concentrated, organized, and continuously engaged. Change imposes coordination demands: agreement on wording, timing, sequencing, enforcement, funding, and tradeoffs. Opposition often needs only to block one stage. If we alter this detail — make majority support institutionally organized and persistent — resistance weakens. So intensity and organization, not raw numbers, are load-bearing.

Third, political systems are often designed to **filter mass sentiment through territorial and temporal structures** rather than count it directly. Malapportionment, staggered terms, primary systems, and districting mean that representatives respond to electorally decisive sub-publics, not national supermajorities. Change this representational structure, and outcomes change even with identical public opinion. That makes representational distortion a core constraint.

Fourth, incumbents often sit inside **self-reinforcing loops**: existing rules help select actors who benefit from those rules; those actors control the agenda and reform process; delay preserves their position. This is especially powerful when the proposed reform would alter the mechanism by which future power is allocated. In that case, reform asks winners to ratify the weakening of their own winning strategy. If we remove incumbent control over the reform pathway, resistance often falls. So self-reference is load-bearing.

What this illuminates is that “supermajority support” and “capacity to reform” are different system properties. A political order can be highly responsive in rhetoric yet structurally conservative in operation because its architecture privileges **preference aggregation** weakly but **stability filters** strongly. The hidden constraint is not lack of demand for reform; it is that demand must survive a gauntlet designed by and for actors already adapted to the existing rules.
