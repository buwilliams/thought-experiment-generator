## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies systematically favor short-term outcomes because they are built to revise *leaders* more easily than they revise the *goal-selection machinery* that governs collective choice. When context changes, the system can punish officeholders quickly, but it has weak capacity to inspect and correct inherited objective functions like quarterly growth, annual budgets, electoral cycles, media attention horizons, and jurisdictional fragmentation. As a result, even if majorities sincerely prefer long-term outcomes, the decision system converts those preferences into short-term actions because its internal feedback loops are tuned to near-term signals.

From this perspective, the key safety failure is not merely “politicians are myopic.” It is that democratic systems often cannot adequately criticize and rewrite the operational goals embedded in their own institutions. Elections are a powerful correction mechanism, but they are a coarse one: they remove agents without necessarily exposing or changing the underlying reward structure those agents faced. So a democracy can repeatedly select for short-termist behavior even while voters repeatedly endorse long-term aims.

Historically, this makes sense. Representative democracy evolved as a solution to tyranny, faction, and unaccountable rule. Its strongest self-correction mechanism was frequent public contestation over who governs. But that design inheritance assumes that replacing rulers is the main problem. In modern high-complexity societies, a different problem dominates: many harms are delayed, diffuse, cross-generational, and technically mediated. The institutions that once made power corrigible now also compress political attention into cycles too short for many real-world feedback signals to arrive.

Systems-wise, the structure is clear:

- **Fast feedback loops:** elections, news cycles, market reactions, interest-group pressure.
- **Slow feedback loops:** climate resilience, public health capacity, infrastructure quality, institutional trust, demographic stability.
- **Mismatch:** decision-makers are rewarded by the fast loops, while the majority’s long-term preferences depend on the slow ones.

This creates a kind of goal-locking error. The system’s explicit rhetoric may be “serve the public interest,” but its effective objective becomes “avoid immediate punishment under current measurement regimes.” Long-term preferences exist in the population, yet they are underrepresented because they are harder to measure, attribute, and defend under adversarial politics.

What follows is that improving democratic long-termism is less about asking leaders to be wiser and more about making the polity more *self-correcting about its own objectives*. A safer democracy would contain institutions that inspect whether current incentive structures are distorting public aims: independent long-horizon bodies, constitutional protections for future interests, multi-decade budgeting, better measures of delayed harms, and mechanisms that force periodic criticism of inherited policy goals rather than only competition over short-run outputs.

So the deep claim is: democracies become short-termist when their capacity for changing officeholders outpaces their capacity for revising the institutional goals and feedback structures that officeholders inherit. Majorities can want the long term and still get the short term if the system is not corrigible at the level where goals are operationalized.
