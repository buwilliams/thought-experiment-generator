## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies skew short-term not mainly because citizens “want” short-term gains, but because democratic self-government is a **strange loop with mismatched timescales**: the public must govern itself through institutions that continually refer back to the public for legitimacy, yet the public encountered at each loop is a **different temporal slice** of itself. The system can represent “the people now” far more crisply than “the same people later,” so feedback becomes dominated by near-term self-reference.

Here the strange loop is: citizens create institutions to express their collective will; those institutions shape incentives, attention, and identity; citizens then evaluate institutions through those shaped preferences and elect again. In principle this loop could stabilize long-term purposes. In practice, each pass through the loop compresses the future into a weakly represented abstraction while the present appears as a vivid, punish-or-reward signal. Elections, media cycles, budgets, and partisan competition all increase the system’s responsiveness to the immediately legible version of “the people,” not the temporally extended one.

So the puzzle—majorities say they prefer long-term outcomes, yet systems produce short-term ones—is illuminated as a problem of **self-reference failure across time**. The demos is trying to govern itself, but the “self” doing the governing is not a single stable entity. It is a recursively updated, politically constructed self-model. At high frequency, this model tracks current prices, scandals, and visible disruptions; at low frequency, it only weakly encodes climate stability, institutional health, infrastructure resilience, or intergenerational welfare. What cannot be recursively re-entered into the loop in salient form gets systematically underweighted.

This also explains why information alone rarely fixes the problem. The issue is not ignorance in the simple sense, but a structural asymmetry in recursive representation. Future citizens cannot vote; future costs are mediated through forecasts; long-term benefits are politically ownerless; short-term pains are concentrated and immediately attributable. The loop therefore learns the wrong lesson: “responsiveness” becomes responsiveness to what is rapidly self-referentially visible.

Historically, democracy solved the problem of unchecked power by making rulers answerable to the ruled. But this solution imported an unexamined assumption: that frequent accountability tracks the public good. In a strange-loop frame, frequent accountability can instead trap the system in **recursive presentism**, where legitimacy depends on repeated confirmation by the current electorate, preventing institutions from acting as the long-memory of the polity.

What follows is that the highest-leverage reforms are not moral appeals to think long-term, but institutional devices that let the polity **refer to its future self as part of its present self-model**: constitutional commitments, independent long-horizon bodies, automatic stabilizers, future-impact accounting, citizens’ assemblies with delayed review, or representation for future generations. These are not constraints on democracy but prosthetics for democratic self-reference across time. The problem is not that democracy is insufficiently responsive; it is that its loop closes too quickly to include the self it claims to govern.
