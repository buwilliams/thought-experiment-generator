## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional survival under major social stress depends less on formal design than on whether the constitution successfully provides a *public good of coordinated restraint* that remains non-excludable, widely legible, and self-enforcing across rival factions during crisis. Collapse occurs when this good is structurally underprovided: each major actor benefits if *others* continue honoring constitutional limits, but under stress each has an individually rational incentive to defect first, producing a stable no-restraint equilibrium even when most actors would prefer continued constitutional order.

This lens clarifies that breakdown is not primarily explained by bad motives, confusion, or insufficient civic virtue. The deeper issue is strategic structure. Constitutional order is a collective good: if it exists, even opponents benefit from reduced uncertainty, bounded conflict, and predictable succession. But no actor can be selectively excluded from those benefits, and each actor bears concentrated short-term costs for upholding restraints when rivals may not. In ordinary times, norms, courts, parties, and administrative routines mask this dilemma. Under major stress—war, depression, mass polarization, succession crisis, state incapacity—the incentives sharpen. Elites, parties, military leaders, regional blocs, and social movements face a choice: incur the cost of restraint now, or seize exceptional advantage before others do.

What determines survival, then, is whether institutions alter that payoff structure enough to prevent first-order defection. Durable constitutions are those that create credible expectations that restraint will be reciprocated and defection punished. They do this through overlapping enforcement centers, reserve capacities for crisis adjustment, focal procedures for emergency action, and organizations with a stake in preserving the whole game rather than winning a single round. Historically, constitutions often inherit assumptions of trust, elite cohesion, or slow-moving conflict from the conditions of their founding. Stress reveals these inherited assumptions. Arrangements collapse when they depended on informal cooperation that was never robustly provisioned as a public good.

This suggests a practical distinction: some constitutional crises are interpretive disputes, but others are public-goods failures. In the latter, better argument or clearer text alone will not save the regime. The key variable is whether actors believe enough others will continue investing in constitutional compliance to make their own compliance rational.

So the illuminating claim is: **constitutional resilience is a coordination-and-collective-action achievement, not merely a legal one.** Major social stress becomes regime change when the system cannot supply mutual assurance at scale. Survival requires institutions that transform restraint from a morally admirable sacrifice into a strategically stable expectation.
