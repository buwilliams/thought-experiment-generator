## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What chiefly determines whether constitutional arrangements survive major social stress is not the *severity of stress itself* but whether key actors update their beliefs that staying within the constitutional order remains more likely to secure their interests than defecting from it. The Bayesian lens clarifies that collapse is often misexplained by reversing the conditional: analysts observe crisis before regime change and infer that crisis causes collapse, but the more probative question is whether constitutional collapse was already likely *given the prior structure* of elite trust, enforcement capacity, and legitimacy.

In Bayesian terms, people often mistake **P(major stress | constitutional collapse)** for **P(collapse | major stress)**. Many collapsed constitutions were preceded by war, depression, polarization, or insurgency. But that does not imply that such stresses usually destroy constitutions. To assess survival, we need priors: how often do constitutions under comparable stress survive, conditional on institutional depth, elite coordination, military subordination, fiscal capacity, and public legitimacy?

This shifts attention from dramatic shocks to **base rates and updating structure**. Major social stress is common in history; constitutional collapse is less common and unevenly distributed. Therefore stress alone has weak evidentiary force unless paired with features that raise the prior probability of breakdown. The strongest hidden priors are:

1. **Elite commitment prior:** Do governing and opposition elites believe losers today can still compete tomorrow?  
2. **Coercion prior:** Is the military/security apparatus expected to obey constitutional authority even under strain?  
3. **Legitimacy prior:** Do citizens and officials treat the constitution as the focal rule-set for resolving conflict, or merely as a temporary instrument?  
4. **Administrative prior:** Can the state still collect revenue, enforce decisions, and supply minimum order?  

Under this view, stress acts less as a direct cause than as **evidence that updates these priors**. A banking panic, mass protest, or external war matters when it reveals that constitutional procedures no longer coordinate expectations. If actors infer that others are likely to abandon the rules, then defection becomes rational, creating a self-confirming cascade. Survival occurs when institutions generate opposite inferences: that even under pain, others will keep playing by constitutional rules.

So the real determinant is the **information structure of the regime**: whether stress is interpreted as temporary turbulence inside a trusted framework, or as a signal that the framework itself is no longer credible. Constitutions survive when they preserve high prior confidence in future compliance and make bad news only weakly diagnostic of total breakdown. They collapse when stress sharply updates beliefs toward “the rules no longer bind,” after which regime replacement becomes more probable than constitutional repair.

Thus the Bayesian collision illuminates a deeper claim: **constitutional resilience is fundamentally a problem of belief coordination under uncertainty, not merely institutional design under pressure.**
