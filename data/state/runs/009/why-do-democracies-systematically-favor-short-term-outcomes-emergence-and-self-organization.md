## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies do not systematically produce short-term outcomes mainly because voters are individually short-sighted. They do so because the **interaction structure** of democratic systems converts diffuse long-term preferences into weak political signals, while converting concentrated short-term pressures into strong, self-reinforcing feedback. The bias is therefore an **emergent property of the system’s incentive topology**.

What is true at the component level can be misleading: many citizens may sincerely prefer long-term goods—stable climate policy, infrastructure, debt sustainability, institutional health. But these preferences are typically **low-frequency, weakly coordinated, and cross-cutting**. By contrast, short-term interests are often **immediate, organized, legible, and sanction-capable**. Elections, party competition, media cycles, budgeting rules, and lobbying channels interact so that what gets rewarded is not “what most people ultimately want,” but **what can repeatedly and visibly affect officeholders’ survival**.

The key explanatory shift is from preferences to **feedback loops**:

- **Electoral loop:** Politicians are judged at short intervals using noisy, recent indicators. This favors actions with visible near-term gains and deferred costs.
- **Attention loop:** Media and public attention amplify urgent, concrete effects over slow, distributed harms, making long-term policy politically faint.
- **Organization loop:** Groups bearing concentrated short-term stakes coordinate better than majorities sharing abstract future benefits.
- **Credibility loop:** Long-term policies often require present sacrifice in exchange for future gains that may be undone by successors. This makes voters rationally reluctant to support them, further weakening demand.
- **Blame asymmetry:** Immediate losses are attributable; avoided future catastrophes are not. So politicians are punished for present pain more reliably than rewarded for future resilience.

From this perspective, the paradox disappears: a majority can “prefer the long term” in surveys while the democratic system still selects short-term outcomes, because **preferences do not enter politics directly**. They are filtered through structures that privilege salience, coordination, attribution, and repetition. The system is not failing to represent a preexisting stable will; it is **transforming scattered long-term sentiment into politically inert noise**.

Historically, this makes sense. Democratic mechanisms evolved as solutions to problems of legitimacy, peaceful power transfer, and protection against tyranny—not primarily to optimize intertemporal governance. They inherited institutions tuned for **responsiveness to current constituents**, not for representing future persons, cumulative risks, or slow variables. The short-term bias is thus not a moral defect added onto democracy; it is a structural consequence of what these institutions were built to solve.

What follows is that fixes aimed at exhorting voters or electing “better leaders” will have limited effect unless they alter the **interaction structure**. The leverage lies in institutions that strengthen long-term signals: independent planning bodies, durable cross-party commitments, automatic stabilizers, constitutional future-oriented constraints, citizens’ assemblies, sovereign funds, and rules that make deferred harms more visible and immediate in present decision-making.

So the core claim is: **democratic short-termism is an emergent systems effect produced by feedback architecture, not merely a sum of myopic individual choices.**
