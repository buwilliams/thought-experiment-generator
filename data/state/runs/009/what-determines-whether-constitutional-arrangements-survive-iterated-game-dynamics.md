## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress when the political system preserves the conditions of a repeated game among elites, institutions, and mass publics; they collapse into new regimes when stress converts politics into a sequence of effectively one-shot interactions. The key variable is not constitutional text alone, nor civic virtue alone, but whether actors still expect to keep dealing with one another under the same rules tomorrow.

From an iterated-game perspective, a constitution is not just a legal document. It is a framework that makes repeated bargaining possible by stabilizing expectations, preserving reputations, and making defections legible. Major social stress—war, economic breakdown, demographic upheaval, legitimacy crises—tests whether that repeated-game structure still exists.

Constitutional survival becomes more likely when three structural conditions hold:

1. **The shadow of the future remains long.** Political actors believe they will continue interacting within the same institutional order, so short-term constitutional hardball is restrained by fear of future retaliation and loss of future gains.
2. **Defection is observable and attributable.** If coups, emergency overreach, electoral manipulation, or constitutional violations are publicly legible, reputational punishment and coordinated resistance become possible.
3. **Reciprocity remains feasible.** Opponents retain enough organizational continuity and mutual recognition to punish defectors today and cooperate tomorrow. This is what makes “forbearance” rational rather than naive.

Collapse into a new regime becomes likely when stress shortens horizons and obscures defection. If actors believe that this may be their last meaningful turn—because of civil war risk, revolutionary mobilization, external invasion, or total economic ruin—then future cooperation is heavily discounted. Under those conditions, seizing the courts, suspending elections, or abandoning constitutional constraints becomes individually rational even if collectively ruinous. Likewise, if propaganda, fragmented media, or institutional complexity make violations hard to detect or agree upon, reciprocal enforcement breaks down: each side can defect while denying that it has done so.

This perspective illuminates why constitutions often fail not at the moment of greatest formal violation but earlier, when repeated-game expectations erode. The decisive event is often a shift in beliefs: from “we will have to live with one another under these rules” to “if we do not win now, there may be no next round.” Once that belief spreads, constitutional compliance unravels quickly.

So the deeper determinant of constitutional endurance under stress is the maintenance of **credible political futurity**: durable expectation of continued interaction, visible rule-breaking, and institutions capable of reciprocal response. Constitutional design matters insofar as it protects those features. Where stress destroys them, no parchment barrier will save the regime.
