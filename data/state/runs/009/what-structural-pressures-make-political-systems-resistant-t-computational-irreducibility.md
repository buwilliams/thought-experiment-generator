## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems are resistant to reform under supermajority support because they are **computationally irreducible coalition-processing systems**: the relevant outcome is not determined by aggregate preference alone, but by the stepwise interaction of many veto points, procedures, incentive loops, and strategic adaptations. Therefore, “most people want change” does not compress into “change will happen.” It must still be computed through the full institutional sequence.

This perspective shifts the explanation away from ignorance, corruption alone, or irrationality as primary causes. The deeper issue is structural: political systems are built from layered rules that evolved to solve older problems — preventing tyranny, stabilizing succession, protecting minorities, distributing patronage, slowing passion, preserving elite bargains. These inherited mechanisms create a dense network of **feedback loops and local decision nodes**. Reform must survive each one in order.

What follows is that supermajority opinion is only one input into a system whose behavior depends on the **minimal generative rule**: proposals are transformed as they pass through committees, courts, chambers, parties, bureaucracies, media cycles, donor networks, federal divisions, and implementation agencies. Each stage changes incentives for the next. Actors respond not to the abstract public will, but to proximate signals: primary threats, procedural choke points, lobbying pressure, precedent risk, coalition fragility, institutional turf. Because those responses are adaptive and sequential, the final outcome often cannot be inferred from public support in any shortcut way.

So resistance to reform is not accidental friction added onto an otherwise majoritarian machine. It is often the system’s native mode. Systems designed to require repeated coordination under uncertainty naturally favor the status quo, because **inaction is the default basin of attraction** requiring less coordinated computation than action. Reform demands alignment across multiple heterogeneous nodes; blocking reform may require only one defection, delay, reinterpretation, or dilution.

This also illuminates why reform windows are brief and hard to generalize from. A successful reform is less like the expression of public preference and more like a rare trajectory through a highly path-dependent state space. Slight changes in timing, framing, leadership, court doctrine, economic conditions, or agenda congestion can alter the entire sequence. That is a signature of irreducible systems: macro-intentions do not straightforwardly determine micro-transitions.

The practical implication is that reformers err when they treat persuasion as sufficient. If the system is irreducible, the leverage point is not merely increasing support from 60% to 70%, but **changing the generative rule**: removing veto points, simplifying procedures, aligning implementation authority, reducing opportunities for sequential obstruction, and redesigning feedback loops so that public preference remains legible through each stage. In such systems, durable reform depends less on overwhelming opinion than on institutional architectures that make collective preference computationally tractable.
