## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Political systems resist reform under supermajority support when reform functions as a **public good with concentrated transition costs, diffuse long-term benefits, and weak excludability**, creating a stable **collective action trap** rather than merely a failure of public understanding or will.

### What is true in this lens
A supermajority favoring change does not by itself generate reform capacity. If the benefits of reform are broad, delayed, and non-excludable, then each supporter has little individual incentive to bear the costs of organizing, coordinating, monitoring, and sustaining pressure. The outcome everyone prefers in aggregate can remain underprovided because the inputs required to produce it are themselves public goods.

Meanwhile, the status quo usually has **organized beneficiaries**: actors who receive concentrated, immediate advantages from existing rules and can therefore justify investing heavily in defense of them. Reform coalitions, by contrast, often consist of people who each gain only modestly, probabilistically, or in the future. So even a numerical majority can be structurally outmatched by a smaller minority with better incentive alignment.

### Structural pressures that follow
1. **Asymmetric organization**  
   Incumbent interests can coordinate cheaply because their stakes are high and specific. Reform supporters face high coordination costs because their gains are diffuse.

2. **Transition-cost concentration**  
   Even beneficial reforms create visible short-term disruptions for identifiable groups. Those groups mobilize intensely; beneficiaries remain latent.

3. **Information and monitoring as public goods**  
   Voters may sincerely support reform but cannot individually justify paying the cost to track legislative detail, detect sabotage, or punish procedural obstruction. This creates room for symbolic support without implementation.

4. **Institutional veto multiplication**  
   Systems with many checkpoints convert diffuse support into attrition. Each veto point lowers the probability that any individual supporter’s effort matters, which further suppresses investment in collective action.

5. **Temporal discounting built into institutions**  
   Political actors operate on election cycles, while many reforms pay off later. Even supporters may rationally prioritize immediate, targeted benefits over systemic change.

### What this illuminates
Resistance to reform is often misdiagnosed as hypocrisy, ignorance, or weak conviction. But the deeper issue is **underprovision of reform-producing collective action**. The system is not merely failing to register preferences; it is reliably producing an equilibrium in which preference majorities do not become implementation majorities.

This also explains why public opinion can remain stable for years while policy does not move: support is a stock, but reform capacity is a flow. Without institutions that convert diffuse approval into organized pressure, the stock never becomes action.

### Implication
If this conjecture is right, the leverage point is not mainly persuasion. It is building mechanisms that **make reform effort more excludable, lower coordination costs, and reduce veto opportunities**: durable organizations, automatic triggers, simpler procedures, concentrated incentives for pro-reform actors, and institutions that socialize transition costs. Reform becomes possible not when more people agree, but when the structure stops treating implementation as an unfunded public good.
