## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress not primarily because their rules are well-designed in ordinary conditions, but because they retain a functioning **error-correction loop at the boundary case** — the point where groups are tempted to treat loss, fear, or emergency as a reason to suspend the constitution itself. Collapse occurs when stress drives key actors past a threshold where they no longer expect conflicts to remain corrigible within the system, and instead seek safety in extra-constitutional power.

Pushing the problem to the limit clarifies this. In calm periods, many constitutions appear stable because most disputes are low-stakes and reversible. Their apparent legitimacy may be misleading. The real test is the extreme case: civil unrest, war, fiscal breakdown, demographic shock, elite deadlock, or a legitimacy crisis after an election. At that boundary, ordinary constitutional behavior breaks down unless the system can still answer the question: **“How do we go on disagreeing without one side needing final annihilation of the other?”**

This suggests that what survives is not a fixed arrangement of institutions alone, but a deeper structure of **mutual expectation**:

1. losers expect to have a future inside the regime;
2. winners expect restraint to be more profitable than domination;
3. coercive institutions expect divided authority to remain meaningful;
4. the public expects constitutional procedures to remain a live path for correction.

At the extreme, if any of these expectations goes to zero, constitutional survival also approaches zero. A constitution collapses when too many actors conclude that the cost of staying within the rules exceeds the expected value of future participation under those rules.

This reframes the determinant of survival. It is less about textual rigidity versus flexibility in the abstract, and more about whether the system preserves **nonterminal conflict** under stress. Durable constitutions are those that convert existential conflicts back into political conflicts. Fragile constitutions are those in which stress transforms political rivalry into ontological struggle — who belongs, who counts, who may rule at all.

Historically, many constitutional devices inherit peacetime assumptions: trust in electoral loss, slow institutional bargaining, loyalty of administrators, bounded emergency powers. Stress reveals these as hidden stocks that can be depleted. Once depleted, positive feedback takes over: emergency centralization breeds distrust, distrust justifies further centralization, and the constitution becomes a ceremonial shell around a new regime.

So the conjecture is: **constitutional endurance is determined by the survival of recursive error-correction under extreme stress — the capacity to preserve reversible politics when pressures make irreversibility attractive.** Institutional forms matter insofar as they sustain that loop at the edge. The boundary case reveals the essence: a constitution survives if enough actors still prefer an improvable order to a decisive rupture.
