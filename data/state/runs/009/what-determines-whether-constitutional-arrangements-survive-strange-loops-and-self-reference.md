## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress when they contain a *stable strange loop of self-reference*: institutions can observe, interpret, criticize, and revise their own operation **from within the constitutional order** without requiring an external sovereign to reset the system. They collapse when that loop breaks—when the constitution can no longer successfully represent the society whose conflicts it is meant to govern, and attempts at self-correction recurse into contradiction, paralysis, or extra-constitutional force.

From the strange-loop perspective, a constitution is not just a set of rules. It is a hierarchy that turns back on itself: the people authorize institutions, institutions interpret the constitution, and constitutional interpretation redefines who “the people” are, what counts as authority, and what forms of conflict are legitimate. Survival depends on whether this recursive structure remains productive rather than pathological.

What determines this? Not rigidity versus flexibility in the abstract, but whether the system has *institutionalized self-modeling capacity*. Under stress, every constitutional order faces a Gödel-like problem: no fixed rule set can fully anticipate the conflicts that arise within the society it governs. So survival requires mechanisms that let the system treat its own failures as inputs for revision—courts, amendment procedures, elections, federalism, emergency constraints, elite bargaining norms, and civic narratives that preserve loyalty while allowing reinterpretation.

Collapse becomes likely when recursion intensifies but loses closure. This happens when:
1. **Representation decouples from social reality** — key groups no longer see themselves in the constitutional mirror.
2. **Conflict cannot be translated into legitimate constitutional language** — grievances become unintelligible within the order’s categories.
3. **Meta-rules lose authority** — actors no longer agree on how to resolve disagreement about the rules.
4. **Self-reference is monopolized** — one branch, party, class, army, or leader claims to embody “the constitution” directly, short-circuiting distributed recursion.
5. **Adaptation requires violating identity** — reform becomes imaginable only as regime replacement.

Historically, constitutions emerge to solve the problem of power after prior breakdowns—civil war, dynastic struggle, colonial exit, revolution. They therefore inherit unexamined assumptions about who counts, what threats matter, and which conflicts are “normal.” Major social stress exposes these inherited blind spots. A constitution survives if it can recursively re-describe itself without ceasing to be itself; it collapses if self-revision appears as self-betrayal.

So the key variable is not merely coercive strength, economic performance, or legal design alone. It is whether the constitutional order sustains a **credible loop between rule, interpretation, and collective identity**. Where that loop remains open, stress generates constitutional evolution. Where it closes or fractures, stress generates a new regime.
