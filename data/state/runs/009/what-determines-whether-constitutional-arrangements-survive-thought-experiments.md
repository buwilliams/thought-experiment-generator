## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a stripped-down scenario: two societies face the same major shock — war, depression, demographic upheaval. They have similar wealth, coercive capacity, and written constitutions. The key variable differs only here: in one society, the constitution is widely treated as a *live problem-solving framework* for handling unforeseen conflict; in the other, it is treated as a *settlement of prior conflicts* whose authority depends on inherited obedience.

Assume this difference is real and follow it through.

Under stress, constitutional survival is not determined primarily by the severity of the shock, nor by the elegance of institutional design in the abstract, but by whether the constitutional order contains effective **error-correcting mechanisms for elite and mass conflict** that actors still believe are preferable to extra-constitutional victory. A constitution survives when powerful groups expect that losing within it is less dangerous than winning outside it.

What follows is that constitutional arrangements are less like static legal texts and more like epistemic systems. Stress introduces novel problems the framers did not and could not specify. If institutions are organized to admit criticism, redistribute losses, revise policy, and rotate authority without destroying legitimacy, then social stress increases contestation *inside* the regime. If not, stress reveals that the constitution was only a temporary compression of past power relations. Contestation then migrates *outside* the regime, where coups, revolutionary assemblies, emergency executives, or secessionary authorities become more adaptive than constitutional procedure.

This isolates the decisive variable: not “legitimacy” in the vague sense, but the **capacity of the constitutional system to convert existential conflict into revisable disagreement**. That capacity depends on feedback loops: credible elections, lawful emergency rules, independent adjudication, fiscal adaptability, elite circulation, and some shared expectation that today’s defeat can be tomorrow’s correction. Where these loops fail, actors stop treating opponents as rivals within a common framework and start treating them as threats to survival. At that point, constitutional forms may remain on paper, but the regime has already changed in substance.

Historically, many constitutions are born as solutions to previous breakdowns. They inherit unexamined assumptions about who counts, what conflicts are normal, and which shocks are imaginable. Major social stress matters because it activates excluded problems. Collapse occurs when the constitution cannot absorb newly mobilized groups, new material scarcities, or new scales of executive action without suspending its own rules.

So the conjecture is: **constitutional arrangements survive major social stress when they function as adaptive knowledge-creating systems for political conflict; they collapse into new regimes when they function mainly as frozen records of a past settlement that can no longer process present contradictions.** The critical determinant is whether the constitution remains the best available method for error correction under pressure.
