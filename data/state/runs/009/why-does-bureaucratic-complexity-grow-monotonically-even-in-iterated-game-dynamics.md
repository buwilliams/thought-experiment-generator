## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically because government is not a one-shot design problem but an iterated game among officials, agencies, courts, politicians, regulated parties, and citizens; under repeated interaction, actors create rules, documentation, review layers, and procedural safeguards as cooperation technologies that stabilize expectations and protect against future opportunism. Even governments sincerely trying to simplify face game-structural incentives that regenerate complexity faster than they remove it.

**Why this follows from iterated game dynamics:**

In repeated play, actors are less concerned with solving only the current case than with shaping the terms of future interaction. A bureaucratic rule is not just a decision aid; it is a move in an ongoing game. It records commitments, makes behavior legible, creates evidence for later disputes, and reduces vulnerability to blame, reversal, or exploitation in subsequent rounds.

This means complexity is often the equilibrium product of repeated mistrust:
- **Officials** add procedures to protect themselves from future accusations.
- **Agencies** formalize criteria to survive judicial or political challenge.
- **Politicians** demand reporting and oversight because they expect future scandals.
- **Citizens and firms** exploit ambiguity, forcing governments to specify exceptions and edge cases.
- **Successors** may not share current goals, so present actors entrench procedures to constrain future defections.

Each added layer is locally rational in an iterated environment. Simplicity, by contrast, is fragile: it works only when actors trust that others in future rounds will interpret discretion benignly, refrain from opportunism, and not punish unavoidable mistakes. That trust is usually weak.

So anti-bureaucracy campaigns fail because they treat complexity as accumulated waste from bad intentions, when much of it is accumulated strategy in a repeated game. Cutting rules removes not only friction but also reputation mechanisms, audit trails, and reciprocal assurances. Once these are removed, actors anticipate higher risk from future interactions and respond by rebuilding controls elsewhere. Complexity reappears in new form.

This perspective also predicts **where simplification succeeds**: where repeated interactions are stable, transparent, and high-trust. If the “shadow of the future” is long and defection is observable, actors can sustain simpler arrangements because reputation and reciprocity substitute for formal process. But if time horizons shorten—due to political turnover, adversarial media cycles, polarized elections, or frequent legal challenge—cooperation through discretion collapses, and proceduralization expands.

Thus monotonic complexity growth is not mainly a failure of will. It is the structural outcome of repeated play under uncertain trust, asymmetric blame, and visible punishment for under-specification but weak rewards for elegant simplicity.

**What this illuminates:** To reduce bureaucracy durably, a government must alter the game, not merely exhort simplification. It must increase trust across rounds, lengthen time horizons, improve observability of genuine defection, and reduce asymmetric punishment for good-faith discretion. Otherwise, repeated interaction will keep regenerating complexity no matter the rhetoric of reform.
