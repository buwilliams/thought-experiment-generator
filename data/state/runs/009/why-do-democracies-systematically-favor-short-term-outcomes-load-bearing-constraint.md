## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies favor short-term outcomes not mainly because voters are myopic, but because the institutions that translate preferences into action are built from components whose explanatory role is *time-sensitive*: electoral cycles, annual budgets, media attention, coalition bargaining, and judicial/administrative review all reward outcomes that can be demonstrated, credited, and defended quickly. Long-term public preference is real, but it is structurally “non-load-bearing” unless it is converted into short-term political constraints.

From the load-bearing perspective, ask: which details cannot be altered without changing the phenomenon? If we changed voter opinion alone—say, made majorities more future-oriented—would the bias disappear? Usually not. That suggests preference myopia is not the core explanation. But if we lengthened electoral horizons, insulated some investment decisions from annual appropriations, changed performance metrics from immediate outputs to durable outcomes, or created credible cross-party commitment devices, the pattern would change substantially. Those details are load-bearing.

What follows is that the short-term bias is generated by a **mismatch of temporal granularity** across the democratic system. Voters can hold diffuse, long-run aims (“protect the climate,” “maintain infrastructure,” “reduce debt”), but politicians are selected and punished through frequent, high-noise events. Bureaucracies are funded in cycles shorter than the problems they manage. Media and opposition actors can always exploit immediate costs more easily than delayed benefits. Interest groups representing concentrated present losses are easier to mobilize than beneficiaries of distributed future gains. Each part fits the others: remove one and compensating changes are needed elsewhere.

This also explains why democracies can support long-term goals in surveys yet repeatedly underproduce them in policy. “Majority preference” is not a unitary force; it must survive agenda setting, coalition assembly, fiscal scoring, legal challenge, and electoral attribution. At each stage, long-term aims are discounted unless attached to near-term payoffs or avoided near-term pain. The explanation is tighter if these institutional filters are treated as the cause, and voter shortsightedness as at most an amplifier.

Historically, this makes sense: democratic accountability mechanisms were designed to solve the problem of rulers ignoring current harms and escaping correction. Frequent elections, transparent contestation, and immediate blame assignment were solutions to that older problem. The long-term bias is in part an inherited side effect of institutions optimized for responsiveness, not stewardship.

So the illuminating claim is: **democracy’s short-termism is a systems property of accountability architecture, not a simple defect in citizen values.** Long-term outcomes are systematically disfavored when they lack institutions that make them politically present *now*. Therefore the leverage point is not moralizing at voters, but redesigning the conversion layer between preference and policy—through commitment devices, independent long-horizon agencies, multi-term budgeting, automatic stabilizers, and metrics that make future loss legible in present politics.
