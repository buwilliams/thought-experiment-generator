## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies’ short-term bias is often an **equilibrium lock-in**, not mainly a failure of civic virtue or information. Even when most voters prefer long-term outcomes in the abstract, the institutional game rewards each actor for defecting from that preference in the near term, so no one can safely move first.

**What is true in this perspective**

The key structure is a multi-level coordination trap among voters, politicians, parties, media, and interest groups:

- **Politicians** know long-term policies usually impose visible short-term costs and yield delayed, uncertain benefits. If one party enacts them while rivals promise immediate relief, the reformer is punished electorally.
- **Voters** may sincerely want sustainability, fiscal prudence, climate resilience, or institutional investment, but each voter also has reason to protect against being the one who absorbs near-term pain while others free-ride.
- **Parties and media** amplify this trap because they compete on short electoral and attention cycles. Immediate harms are attributable; future gains are diffuse and easy to contest.
- **Interest groups** are organized around concentrated present stakes, while beneficiaries of long-run policy are diffuse, unborn, or not yet politically mobilized.

This creates a Nash-like equilibrium: all actors may prefer a polity capable of long-term action, but given others’ incentives, each does better by prioritizing short-term signals. The result is systematic temporal discounting built into the structure.

**What follows**

1. **Preference surveys mislead.** A majority preferring “the long term” does not imply a politically actionable majority for any specific long-term policy with transitional losers. The relevant preference is conditional: what people support when others may defect.
2. **Moralizing diagnoses underperform.** Telling voters and leaders to be more farsighted does little if the penalty for unilateral farsightedness remains high.
3. **Information alone is insufficient.** Better forecasts or civic education may matter at the margin, but they do not dissolve the equilibrium if electoral competition still rewards immediate appeasement.
4. **The bias is strongest where benefits are delayed, causal chains are noisy, and blame is immediate.** Hence underinvestment in infrastructure, prevention, basic research, climate adaptation, and fiscal repair.

**What this illuminates**

Historically, democratic institutions were built to solve older problems: tyranny, arbitrary rule, and unaccountable elites. Frequent elections, adversarial parties, and responsiveness to present constituents were features, not bugs. But these inheritances become constraints when problems are intergenerational and system-scale. The very mechanisms that protect against domination can lock in myopia.

So the leverage point is not exhortation but **institutional redesign that changes payoffs**: cross-party commitment devices, independent long-horizon bodies with real authority, automatic stabilizers and investment rules, compensation for transitional losers, longer evaluation windows, and mechanisms that represent future stakeholders. These work not because they make actors nobler, but because they let them cooperate without being punished for moving first.

So the short-termism of democracies is best understood as a structural coordination trap: majorities may prefer long-term goods, yet the competitive equilibrium systematically converts those preferences into short-term policy.
