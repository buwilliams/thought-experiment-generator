## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they contain a functioning **self-referential loop**: a structured way to observe how they are being perceived, compare that perception to their stated purpose, and then revise their own rules without dissolving their identity. Most institutions fail to adapt because their self-model is too weak, too slow, or too insulated from the environments they govern; they can regulate operations, but not the legitimacy of the rules that regulate those operations.

From the strange-loop perspective, legitimacy is not a static asset but a recursive relation: the institution governs people, people interpret the institution, and those interpretations feed back into whether its authority counts as valid. An institution is legitimate when its higher-level narrative (“what we are for”) can descend into concrete action, and the results of that action can ascend back into revision of the narrative without breaking the loop. That is a strange loop: the institution must rule, but also become an object within its own field of rule-evaluation.

The key structural features, then, are:

1. **Reflexive sensing** — channels that detect not just outcomes but how constituencies are interpreting those outcomes.
2. **Translation across levels** — mechanisms that connect daily operations to mission, law, and public meaning.
3. **Authorized self-revision** — procedures for changing rules, norms, and interpretations from within, without requiring total collapse or revolution.
4. **Identity constraints with interpretive flexibility** — a stable core purpose paired with adaptable implementations.

Institutions fail when hierarchy becomes one-way. Information flows upward about compliance, performance, and efficiency, but not about whether the categories themselves still make sense. In that case, the institution can model its outputs without modeling its own model. It becomes trapped in a brittle pseudo-legitimacy: internally coherent, externally unintelligible. Rapid change exposes this because old classifications stop matching lived reality, yet the institution continues acting as if its map were the territory.

Historically, many institutions were built to solve a prior legitimacy problem: how to make authority impersonal, rule-bound, and predictable. Bureaucracy replaced arbitrary personal rule. But in solving arbitrariness, institutions often inherited rigidity. Their legitimacy came to depend on procedural closure rather than recursive learning. What once stabilized trust later blocks adaptation.

So the collision yields this illumination: **legitimacy is the successful maintenance of a strange loop between institutional self-description and social recognition**. Institutions endure change not by being more rigid or more fluid in general, but by preserving the loop that lets them re-describe themselves in response to being redescribed by others. Most fail because they suppress this loop in the name of stability, and thereby lose the very reflexivity legitimacy requires.
