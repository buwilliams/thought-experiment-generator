## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically because it is an emergent property of the state’s control-and-accountability network, not merely the sum of individual rules, agencies, or bad incentives. Efforts to “reduce bureaucracy” usually act on visible components — forms, departments, headcount, regulations — while leaving intact, or even strengthening, the interaction structure that generates complexity: distributed risk aversion, cross-checking obligations, appeal pathways, exception handling, auditability, and political responsiveness.

What is true on this view is that bureaucracy behaves like a feedback-stabilized system. Every failure, scandal, inequity, or misuse creates pressure for new oversight, documentation, review layers, and procedural safeguards. These additions are easy to justify locally because they solve specific observed problems. But subtraction is structurally harder: removing a rule concentrates risk on whoever authorizes its removal, while adding a rule diffuses responsibility across the system. So the system has an asymmetrical error-correction mechanism: it readily incorporates new constraints after failure, but has weak mechanisms for removing old ones after success.

This explains the monotonicity. Complexity does not mainly persist because no one wants simplification; it persists because the network rewards redundancy, traceability, and blame insulation more reliably than it rewards simplicity. “Simplification” campaigns often intensify these dynamics by imposing new reporting requirements, central reviews, performance metrics, or compliance offices to ensure simplification actually occurs. In systems terms, reform becomes another layer in the same architecture of control.

The historical genesis matters. Modern bureaucracy inherited functions from earlier forms of administration that were more discretionary, local, and personal. Bureaucratic procedure originally solved problems of arbitrariness, corruption, favoritism, and unaccountable power. Its complexity is therefore path-dependent: many layers are fossilized solutions to prior abuses. To remove them safely requires solving the old problem in a new way, not merely declaring the layer unnecessary. Where no replacement mechanism exists, complexity reappears.

What follows is that meaningful simplification cannot be achieved primarily by cutting parts. It requires changing the system’s feedback structure — especially the incentives around blame, exception management, and review. A bureaucracy will only become durably simpler if it gains credible ways to absorb failure without automatically converting each failure into a permanent new procedure. That means higher-level tolerance for bounded discretion, expiration mechanisms for rules, institutionalized deletion processes, and decision architectures that preserve accountability without multiplying checkpoints.

So the key illumination is: bureaucratic growth is not a paradox of weak political will. It is the normal output of a system designed to prevent visible mistakes under conditions of distributed responsibility and recurring public scrutiny. Complexity grows because the structure makes addition safer than subtraction.
