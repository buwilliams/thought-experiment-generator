## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they can reliably produce and update **public epistemic goods**—shared rules, explanations, dispute-resolution, and visible error-correction—that are non-excludable and broadly beneficial. Most institutions fail to adapt because these goods are structurally underprovided: everyone benefits from institutional learning and legitimacy, but no actor has sufficient private incentive to bear the costs of revising rules, admitting error, or investing in trust-preserving adaptation.

This lens distinguishes a structural trap from mere incompetence. Under rapid change, legitimacy depends less on preserving old outputs and more on preserving a **credible process** for responding to novelty. That process itself is a public good. If an institution openly revises assumptions, builds feedback channels, and explains changes intelligibly, all stakeholders gain from continued coordination. But the costs are concentrated: leaders bear blame for reversals, internal groups risk loss of status, and reformers incur uncertainty and transition costs. The benefits—restored trust, social stability, better fit to reality—are diffuse. So individually rational actors often defend local interests, ambiguity, or symbolic continuity, even when nearly all would prefer a genuinely adaptive institution.

Historically, many institutions were built to solve problems of **order, predictability, and bounded information**. Their legitimacy came from consistency, not agility. They inherited procedures optimized for filtering change, slowing error propagation, and protecting authority. Under conditions of rapid technological, social, or economic change, those same features become liabilities. The institution now needs higher-rate learning, but its internal incentive structure still rewards rule preservation over knowledge creation. What once stabilized legitimacy now blocks its renewal.

The result is a stable no-provision equilibrium: everyone complains that the institution is “out of touch,” but key legitimacy-maintaining goods—transparent adaptation, institutional self-critique, participatory feedback, and shared interpretive frameworks—remain undersupplied. Actors substitute cheaper private responses: exit, cynicism, factional narratives, short-term signaling, or attempts to capture the institution for narrower ends. These are rational under the structure, but collectively they further erode legitimacy.

What follows is that legitimacy under rapid change is not mainly a matter of better messaging or stronger motivation. It is a **collective action problem around adaptive capacity**. Institutions succeed when they internalize the production of epistemic public goods: mechanisms that reward error detection, distribute the costs of revision, make adaptation legible, and prevent reform burdens from falling only on a few exposed actors. They fail when adaptation remains optional, reputationally punished, or privately costly while the benefits remain public.

So the key structural feature is not flexibility in the abstract, but whether the institution has solved the public-goods problem of **organized self-correction**. Where it has, legitimacy can survive change. Where it has not, even well-intentioned institutions drift into paralysis, denial, or performative reform.
