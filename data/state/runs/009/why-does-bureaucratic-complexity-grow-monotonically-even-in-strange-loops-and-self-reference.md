## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically because any government that tries to reduce bureaucracy must use bureaucracy to observe, define, measure, and execute that reduction; this creates a strange loop in which the system becomes an object of its own administration, and each attempt at simplification adds a new layer of self-description that persists even when the original problem changes.

From the strange-loop perspective, bureaucracy is not merely a collection of rules. It is a hierarchy of representations: rules about actions, rules about exceptions, rules about who may interpret the rules, and rules about revising all the above. Once a state becomes reflective—once it must govern not only society but also its own governing process—it acquires self-referential machinery. “Reduce red tape” then becomes another administrable object. The system must ask: What counts as complexity? Who audits it? Which agencies qualify? How are reductions verified? What prevents harmful deregulation disguised as simplification? Each question requires procedures, metrics, reporting channels, appeal paths, and oversight bodies.

The key claim is that complexity growth is not mainly a failure of political will. It is a structural consequence of self-governing systems with sufficient expressive power. A bureaucracy that can modify itself must represent itself, and self-representation is never free. It generates meta-rules. Meta-rules generate edge cases. Edge cases generate review mechanisms. Review mechanisms require standardization. Standardization generates exceptions. The loop closes: simplification efforts become new sources of complexity.

Historically, bureaucracies arise to solve prior failures of arbitrariness, corruption, local discretion, and informational loss. Rules are retained because they encode remembered dangers. Complexity is therefore a fossil record of past criticisms. Governments inherit these layers asymmetrically: adding a safeguard is politically legible; removing one risks blame if the old failure reappears. So the loop is reinforced by selection pressure: errors of omission are punished more sharply than accretions of procedure.

What follows is that “anti-bureaucratic” reforms usually fail when they target the visible stock of rules rather than the generative loop that reproduces them. The leverage point is not rule-count reduction but redesign of self-reference. A system will simplify only if it can *forget safely*—if it has institutions that delete obsolete self-descriptions as reliably as it creates new ones. That means sunset clauses with real enforcement, default expiration of reporting requirements, jurisdictional compression, and decision rights pushed downward so fewer meta-level representations are needed.

So the collision illuminates this: bureaucratic complexity grows because administration eventually becomes administration of administration. Once the state loops back onto itself, complexity is not an accident but an emergent property of institutional self-awareness.
