## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically because, in modern states, complexity is not mainly accidental “bloat” but a **load-bearing adaptation** to conflicting demands the state is expected to satisfy simultaneously. Efforts to reduce complexity usually target visible forms — rules, offices, procedures — while leaving the generating constraints intact. So the removed complexity must be reintroduced elsewhere, often in less legible form.

From the load-bearing perspective, ask: which parts of bureaucracy can be changed without forcing compensating changes? Surprisingly few. Consider some pressures governments must jointly satisfy: legality, uniformity, accountability, fraud-resistance, auditability, political responsiveness, budget control, fairness across cases, and capacity to handle exceptions. Each procedural layer often exists because removing it worsens one of these dimensions, which then provokes a repair. If you simplify procurement rules, discretion rises; then scandals or favoritism produce oversight mechanisms. If you collapse reporting requirements, legislators and auditors lose visibility; then new dashboards, certifications, or review boards appear. If you decentralize decisions, variation increases; then standardization systems return.

That is the key structural claim: **bureaucratic complexity is tightly coupled to unresolved contradictions in state purposes**. Governments are asked to be fast *and* fair, flexible *and* rule-bound, cheap *and* exhaustive, decentralized *and* centrally controllable. Bureaucracy grows as the machinery that metabolizes these contradictions without resolving them. Since the contradictions persist, simplification programs mostly displace complexity rather than eliminate it.

This explains the monotonic pattern. Complexity is asymmetric: adding a rule to prevent a failure is politically easy because the failure is concrete; removing a rule is hard because the harm prevented is uncertain and distributed. Errors of omission in administration become scandals; burdens imposed by precaution diffuse into background irritation. So feedback is stronger for **accreting controls** than for deleting them. Historical layers therefore persist: each reform inherits earlier safeguards, legal precedents, and categories, and adds new ones for new problems. The state almost never gets to redesign from zero; it must preserve continuity while changing, which makes sedimentation load-bearing too.

What follows is that “anti-bureaucracy” rhetoric fails when it treats complexity as mere inefficiency. If the explanation is right, then durable simplification requires removing or reprioritizing the underlying demands, not just compressing forms. You must decide which values to relax: less ex ante fairness for more speed, less auditability for more discretion, less universality for fewer edge-case procedures, fewer legal rights to contest decisions for thinner review structures. Where governments will not make these tradeoffs explicit, complexity will regrow.

So the deeper illumination is this: bureaucratic complexity is often a **physical trace of political evasion**. It marks places where society refuses to choose among incompatible aims and instead encodes the non-choice into procedure. Monotonic growth is therefore not a mystery but the expected outcome of a system whose contradictions are stable, whose failures are punished locally, and whose simplifications do not alter the load-bearing structure.
