## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems can remain resistant to reform under supermajority public support when the *relevant game is iterated among officeholders, parties, donors, bureaucracies, courts, and veto-holding minorities—not between the public and the system in a single decisive moment*. In that repeated game, actors are rewarded more for preserving reliable future relationships, retaliatory credibility, and coalition discipline than for yielding to one episode of mass preference.

From an iterated-game perspective, the key question is not “what do most people want?” but “who must keep dealing with whom tomorrow?” Political insiders face repeated interactions with each other. Voters, by contrast, often appear to insiders as noisy, intermittent, weakly coordinated participants whose punishments are delayed and diluted by electoral bundling, districting, agenda control, and information decay. This creates a structural asymmetry: elite actors inhabit a high-frequency repeated game; the public often participates in a low-frequency, obscured one.

What follows is that **blocking reform can be more strategically rational than passing it**, even when reform is popular. A legislator who defects from party leadership, donor networks, committee alliances, or constitutional hardball norms may win short-term public approval but incur durable future costs in the repeated game: loss of endorsements, funding, procedural access, reciprocal support, and protection against future retaliation. Conversely, veto players gain by cultivating a reputation for intransigence. If others know you will reliably obstruct, they preemptively accommodate you. Resistance itself becomes a strategic asset.

This perspective also illuminates why **institutional friction strengthens anti-reform equilibria**. Supermajority rules, bicameralism, judicial review, federalism, and committee gatekeeping increase the number of repeated-game nodes where defection from reform can be rewarded. Each veto point is not just a hurdle; it is an opportunity for some actor to signal loyalty to their long-run coalition. Reform therefore requires temporary coordination across many actors, while obstruction only requires that enough players maintain ongoing reciprocal ties.

The collision also predicts when resistance weakens: when the public can convert its preferences into an *iterated* punishment/reward structure visible to insiders. That means shortening feedback loops, increasing observability of obstruction, reducing ambiguity about responsibility, and making noncompliance costly across multiple future rounds. In other words, reform succeeds not merely when support is broad, but when supporters can behave like a repeated-game coalition rather than a one-shot majority.

So the deeper claim is: **political systems resist reform under supermajority demand when institutional design gives elites a longer shadow of the future with one another than with the public**. Under those conditions, cooperation forms around maintaining the system’s internal bargains, not around implementing popular change. Supermajority opinion alone is insufficient; what matters is whether it can alter the repeated strategic environment.
