## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies skew short-term not mainly because voters are myopic, but because **the mechanisms that translate preferences into action lose resolution as the time horizon lengthens**. At the boundary — very short vs. very long horizons — the structure becomes visible: democracies are highly sensitive to near-term feedback and nearly blind to delayed consequences.

Push it to the limit.

At the **zero-horizon boundary**, democratic systems work well. Voters can observe outcomes, assign blame, and revise leadership. Information is relatively cheap, causal links are legible, and electoral feedback is fast. The loop closes: preference → policy → visible effect → political reward/punishment.

At the **infinite-horizon boundary**, this loop breaks. Long-term outcomes are abstract, causally overdetermined, and often realized after the responsible coalition has dissolved. Benefits are diffuse and delayed; costs are concentrated and immediate. So even if a majority sincerely prefers “the long term,” that preference arrives in politics as weak, low-fidelity information. It cannot compete structurally with near-term pain signals.

This suggests the core problem is not hypocrisy but **temporal impedance mismatch** between democratic preference formation and democratic control systems. Elections are periodic, coalitions fragile, media attention scarce, and bureaucratic incentives tied to current budgets and visible outputs. These institutions amplify what can be measured before the next accountability event and attenuate what cannot.

A systems view sharpens this: democracies contain strong **reinforcing feedback** for short-term responsiveness and weak or broken **balancing feedback** for long-term stewardship. Politicians are punished for immediate sacrifices, but rarely rewarded proportionally for preventing future harms that remain invisible or are claimed by successors. Thus rational actors inside the system optimize for what the system can actually register.

The limiting case reveals a deeper point: “majority preference for the long term” is often **ontologically thin** compared with short-term preference. It exists as opinion, but not as an equally actionable political object. A voter may endorse climate stability, fiscal sustainability, or infrastructure maintenance in principle, yet reverse when faced with present taxes, local disruption, or reduced consumption. The contradiction is not merely psychological inconsistency; it is built into how democracy samples preferences under constraint.

What follows is that fixing this bias is less about moralizing citizens and more about **changing the temporal structure of decision-making**: creating institutions that preserve agency across longer causal chains — e.g. constitutional commitments, independent long-horizon bodies, automatic stabilizers, sovereign wealth structures, intergenerational accounting, or decision rules that force present representation of future costs.

So the boundary lesson is: **democracy favors the short term wherever accountability outruns consequence**. The shorter the feedback loop relative to the problem’s causal timescale, the more systematically the system will select short-term outcomes — even against declared majority wishes.
