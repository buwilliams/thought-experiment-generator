## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically because most governments are designed to optimize for *error-avoidance under fixed goals*, not for *goal criticism under changing contexts*. As capability to monitor, regulate, audit, and document increases faster than institutions’ ability to revise what they are trying to preserve, each failure or exception is treated as evidence for adding control layers rather than reconsidering objectives, jurisdiction, or obsolete constraints.

From this perspective, the key safety failure is not “too much state capacity” in itself. It is that bureaucratic systems rarely contain strong internal mechanisms for asking: *Do these rules still serve the purpose they were created for? Are the goals in conflict? Has the environment changed enough that simplification is now safer than further accretion?* Without that capacity, inherited aims become locked-in. Complexity then becomes the default adaptation strategy.

Historically, bureaucracy solves prior coordination failures: corruption, arbitrariness, local discretion, missing records, unequal treatment, fiscal leakage. Each reform usually arrives as a patch against a known failure mode. But the patch is added atop older structures because deleting old rules is politically riskier than adding new safeguards. Old goals persist even when the conditions that justified them have changed. Thus complexity is path-dependent: each layer remains as a fossilized answer to a past problem, and new layers must remain compatible with accumulated residues.

What follows is a structural asymmetry. In bureaucracies, the feedback for *under-regulation* is usually sharp and visible — scandal, fraud, harm, lawsuit, media exposure. The feedback for *over-complexity* is diffuse — delay, confusion, compliance cost, loss of initiative, public exhaustion. Systems respond more strongly to visible short-term failures than to distributed long-term degradation. So every incident generates demands for an added check, while successful simplification has few defenders when the next failure occurs. Reduction programs therefore often increase complexity: they create offices, metrics, review processes, sunset criteria, and compliance regimes to ensure simplification “works.”

This also explains why explicit anti-bureaucracy goals fail. “Reduce complexity” is typically introduced as an additional objective inside a system whose deeper goals — legality, accountability, fairness, risk minimization, blame avoidance, continuity — remain unquestioned. Since the system cannot revise the hierarchy among those aims, simplification is subordinated whenever tradeoffs appear. Anti-complexity becomes another reporting layer, not a genuine redesign.

So the illuminating claim is: **bureaucratic growth is a safety strategy used by goal-rigid institutions facing changing environments.** Complexity accumulates not because governments love complication, but because additive controls are safer, locally legible responses when institutions lack meta-capacity to inspect and rewrite their own purposes.

Therefore, the leverage point is not merely deregulation or administrative trimming. It is building institutions that can explicitly criticize inherited goals, retire obsolete rule-problems, and treat deletion as a legitimate corrective act rather than as irresponsible exposure. Without that self-revising capacity, complexity will continue to grow even under governments sincerely committed to reducing it.
