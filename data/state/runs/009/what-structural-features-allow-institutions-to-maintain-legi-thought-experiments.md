## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Assume a hypothetical institution whose environment changes faster than its formal rules can be rewritten. Isolate the key variable: **the rate at which the institution can detect, correct, and publicly explain error**. Hold resources, goodwill, and legal authority constant. Then legitimacy under rapid change depends less on stability of outcomes than on the institution’s **visible capacity for self-correction without self-dissolution**.

What follows is that legitimacy is not primarily a stock of trust accumulated in the past; it is a **flow property** generated in real time by reliable error-handling. In stable periods, institutions can appear legitimate by enforcing inherited routines. Under rapid change, those routines are exposed as conjectures about how the world works. An institution remains legitimate if it has structures that treat policies as revisable, criticism as input rather than threat, and explanation as part of governance rather than public relations.

So the structural features that matter are:

1. **Short feedback loops** between consequences and decision-makers.  
   If signals of failure are delayed, filtered, or politicized, adaptation lags behind reality.

2. **Protected channels for criticism** from inside and outside.  
   Rapid change creates novel errors. Institutions that punish dissent destroy the very mechanism that could update them.

3. **Modularity of rules and authority.**  
   If every revision threatens the whole system, leaders defend bad rules to protect institutional continuity. Modular structures allow local revision without existential crisis.

4. **A public grammar of revision.**  
   The institution must be able to say: “Here is what we believed, here is what failed, here is what we are changing.” Without this, adaptation looks like arbitrariness or capitulation, and legitimacy erodes even when correction is substantively good.

5. **Identity anchored in purpose, not procedure.**  
   If the institution confuses its mission with its current methods, any change feels like betrayal. If identity is tied to the problem it exists to solve, methods can change while continuity is preserved.

Why do most fail? Because most institutions were historically optimized for **error suppression, not error correction**. They inherited legitimacy from continuity, hierarchy, ritual, and rule-enforcement in environments where change was slower than bureaucratic adaptation. Those same features become liabilities under rapid change. Feedback is muffled; criticism is treated as disloyalty; tightly coupled procedures make revision costly; leaders defend symbolic consistency over functional success.

The deeper point is that rapid change does not merely pressure institutions from outside. It reveals what legitimacy always was: not authority without error, but authority that can survive error by correcting it intelligibly. Most institutions fail because they were built to appear reliable, not to be corrigible. When the environment accelerates, the appearance collapses before the structure can learn.
