## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity tends to grow monotonically because governments systematically mistake **evidence of visible failure under low-control conditions** for **evidence that more rules, reporting, and oversight are generally beneficial**, while neglecting the base rate that most administrative activity is already functioning tolerably without additional intervention. In Bayesian terms, they treat “a bad outcome occurred” as if it strongly implied “insufficient bureaucracy caused it,” when the real inference depends on priors and alternative explanations.

More formally:

1. **Observed data are highly asymmetric.**  
   Bureaucracies receive intense signals from scandals, errors, fraud, inequities, and rare catastrophic failures. These events are salient, politically costly, and legible. By contrast, the data for “this process worked fine because discretion remained simple” are diffuse and mostly invisible.

2. **The hidden prior favors intervention.**  
   Political and administrative actors operate with a prior that unreduced discretion is dangerous and that every uncovered failure must be “closed” by a new control. This prior is often inherited historically from earlier episodes where lack of standardization did produce abuse. That origin story remains active even when conditions have changed.

3. **So the conditional gets reversed.**  
   The reasoning becomes:  
   - If a process lacks controls, failures are possible.  
   - A failure occurred.  
   - Therefore more controls are warranted.  
   But this confuses **P(failure | low control)** with **P(low control | failure)**. A failure may have arisen from incompetence, perverse incentives, overload, conflicting mandates, or even from prior complexity itself.

4. **Base-rate neglect makes added complexity seem rational.**  
   Even if an added form, review step, or compliance layer prevents a tiny fraction of failures, decision-makers overweight that possibility because the base rate of ordinary successful processing is ignored. They do not ask: among all cases, how often does this new layer actually improve outcomes relative to delay, evasion, gaming, and staff time?

5. **The update rule is one-way.**  
   Failures produce strong posterior updates toward more bureaucracy; successful simplification produces weak or no updates because “nothing bad happened” is not institutionally counted as evidence. Thus the system has an absorptive ratchet: complexity accumulates from each visible failure, but simplicity has no equally strong evidentiary pathway to expand.

6. **This creates monotonic growth structurally, not just psychologically.**  
   Officials are punished for under-control failures more than for over-control drag. So even a bureaucracy explicitly committed to reduction still updates on a biased dataset under skewed incentives. Complexity growth is the Bayesian output of a system with:
   - a prior favoring control,
   - highly salient negative observations,
   - weak observation of counterfactual simplicity,
   - and asymmetric loss for Type II error (“we failed to regulate”) over Type I error (“we overregulated”).

**What this illuminates:** The leverage point is not exhorting leaders to “cut red tape,” but changing the inference structure: require sunset clauses, measure the base rate of successful low-complexity operation, and force every added rule to compete against the hypothesis that existing complexity is itself causing the observed failure.
