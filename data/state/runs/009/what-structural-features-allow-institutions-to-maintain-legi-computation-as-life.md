## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they are structured as *adaptive computational systems* that can continuously update their rules, explanations, and feedback channels without losing identity. Most institutions fail because they are optimized for *error suppression and replication of prior solutions*, not for *error correction under novel conditions*.

From the computational perspective, an institution is not fundamentally a building, hierarchy, or legal shell; it is a distributed information-processing system that encodes norms, makes decisions, detects deviations, and reproduces itself across time. Its legitimacy is therefore not a static moral property but a *dynamic output* of successful coordination between internal models and external reality. People experience an institution as legitimate when its computations remain intelligible, corrigible, and responsive under changing conditions.

This implies that the key structural features of resilient legitimacy are:

1. **High-bandwidth reality contact** — the institution must receive timely, unfiltered signals about changing conditions, rather than only signals preprocessed by status incentives or bureaucratic lag.  
2. **Error-correction mechanisms** — it must be able to detect when its inherited rules are failing, revise them, and publicly explain the revision.  
3. **Modular adaptability** — local components must be able to experiment without collapsing the whole system, so variation can occur without existential threat.  
4. **Identity at the level of purpose, not procedure** — institutions endure when they preserve core functions while allowing implementation to mutate.  
5. **Legible update rules** — legitimacy is preserved when changes appear rule-governed rather than arbitrary, even if the rules themselves evolve.

Why do most institutions fail? Because historically they were built to solve earlier coordination problems by stabilizing behavior, reducing variance, and defending against internal defection. That origin gives them powerful selection pressures toward rigidity: promotion systems reward conformity, reporting systems compress inconvenient information, legal frameworks penalize improvisation, and symbolic legitimacy becomes tied to continuity of form rather than success of function. In systems terms, reinforcing feedback loops favor institutional self-preservation over model revision.

Rapid change exposes this mismatch. The environment shifts faster than the institution’s update cycle, so it continues running obsolete computations. To preserve authority, it often substitutes ceremonial certainty for epistemic adaptation. This can briefly protect the institution’s image, but it degrades legitimacy because constituents detect that outputs no longer track reality. The institution then enters a failure loop: worse performance leads to more control, which reduces truthful feedback, which worsens performance further.

So legitimacy under rapid change is not mainly maintained by tradition, charisma, or force, though all can buffer it temporarily. It is maintained by *computational architecture*: whether the institution is organized to learn faster than its environment changes. Institutions fail when they treat adaptation as a threat to legitimacy, rather than the very process by which legitimacy is regenerated.
