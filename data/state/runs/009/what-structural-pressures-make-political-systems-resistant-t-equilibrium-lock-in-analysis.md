## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform under supermajoritarian public support when reform requires coordinated defection from multiple actors whose individually rational strategies are to preserve the status quo; the obstacle is not mainly disagreement over ends, but an equilibrium lock-in created by asymmetric transition costs, veto points, and incentive misalignment between mass preference and elite survival.

What this perspective illuminates is that “everyone wants change” is often too coarse a description of the system. The relevant unit is not the public in aggregate, but the set of institutional actors who must each move for reform to occur: legislators, parties, committee chairs, courts, agencies, donors, media coalitions, and organized interests. If any one of these actors bears concentrated risk from reform while receiving only diffuse gains, its best response is obstruction. When enough such veto-capable actors exist, the status quo becomes a Nash equilibrium: no actor can improve its own position by unilaterally supporting reform, even if all would prefer a different equilibrium in which reform had already happened.

Several structural pressures generate this lock-in:

1. **Diffuse benefits, concentrated losses.** Supermajorities may weakly prefer reform, but small organized groups may intensely oppose it. Intensity converts into lobbying, primary threats, litigation, and administrative delay. Reform’s winners are many but uncoordinated; its losers are few but strategic.

2. **Transition-risk asymmetry.** Actors are punished for visible disruptions caused by change, but rarely rewarded for harms avoided in the long run. So even reform-friendly politicians prefer symbolic support over actual enactment if implementation could create short-term instability.

3. **Multiple veto points.** Bicameralism, filibusters, judicial review, federal fragmentation, party gatekeeping, and bureaucratic discretion raise the number of actors whose consent is needed. Each additional veto point increases the chance that at least one actor rationally blocks.

4. **Electoral and career incentives.** Representatives respond not to supermajorities in the abstract but to primaries, donors, activist factions, and local coalitions. If these are more punitive than the general electorate is rewarding, anti-reform behavior is individually rational.

5. **Common-knowledge failure.** Even where many elites privately support reform, each doubts that others will incur the same risks. So all wait. This produces public drift: latent consensus without coordinated action.

The key implication is that resistance to reform is often misdiagnosed as ignorance, corruption, or bad will alone. Those matter, but they are downstream of a deeper structural trap. Reform succeeds less by persuading one more actor of the merits than by changing the payoff matrix: reducing transition costs, compensating concentrated losers, bundling reforms to create credible coalitions, lowering veto opportunities, or creating commitment mechanisms that let actors move together.

So the supermajority puzzle is solved by distinguishing **preference for outcomes** from **incentives under current rules**. A political system can be democratically pro-reform at the level of opinion while anti-reform at the level of equilibrium behavior.
