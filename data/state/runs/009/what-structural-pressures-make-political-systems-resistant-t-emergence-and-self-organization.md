## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform under supermajoritarian public support because the effective decision-making system is not “the electorate expressing preferences,” but a multi-layered network of veto points, incentive loops, and risk-asymmetric institutions whose emergent property is **status quo bias**. The resistance is structural, not primarily ideological.

### What is true from this perspective
A political system is not a channel that simply converts opinion into policy. It is a system of interacting nodes: parties, chambers, courts, bureaucracies, donors, media, procedures, federal units, and electoral calendars. Each node responds to different incentives, and those incentives are often misaligned with aggregate public preference.

When these elements interact, they generate a stable pattern: **blocking reform is easier, safer, and often more locally rational than enacting it**. No single actor needs to oppose reform in principle. The system can still produce nonresponse because each actor faces feedback that rewards caution, delay, blame-avoidance, and coalition fragmentation.

### Structural pressures at work
1. **Many veto points create asymmetric effort**  
   Reform usually requires coordination across multiple institutions; obstruction often requires only one successful block. This makes the status quo the default emergent outcome.

2. **Concentrated costs vs. diffuse benefits**  
   Beneficiaries of reform may be numerous but weakly organized; those facing losses are fewer, more coordinated, and more intensely motivated. The system amplifies organized intensity over broad preference.

3. **Electoral and reputational risk asymmetry**  
   Officeholders are punished more for visible disruptions caused by change than for invisible harms of inaction. So even reform supporters hedge, dilute, or defer.

4. **Temporal mismatch**  
   Public support can be broad but shallow and short-lived; institutions move slowly. Delay itself becomes a filtering mechanism that lets majorities dissipate while organized minorities persist.

5. **Mediation by representation and agenda control**  
   “Supermajority support” does not directly act; it must be translated through party leadership, committees, legislative procedure, and administrative implementation. Each translation layer can reshape or stall the demand.

6. **Feedback loops that preserve incumbent arrangements**  
   Existing rules influence who gets elected, who funds campaigns, what counts as “realistic,” and which proposals are thinkable. Thus the status quo reproduces the conditions of its own defense.

### What follows
The apparent paradox—“why no reform if most people want it?”—dissolves once the explanatory target shifts from preferences to **system architecture**. The relevant question is not whether enough people agree, but whether the structure allows agreement to accumulate into action faster than countervailing incentives dissipate it.

### What this illuminates
Reform failure is often misdiagnosed as ignorance, corruption, or hypocrisy in individuals. Those matter, but the deeper explanation is emergent: a system designed—or historically evolved—to require high coordination for change while permitting low-coordination obstruction will reliably underperform democratic majorities.

So the leverage point is not merely persuasion. It is redesigning interaction structure: fewer veto points, stronger agenda-setting pathways, institutions that convert broad support into durable capacity, and mechanisms that reduce the advantage of organized obstruction over diffuse consent.
