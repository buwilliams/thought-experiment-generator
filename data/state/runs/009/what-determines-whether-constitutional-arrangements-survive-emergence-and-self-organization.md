## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional survival under major social stress is determined less by the formal design or virtue of individual officeholders than by the **interaction structure** linking institutions, elites, and mass publics — especially whether the system contains **feedback loops that convert conflict into revisable political disagreement rather than existential zero-sum struggle**.

From this perspective, a constitution is not primarily a text or set of offices. It is a **coordination system**: courts, parties, bureaucracies, elections, media, coercive institutions, and civic norms form a network of expectations about how losses are borne, grievances are voiced, and authority is transferred. Stress tests this network. Collapse occurs when the structure amplifies defection faster than it restores cooperation.

What matters, then, is not just separation of powers or federalism in the abstract, but whether these arrangements create stabilizing loops:

- **Loss absorption:** Can powerful actors lose today without concluding they are excluded permanently?
- **Cross-cutting coalitions:** Do institutions force bargaining across social divisions, or allow them to harden into mutually sealed blocs?
- **Legibility and trust repair:** Are there credible mechanisms for adjudicating disputes that enough actors still treat as binding under pressure?
- **Civil-military insulation:** Is coercive force structurally prevented from becoming an ordinary political instrument?
- **Adaptation capacity:** Can the regime revise rules without making every revision appear to invalidate the whole order?

Survival is most likely when stress triggers **negative feedback**: protest leads to negotiation, electoral loss leads to opposition rather than rebellion, emergency powers trigger sunset and review, elite conflict remains bounded by shared expectations. Collapse is likely when stress triggers **positive feedback**: fear produces constitutional hardball, hardball produces distrust, distrust produces norm violation, and each actor’s defensive escalation becomes evidence that restraint is irrational.

This illuminates why constitutions that look similar on paper diverge sharply in crises. The decisive variable is often the **density and quality of coupling** between formal and informal institutions. A well-written constitution can fail if parties become vehicles for total social antagonism or if media and patronage networks reward permanent mobilization. A formally imperfect constitution can endure if elites, associations, and public expectations keep conflicts interoperable within the regime.

Historically, many constitutional arrangements were built to solve earlier problems — monarchy, civil war, regional fragmentation, class conflict — and therefore inherit assumptions about who counts as a legitimate participant and what kinds of conflict are normal. Major social stress exposes when new cleavages no longer fit old channels. Regime change occurs when the inherited interaction structure cannot metabolize those conflicts.

So the key question is not “How strong is the constitution?” but **“What patterns of interaction does it generate under stress?”** Constitutional order survives when its structure makes continued constitutionalism the least bad option for enough actors, even amid deep disagreement.
