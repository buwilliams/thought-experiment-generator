## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress when they function as **high-bandwidth, error-correcting computational systems** for processing conflict, updating rules, and reallocating legitimacy faster than social pressures generate destabilizing information. They collapse into new regimes when their institutional “compute” becomes too rigid, too slow, or too narrow to integrate shocks, causing unresolved conflicts to spill out of formal channels and be processed instead by rival systems—military command, charismatic authority, party machinery, sectarian networks, or markets of violence.

From the computational perspective, a constitution is not mainly a text or a set of ideals. It is a **protocol stack** for collective information processing: who can signal distress, how disputes are encoded, where decisions are routed, how errors are corrected, and how adaptations are ratified. Major social stress—war, economic breakdown, demographic upheaval, technological change, legitimacy crises—acts like a surge in input complexity. The key question is whether the constitutional system can still compress, transmit, and act on that information without catastrophic loss.

What follows is that regime survival depends less on formal rigidity versus flexibility in the abstract, and more on whether the system preserves three computational properties:

1. **Distributed sensing:** institutions must detect real grievances and power shifts before they become existential. Elections, courts, federal units, press, parties, unions, and local governance are parallel input channels. If these channels are censored or decorrelated from reality, the regime becomes informationally blind.

2. **Error-correction capacity:** constitutions survive when they contain legitimate mechanisms for revising policy, personnel, and even constitutional meaning without requiring regime overthrow. Amendment rules, emergency powers with credible limits, judicial review, negotiated elite turnover, and administrative adaptability are all forms of recursive debugging.

3. **Legitimacy as compression:** legitimacy is the shared encoding that lets millions accept losses without immediate defection. When constitutional language no longer compresses social reality—when groups no longer see themselves as represented in the regime’s categories—the system fragments into incompatible interpretive codes, and rival coordination protocols emerge.

Historically, constitutions often arose to solve prior problems of succession, violence, extraction, or faction. Their inherited weakness is that they often optimize for the last crisis. Under new stress, obsolete assumptions become computational bottlenecks.

So collapse into a new regime is not just “failure of norms” or “too much conflict.” It is a **substrate switch** in collective computation: society stops using constitutional procedures to process selection pressures and begins using another machinery better able to coordinate action under current conditions. The new regime wins not because it is morally superior, but because it computes adaptation more effectively under stress.

Therefore, the deepest determinant of constitutional survival is whether the arrangement remains the most effective available system for **information transformation under selection**—that is, for turning conflict into revisable order before alternative processors seize the task.
