## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies systematically favor short-term outcomes because, although citizens and politicians interact repeatedly, the *effective game* they face is organized as a sequence of short-horizon, weakly monitored, partially anonymous subgames. That structure suppresses the cooperative dynamics that repeated interaction would otherwise generate around long-term public goods.

From an iterated-game perspective, the puzzle is not “why are democratic actors selfish?” but “why doesn’t repeated play stabilize long-term cooperation?” The answer is that the relevant forms of reciprocity are broken. Voters can punish incumbents only intermittently, with noisy information, and usually on bundles of issues rather than specific long-term choices. Politicians, meanwhile, are rewarded on electoral cycles, media cycles, and donor cycles that are much shorter than the payoff horizon of climate resilience, infrastructure maintenance, fiscal prudence, or institutional investment.

So although democracy is formally repeated, the **shadow of the future is truncated**. Each election resembles an endgame-prone round: officeholders discount distant returns because they may not be in office to collect them, while voters cannot reliably attribute future benefits or present sacrifices to any one actor. In repeated games, cooperation depends on visible defection and credible retaliation. In democratic politics, long-term defection is often **obscured**: underinvestment, deferred maintenance, regulatory erosion, and debt-financed consumption produce immediate gains while shifting costs into the future, often beyond the next election and onto diffuse publics.

This implies that democratic short-termism is primarily a **structural information-and-horizon problem**, not a mere defect of civic virtue. Even if a majority sincerely prefers long-term outcomes, that preference does not aggregate into stable cooperation unless institutions make future-oriented behavior legible and enforceable in repeated play. Majorities may want “responsible stewardship” in general, yet punish the concrete short-term sacrifices needed to sustain it because each actor expects others to defect, free-ride, or escape blame.

What follows is that reforms aimed at character—better leaders, more civic education, appeals to responsibility—will have limited effect unless they alter the game’s temporal structure. Cooperation on long-term goals should increase where institutions lengthen horizons, sharpen attribution, and preserve memory across rounds: independent fiscal and planning bodies, durable policy commitments, automatic stabilizers, intergenerational budgeting, transparent maintenance accounts, and constitutional or procedural devices that raise the visibility of long-term defection.

The broader illumination is this: democracy does not fail at long-termism because repeated interaction is absent, but because repetition is *misaligned* with payoff timing. When the benefits of cooperation arrive after the relevant players have rotated out, and when defection is hard to detect in time, democratic politics behaves like a one-shot game embedded inside a repeated shell. Under those conditions, short-term outcomes are the predictable equilibrium even when most participants would prefer otherwise.
