## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Institutional legitimacy under rapid change is maintained not by superior prediction or tighter control, but by possessing **simple generative rules for error-correction** that can be executed repeatedly under novel conditions. Most institutions fail to adapt because they are built on the assumption that legitimacy depends on preserving stable outputs, when in computationally irreducible environments legitimacy depends on preserving a credible *process for updating*.

From the perspective of computational irreducibility, rapid social, technological, and economic change means the institution cannot reliably forecast downstream consequences through planning alone. There is no shortcut model that lets leaders “solve” legitimacy in advance. The relevant question becomes: what minimal rule-set allows the institution to keep producing acceptable decisions as unforeseen situations unfold?

The institutions that remain legitimate tend to exhibit a few structural features:

1. **Short feedback loops**  
   They can detect error quickly from the people affected by decisions. Legitimacy is preserved when correction is visible, not when perfection is claimed.

2. **Local experimentation within bounded rules**  
   They allow lower-level units to try responses without requiring full central comprehension. In irreducible systems, adaptation must be partly exploratory.

3. **Transparent revision procedures**  
   People will tolerate surprising outcomes if they can see how decisions are made, challenged, and revised. This makes the institution’s generative rule legible even when its outputs are unpredictable.

4. **Modularity**  
   Failures can be contained instead of cascading across the whole system. Institutions lose legitimacy fast when every mistake becomes systemic.

5. **Narratives that redefine legitimacy as corrigibility**  
   The institution must culturally frame legitimacy not as infallibility, but as the reliable ability to learn without disintegrating.

Why do most fail? Because their structure encodes the opposite logic. Historically, many institutions were designed to solve problems of coordination, continuity, and authority under slower conditions. They inherited legitimacy mechanisms suited to relatively predictable environments: standardized procedure, hierarchy, delayed review, and symbolic consistency. These work when change is gradual and reality is compressible into rules and forecasts. Under rapid change, however, the institution encounters cases that cannot be pre-classified. It then responds by increasing control, adding policy layers, or defending past outputs—attempting to compress what is not compressible.

This creates a legitimacy trap: the more the institution treats unpredictability as a reputational threat, the more it suppresses the experimentation and criticism needed for adaptation. Error accumulates faster than the institution can publicly metabolize it. People then interpret rigidity as bad faith, incompetence, or capture.

So the collision reveals a shift in explanatory focus: legitimacy in fast-changing conditions is less a stock of trust than a **dynamic property of an institution’s update architecture**. Institutions endure when they can run the social equivalent of step-by-step computation in public—absorbing feedback, revising rules, and demonstrating that unpredictability is being handled by a trustworthy process rather than denied.
