## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Assume a simplified government with one ministry and one explicit goal: reduce bureaucracy. Isolate the key variable as **the cost asymmetry between adding and removing rules**.

In the scenario, every administrative rule exists because at some point it solved a problem: fraud, inconsistency, corruption, delay, legal liability, political embarrassment, or unequal treatment. A new failure is concrete and attributable; the absence of a removed rule is not. So when something goes wrong, there is always a visible path to response: add a procedure, approval layer, reporting requirement, exception protocol, or audit trail. But when things go right after simplification, the benefit is diffuse, hard to attribute, and politically fragile.

Now follow the logic strictly.

A government that wants less bureaucracy must decide which controls to remove. But removing a control creates **tail risk**: if a bad outcome follows, someone can say the simplification caused it. Keeping the control imposes only distributed friction, whose harms are real but harder to trace to a single decision-maker. Therefore each actor inside the system faces an incentive gradient: **additions are locally rational; deletions are locally dangerous**.

This generates a one-way ratchet.

Even anti-bureaucracy programs enter the same structure. To reduce complexity “responsibly,” leaders create review offices, metrics, sunset committees, compliance thresholds for elimination, stakeholder consultations, and safeguards against reckless cuts. Each of these is itself administrative machinery. The reform effort must prove that simplification will not endanger legality, fairness, continuity, or accountability; thus it inherits the burden of the very system it opposes.

Historically, this makes sense. Bureaucracy is not primarily built from a love of complexity. It is sedimented problem-solving. Each layer is a memory of prior failure encoded as process. The hidden assumption inherited from those origins is that **every remembered failure deserves permanent structural prevention**, while the cumulative cost of all preventions remains backgrounded.

What follows is that bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically not because governments are insincere, but because the system’s feedback loops reward **error-prevention by accretion** more than **performance-improvement by subtraction**. Complexity is a stock fed by many small inflows (new rules after incidents, court decisions, oversight demands, edge cases) and drained only by politically risky, epistemically difficult outflows (repeal, consolidation, discretion). Since inflows are easier to justify than outflows, the stock rises.

So the core claim is:

**Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically whenever institutions are structured so that specific failures are more legible and punishable than generalized friction.** In that world, even sincere simplification efforts mostly add second-order bureaucracy unless they alter the underlying accountability structure — for example, by making the costs of complexity as visible, attributable, and governable as the failures that complexity was built to prevent.
