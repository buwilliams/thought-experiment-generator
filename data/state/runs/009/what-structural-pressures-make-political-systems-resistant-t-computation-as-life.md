## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform despite supermajority support because they are not optimized to represent static preference totals; they are evolved computational systems optimized to preserve continuity under adversarial conditions. In such systems, the decisive variable is not how many citizens want change, but whether reform can propagate through the system’s error-correcting bottlenecks without triggering the self-protective routines of the institutions, coalitions, and procedures that currently encode the political order.

From the computational perspective, a political system is a distributed information-processing architecture. Votes, parties, laws, committees, courts, media narratives, donor networks, bureaucracies, and constitutions are not just “parts” but memory stores, filters, routing protocols, and replication mechanisms. Reform is therefore not simply a new input; it is a proposed rewrite of the code that governs how future inputs will be processed.

That creates a structural asymmetry: preserving the current rule-set is computationally cheaper and less risky than rewriting it. Existing institutions embody accumulated adaptations to past conflicts, so they treat major change as potentially catastrophic noise. Veto points, supermajority requirements, judicial review, bicameralism, federalism, party gatekeeping, and procedural delay function like redundancy and checksum layers in a fault-tolerant system. Their purpose is not primarily to enact popular will efficiently, but to prevent rapid state transitions from transient coalitions, manipulated signals, or coordination failures.

This helps explain why even broad support can fail. “Supermajority favor” is usually an aggregate preference signal, but political systems act on more than aggregates. They weight intensity, organization, persistence, elite compatibility, legal path dependence, and threat to incumbent control. A diffuse 70% public preference often loses to a concentrated 10% whose interests are encoded in the system with higher fidelity through money, procedural expertise, institutional placement, or constitutional entrenchment. In computational terms, some signals have higher bandwidth and lower transmission loss.

Historical genesis matters too. Many political systems were designed in response to fears of faction, tyranny, civil conflict, or rapid populist swings. So they inherit anti-volatility mechanisms that continue operating even when the present demand is legitimate and stable. What looks like irrational resistance is often the continued execution of old defensive code under new conditions.

What follows is that reform succeeds not when support merely increases, but when reformers solve the system’s compatibility problem: translating mass preference into durable, high-fidelity, cross-institutional representations that can survive veto layers. The leverage point is structural, not rhetorical. Reform must either (1) re-encode itself into existing channels—courts, parties, agencies, constitutional doctrines, elite coalitions—or (2) alter the system’s update rules so that public preference is processed with less loss.

So the core claim is: **political resistance to reform emerges because political systems compute for regime stability first and preference responsiveness second; supermajorities fail when they are informationally real at the population level but computationally weak at the institutional level.**
