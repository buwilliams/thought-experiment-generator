## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Democracies systematically favor short-term outcomes because collective long-term preference is not a directly executable input to the political system; it must be translated through many sequential, locally adaptive processes whose outcomes are computationally irreducible. The system cannot “jump” from “most people want long-term flourishing” to a stable long-term policy equilibrium by any compact rule, because the relevant behavior is generated through stepwise interactions among elections, media cycles, budget constraints, party competition, lobbying, bureaucratic implementation, and voter attention. What looks like irrational short-termism is often the minimal generative consequence of this structure.

What follows is that majority preference for long-term outcomes is too coarse-grained to govern the system. A voter may prefer climate stability, pension solvency, or infrastructure resilience in the abstract, yet when that preference is instantiated through repeated political micro-decisions, each node selects for immediate legibility: this year’s prices, this quarter’s jobs, this news cycle’s blame. Since no actor can compute the full downstream effects of present sacrifice across the whole system, they rely on proxies with shorter feedback loops. Those proxies systematically outperform long-horizon commitments because they generate faster, clearer reinforcement.

From a systems view, democracy contains asymmetric feedback: short-term harms are concentrated, attributable, and electorally salient; long-term benefits are diffuse, delayed, and often politically ownerless. That asymmetry is not merely a cognitive bias layered onto an otherwise tractable decision problem. It is a structural consequence of trying to steer a high-dimensional social process whose consequences can only be discovered by iterating through time. In computationally irreducible systems, “the future” is not available in compressed form for current decision. Therefore institutions reward what can be observed, narrated, and punished now.

This reframes the usual diagnosis. The problem is not simply hypocrisy, voter ignorance, or moral weakness. It is that democratic legitimacy is refreshed in short intervals, while many policy effects are only knowable through long runs of social computation. Elections therefore sample the process too frequently relative to the timescale on which many good outcomes emerge. Frequent accountability, under these conditions, can function like interrupting a computation before it stabilizes.

The practical implication is that reform should target the generative rule, not merely exhort citizens to “care more about the future.” If short-termism arises from irreducible stepwise selection, then leverage lies in creating institutions that preserve long-run commitments across many local updates: independent funds, automatic stabilizers, constitutional investment floors, citizens’ assemblies with delayed review, multi-decade budgeting, or policy designs whose long-term benefits are made locally visible sooner. The aim is not to out-predict the system, but to alter the rule by which present signals dominate future goods.

So the illuminating claim is: democracies do not fail to realize long-term majorities because they misunderstand their preferences; they fail because translating those preferences into policy requires traversing an irreducible computation that structurally favors actors and signals with shorter feedback loops.
