## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Bureaucratic complexity grows monotonically not mainly because governments fail to desire simplicity, but because they are trapped in an **equilibrium of defensive specification**: each actor can reduce their own risk, blame exposure, and uncertainty only by adding rules, reviews, exceptions, reporting layers, and compliance checks, while no single actor can safely remove them unilaterally.

This makes complexity a **Nash-equilibrium phenomenon**, not just a managerial mistake.

### What is structurally true
In a bureaucracy, the main agents — legislators, ministers, regulators, auditors, civil servants, courts, and affected stakeholders — face asymmetric punishment. They are punished far more for **visible failures of omission** (“why wasn’t this checked?”) than for **diffuse failures of commission** (“the process is too cumbersome”). So each agent rationally demands more documentation, more consultation, narrower discretion, and more appeal routes.

Each addition is locally beneficial:
- officials gain procedural cover,
- politicians gain signaling value,
- auditors gain measurable criteria,
- courts gain reviewable records,
- stakeholders gain carve-outs and protections.

But the aggregate result is a thicker administrative maze that almost nobody prefers in total.

### Why simplification fails
A government that “wants less bureaucracy” confronts a lock-in: removing a rule increases immediate, concentrated political and legal risk for the remover, while the gains from simplification are delayed, dispersed, and hard to attribute. If a minister cuts approvals and one scandal follows, the causal story is simple and punishable. If they leave the approvals in place and thousands of productive actions are delayed, the harm is real but politically low-resolution.

Thus “reduce complexity” is not a move available to any single player unless many others simultaneously change:
- auditors must tolerate more discretion,
- courts must defer more,
- politicians must accept more visible mistakes,
- media and publics must reward fewer process safeguards,
- agencies must share risk rather than offload it.

Absent coordinated change, complexity regrows.

### Historical genesis
Most bureaucratic layers originate as solutions to prior failures: corruption led to procurement rules; arbitrariness led to standardization; favoritism led to documentation; abuse led to appeal rights; scandal led to oversight. Complexity therefore inherits moral legitimacy from the problem it once solved. Simplification appears, to opponents, as amnesia about those past harms.

### What follows
This perspective implies that bureaucracy does not primarily expand because states are irrationally inefficient, but because **anti-error mechanisms compound while anti-complexity mechanisms are weak or absent**. There is no symmetric institutional force that automatically deletes obsolete controls. Addition is individually rational and morally legible; subtraction is collectively beneficial but individually dangerous.

So the key illumination is: bureaucratic growth is monotonic because **the system rewards precautionary accretion and punishes discretionary subtraction**. Reform requires changing the payoff structure — creating institutions that can safely remove rules in bundles, absorb transitional blame, and impose expiry on procedural layers — rather than merely exhorting officials to “cut red tape.”
