## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems become most resistant to reform not when support is weak, but when support is broad, diffuse, and non-catastrophic. At the limit, a system can face near-universal preference for change and still remain stable if the institutional costs of coordinated action are concentrated while the costs of inaction are distributed, delayed, or absorbed unevenly.

Pushing the problem to the boundary clarifies the structure. Imagine two extremes:

- **At 0% support for reform**, resistance is trivial: the system reflects preference.
- **At 100% support**, we might expect reform to be automatic. But this only follows if “support” is itself an effective causal force. Usually it is not.

A political system is not a preference-aggregator in the simple sense; it is a **conversion mechanism** that turns sentiment into action through veto points, agenda control, timing rules, party incentives, legal constraints, and bureaucratic interpretation. If every stage in that chain can stall without bearing the full cost of stalling, then even overwhelming public desire does not reliably become reform.

The hidden structure appears at the limit: **resistance is produced by asymmetry between intensity, organization, and interruption**.

What follows:

1. **Diffuse majorities lose to concentrated minorities** not mainly because they are smaller, but because they are structurally easier to coordinate against than for. A minority needs only to block one channel; a majority must successfully traverse all channels.

2. **Status quo institutions have compounding defensive advantages.** Existing law, office, funding streams, and legitimacy narratives are already instantiated. Reform must not only win approval but also overcome sunk commitments embedded across the system.

3. **Supermajority support is often shallow at the action margin.** Near everyone may say “yes” in abstraction, but if only a few actors face immediate losses, those actors will invest far more in prevention than supporters will invest in enactment. Thus the relevant variable is not numerical support but **organized willingness to bear transition costs**.

4. **The more fragmented the system, the more reform requires unanimity across time rather than agreement at one moment.** A supermajority opinion can exist briefly; institutional passage often requires persistence across elections, chambers, courts, agencies, and implementation phases. Time itself becomes a veto point.

5. **Political systems are often optimized for error-avoidance, not preference-realization.** Their deep design problem is: “How do we prevent dangerous change?” That means they are structurally biased toward false negatives (failing to enact good reforms) over false positives (enacting bad ones).

So the core claim is: **Reform resistance arises when institutions are designed such that stopping change is cheaper, safer, and more locally rational than producing it.** Supermajorities matter only when they alter that structure — by concentrating costs on obstructors, reducing veto points, increasing durability of public demand, or making nonresponse itself system-threatening.

In short: the boundary case reveals that public will is not the opposite of institutional inertia. It is merely one input into a system whose deeper logic may privilege nonconversion.
