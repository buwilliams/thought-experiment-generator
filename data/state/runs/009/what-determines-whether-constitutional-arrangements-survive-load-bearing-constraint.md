## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Constitutional arrangements survive major social stress when their core rules are *load-bearing in practice*—that is, when changing any one of them would force costly compensations across multiple institutions, coalitions, and expectations. They collapse when the constitution is mostly a narrative shell: formally revered, but with details that can be swapped out without disrupting how power is actually organized.

From this perspective, the key question is not whether a constitution is well-designed on paper, but whether its provisions are tightly integrated with the society’s operative feedback loops. A surviving constitution has rules that do real explanatory work: succession rules, fiscal authority, civil-military relations, judicial settlement, territorial bargaining, and legitimacy-creating rituals all constrain one another. Under stress, actors can still fight within the system because each part depends on the others. Violating one rule threatens wider breakdown, so even powerful factions have reason to preserve the arrangement.

A collapsing constitution is one whose parts are not jointly constraining. Its clauses may be symbolically important, but they are institutionally interchangeable because actual coordination occurs elsewhere: in party machines, patronage networks, military command, regional oligarchies, foreign guarantors, or revolutionary organizations. In that case, social stress does not merely “pressure” the constitution; it reveals that the real system was outside it all along. Regime change then appears sudden, but the explanatory structure had already shifted.

What follows is a sharper test for resilience. Ask of each constitutional feature: if this were altered, what else would have to change for the political order to keep functioning? If the answer is “not much,” that feature is decorative. If changing it would disrupt revenue collection, elite bargains, coercive command, dispute resolution, and public expectations all at once, it is load-bearing. A constitution survives insofar as enough of its central features are like that.

Historically, constitutions often originate as solutions to prior coordination failures—civil war, dynastic conflict, regional fragmentation, military predation, class struggle. Their endurance depends on whether they continue solving those problems better than rival arrangements. Major social stress becomes regime-ending when it changes the underlying problem structure faster than constitutional adaptation can occur. Then formerly load-bearing provisions become obsolete, and new institutions arise to carry the constraints.

So the determinant is not “legitimacy” or “force” alone, but *structural entanglement*: the degree to which constitutional rules are embedded in the real production of order. Survival means the constitution remains the least bad way for major actors to coordinate under new stress. Collapse means the stress exposes that coordination has migrated elsewhere, making a new regime not just possible but explanatorily cheaper.
