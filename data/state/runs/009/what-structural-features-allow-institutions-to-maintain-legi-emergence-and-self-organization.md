## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they are organized not around preserving specific rules or roles, but around preserving a credible *process of error-correction* that the public can observe, influence, and trust. Most institutions fail to adapt because their internal feedback loops are optimized for stability, reputation protection, and elite coordination rather than for detecting and correcting mismatch with changing reality.

From an emergence perspective, legitimacy is not a property possessed by an institution’s components — leaders, laws, procedures, or symbols — taken separately. It is an emergent system-level relation between institutional decisions, public expectations, informational flows, and visible mechanisms of revision. An institution is seen as legitimate when people can model it as responsive to reasons, not merely as consistent or powerful.

Under rapid change, the core problem is not just that old policies become outdated. It is that the *feedback architecture* that once linked reality to institutional learning becomes too slow, too filtered, or too punitive toward dissent. Signals from the environment arrive distorted; internal actors are rewarded for defending inherited categories; anomalies are treated as threats to authority rather than inputs to adaptation. The institution then appears incoherent: formally stable but substantively detached. Legitimacy declines not simply because it errs, but because it cannot metabolize error in public.

So the structural features that preserve legitimacy are those that make adaptation legible and non-catastrophic:

1. **Short feedback loops** between outcomes and decision revision.  
2. **Distributed sensing**, so peripheral actors can transmit novel information upward.  
3. **Protected internal criticism**, allowing error reports without existential penalty.  
4. **Modular authority**, so parts can change without total institutional breakdown.  
5. **Publicly intelligible update rules**, so change appears principled rather than arbitrary.  
6. **Narrative continuity**, connecting revisions to the institution’s enduring purpose.

These features matter because legitimacy under change depends on a paradox: institutions must remain recognizable while altering themselves. That is a systems problem, not a component problem. The institution must preserve identity at the level of function and purpose while varying structure at the level of implementation.

Most institutions fail because they are built from historical solutions to earlier threats — often threats of disorder, fragmentation, or loss of control. They inherit rigid hierarchies, centralized filtering, ceremonial consistency, and incentive systems that equate admitting error with weakening authority. These structures generate success in stable environments, but under rapid change they create runaway reinforcing loops: more uncertainty produces more message control, which reduces learning, which worsens mismatch, which further erodes trust.

Thus, legitimacy is best understood not as static moral prestige but as an emergent property of a system that can visibly learn without disintegrating. Institutions endure rapid change when they institutionalize self-revision. They fail when they confuse the preservation of legitimacy with the preservation of existing form.
