## Conjecture

Conjecture: Constitutional survival under major social stress is determined less by the nominal design of institutions than by whether the constitutional order preserves a workable set of political invariants across shock-induced transformations in society.

Mathematically, model a constitution not as a text but as a constraint system on political interaction. Let:

- \(S(t)\): level and type of social stress
- \(I\): institutional structure
- \(L\): legitimacy stock
- \(C\): coercive capacity
- \(E\): elite coordination
- \(M\): mass compliance
- \(A\): adaptation capacity — the ability to revise policy, interpretation, and personnel without breaking the constitutional frame

A constitutional regime survives if, under rising \(S(t)\), the system can transform internal states \((L, E, M, C)\) while preserving a small set of invariants:

1. authoritative decision remains possible,
2. succession remains rule-governed,
3. conflict remains bounded,
4. enough actors expect future gains from staying inside the rules.

These are the real constitutional invariants. Everything else — party systems, doctrinal interpretations, administrative form — may vary.

Collapse occurs when stress pushes the system past a critical threshold where these invariants can no longer be jointly maintained. Formally: if adaptation capacity \(A\) is too low relative to stress \(S\), then legitimacy \(L\) and elite coordination \(E\) decay faster than the system can restore them. Once \(E\) fragments and mass compliance \(M\) falls below some threshold, coercive capacity \(C\) must substitute for legitimacy. But that substitution is unstable: coercion can enforce order locally while destroying the expectation that rules, not force, govern outcomes. The regime then undergoes a phase transition into a new attractor: dictatorship, civil conflict, revolutionary refoundation, or informal oligarchy.

This perspective illuminates why rigid constitutions often fail not because they are insufficiently revered, but because they have too few admissible transformations. A robust constitution is one that allows high internal flexibility while preserving a narrow constitutional identity. In geometric terms: survival requires a large basin of deformation around the same regime core.

It also explains why some apparently weak constitutions endure: they encode strong feedback loops for error correction — elections, bargaining forums, federal buffers, emergency but reversible powers, legitimate amendment pathways. These mechanisms increase \(A\), preventing stress from converting disagreement into regime exit.

So the determining variable is not “strength” in the ordinary sense, but the ratio:

\[
R = \frac{A + L + E}{S}
\]

When \(R\) stays above a critical value, constitutional order absorbs shock. When it falls below it, constitutional rules cease to coordinate expectations, and a new regime becomes mathematically more stable than the old one. The core claim is that constitutions survive when they can change enough to keep their invariants intact.
