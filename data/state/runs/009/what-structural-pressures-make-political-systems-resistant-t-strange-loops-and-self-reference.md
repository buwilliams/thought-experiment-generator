## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform under supermajority pressure because they are not merely decision-making machines; they are **self-referential stability-preserving loops**. At sufficient institutional complexity, a political order models and protects the conditions of its own continued legitimacy. Reform is therefore processed not just as a policy proposal but as a perturbation to the system’s self-description. The more a change threatens the loop by which institutions recognize themselves as valid, the more likely it is to be absorbed, delayed, translated, or nullified.

From the strange-loops perspective, the key structure is this: citizens authorize institutions; institutions define the valid forms of citizen authorization; those definitions then determine which demands count as legitimate reform. Traversing the hierarchy—from public will, to representation, to procedure, to constitutional identity—returns to the starting point, but transformed: “the people” can act only through the channels the system already certifies as “the people’s will.” Thus a supermajority may exist sociologically while failing to exist institutionally.

This illuminates why resistance often persists even when preferences are clear. The issue is not only veto points or elite interests, though those matter. More deeply, political systems are organized to solve a prior problem: preventing arbitrary power and preserving continuity across time. They inherit procedures, thresholds, bicameralism, judicial review, federalism, party mediation, and administrative layering as safeguards against impulsive self-overwrite. These mechanisms become parts of a recursive identity function: the system proves its legitimacy by showing that it cannot be easily altered, even by large majorities.

So the structural pressure is not simply conservatism in the psychological sense; it is **ontological self-maintenance**. Institutions face a paradox: to reform themselves they must use rules designed partly to prevent self-dissolution. This creates a double bind. If change flows outside the rules, it appears illegitimate. If it flows inside them, it encounters actors and checkpoints whose role is precisely to test whether the system should remain what it already is. Supermajorities therefore meet a machine optimized to reinterpret demand as noise unless it can be recursively encoded in the system’s own language.

What follows is that reform succeeds less by increasing raw support than by altering the loop through which support is recognized. The leverage point is meta-political: change the representational grammar, agenda-setting rules, jurisdictional boundaries, or legitimacy narratives that convert preference into authorized action. In systems terms, feedback about public desire is weak, delayed, and filtered, while feedback preserving institutional identity is immediate and reinforced.

So the collision yields this claim: **political resistance to reform is strongest where the system’s self-model equates procedural continuity with legitimacy itself.** Under those conditions, supermajorities are insufficient unless they also become legible to the system at the level where the system tells itself what counts as valid change.
