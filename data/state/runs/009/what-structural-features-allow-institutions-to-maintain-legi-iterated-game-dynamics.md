## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Institutions maintain legitimacy under rapid change when they preserve the conditions of an **iterated game** between themselves and their publics: repeated, legible interactions; credible reciprocity; visible correction after error; and a sufficiently long shared time horizon. Most institutions fail to adapt not because change is too fast in itself, but because rapid change **disrupts the game structure** that previously sustained cooperation, turning repeated reciprocal relations into something closer to a sequence of one-shot encounters.

From this perspective, legitimacy is not mainly a stock of moral authority or tradition. It is the equilibrium outcome of ongoing interaction: people comply, defer, and cooperate when they expect the institution to continue existing, to remember past behavior, and to respond predictably to cooperation and defection. An institution is legitimate when actors believe, in effect, “this relationship continues, and today’s restraint will matter tomorrow.”

Rapid change undermines those expectations in four structural ways:

1. **It shortens time horizons.**  
   Crisis, leadership churn, technological disruption, and political volatility make future interaction less predictable. When the shadow of the future contracts, both institutions and publics shift toward extraction, signaling, or opportunism.

2. **It obscures action and accountability.**  
   In stable settings, defections are legible: broken promises, corruption, nonperformance. Under rapid change, it becomes harder to tell whether failure came from bad faith, incapacity, or genuine uncertainty. Reciprocity breaks when punishment and reward can no longer be reliably targeted.

3. **It fragments reputation.**  
   Institutions often depend on accumulated reputational capital, but rapid change creates many new arenas of interaction faster than reputation can transfer. A previously trusted institution may face publics who experience it as effectively anonymous or inconsistent.

4. **It disables credible adaptation.**  
   If institutions cannot visibly update rules while maintaining continuity of commitment, reform itself looks like defection: “the terms changed after we cooperated.” Adaptation then erodes legitimacy instead of restoring it.

What follows is that resilient legitimacy requires not rigidity, but **structures that keep repeated play intact during turbulence**: transparent feedback loops, public error-correction, memory of prior commitments, stable interfaces even when underlying rules change, and mechanisms that make defections—by either officials or publics—observable and proportionately answerable.

Most institutions fail because they were built for slower environments in which iteration happened automatically. They inherited legitimacy from repetition without needing to consciously reproduce the underlying game conditions. Under rapid change, they often respond by centralizing discretion, accelerating opaque decisions, or communicating in one-way bursts—all moves that further convert reciprocal relationships into one-shot games.

So the key structural feature is this: institutions remain legitimate when they can **preserve reciprocity under accelerated conditions**. They fail when change destroys the visibility, memory, and future orientation required for cooperative equilibrium.
