## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Political systems resist reform under supermajority support when the institutions that must enact change are optimized to protect incumbent coordination structures, not to mirror aggregate preference. In that case, “majority will” is not the decisive variable; **institutional veto density** is.

To isolate the key variable, imagine a hypothetical polity in which 80% of citizens favor a reform continuously for ten years. Assume preference is stable, informed, and sincere. Remove persuasion failure, misinformation, and low turnout as explanations. The reform still does not pass.

What must be true for this to happen? The system must contain multiple decision points controlled by actors whose survival depends more on preserving existing bargains than on satisfying mass preference. These actors may be legislators, courts, parties, committees, bureaucracies, subnational units, donors, or agenda-setters. Each can block change at lower cost than they can build agreement around a replacement. So the structure rewards refusal more reliably than construction.

Following that logic, the relevant pressure is not “public disagreement” but **asymmetry between the ease of blocking and the difficulty of recomposing the system after change**. Existing arrangements are embedded in budgets, legal precedent, careers, territorial representation, patronage networks, and constitutional rules. Reform threatens to redistribute not just benefits but decision rights. Even actors who agree with the reform in principle may resist it because they cannot predict the second-order effects on the wider settlement that currently stabilizes their position.

Thus political systems become resistant to reform when they are solving a prior problem: preventing rapid domination, factional capture, or regime instability. Historically, many institutions were built to slow action, fragment authority, and force bargains across competing elites. Those anti-concentration mechanisms persist even when they now obstruct broadly desired change. What was inherited as a safeguard becomes, under new conditions, a brake.

This yields a sharper claim: **supermajority opinion is often structurally weaker than a well-placed minority embedded in veto points**. A dispersed majority expresses preference; an organized minority controls sequence, procedure, interpretation, or implementation. Because the status quo is already coordinated, it enjoys the advantage of sunk structure. Reform must coordinate a new equilibrium across many nodes simultaneously.

What follows is that legitimacy crises emerge not simply from unpopular outcomes, but from repeated demonstrations that preference aggregation does not govern institutional behavior. Citizens then misdiagnose the problem as moral failure or corruption alone, when the deeper cause is architectural: the system converts intensity, placement, and procedural control into power more effectively than it converts numerical support into action.

So the central illumination is this: **political resistance to reform is a property of system design under conditions of high veto density and high transition cost**. If that is right, then efforts aimed only at increasing public support will predictably fail once support exceeds a threshold. The leverage point is not more agreement, but reducing the number, insulation, or strategic value of choke points that let incumbency outcompete consent.
