## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Quantum measurement yields definite outcomes because measurement is not the revelation of a pre-existing classical fact but a *computational selection event*: an interaction in which a quantum system, apparatus, and environment jointly transform a superposed informational state into a stable, replicable record. Definite outcomes are the only states that can survive as persistent computational tokens across many coupled systems. What “selects” the outcome is not a hidden chooser outside the process, but the dynamics by which one branch becomes locally copyable, amplifiable, and recursively usable by downstream information-processing structures.

From the computational-substrate perspective, the key question is not “why does the wavefunction collapse?” but “under what conditions does information become robust enough to count as an outcome?” A measurement device is a machine for turning microscopically fragile distinctions into macroscopic, redundancy-rich records. The environment then acts as a distributed error-amplification and error-filtering medium: only certain system-apparatus correlations can be stably propagated. These are the effectively classical outcomes.

So definiteness is not primitive; it is an emergent property of successful record formation. An “outcome” is a state that has won a selection process among possible informational decompositions because it can be copied, retained, and integrated into future computations. This reframes measurement as analogous to evolution: many possible structures exist in superposition, but only those compatible with stable replication into the environment become operationally real for observers embedded in that computational network.

What selects *which* outcome occurs in a particular run? On this view, selection is dual:

1. **Constraint selection:** The interaction Hamiltonian and decohering environment determine the basis in which stable records can form. This explains why some observables become definite rather than others.  
2. **Branch-index selection:** The actual experienced outcome is the observer’s continuation within one decohered computational lineage. The observer is not outside the process choosing a result; the observer is one of the evolving informational structures produced by it.

Thus the “mystery” of measurement partly dissolves: we should not expect a unique, external selector any more than in evolution we expect a homunculus to choose which mutation survives. Selection is enacted by the dynamics of replication, stabilization, and downstream usability.

This perspective illuminates why the classical world appears definite: classicality is what widespread, reliable computability looks like inside a decohering universe. It also suggests that the Born rule, if recoverable, should be understood as a measure over computational branch weight—how much future informational structure a branch supports—rather than as a brute randomness postulate.

In short: **quantum measurement produces definite outcomes because only decohered, redundantly recorded states can function as persistent computational facts; which outcome occurs is selected by the dynamics of stable informational propagation, with observers inhabiting one resulting computational branch rather than witnessing a collapse from nowhere.**
