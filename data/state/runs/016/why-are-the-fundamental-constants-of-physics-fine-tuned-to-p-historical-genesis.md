## Conjecture

Conjecture: “Fine-tuning” is partly a legacy concept from an inherited explanatory frame: we treat the fundamental constants as if they were adjustable design parameters because physics arose by solving the problem of compressing observed regularities into laws, and that style of explanation imported an engineer’s picture of a system with knobs. What goes unexamined is whether the constants are really the right ontological units of explanation at all.

Origin story first: the modern constants emerged as bookkeeping devices in theories that unified phenomena previously treated separately. They solved the problem of expressing stable quantitative relations across experiments. Later, as cosmology and particle physics expanded, those same constants were reinterpreted as “inputs” to reality itself. A practical representational convenience hardened into a metaphysical question: why these values rather than others? That shift inherits a hidden assumption — that the space of possible constants is prior, real, and comparably variable, and that our universe is one draw from it.

From this perspective, the fine-tuning problem may be mislocated. It may not fundamentally be “Why do the constants permit complexity?” but “Why are we using a formulation of physics in which the existence of complexity appears to depend on many independently specifiable numerical parameters?” The problem as posed inherits the reductionist decomposition of theories into fixed laws plus contingent constants. But that decomposition may be historically contingent, not ontologically deep.

What follows is a different conjectural direction: if future physics explains more, it may do so not by selecting our constants from a larger menu, but by replacing the menu. Some or all “constants” may turn out to be artifacts of a provisional description, like epicycles or separate electric and magnetic fields before deeper unification. In that case, apparent fine-tuning would be evidence less of cosmic improbability than of explanatory incompleteness.

This also illuminates why anthropic reasoning feels simultaneously powerful and unsatisfying. It solves a newer problem — how to reason under observational selection effects, especially in multiverse-style contexts — but it leaves untouched the older inherited assumption that the constants are brute tunable degrees of freedom. It explains why observers see observer-permitting worlds, but not why fundamental description should take this parameterized form.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a reframing: fine-tuning is not only a puzzle about nature; it is a clue about the historical architecture of our theories. The deepest answer may come from asking what prior explanatory problems gave us “constants” in the first place, and whether a better theory would make complex structure not a lucky outcome of tuned inputs, but a robust consequence of a different underlying ontology.
