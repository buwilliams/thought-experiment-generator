## Conjecture

Conjecture: In an Everettian setting, Born-rule weights can be understood not as primitive chances but as the equilibrium frequencies with which decohering branches succeed in producing durable, reproducible records under a public-goods constraint on record formation.

Why this lens helps: a stable measurement outcome is not just a branch existing. It must generate a robust record that many downstream subsystems can access, copy, and use. That record is effectively a non-excludable good within a branch: once formed, many agents and devices can benefit from it, while no single microscopic interaction “pays for” the whole macroscopic stability of the record. Record formation therefore depends on a coordination structure among many environmental degrees of freedom. The issue is not mere observer ignorance or weak motivation; it is structural underprovision. Most fine-grained branchings fail to accumulate enough mutually reinforcing imprinting to become durable public records.

What follows is this: branch “probability” should track not bare branch-counting, but the measure of branches whose amplitudes are sufficient to sustain redundant environmental encoding. Amplitude-squared then appears as a natural candidate for the rate at which branch structure can finance its own reproducibility. High-weight branches can support many redundant copies of the same outcome across the environment; low-weight branches are systematically underprovisioned and tend to remain too fragile, too sparsely encoded, or too interference-vulnerable to count as stable records.

In systems terms, decoherence creates a positive feedback loop: stronger environmental imprinting makes a branch more classical, which enables more copying, which further stabilizes the record. But this loop has a threshold. Below threshold, record production collapses; above threshold, it scales. The Born rule would then characterize the attractor distribution of record-supported branches, not an axiom about subjective uncertainty. The relevant “frequency” is how often branch structures cross the durability threshold in repeated measurement-like interactions.

This also illuminates why branch counting is misleading. Counting treats every formal branch as equally real for evidential purposes, but record formation is a collective-action problem among microphysical components. Just as public goods are underprovided when benefits are diffuse, durable classicality is underproduced in low-measure sectors because no sufficient structural support exists there for widespread, persistent encoding. The result is a stable no-record equilibrium for many nominal branches and a provision equilibrium for branches weighted by squared amplitude.

So the conjecture is not that Born probabilities emerge from ignorance, but that they emerge from selection on which branches can become publicly accessible facts. If correct, the Born rule is the law of branch recordability: squared amplitude measures a branch’s capacity to generate and maintain reproducible records across repeated decohering interactions.
