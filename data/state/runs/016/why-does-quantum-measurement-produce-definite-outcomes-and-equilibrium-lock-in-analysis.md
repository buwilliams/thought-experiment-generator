## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Quantum measurement yields definite outcomes because “having a definite outcome” is not primarily a dynamical miracle but a **stability condition imposed by an equilibrium between system, apparatus, and environment**. The measurement problem persists because we try to explain outcome selection from the perspective of a single subsystem evolving unitarily, when definiteness is a **lock-in phenomenon of the larger interaction structure**. What gets selected is whichever branch becomes embedded in a self-reinforcing informational equilibrium: one in which records in the apparatus, environment, and future observers become mutually consistent and costly to reverse.

From an equilibrium lock-in perspective, the key question is not “what magical event collapses the wavefunction?” but “why does one coarse-grained record become the only locally stable description available to agents inside the measurement process?” Before measurement, amplitudes coexist because no macroscopic informational structure yet forces exclusivity. During measurement, the apparatus amplifies microscopic differences into macroscopically distinct record states. Decoherence then couples those record states to the environment so strongly that interference between them becomes inaccessible. At that point, no single local agent — not the particle, not the apparatus, not an observer — can restore the prior superposed description in practice. The system has fallen into an **equilibrium trap of classical definiteness**.

This illuminates why definite outcomes appear unavoidable even if the underlying dynamics are continuous and reversible. Once many degrees of freedom encode the same result, “this detector clicked” becomes a Nash-like equilibrium among records: each component is best described conditional on the others, and no component can unilaterally shift to a rival outcome description without creating global inconsistency. Definite outcomes are thus structurally selected pointer states: states robust under environmental monitoring because they participate in stable informational equilibria.

What, then, selects *which* outcome occurs? This perspective suggests that selection is not explained by the local measurement interaction alone, because prior to lock-in multiple outcome-branches remain available in the formalism. The actualized outcome is the branch that enters the stable record equilibrium from the observer’s standpoint. The Born rule would then express not a physical “kick” choosing one branch, but the measure over which equilibria are available from within the pre-lock-in state.

So the measurement problem may be partly misframed. It is not mainly: “How does unitary evolution produce a single outcome?” It is: “How do branching possibilities become locked into mutually reinforcing informational structures such that only one outcome remains locally actionable and reportable for an embedded observer?” On this view, definiteness is an emergent equilibrium constraint, and outcome selection is the observer-relative occupation of one decohered equilibrium branch rather than a further dynamical event added to quantum theory.
