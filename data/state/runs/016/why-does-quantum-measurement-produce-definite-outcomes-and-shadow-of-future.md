## Conjecture

Conjecture: What looks like “selection of one outcome” in quantum measurement is best understood not as a single mysterious event requiring a special chooser, but as the stabilization of one branch of interaction under conditions of repeated coupling, memory, and irreversible consequence. Definite outcomes arise when a quantum system enters a game it cannot exit: repeated interactions with an environment that records, amplifies, and feeds back the same information until alternative histories no longer remain mutually actionable.

From this perspective, the key question is not “what inner virtue of nature collapses the wavefunction?” but “what interaction structure makes one outcome the only strategically stable one for future physical interactions?” A microscopic superposition can persist when interactions are sparse, reversible, and hidden. But measurement is precisely the opposite regime: visibility increases, records proliferate, and many subsystems become correlated with the same variable. The environment “remembers.” Once that happens, future interactions respond to that recorded variable, not to the prior superposition as such.

So definite outcomes are produced by a transition from one-shot interaction to iterated interaction with memory. Decoherence is then illuminated as the process by which defection from definiteness — i.e. phase-coherent coexistence of alternatives — becomes dynamically inaccessible because the environment has already copied the relevant information into too many places. The system is no longer playing only against a measuring device; it is in repeated play with an entire network of witnesses. In such a system, only robust, redundantly recorded states can persist as bases for future interaction. These are the “pointer states.”

What, then, selects which outcome occurs? This perspective suggests a split answer. The measurement interaction selects the menu of stable outcomes by structuring which observables can be redundantly recorded. The actual outcome is the branch that becomes locally self-reinforcing through the specific pattern of entangling interactions that occurs. In other words: the apparatus-environment system determines what can count as a definite outcome; the detailed entanglement history determines which one is realized for an observer within a branch.

This does not by itself solve the full probability problem. It illuminates why outcomes are definite-for-observers and why only certain observables become classical, but it does not yet derive why one branch is experienced with Born-rule weight rather than another unless supplemented by an interpretation. Still, the collision is productive: it shifts the explanatory burden away from a metaphysical “collapse trigger” and toward the structure of repeated interaction. Measurement yields definiteness when future consequences of a recorded distinction become unavoidable, legible, and recursively reinforced across many coupled systems. In that regime, “an outcome” is not chosen by virtue but selected by the stability of a branch under iterated physical games.
