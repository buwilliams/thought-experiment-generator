## Conjecture

Conjecture: In a quantum world, Born-rule probabilities can be approached as the unique weighting that makes record formation stable under repeated interaction, coarse-graining, and cross-context reuse of information; if so, probabilities are not primitive chances but emergent frequencies of branches that successfully produce durable, reproducible records.

From the “invariants vs shortcuts” lens, the key question is: what must a predictive scheme track if it is to keep working across interventions and changes of basis, apparatus, environment, and scale? A shortcut predictor could assign ad hoc branch weights that fit one measurement context. But such weights will generally fail when the same underlying state is probed through different decohering interactions, or when records are copied, compared, and embedded in larger systems. Stable prediction across those variations seems to require discovering an invariant structure beneath the phenomenology of outcomes.

That structure may be this: a “record” is not just any branch-local event, but a pattern that survives decoherence, can be redundantly copied into the environment, and remains mutually consistent when accessed by many subsystems. The branches that count operationally are therefore not all mathematically definable branches, but the subset that become durable enough to function as reproducible records. The conjecture is that the measure of such durable record-bearing branches is given by squared amplitude, because only that measure is preserved in the right way under unitary evolution, branching refinements, and composition of subsystems.

What follows is a systems picture. The stock is accessible recorded information; the flows are decohering interactions that create, amplify, or erase records; the feedback loop is that records which are more reproducible get reused in future interactions, thereby becoming the effective empirical world. A candidate probability rule must be stable under this dynamics. If one uses any weighting other than Born weights, one can likely fit isolated cases, but not maintain coherence across recursive copying, observer agreement, and repeated experiments. Those alternatives are shortcuts: locally adequate, globally unstable.

So the illuminating claim is not merely “Born rule from decision theory” or “Born rule from symmetry,” but: the environment-induced selection of records imposes a generalization pressure. To support reliable prediction, a branch measure must remain invariant under the transformations involved in record production. Squared amplitude is plausibly singled out because it tracks the branches whose informational patterns persist and propagate, while rival measures overcount fragile distinctions or undercount robust ones.

If correct, this reframes quantum probability. It is not about subjective uncertainty laid atop branching, nor about brute stochastic law. It is about which branch structures can actually become facts for a community of interacting systems. Born-rule probabilities would then express the invariant geometry of durable record formation under decoherence.
