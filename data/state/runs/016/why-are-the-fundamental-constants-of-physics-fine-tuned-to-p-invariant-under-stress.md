## Conjecture

From the given lens, the key question is: does successful “prediction” of a universe require discovering deep structure, or can one get low loss through shortcuts? Applied cosmologically, a universe with no stable atoms, chemistry, stars, or long-lived disequilibria offers almost no persistent structure to be modeled. It is a world of low generalizability: patterns do not survive perturbation, scale change, or intervention. By contrast, a universe like ours contains invariants that reappear under many transformations—orbital mechanics, nuclear stability bands, chemical valence, error-correcting biological replication. These are exactly the kinds of structures that support compression, prediction, and knowledge growth.

So the collision suggests this: **fine-tuning should be reframed as a selection effect over learnable worlds**. If constants varied widely, most combinations would produce only transient or sterile dynamics, where no complex predictor could exist because there would be no enduring regularities to latch onto. Observers therefore should expect to find themselves only in universes where superficial correlations fail and deeper invariants are available—where variation and intervention expose stable structure rather than dissolve it.

What follows is not merely anthropic triviality (“we observe a life-permitting universe because otherwise we wouldn’t be here”). The stronger claim is epistemic-structural: **complex observers require not just habitability, but a law-space rich in counterfactually stable structure**. Fine-tuning for life is likely downstream of fine-tuning for multiscale robustness, because life and knowledge are exploiters of reusable regularity.

This perspective also weakens one common intuition: that each constant is an independently adjustable knob whose narrow viable range cries out for external tuning. That may be a shortcut description, analogous to a predictive model that treats correlated latent variables as separate features. Deeper theory may reveal that many constants covary because they arise from one underlying structure; the apparent fine-tuning then partly reflects our current factorization, not reality’s.

So the illuminated possibility is: the constants look fine-tuned because only universes with deep, intervention-stable invariants can host complexity, and because our current parameterization may obscure the deeper constraints that make such invariants possible. The real explanatory target is not “why these numbers?” in isolation, but “what underlying structure makes a universe generative of durable patterns, and are these numbers merely its surface encoding?”
