## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A major obstacle to unifying general relativity and quantum mechanics is not just technical difficulty or conceptual confusion, but a *structural underprovision of integrative explanatory work*: the payoff from a true unification is broad, diffuse, and non-excludable across physics, while the costs of pursuing it are concentrated, high-risk, and often borne by small communities working against entrenched disciplinary feedback loops. This helps lock the field into a stable equilibrium of partial progress without decisive unification.

From this lens, the problem is illuminated as a public-goods problem inside knowledge production. A unified theory would generate explanatory gains for everyone: high-energy physics, cosmology, black hole theory, foundations of quantum mechanics, and perhaps even philosophy of space, time, and information. But no single subfield can fully capture those benefits. Meanwhile, researchers are rewarded for tractable, local advances within existing frameworks—precision calculations in quantum field theory, phenomenology, cosmological modeling, mathematical consistency results—rather than for long-horizon integrative conjectures with uncertain payoff. Individually rational career and institutional choices can therefore reproduce collective underinvestment in the hardest bridging questions.

What follows is that some persistent fragmentation may be structural rather than merely intellectual. General relativity and quantum mechanics embody different explanatory architectures: GR makes spacetime dynamical and geometrical; QM treats physical systems probabilistically, often against a fixed background structure. Unification requires more than combining equations. It requires supplying a new framework in which both appear as limiting cases, and in which their current incompatibilities are explained rather than patched over. That kind of framework is especially hard to produce because its success criteria are distributed across many domains and because existing specialties can each advance without solving the whole.

A unified theory would need to explain at least four things:

1. **Why both current theories work so well where they do** — it must recover GR at large scales and low curvature, and QM/QFT in regimes where quantum effects dominate but gravity is negligible.  
2. **What spacetime is** — whether fundamental, emergent, discrete, continuous, informational, or something else, and why gravity takes the form of spacetime dynamics.  
3. **What happens in regimes where both are indispensable** — black hole interiors and evaporation, the Big Bang or early universe, singularities, and Planck-scale phenomena.  
4. **How information, causality, and measurement fit together with gravity** — especially in contexts where horizon physics and quantum entanglement seem to force a rethinking of locality and geometry.

So the collision between this lens and the problem suggests: the barrier is partly a knowledge-coordination problem. The missing theory is not merely undiscovered; the search for it is structurally underprovided because its benefits are public, while the incentives governing inquiry remain local.
