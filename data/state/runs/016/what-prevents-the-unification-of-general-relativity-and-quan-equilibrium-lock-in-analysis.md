## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The failure to unify general relativity and quantum mechanics is partly an **equilibrium lock-in in theory space**: each framework is internally successful only when its own core assumptions are held fixed, and no single modification to either side yields a better overall theory. As a result, inquiry gets trapped between two locally stable equilibria — quantum theory with fixed background structure, and general relativity with dynamical spacetime geometry — even though both likely arise as limiting cases of a deeper account.

What is structurally locked in is not merely sociology or lack of effort, but the **mutual dependence of concepts**. Quantum mechanics presupposes a stable causal and temporal arena in which states evolve, probabilities are defined, and observables are related. General relativity denies exactly that fixity: spacetime geometry is itself dynamical, observer-dependent, and responsive to energy. If one tries to quantize gravity by minimally importing quantum rules into GR, one inherits nonrenormalizability and conceptual problems about time, locality, and observables. If one tries to gravitationalize quantum theory without changing its foundations, one preserves the very background dependence that gravity undermines. Neither camp can unilaterally “move” without losing explanatory power relative to its home domain.

This suggests the obstacle is not simply missing mathematics, but a **coordination problem among explanatory principles**. A unified theory must change several interlocking commitments at once: what counts as time, locality, measurement, causation, and perhaps even what the basic entities are. Partial reforms are punished because they break successful predictions before they generate compensating explanatory gains. That is the signature of lock-in.

From this perspective, a successful unification would need to explain not just both sets of equations, but **why each theory is such a robust equilibrium approximation** in its domain. It would need to recover:

1. **Classical spacetime dynamics** — why smooth geometry and Einstein’s field equations emerge.
2. **Quantum phenomena** — interference, entanglement, discreteness of outcomes or their effective appearance, and the statistical structure of quantum predictions.
3. **The relation between geometry and information/matter** — why energy, causation, and perhaps entanglement are tied to spacetime structure.
4. **The domains of validity** — why GR works when quantum gravitational effects are negligible, and QM works when background geometry can be treated as fixed.
5. **Singular and extreme regimes** — black holes, early-universe conditions, and the fate of singularities, where the old equilibria fail simultaneously.
6. **The problem of time and observables** — how physical change is defined when spacetime itself is not fixed.

So the illuminating claim is: the unification problem persists because GR and QM are not just incompatible theories but **self-stabilizing explanatory regimes**. Progress likely requires a framework in which both are seen as emergent strategies of a deeper substrate, rather than one being straightforwardly absorbed into the other.
