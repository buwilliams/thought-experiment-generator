## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Quantum measurement yields definite outcomes because “measurement” is the point at which an irreducibly complex unitary evolution is coarse-grained into a stable classical record, and the specific outcome is not selected by a hidden shortcut rule but by the actual microscopic computation carried out in the interaction. What appears as “collapse” is our replacement of an intractable entangled evolution with one realized branch-compatible record.

From the perspective of **computational irreducibility**, the key mistake is to assume that if quantum theory is fundamental, there must exist a compact predictive story that maps pre-measurement state directly to a unique observed outcome. But in many systems, no such shortcut exists: the only way to know what happens is to let the dynamics run. A measurement interaction is plausibly such a case. System, apparatus, and environment become entangled in an astronomically high-dimensional evolution whose exact branch structure cannot be compressed into a simpler predictor that says, in advance and in classical terms, “this outcome will occur.”

This reframes the problem in two parts:

1. **Why definite outcomes?**  
   Because macroscopic definiteness is not a mysterious extra ingredient but the emergence of robust, decohered records from the full computation. Definite outcomes are those stable patterns that survive amplification and environmental monitoring. “Definite” means not metaphysically singled out by a collapse law, but dynamically selected as record-stable attractors under coarse-graining.

2. **What selects which outcome occurs?**  
   Not a further concise law over and above the evolution, but the fine-grained details of the interaction itself. The outcome is selected the way a particular turbulent eddy forms: by the actual unfolding of the underlying process, not by a closed-form summary. If one asks for a faster principle that extracts the unique result without running the interaction, computational irreducibility suggests that this demand may be misplaced.

What follows is a shift in explanatory ambition. The measurement problem may be less about finding a hidden selector and more about identifying the **minimal generative rule** that produces:
- entanglement,
- decoherence,
- amplification,
- stable records,
- and the Born-weighted statistics of outcomes.

On this view, the explanatory success condition is not “derive uniqueness from a simpler essence,” but “show how definite classical records are generated by the quantum computation, and why no observer can access interference between alternatives after that process.”

This does not by itself decide between interpretations. But it illuminates why the demand for a simple answer to “which outcome occurs?” may fail: the actual outcome may be a computationally irreducible fact of the interaction, while the law only fixes the space of possible outcomes and their statistical weights. The mystery then is not that physics lacks a shortcut, but that we expected one.
