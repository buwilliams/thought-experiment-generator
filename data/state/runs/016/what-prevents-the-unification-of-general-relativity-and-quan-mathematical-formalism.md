## Conjecture

Conjecture: The main obstacle to unifying general relativity and quantum mechanics is not merely technical incompatibility but a mismatch of mathematical roles: in quantum mechanics, the fundamental object is a state evolving on a fixed background with externally supplied causal structure, while in general relativity, the causal and metric structure itself is dynamical. A unified theory must therefore be formulated in terms of structures for which “state,” “geometry,” and “causal relation” are not separate inputs but different aspects of a single underlying invariant.

In more mathematical terms, quantum mechanics assumes a decomposition:

- kinematics: Hilbert space \( \mathcal{H} \),
- dynamics: linear/unitary evolution,
- observables: operator algebra,
- background: fixed time parameter and often fixed spacetime manifold.

General relativity instead begins with:

- manifold \( M \),
- dynamical metric \( g_{\mu\nu} \),
- diffeomorphism invariance,
- observables meaningful only modulo coordinate redundancy.

These are not two theories with conflicting equations; they encode reality with different symmetry principles. Quantum theory is built around linear superposition and operator noncommutativity. GR is built around background independence and geometric invariance under diffeomorphisms. The collision occurs because quantization usually presupposes a fixed arena in which degrees of freedom fluctuate, while GR says the arena itself is one of the degrees of freedom.

The key invariant problem is this: what is preserved when coordinates, geometry, and quantum description are all allowed to vary? Existing formalisms preserve too much of one side. Perturbative quantum gravity preserves quantum field-theoretic structure by expanding around a fixed metric, but then loses true background independence and becomes nonrenormalizable. Canonical approaches preserve background independence more faithfully, but struggle to recover time, locality, and empirical observables in the usual quantum sense.

So the conjecture is: unification requires a mathematical framework in which spacetime is emergent from a more primitive relational or algebraic structure, and in which both quantum interference and gravitational geometry arise as limiting descriptions of that structure under different coarse-grainings. The unified theory will not simply “quantize gravity” in the naive sense; it must explain why classical spacetime with Lorentzian geometry appears, why quantum theory has the Hilbert-space/probability structure it does, and how both emerge together consistently.

What must such a theory explain?

1. Low-energy limits: why Einstein’s equations and quantum field theory on curved spacetime are both accurate approximations.
2. Background independence: no fixed geometry inserted by hand.
3. Quantum coherence of geometry: whether superpositions of causal/metric structure are meaningful and how they decohere.
4. Observables and time: how to define measurable quantities when time is not external.
5. Singularities and UV behavior: black holes, the Big Bang, and the removal or reinterpretation of short-distance divergences.
6. Entropy/information relations: why horizon area counts information and how geometry encodes it.

In short: the barrier is a structural mismatch of symmetries and primitives. A true unification must identify the deeper mathematical object of which “quantum state” and “spacetime geometry” are merely different projections.
