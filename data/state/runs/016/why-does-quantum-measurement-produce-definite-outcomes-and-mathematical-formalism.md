What, then, selects which outcome occurs? The mathematical collision suggests a distinction between two problems often conflated:

1. Basis selection: solved structurally by the interaction. The measurement coupling defines the preferred decomposition — the pointer basis — as the set of states most nearly invariant under environmental monitoring.

2. Outcome selection: not solved by unitary structure alone if one demands a single actual result from the global state. Unitary evolution preserves the full superposition; it yields branching, not unique selection. Thus if only unitary evolution is allowed, “which outcome occurs” is indexical rather than dynamical: each decohered branch contains an observer-relative definite record. If instead one insists on one branch being singled out absolutely, an additional non-unitary rule must be added.

So the conjecture is: quantum measurement produces definite outcomes exactly when the dynamics create decoherence-defined invariant record sectors, and the only selection supplied by quantum structure itself is the decomposition into those sectors plus their Born weights \(|c_i|^2\). A uniquely actual outcome is not selected by the linear formalism; it requires either an added collapse law or acceptance that definiteness is branch-relative. Mathematics here reveals the non-negotiable constraint: linearity preserves superposition globally, so any account of unique selection must enter by changing the transformation rules, not by reinterpretation alone.
