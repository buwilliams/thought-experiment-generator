This also illuminates why low entropy at the beginning remains important without becoming mysterious in the old way. A low-entropy start may be less a “miracle for us” than a necessary boundary condition for a universe capable of long, nontrivial computation. If the universe had begun nearer equilibrium, the computation would terminate into triviality too quickly: no sustained gradients, no lasting records, no growth of structure. So the “specialness” may be the price of nontrivial generativity, not evidence of purpose.

This does not solve the problem completely; it relocates it. One must still explain why the laws and boundary conditions are of the nontrivial, gradient-supporting kind. But it blocks a bad inference: from “the beginning was special” to “the beginning was specially *for* us.” In an irreducible universe, hindsight can mistake generative fecundity for design. The explanatory task is therefore to find the simplest underlying cosmological rule and boundary structure from which irreversible complexity follows, while accepting that no compact prediction may exist that makes the result look unsurprising in advance.
