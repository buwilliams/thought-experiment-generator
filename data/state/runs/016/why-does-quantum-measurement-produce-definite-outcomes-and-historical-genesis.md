## Conjecture

Conjecture: the “measurement problem” exists largely because quantum theory inherited an older explanatory role for measurement from pre-quantum physics: measurement was assumed to be the act that reveals a pre-existing definite property of a system. Quantum theory replaced the dynamics of classical microphysics, but it did not fully replace that inherited concept of what a measurement is for. The apparent mystery arises when a new formalism is forced to answer an old question it was not built to preserve.

Origin story first. In classical physics, there was no deep distinction between system-evolution and measurement-outcome. A measurement was just another interaction that disclosed a value the system already had, perhaps with some disturbance or ignorance. “What outcome occurs?” meant “Which underlying actual value is revealed?” That conceptual package was carried forward into quantum theory almost unexamined.

But quantum theory changed the explanatory task. The formalism does not straightforwardly describe a world of simultaneously definite values awaiting revelation. It gives a structure for amplitudes, interference, and constraints on what can be jointly predicted. Measurement entered as a pragmatic bridge between the formalism and the classical language of laboratory records. The projection postulate and the Born rule then functioned as patch rules: they connected a theory of superposition to a world of definite reports without deriving definiteness from the same unitary dynamics.

So the hidden inherited assumption is: every successful physical theory must contain, at bottom, a mechanism by which observations disclose a single already-eligible actuality. Once that is assumed, quantum measurement looks like a special process demanding a selector: collapse, hidden variables, branching, etc.

What follows if we question the inheritance? “Definite outcome” may not be a primitive event needing a special trigger, but an emergent feature of interactions that produce stable, reproducible records in subsystems capable of acting as memories. Then the real explanatory problem shifts. Not “why does nature suddenly choose?” but “under what conditions do interactions generate effectively classical, persistent records, and why do agents embedded in those records encounter a single history?” This reframes selection as a structural problem about information flow, decoherence, record stability, and observer-location within the overall state.

This does not by itself solve interpretation. But it illuminates why the problem is so persistent: it is a hybrid of two incompatible legacies. Quantum dynamics was invented to solve failures of classical microphysics; the measurement concept was inherited from classical epistemology about observation. Their collision produces the demand for a unique outcome-selector.

So the conjecture is: the deepest obstacle is not merely physical but historical-conceptual. We ask quantum theory to preserve the classical function of measurement while abandoning the classical ontology that made that function natural. Progress will come less from adding one more collapse mechanism than from replacing the inherited concept of measurement with one rooted in information-bearing interactions, stable records, and the emergence of effective definiteness.
