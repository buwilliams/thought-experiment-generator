## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What looks like “fine-tuning” of the fundamental constants can be illuminated as an **equilibrium-selection problem in possibility space**: complex structure exists only in a narrow basin where multiple physical processes mutually stabilize one another, while outside that basin no single “parameter change” can recover complexity because the system falls into structurally locked simple regimes.

From the perspective of **Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis**, the key idea is not merely that life requires certain constants, but that **complexity itself may depend on a coordinated package of values**. Stable atoms, long-lived stars, chemical diversity, and nontrivial energy gradients form an interdependent system. If one constant shifts, the loss is often not local or compensable. For example, weakening one force or changing a mass ratio may destabilize nuclei, eliminate stellar burning windows, or collapse chemistry into triviality. In that case, no other single feature of the universe can “act alone” to restore complexity. The system is locked into a barren equilibrium.

This reframes fine-tuning. Instead of asking only, “Why these exact numbers?”, we ask: **What structural constraints make complex universes rare attractors?** The answer suggested by this lens is that complexity requires several subsystems of physics to sit in a mutually compatible regime. That compatibility is analogous to escaping a bad Nash equilibrium: if each variable is altered independently, the result is worse, and only a coordinated move across many parameters reaches another complexity-permitting regime. Thus the apparent improbability may reflect not arbitrary precision but **the fragility of cooperative alignment among constants**.

What follows is a useful distinction between two explanatory possibilities:

1. **True equilibrium lock-in:** complexity is possible only in very narrow, coordinated regions of parameter space. Then fine-tuning is not a superficial coincidence but a deep structural fact about the space of laws.
2. **Contingent apparent tuning:** our current models overstate sensitivity because we vary constants one at a time and hold deeper structure fixed. Then the “trap” may be methodological, not ontological; broader theories could reveal families of jointly varying constants that preserve complexity.

So this perspective does not by itself solve fine-tuning through anthropics, design, or multiverse hypotheses. Rather, it clarifies what needs explaining: **whether complexity-permitting physics is a locked equilibrium requiring rare global coordination, or whether the lock-in is an artifact of how we currently parameterize fundamental theory**.

The main illumination is that fine-tuning is best understood not as isolated numerical delicacy but as a **systems-level coordination problem among the constants**. Complex structure appears where physical processes are in reciprocal compatibility; universes outside that compatibility set cannot bootstrap themselves into richness by any local adjustment. That is why the phenomenon feels so resistant to simple reformulation: if it is real, it is a structural trap in the architecture of possible worlds.
