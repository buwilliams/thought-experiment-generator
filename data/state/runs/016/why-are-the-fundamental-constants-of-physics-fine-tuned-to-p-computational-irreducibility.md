## Conjecture

Conjecture: The “fine-tuning” of the fundamental constants is better understood not as evidence of hidden purpose, nor as a parameter puzzle awaiting compression, but as a computational boundary condition: only a narrow region of parameter space supports open-ended generative processes whose consequences cannot be shortcut and therefore can accumulate into complex structure.

From the perspective of computational irreducibility, the key fact is not merely that matter exists, but that the universe’s laws permit long chains of interactions whose outcomes must, in important respects, be unfolded rather than pre-collapsed into trivial regularities. A universe hospitable to stars, chemistry, evolution, and minds is one in which local rules generate rich higher-level behavior. That richness requires a delicate middle regime: constants must not force the system into sterile simplicity too quickly, nor into chaotic dissipation too completely.

What follows is a reframing of fine-tuning. The question is usually posed as: why do the constants take values that allow complexity? But under computational irreducibility, complexity-permitting constants are precisely those that place the universe in a generative zone where structure formation is dynamically possible and not analytically exhausted in advance. If gravity were too strong or weak, if electromagnetic or nuclear couplings shifted too far, the universe could collapse into uniformity, instability, or chemically barren simplicity. In such cases, the system would be “computationally shallow”: its large-scale outcomes would be rapidly constrained into a small set of trivial attractors. Fine-tuning marks the avoidance of these shallow regimes.

This does not explain why these constants were chosen in a metaphysical sense. Rather, it illuminates what is special about them: they sustain a world in which information can be preserved, transformed, and built upon across scales. Stars can cook elements, atoms can form varied bonds, molecules can store and copy patterns, and selection can explore possibilities over time. These are not separate miracles; they are successive layers of an irreducible computation.

So the strongest claim is this: apparent fine-tuning may be the signature of a universe poised not just for “life,” but for nontrivial computation in the broad physical sense — for processes whose future cannot be compressed away, and whose unfolding can therefore create genuinely novel structure. Complexity exists because the constants define a rule-set under which the universe computes richly rather than terminating in simple equilibrium.

The leverage of this perspective is explanatory modesty. It rejects the demand for a shortcut reason behind complexity-friendly constants and instead identifies the structural property they share: they enable deep generative unfolding. Fine-tuning is then less like a lock requiring a designer’s key and more like the narrow set of rules under which a world can keep producing consequences interesting enough that prediction, adaptation, and knowledge themselves become possible.
