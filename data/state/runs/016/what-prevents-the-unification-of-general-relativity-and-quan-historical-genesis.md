## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** What blocks unification is not merely a technical mismatch between two successful theories, but a historical inheritance from the different problems they were built to solve. General relativity was created to explain gravitation as the structure of spacetime itself, replacing force-based pictures with geometry. Quantum mechanics was created to explain discreteness, interference, and atomic stability, replacing classical trajectories with probabilistic amplitudes and operator structure. Each theory then treated as fundamental what had only been elevated to solve its own founding problem: GR reified smooth dynamical spacetime; QM reified a fixed kinematic arena in which states evolve. Their apparent incompatibility is the collision of these unexamined inheritances.

On this view, the deepest obstacle is not “gravity is weak” or “the equations diverge,” though those are symptoms. It is that each theory universalized a local success. GR solved the prior problem of Newtonian action-at-a-distance and the equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass by making geometry dynamical. QM solved the prior problem of classical failure in the microscopic domain by making observables, superposition, and quantization fundamental against an effectively fixed background. When combined naively, each demands jurisdiction over what the other had held fixed: GR says spacetime is a physical participant; QM says physical systems require a definite structure of states, observables, and evolution. The resulting crisis appears in nonrenormalizability, the problem of time, black hole information, and the difficulty of defining local observables in quantum gravity.

What this illuminates is that a unified theory probably cannot be a mere quantization of GR or a gravitational extension of standard QM in their inherited forms. It must explain why both smooth spacetime and quantum field-theoretic locality work so well as approximations, while showing which of their “fundamentals” are emergent. In historical terms, it must solve the prior problems both theories solved *without* preserving the unnoticed scaffolding they brought along.

So a unified theory would need to explain at least five things:

1. **Why spacetime is dynamical yet apparently continuous** at accessible scales.
2. **Why quantum phenomena are universal**, including for gravitation, without assuming a fixed background structure.
3. **How classical spacetime and Einstein’s equations emerge** from deeper degrees of freedom.
4. **How locality, causality, and time arise or are revised**, especially where current formalisms break down.
5. **How information behaves in gravitating systems**, including black holes, horizons, and entropy.

The likely lesson of the origin story is that “spacetime” and “quantum state” are both effective concepts, not final furniture. Unification will come from a framework in which geometry and quantumness are co-emergent from a deeper informational or relational structure, rather than one being forced into the conceptual mold of the other. The barrier is therefore philosophical as much as mathematical: we are still trying to preserve two historical solutions after the problems that defined their terms have changed.
