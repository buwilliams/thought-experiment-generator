## Conjecture

Conjecture: what looks like “fine-tuning” may be illuminated by treating stable complexity as the outcome of a repeated-selection process over possible law-sets, where only universes whose dynamics make structure persist become “visible” to inquiry. On this view, the constants need not be explained as benevolent calibration; they may be the equilibrium conditions of a system in which only complexity-supporting regimes generate observers, memory, and therefore questions.

From the repeated-interaction perspective, cooperation does not arise from virtue but from a game structure in which present actions affect future payoffs through visibility, memory, and response. Translating that into cosmology: complex structures require something analogous to a long shadow of the future. Matter must interact repeatedly and reliably enough for atoms, stars, chemistry, and organisms to form. If the constants were slightly different, interactions might become too violent, too short-lived, or too insensitive to prior states for higher-order organization to accumulate.

In that sense, the “cooperation” here is not moral but dynamical. Forces, particles, and initial conditions must participate in a regime where:
1. interactions are repeated rather than one-shot,
2. outcomes are legible in persistent records,
3. systems retain memory of prior states,
4. deviations have consequences that propagate rather than instantly wash out.

A universe with no stable nuclei, no long-lived stars, or no reliable chemical bonds is like a one-shot game: no durable reputation, no compounding, no future to coordinate around. Complexity collapses because there is no mechanism by which partial structure can survive long enough to be built upon. By contrast, our universe’s constants allow feedback loops across scales: stars manufacture elements, chemistry stores information, biology exploits memory, and cognition models consequences. These are exactly the kinds of structures repeated games reward.

What follows is a reframing of the fine-tuning question. Instead of asking, “Why were the constants chosen so precisely?” ask, “What ranges of constants permit interactions with enough persistence, legibility, and downstream consequence for complexity to become self-amplifying?” Fine-tuning then names the narrowness of the cooperative regime in parameter space: most law-sets may destroy the possibility of cumulative organization because they shorten time horizons or erase the record of interactions.

This perspective does not solve the metaphysical question of why these constants exist rather than others. But it illuminates why complexity depends on them: complex structure is what appears when the universe’s basic rules make future-sensitive accumulation possible. Fine-tuning, on this conjecture, is the condition for a cosmos in which the “payoff-maximizing strategy” for matter is not immediate dissipation but durable organization.
