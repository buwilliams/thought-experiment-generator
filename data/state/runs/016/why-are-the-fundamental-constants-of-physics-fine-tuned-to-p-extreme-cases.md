## Conjecture

Conjecture: Fine-tuning is what any world must look like when it is examined at the boundary between chaos and sterility.

Push the problem to the extremes. If the fundamental constants are taken toward one limit, interactions become too weak, too diffuse, too slow, or too unstable to preserve structure: matter does not bind, stars do not ignite properly, chemistry thins out, complexity never bootstraps. Push them toward the opposite limit, and interactions become too strong, too rapid, or too dominant: matter collapses too efficiently, stars burn too violently, nuclei overbind, and the space of durable intermediate-scale structures disappears. In both directions, the system loses what complexity requires: a hierarchy of semi-stable levels.

That suggests the key variable is not “life” or “observers” specifically, but the existence of a broad middle regime in which multiple scales can coexist: particles, nuclei, atoms, molecules, stars, long-lived energy gradients, and error-correcting structures. Complexity needs neither maximal order nor maximal freedom. It needs bottlenecks, delays, partial bindings, and imperfect isolation — enough stability to store information, enough instability to transform it.

So the conjecture is this: the apparent fine-tuning of constants reflects the requirement that a universe contain a large recursively usable zone of metastability, and only a narrow region of parameter space has that property.

This reframes the issue. The surprising thing is not that “our” constants permit complexity, but that complexity is structurally fragile at the boundaries. At the limit of zero structure, nothing accumulates. At the limit of total lock-in, nothing explores. Knowledge-bearing systems — including chemistry, evolution, and minds — require repeated interactions with memory under nonterminal conditions. In iterated-game terms, the universe must have a long shadow of the future built into its physics: stable particles, persistent gradients, and reliable regularities allow successful structures to persist, recombine, and improve. If constants destroy repetition — by making everything decay too fast, collapse too fast, or never bind at all — cumulative complexity is impossible.

What follows is that fine-tuning may be less like an arbitrary coincidence and more like a generic boundary condition for worlds capable of open-ended knowledge growth. A universe hospitable to complex structure must sit in a narrow corridor where competing processes constrain but do not extinguish one another.

This does not by itself explain why these constants have those values. But it illuminates what any explanation must account for: not merely carbon or life, but the existence of a deep, multiscale architecture of retained information. Fine-tuning is the signature of a world balanced not for comfort, but for the possibility of cumulative structure.
