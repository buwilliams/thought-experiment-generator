## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** What blocks unification is not merely technical mismatch but a conflict between **which structures are load-bearing** in each theory. In general relativity, spacetime geometry is explanatory infrastructure: change the metric dynamics and you must change what counts as gravity, inertia, causality, and energy flow. In quantum mechanics, the load-bearing structure is different: Hilbert-space evolution, noncommuting observables, compositionality, and probabilistic constraints are what make interference, entanglement, and measurement phenomena intelligible. Each theory treats as background what the other makes dynamical or problematic.

That matters because many apparent “unifications” are narrative shells: they redescribe one theory in the language of the other without preserving the constraints that make each successful. If spacetime is fundamental, quantum theory must explain why geometry can fluctuate, superpose, and still yield stable causal structure. If quantum information or amplitudes are fundamental, then the theory must explain why classical spacetime with Einstein dynamics emerges with the right universality, locality, and equivalence-principle behavior. In either direction, the non-negotiable test is: if one alters a proposed bridge principle, do the compensating consequences propagate through all the phenomena both theories currently explain? If not, the bridge was not load-bearing.

So the obstruction is that GR and QM succeed by organizing explanation around **different deep invariants**. GR’s core is diffeomorphism-invariant dynamical geometry; QM’s core is linear state evolution plus constraints on observable structure and correlation. Their combination generates pressures that are not superficial: time becomes ambiguous, locality becomes unstable, observables become hard to define, and the usual renormalization strategies fail because gravity is not just another field on spacetime but a theory of spacetime structure itself. These are signs of incompatible explanatory scaffolding, not just missing equations.

A unified theory would therefore need to explain, in one connected structure:

1. **Why spacetime geometry is dynamical yet compatible with quantum coherence.**  
2. **Why classical spacetime and Einstein’s equations emerge** in the regime where GR works.  
3. **Why quantum phenomena—interference, entanglement, measurement statistics—survive** in the regimes where QM works.  
4. **How causality, locality, and observables are defined** when geometry itself may be indefinite or emergent.  
5. **What replaces the separate notions of time** used in each theory, and why the replacement reduces to both familiar forms in the right limits.  
6. **How black holes, horizons, and singularity-avoiding physics work**, since these are where both frameworks are jointly load-bearing.  
7. **Why information is neither lost nor naively localized** if spacetime itself is not fundamental.

The best clue is that any genuine unification will make currently separate concepts co-constraining: information, geometry, and dynamics will not be independently postulated. If they can be swapped out piecemeal, the theory is probably only a patchwork.
