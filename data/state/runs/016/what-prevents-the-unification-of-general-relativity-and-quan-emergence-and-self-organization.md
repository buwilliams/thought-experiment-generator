## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
What blocks unification is not merely a mismatch between the *components* of general relativity and quantum mechanics, but a mismatch between the *interaction structures* each theory takes as fundamental. General relativity explains physics through a dynamical relational structure — spacetime geometry shaped by energy and momentum — while quantum mechanics explains physics through a different relational structure — state spaces, superposition, entanglement, and measurement-defined correlations. The conflict arises because each theory treats as background what the other renders emergent or dynamical.

From a systems perspective, this is why standard reductionist approaches stall. Trying to “quantize gravity” by treating spacetime like another field, or trying to derive quantum theory from classical geometry plus small corrections, assumes the parts are primary. But the paradoxes cluster at the level of system organization: nonlocal entanglement versus relativistic locality, smooth geometry versus quantum discreteness or indeterminacy, unitary evolution versus horizon/information loss puzzles, observer-independent spacetime versus measurement-dependent event structure. These are not isolated anomalies; they are signs that the feedback architecture connecting matter, information, causality, and geometry has been misidentified.

So the likely explanatory target is a deeper substrate in which both spacetime and quantum states are emergent summaries of a more fundamental network of informational or causal relations. On this view, geometry is not the stage and quantum fields are not simply actors on it; both are large-scale effective descriptions of an underlying interaction system. The key question becomes: what relational structure yields, in one regime, the smooth causal manifold of GR, and in another, the interference and correlation structure of QM?

A unified theory would therefore need to explain:

1. **Why spacetime geometry emerges at all** from a deeper non-geometric or pre-geometric structure.  
2. **Why quantum coherence and entanglement exist** as robust features of that same structure.  
3. **How classical spacetime locality emerges** from something that may be fundamentally nonlocal in its connectivity.  
4. **How measurement, observers, and records arise** without treating them as external exceptions.  
5. **Why black holes, horizons, and cosmology preserve consistent information flow** across scales.  
6. **Why both GR and QM work so well in their respective domains** as stable effective theories of one deeper system.

The unification problem is thus illuminated as a failure of ontology at the level of system boundaries: GR partitions reality into geometry plus matter; QM partitions it into states, observables, and outcomes. A successful unification will likely dissolve both partitions and replace them with a common generative structure whose emergent regimes we currently call “spacetime” and “quantum phenomena.” The obstacle is not just technical incompleteness, but that our present theories encode different architectures of interaction, and nature may be forcing us to explain the architecture itself.
