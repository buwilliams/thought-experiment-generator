**Conjecture**

Construct a scenario in which the only thing that matters is whether a “measurement” creates a **stable, copyable record**. Assume the quantum state always evolves unitarily, and assume no extra collapse law. In that scenario, “definite outcome” is not a further physical event added to quantum evolution. It is the **emergence of a branch-relative fact**: once a record is redundantly encoded, every subsystem that accesses that record is constrained into a mutually consistent history. Definite outcomes appear because knowledge has become structurally locked in through amplification and feedback. What looks like collapse is the local experience of entering one such knowledge-compatible branch.

What, then, selects which outcome occurs? In this scenario, nothing selects a unique outcome **globally**. The demand for a single selector is a carryover from a one-history picture. The actual selection event is **indexical**: an observer becomes correlated with one outcome-record rather than another. The question is not “what law picks one result out of all possibilities?” but “what structure makes outcome reports non-interfering and internally definite?” The answer is: decohered, redundantly propagated information.

This shifts the explanatory target. The real problem is not why one possibility becomes actual and others vanish, but why observers encounter a world of stable definiteness at all. They do so because only states that generate robust, transmissible records can function as experienced facts. “Measurement” is therefore the transition from reversible superposition to **effectively irreversible knowledge creation**.

What follows:

1. **Definiteness is epistemic-structural, not necessarily ontologically singular.**  
   A result is definite when it is embedded in a record network that supports consistent inference and action.

2. **Outcome basis is selected by recordability.**  
   The environment picks out the observables whose states can be stably copied; these become the experienced classical outcomes.

3. **The “which outcome?” question dissolves unless one assumes single-history ontology.**  
   If unitary evolution is kept, there is no further selector to find. What is selected is the observer’s location within the branching information structure.

4. **Probability remains the residue of self-locating uncertainty.**  
   Before record access, an observer can be uncertain which future branch-relative record they will inhabit, even if the global state is fully determined.

So the conjecture is: **Quantum measurement yields definite outcomes because measurement is the creation of stable, redundant knowledge, and what “selects” an outcome is not a global collapse mechanism but the observer’s correlation with one decohered record structure.** The apparent mystery comes from asking a one-world selection question of a process whose logic yields branch-relative definiteness instead.
