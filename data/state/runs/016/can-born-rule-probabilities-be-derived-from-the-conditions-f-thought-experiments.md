## Conjecture

Conjecture:

Assume a hypothetical Everettian universe in which all unitary branchings occur exactly as usual, but “records” only count as physically real in the relevant explanatory sense if they are stable under further interaction, reproducible by multiple subsystems, and mutually consistent across time. This isolates the key variable: not branching itself, but durable record formation.

Now follow the logic. A branch with tiny amplitude is not merely “less weighted” by stipulation; it has less capacity to participate in the redundancy and error-correction required for stable records. For a measurement outcome to become an observational fact, it must propagate into the environment, be copied into many degrees of freedom, survive decoherence, and remain recoverable by future observers. That is a structural requirement on record dynamics, not an extra probabilistic postulate.

If so, then the effective frequency with which outcomes become facts is controlled by whatever quantity governs record durability under repeated copying and environmental monitoring. The conjecture is that this quantity is the squared amplitude. Why squared rather than linear amplitude? Because stability and reproducibility are not properties of a single branch-label but of interaction capacities: copying, reinforcement, and resistance to interference depend on overlap structure and conserved norm, and norm enters dynamically as amplitude squared. A branch’s “measure” is then not primitive probability but its capacity to sustain a persistent causal footprint.

On this picture, Born-rule statistics arise because only branches with relative squared-amplitude weight can support long-run, intersubjectively accessible records at the rates observed. Branches violating Born frequencies are not impossible, but they are systematically worse at producing durable, redundant histories. They are record-fragile. In repeated trials, this compounds: worlds with anomalous frequencies contain observers whose memories, instruments, and environmental traces are less structurally coherent as a package. They do not disappear, but they fail to dominate the space of stable observational histories.

So the collision between the perspective and the problem yields this claim: Born probabilities may be derivable if “probability” is reinterpreted as a selection effect on which branches can become robust epistemic worlds. The crucial move is from asking “which outcomes occur?” to “which outcomes can persist as reproducible facts?” Under that shift, the Born rule is not assumed as a betting axiom or decision principle, but emerges as the law of durable record formation in a branching universe.

What this illuminates is a possible bridge between ontology and epistemology: amplitude-squared would matter because it governs not just branch size, but branch survivability as knowledge-bearing structure. The derivation would succeed only if one can show rigorously that redundancy, stability, and temporal consistency of records scale uniquely with squared amplitude. If that can be shown, Born probabilities are explained rather than postulated.
