## Conjecture

Conjecture: “Fine-tuning” is only a deep explanatory problem if the life-permitting values of constants are load-bearing within a tightly integrated explanatory structure; otherwise it is a narrative placeholder created by treating current parameterizations as more fundamental than they may be.

Applied through the load-bearing lens, the key question is not “Could the constants have been different?” but “What in our explanations actually breaks, and what must be rebuilt, if they are changed?” Some constants are clearly load-bearing relative to our current theories: alter them and stars, chemistry, or stable nuclei disappear. But that shows load-bearingness inside a model of complex structure, not yet at the level of ultimate ontology. The constants constrain outcomes because multiple explanatory strands—cosmology, nuclear physics, chemistry, thermodynamic gradients—jointly depend on them. Their apparent fine-tuning is therefore evidence of explanatory integration, not automatically of design, chance, or multiverse.

This shifts the problem. A good explanation of fine-tuning must make the constants less interchangeable by embedding them in a deeper structure where changing one element forces compensating changes elsewhere. If a deeper theory derives several “constants” from symmetries, selection rules, geometry, or dynamical history, then their values are no longer independent knobs. In that case, the impression of miraculous tuning weakens because what looked like many separately adjustable details were really consequences of one tighter framework.

By contrast, explanations that merely relabel the puzzle are not load-bearing. “They were set this way so we could exist” explains nothing unless tied to a mechanism. “There are infinitely many universes” is explanatory only if the multiverse model itself has rigid consequences and non-interchangeable structure; otherwise it is a shell that absorbs any observation. Even anthropic reasoning has limited load-bearing force: it explains why observers should not be surprised to observe observer-compatible conditions, but it does not explain why the underlying generative structure has the form that makes such selection meaningful.

What follows is that fine-tuning is best interpreted as a diagnostic of missing theory. It marks a region where our current description contains too many free parameters relative to the phenomena explained. The real target is not to “account for life” as a special endpoint, but to discover whether the constants are fundamental at all. If varying a constant always requires hidden compensations in other laws, then the constant was never independently real in the explanatory sense.

So the collision between this perspective and the problem yields a disciplined conjecture: the more genuinely explanatory future physics becomes, the less fine-tuning will appear as a coincidence among independent constants and the more it will appear as a consequence of deeper, mutually constraining structure. If no such deeper integration is possible, then fine-tuning remains real—but only then do anthropic or multiverse explanations become seriously load-bearing.
