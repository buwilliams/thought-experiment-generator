The Born rule is then illuminated as the unique candidate for **cooperation among branches, records, and future observers**: it makes the “game” of repeated measurement converge. In systems terms, they would fail to support a steady state of reproducible evidence. So the conjecture is stronger than “high-amplitude branches are seen more often.” It is that **empirical objectivity itself**—the existence of durable facts recoverable by multiple observers over time—imposes constraints that single out Born-weighted branching. Probabilities are therefore not an extra axiom but a reflection of the conditions under which records can become **durable enough to matter in future interactions**.

What this perspective illuminates is that quantum probability may be less about subjective uncertainty and more about the **structural ecology of records**: the rule is whatever makes observed frequencies legible, persistent, and recursively confirmable in a universe of repeated measurement-like interactions. Under that pressure, the Born rule is not just convenient; it may be what stable reality-tracking requires.
