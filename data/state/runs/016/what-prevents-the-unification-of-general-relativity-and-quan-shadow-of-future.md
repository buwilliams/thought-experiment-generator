## Conjecture

Conjecture: The main obstacle to unifying general relativity and quantum mechanics is not merely technical mismatch but a deeper incompatibility in the “interaction structure” each theory assumes: quantum mechanics is built for systems whose alternatives, correlations, and measurement outcomes are legible across repeated interactions, while general relativity treats spacetime itself as the stage that makes such legibility possible. A unified theory must therefore explain how stable, shared, and recursively testable records can exist when the geometry that underwrites causal order is itself quantum.

From the iterated-interaction perspective, cooperation emerges when actions are visible, memory persists, and future consequences feed back on present choice. Physics likewise depends on repeated comparability: experiments must leave records, observers must be able to identify “the same” system again, and consequences must propagate in a stable enough way for laws to be inferable. Quantum theory is highly successful because it formalizes this regime of repeated interaction among systems with fixed background assumptions about time, causal ordering, and what counts as an observable interaction. General relativity is highly successful because it explains the structure of that background—except it makes the background dynamical, observer-dependent, and globally fragile.

The collision is this: in quantum mechanics, prediction depends on a framework in which amplitudes evolve and measurements generate outcomes that can be compared across trials. In general relativity, the structure governing “across trials” comparison—time, simultaneity, causal connectivity, even subsystem separation—is not fixed. So the issue is not just “gravity is hard to quantize”; it is that the conditions for legible, repeatable interaction are themselves put into play.

What follows is that a unified theory must explain more than graviton exchange or Planck-scale corrections. It must explain:

1. How classical spacetime emerges as a stable memory structure from deeper quantum degrees of freedom.
2. Why observers can recover persistent records and shared causal order despite underlying quantum indeterminacy.
3. How locality, causality, and measurement arise when spacetime is not fundamental.
4. Why black holes, horizons, and singularity-like regimes do not destroy the consistency of information across repeated interactions.
5. How the theory reduces to ordinary quantum mechanics where geometry is effectively fixed and to general relativity where quantum fluctuations of geometry are negligible.

This perspective illuminates the unification problem as one of making defection—here, loss of coherence, information, or causal legibility—costly enough that stable classical structure can emerge. A successful unified theory would show the mechanism by which the universe produces durable records and predictable causal relations from a regime where both matter and geometry are quantum. In that sense, unification requires explaining not only what reality is made of, but how reality remains mutually trackable enough for physics itself to work.
