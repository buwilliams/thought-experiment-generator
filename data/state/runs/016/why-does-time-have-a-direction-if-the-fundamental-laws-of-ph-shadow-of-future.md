## Conjecture

Conjecture: The “direction” of time is not something added to time-symmetric laws; it is an emergent asymmetry created by a repeated-update system in which records accumulate, traces persist, and later states are constrained by visible consequences of earlier ones. In that sense, the arrow of time is what it looks like when the universe has memory.

From the iterated-interaction perspective, the key question is not “why do the laws prefer future over past?” but “why is the system structured so that effects of earlier states are legible in later states, while effects of later states are not legible in earlier ones?” A time-symmetric micro-law can still generate an effectively one-way macrodynamics if repeated interactions continually write information forward into durable records: fossils, memories, radiation, entropy gradients, correlations in matter.

This makes the future and past structurally different. The past is a stock of accumulated records. The future is a space of not-yet-written possibilities. That asymmetry is enough to generate temporal direction at the level relevant to experience and thermodynamics.

What follows is that the arrow of time depends less on the equations alone than on the system’s boundary conditions and information architecture. If the universe begins in a low-entropy, low-record state, then repeated physical interactions will tend to spread information into more degrees of freedom, producing many visible consequences of earlier events. This is analogous to cooperation in repeated games: behavior changes not because agents become virtuous, but because actions become observable, memorizable, and consequential. Likewise, temporal direction appears when physical events are embedded in a structure where “moves” leave persistent traces that constrain later states.

So the illuminating claim is: entropy increase is the physical form of record creation and record dispersal. As interactions repeat, the world becomes more correlated with its own past. Broken eggs, aging bodies, and cooling stars are not just “disorder increasing”; they are systems whose prior states have become legibly encoded in many surrounding degrees of freedom. Reversing time would require not merely laws allowing reversal, but an extraordinary erasure or perfect coordination of all those records.

Thus time has a direction because the universe is not a one-shot event but an iterated process with memory, visibility, and path-dependent consequence. Time-symmetric laws permit both directions, but a low-entropy beginning plus ongoing record formation makes one direction dynamically stable and overwhelmingly salient. The arrow of time is the macroscopic signature of a system in which the past is preserved in traces and the future is not.
