## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it preserves a small set of **invariants** across domains while making the **error-correction process explicit**, so that the receiving domain is not merely redescribed but reorganized around a new, criticizable structure.

On this view, an analogy is legitimate not because source and target “look similar,” but because it proposes that certain relations are **load-bearing** in both systems. The productive core of analogy is therefore not transfer of content but transfer of **constraints**. If those constraints survive criticism in the target domain, the analogy has created new knowledge: it has exposed previously unseen possibilities, explanations, or problems.

What is the invariant set? Typically, not surface features, but structural roles: feedback, conservation, selection, bottlenecks, equilibrium, representation, error correction, etc. A good analogy says, in effect: *treat these relations as identity-preserving across contexts, and let everything else vary*. This is why analogies can remain legitimate under rapid conceptual change. Their identity does not lie in fixed imagery but in a compact structural kernel.

What follows is important. First, novelty appears because the target domain contains implications that were **not explicit in the source**. Once imported, the invariant structure interacts with local facts, yielding consequences no one had yet articulated. The analogy does not simply copy knowledge over; it initiates a new round of conjecture and criticism in a new system. The resulting knowledge is genuinely new because it depends on the target’s own structure, not just on the source model.

Second, visible error correction is essential. Institutions retain legitimacy by publicly distinguishing what must remain stable from what may be revised. Analogical reasoning works similarly. It must make clear: which cross-domain commitments are non-negotiable, which are provisional, and what would count as failure. Without this, analogy becomes rhetorical prestige laundering — a narrative shell with interchangeable detail. With it, analogy becomes a disciplined exploratory tool.

Third, this illuminates why some analogies transform fields while others mislead. Transformative analogies are those whose preserved invariants are genuinely load-bearing and whose misfits generate fruitful criticism. Bad analogies either preserve too much, freezing superficial resemblance into dogma, or too little, leaving only vague metaphor. In both cases, trust collapses because identity and adaptation are confused.

So the collision yields this: **analogical reasoning is a mechanism for institutional-style knowledge growth**. It maintains legitimacy by conserving a small, clear structural identity while normalizing revision everywhere else. New knowledge arises not from resemblance alone, but from the disciplined exposure of a target domain to imported constraints that can be publicly tested, corrected, and retained only if they continue to solve problems.
