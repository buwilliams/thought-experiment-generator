## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Mathematics is “unreasonably effective” in physics because both successful physical theories and successful mathematics are selected under a shared equilibrium constraint: only highly compressible, transformation-stable structures can survive as reusable knowledge. What looks miraculous is partly a lock-in between the kinds of regularities the world allows and the kinds of abstractions minds can keep improving.

From the equilibrium-lock-in perspective, the key question is not “Why does abstract math happen to fit nature?” but “What structural trap makes both physicists and mathematicians converge on the same forms without central coordination?” The answer is: no agent can make progress, in either domain, by relying on idiosyncratic descriptions. To predict, explain, or generalize, one must use representations that remain valid across many cases and under many transformations. That pressure pushes inquiry toward invariants, symmetries, conserved quantities, continuous and discrete structures, probability, geometry, and algebraic relations.

This creates a kind of Nash equilibrium of description. Physicists cannot improve theory by using less structured language, because such theories lose predictive power and portability. Mathematicians cannot indefinitely develop arbitrary abstractions, because branches that do not sustain rich inferential networks, generalization, and internal compression tend to become epistemically sterile. So both communities are structurally driven toward the same “load-bearing” abstract objects: groups, manifolds, differential equations, Hilbert spaces, tensors, category-like compositional structures, etc.

What follows is that the effectiveness of mathematics is not primarily evidence that math was secretly designed for nature, nor that nature is literally “made of math,” though either could be further argued. More modestly: **the overlap is expected once we see both enterprises as adaptation to stable constraints on knowledge growth.** Reality contains exploitable regularities; mathematics is the systematic exploration of possible regularity-preserving structure. When physics advances, it preferentially discovers phenomena that can be stabilized into explanatory form. When mathematics advances, it preferentially refines forms that support deep, non-local consequences. The overlap is therefore not accidental but equilibrium-selected.

This perspective also distinguishes genuine mystery from contingent failure. It is not mysterious that mature mathematics often applies in physics. The real puzzle is why the universe contains deep regularities at all, and why they are accessible to finite minds. By contrast, cases where mathematics seems not yet to fit some domain may reflect temporary disequilibrium: missing concepts, poor representations, or measurement limits, not proof that the alignment has broken.

So the “unreasonable effectiveness” is partly a misdescription. It feels unreasonable only if mathematics and physics are imagined as independent projects. Under equilibrium lock-in analysis, they are not independent: each is constrained to converge on structures that no successful knower can avoid.
