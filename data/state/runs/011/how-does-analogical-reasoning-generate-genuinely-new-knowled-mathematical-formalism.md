## Conjecture

Conjecture: **Analogical reasoning generates new knowledge when it constructs a structure-preserving map between two domains that exposes previously unseen invariants, and those invariants support new derivations in the target domain that were not expressible before the mapping.**

Mathematically, let two domains be modeled as relational structures \(A\) and \(B\), each with objects, operations, and constraints. A mere transfer of existing knowledge is just a labeling move: take a fact in \(A\) and restate it in \(B\). That adds nothing. But a productive analogy is closer to a **partial homomorphism** or **functorial correspondence**: it preserves some relations while ignoring others. The crucial point is that this preservation is selective. What is carried over is not content but **form**.

New knowledge appears when three conditions hold:

1. **Nontrivial structural match**  
   There is a mapping \(f: A \to B\) that preserves enough relations to make inference possible:
   \[
   R_A(x,y,\dots) \Rightarrow R_B(f(x),f(y),\dots)
   \]
   This identifies a shared constraint pattern.

2. **Asymmetry of articulation**  
   Structure that is explicit or tractable in \(A\) is only implicit in \(B\). So although the invariant “already existed” in \(B\), it was not available there as usable knowledge. The analogy changes the epistemic state by making latent structure manipulable.

3. **Generative closure**  
   Once the mapping is accepted, consequences can be derived in \(B\) by transporting proof strategies, conserved quantities, symmetry arguments, or transformation rules from \(A\). These consequences need not have been previously known in either domain *as statements about \(B\)*.

So analogy is not copying facts; it is a **change of representation** that enlarges the inferential reach of a domain. In linear algebra terms, two problems may be equivalent under a basis change; the basis change does not alter the underlying object, but it can make solvability, decomposition, or invariants visible. Likewise, an analogy can reveal that what looked like many independent phenomena in \(B\) are instances of one conserved structure.

The load-bearing constraint is this: if the mapping preserves too little, it is metaphor; if it preserves enough, it becomes explanatory. The novelty comes from the mismatch between **ontic structure** and **epistemic access**. Analogical reasoning exploits an isomorphism, homomorphism, or higher-order correspondence between structures to convert hidden constraints into explicit ones.

Therefore, the right mathematical picture is: **analogical reasoning is a structure-discovery operator**. It generates genuinely new knowledge not by creating new facts ex nihilo, but by identifying invariant relations across domains and thereby enabling new proofs, predictions, compressions, and interventions in the target domain. The “newness” lies in newly available consequences under a new structural description.
