## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Compression, understanding, and explanation are related but not identical because they solve different coordination problems over knowledge. Compression minimizes description length; understanding is having a generative model that supports reliable variation and criticism; explanation is a *load-bearing* compressed account whose structure rules out alternatives and lets others reconstruct why things must be this way rather than another. The key distinction is that explanation and understanding are often **underprovided public goods** relative to mere compression.

Why this lens helps: a compressed representation can benefit many people, but the work needed to build an explanatory structure is costly, non-excludable in effect, and its benefits are diffuse. Once someone produces a good explanation, many others can use it to think, teach, predict, and create further knowledge. But any one individual has an incentive to free-ride on shortcuts: summaries, slogans, heuristics, benchmark results, or black-box models. So a community can become trapped in a stable equilibrium rich in compression but poor in understanding.

What follows is a structural distinction:

- **Compression without understanding:** A short encoding that predicts or reproduces outputs but does not expose the constraints that make the phenomenon what it is. This is epistemically cheap and individually rational to consume.
- **Understanding without maximal compression:** A model may initially be more elaborate because it preserves causal or logical structure needed for counterfactual variation. It earns its keep by enabling criticism and extension.
- **Explanation as the bridge:** Good explanations often compress, but only by discarding what is not load-bearing while preserving what is. Their brevity is not mere shorthand; it is *structured economy*. If changing a detail breaks the account, that detail is explanatory. If details are interchangeable, the “compression” is likely just summary.

So the relationship is not linear — not “more compression = more understanding.” Rather:

1. **Compression is a possible symptom of understanding**, because discovering real structure often yields concise formulations.
2. **But compression can also substitute for understanding**, especially in social systems that reward outputs over transmissible insight.
3. **Explanation is the critical discriminator**, because it turns compressed representation into publicly usable knowledge by making the constraints visible.

The public-goods perspective illuminates why modern knowledge systems often overproduce compressed artifacts and underproduce explanations. Explanations require shared standards, pedagogy, and institutions that reward making structure explicit. Without those, individually rational agents choose the low-cost equilibrium: use compressed tools, defer to opaque expertise, and skip the expensive work of explanatory articulation.

So the deepest relationship is this: **understanding is private possession of a generative structure; explanation is its public provisioning; compression is valuable only when disciplined by explanatory constraint.**
