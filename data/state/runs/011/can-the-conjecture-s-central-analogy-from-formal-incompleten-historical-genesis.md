Why this helps: the phrase *self-knowledge* inherits a pre-formal origin. It came from problems about moral self-scrutiny, sincerity, and first-person authority; later it absorbed assumptions from theology and introspectionism, especially the idea that the self is somehow immediately present to itself. Formal incompleteness arose to solve a different problem: whether a system could finitely codify arithmetic and certify its own adequacy from within. If we import “self-knowledge” into that setting without cleaning up its ancestry, we equivocate between **epistemic access**, **semantic self-description**, and **proof-theoretic self-certification**.

So the non-metaphorical notion should be this:

- An agent has **self-knowledge** when it can form and evaluate propositions about its own internal states, inference procedures, or future outputs, using resources available within the same computational/representational architecture that generates those states and outputs.
- The agent is “self-knowing” not because it has privileged inner light, but because part of the system serves as a model of another part, with feedback.

On this definition, the incompleteness analogy becomes precise. If the architecture is expressive enough to encode arithmetic and to represent claims about its own derivations, then some truths about its own operation will not be derivable within the very framework used to represent them. In stronger terms: attempts at complete self-certification generate diagonal phenomena. What follows is a sharper taxonomy:

1. **Self-acquaintance**: direct access to current states (“I am in pain”) — not the relevant analogue.
2. **Self-description**: representing one’s own structure or behavior.
3. **Self-certification**: proving one’s own reliability, consistency, or total correctness from within.

The real incompleteness analogue attaches primarily to (3), and secondarily to rich forms of (2), not to (1). That is the hidden assumption exposed by the origin story: the traditional concept of self-knowledge bundled these together.
