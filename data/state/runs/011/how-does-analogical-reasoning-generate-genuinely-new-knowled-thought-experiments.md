## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when the source analogy is treated not as a similarity map but as a **hypothesis about shared generative structure**; the novelty comes from consequences of that structure that were not previously noticed in either domain.

To isolate the key variable, imagine a case where two domains are initially disconnected in content but are conjectured to share the same abstract constraint-system. Assume that this conjecture is real. Then the analogy is not doing the trivial work of carrying over known facts; it is asserting: *if these domains instantiate the same structure, then whatever follows from that structure in one should have counterparts in the other, including consequences nobody has yet examined.*

Suppose domain A is well understood and domain B is poorly understood. We notice not surface resemblance but a load-bearing relation in A: a feedback loop, conservation law, symmetry, tradeoff, or threshold effect. We then conjecture that B contains the same relation. If so, the analogy immediately generates candidate explanations and candidate problems in B: hidden variables to look for, failure modes to expect, interventions that should backfire, observables that ought to covary, limits that should appear. These are new because they were not contained in prior knowledge of B, and often were not explicit in A either. The analogy forces us to derive them.

The crucial point is that the transferable item is not a stock of facts but an **abstract explanatory object**. Once imported, it enters a new environment with different boundary conditions. That collision produces genuinely new implications. A feedback loop placed in a biological system does not behave identically to the same loop in an economy; but if the loop-structure is real, then the differences become sources of discovery, not disconfirmation. We learn which features are load-bearing and which were incidental to the original model.

So analogical reasoning is knowledge-creating when it functions as **structural conjecture plus critical elaboration**. It proposes that two apparently different systems are the same in an explanatory respect, then lets deduction and criticism unfold the consequences. Most such conjectures fail. But that is not a defect; it is how knowledge grows. A failed analogy can still reveal that a presumed shared structure is absent, which is itself new knowledge about both domains.

What follows is a sharper criterion: analogy is epistemically productive only when it risks error by committing to constraints. “X is like Y” is empty unless changing the supposed shared structure would change what we predict, explain, or attempt. When the analogy is load-bearing in that way, it can disclose possibilities no one had previously articulated.

Therefore, analogical reasoning does not merely transport knowledge across domains. At its best, it **discovers unseen consequences of abstract structure** by conjecturing that the same deep organization is present in more than one place. The novelty lies in those consequences, and in the criticism that reveals whether the shared structure is real.
