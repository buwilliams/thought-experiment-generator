## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Compression, understanding, and explanation are related but not identical because they solve different coordination problems between minds and the world. **Compression** minimizes description length; **understanding** is possession of a generative model that supports error-correction and transfer; **explanation** is the communicable form of that model, especially its load-bearing constraints. The equilibrium lock-in is that institutions, audiences, and even individual thinkers often reward compression without testing for transfer, so highly compressed summaries can substitute for understanding and crowd out explanation.

What seems like a conceptual overlap is partly a structural trap. In many settings, no single agent benefits from paying the extra cost to distinguish “short description” from “deep model.” Teachers are rewarded for clarity and speed, students for recall, organizations for legibility, media for brevity. So everyone converges on artifacts that are easy to transmit and score: slogans, formulas, compressed narratives. These often *look* like understanding because genuine explanations are usually compressive. But the converse fails: a compressed representation may merely encode patterns or memorized regularities without revealing why they hold, when they fail, or how to modify them.

This perspective suggests a sharper relationship:

- **Compression** captures regularity.  
- **Understanding** captures counterfactual structure: what would happen if assumptions changed.  
- **Explanation** exposes the structure in a form others can criticize and reuse.

The load-bearing distinction is key. If a statement is compressed but its parts are interchangeable without changing what it predicts or illuminates, it is likely only a summary. An explanation has internal necessity: alter a central assumption and the account’s consequences shift. Understanding is what lets a thinker navigate those shifts without starting over.

So compression is neither the same as understanding nor irrelevant to it. It is a **byproduct of discovering real structure**. Good explanations are often compressive because reality contains reusable patterns. But maximizing compression alone can produce equilibrium lock-in around opaque models, clever mnemonics, or elegant equations that nobody can adapt outside their training distribution. In that equilibrium, each participant rationally prefers compressed outputs because they are cheaper to produce and consume, while the system underproduces genuine explanation.

What follows is that the test of understanding cannot be “Can this be stated briefly?” but rather: **Can it generate and survive criticism across variants?** Can it predict novel cases, explain failures, and identify which details are indispensable? Explanation is the social technology that breaks the lock-in by making compressed structure inspectable. It converts private competence or pattern-matching into public, corrigible knowledge.

Thus the relationship is: **compression is evidence of possible structure; understanding is grasp of that structure; explanation is the criticism-ready articulation of it.** Confusing them is not just a conceptual mistake but a stable equilibrium produced by incentives that favor concise outputs over transferable insight.
