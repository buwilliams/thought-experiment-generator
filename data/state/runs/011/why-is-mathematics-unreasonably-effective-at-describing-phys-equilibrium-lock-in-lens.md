## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The “unreasonable effectiveness” of mathematics is not mainly a miracle of pre-adaptation; it is what we should expect once we see mathematics as a **non-excludable stock of abstract knowledge** whose benefits are **highly diffuse across problems, times, and domains**. Because of that structure, mathematics is systematically **underprovided by local instrumental incentives**, yet once created it becomes a general-purpose constraint language for describing reality. Its surprising physical applicability reflects the fact that both successful physics and successful mathematics are selected for **deep invariance and compressibility**, not for one another specifically.

More concretely: if a piece of mathematics captures a real abstract relation — symmetry, continuity, topology, linearity, probability structure, optimization, computability — then it is not “about physics” in a narrow sense, but about a **load-bearing pattern** that many possible systems can instantiate, including physical ones. Physical reality contains stable regularities. Mathematics is the study of possible structures with tight internal constraints. When a physical regime instantiates one of those structures, the matching mathematics suddenly looks prophetic.

This perspective illuminates why the effectiveness seems unreasonable. The production of mathematics is often hard to justify in advance because its benefits are **non-excludable and unpredictable**. A theorem proved for internal mathematical reasons may later unlock physics, engineering, cryptography, or statistics. Since no original producer can capture most downstream benefit, individual rationality would predict chronic underinvestment in “useless” abstract math. Yet civilizations still produce some of it — through curiosity, prestige systems, patronage, teaching, and intellectual traditions. So we observe a filtered set of mathematical ideas that survived criticism and accumulated despite weak direct incentives. Those ideas are disproportionately the ones with **high generative reach**.

What follows is that the puzzle is partly economic and epistemic, not just metaphysical. We notice “unreasonable effectiveness” because there is a background no-provision equilibrium we might have expected: only narrowly applied calculational tools should be funded. Instead, mathematics grows as a partially subsidized public good, and physics repeatedly draws from that reservoir. The surprise is evidence of **spillover from abstract knowledge production**, not evidence that the universe was written in mathematics in some simplistic sense.

The key structural claim is: **mathematics and physics cohere because both are constrained by reality’s discoverable regularities, while mathematics can explore those regularities in advance as abstract possibility space**. Where there are deep, reusable structures, abstract work done without application in mind can later become physically decisive. Thus the effectiveness is “unreasonable” only from the standpoint of short-horizon incentive logic; from a systems view of knowledge as a public good, it is exactly what diffuse-benefit structure should generate.
