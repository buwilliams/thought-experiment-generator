## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it creates a **strange loop between representational levels**: a structure first used to describe one domain becomes a tool for reorganizing another, and in that act the reasoner also reorganizes their own space of possible descriptions. What is new is not merely a transported fact, but a **new self-referential modeling capacity**.

Here is the collision of perspectives.

An analogy is often described as mapping known relations from source to target. That sounds conservative, as if knowledge only moves sideways. But at sufficient expressive richness, the mapping does more than preserve structure: it exposes that the **same abstract pattern governs both domains**, and this recognition changes the ontology of the problem. The reasoner no longer sees “source” and “target” as fundamentally separate; both become instances of a higher-order schema. That schema was not previously explicit. Its articulation is new knowledge.

The strange-loop element is load-bearing. In successful analogy, the mind does not just compare object-level features. It moves up a level to relations, then to relations-between-relations, and then returns downward: the newly abstracted pattern is applied back to reinterpret the original cases. The source teaches the target, but the target also retroactively teaches the source what the source *really was an example of*. Traversing the hierarchy returns you to where you started, but with a transformed description. That recursive redescription is the generative step.

So the novelty does not come from “copying” content. It comes from **closing a loop between model and modeled**. Once the analogy is grasped, the thinker has built a representation that includes its own criteria of transferability: “this is the kind of structure that can recur elsewhere.” That is a self-referential gain. The system now models not just two domains, but its own ability to generate further mappings among domains of that type.

What follows:

1. **Analogical reasoning is creative when it yields a new abstraction layer.**  
   If no new layer appears, the analogy is merely mnemonic or pedagogical.

2. **The strongest evidence of genuine novelty is bidirectional re-description.**  
   A good analogy changes understanding of both source and target, not just one.

3. **Analogies can discover, not merely decorate, because abstract structure is real.**  
   They reveal previously unformulated invariants across domains.

4. **Self-awareness and discovery are linked.**  
   In making an analogy, a mind encounters its own representational habits as objects of thought; this recursive access is part of why analogy expands knowledge rather than just relocating it.

In short: analogical reasoning creates new knowledge when it forms a strange loop in which cross-domain mapping produces a higher-order pattern that reinterprets both domains and the reasoner’s own representational repertoire. The novelty lies in the emergent schema, not in the transferred content.
