## Conjecture

Conjecture: the analogy can be made precise if “self-knowledge” is not assigned to a component of the system but to a structured limitation in a system’s self-modeling feedback loop.

More exactly: define self-knowledge for a formal or cognitive system \(S\) as the class of truths about its own operation that can be stably represented within an internal model \(M(S)\) and used by \(S\) to regulate or revise itself without destroying the interpretation of that model. On this definition, incompleteness and self-knowledge line up only when the system contains a sufficiently expressive self-referential loop: representation of its own states, rules for drawing consequences about those states, and pathways by which those consequences feed back into the system’s behavior.

The point of contact with Gödel is then not “the self” in a metaphorical sense, but a structural fact: once a system can encode claims about its own provability or operation, there arise propositions whose truth outruns what the internal loop can stably certify. This is an emergent property of the representation–inference–feedback architecture, not of any sentence or module taken alone.

What follows:

1. A precise non-metaphorical notion of self-knowledge should be functional, not introspective.  
   It is knowledge a system has of itself insofar as it can internally model and exploit truths about its own dynamics. This avoids equivocation between phenomenological self-awareness and formal self-reference.

2. The relevant analogue of incompleteness is not “there are truths about me I cannot know” in a loose sense.  
   It is: for certain self-modeling systems, there exist well-formed truths about the system that cannot be established within the very self-modeling regime that gives rise to them.

3. The limitation is systemic and emergent.  
   It comes from closing the loop between object-level operation and meta-level representation. Add enough expressive power and feedback, and unresolvable self-ascriptions appear. Remove the loop or weaken expressivity, and the phenomenon disappears.

So the central analogy can be made precise if reformulated as follows:

Formal incompleteness is a theorem about the limits of closed self-representational systems. A non-metaphorical theory of self-knowledge should therefore study when a system’s internally usable self-model is necessarily incomplete relative to truths about that very system.

This also suggests a research program. Instead of asking whether agents “really know themselves,” ask which architectures of self-modeling generate unavoidable blind spots, undecidable self-descriptions, or tradeoffs between expressive self-representation and stable control. That makes the analogy load-bearing: both incompleteness and self-knowledge limits arise from the same interaction structure — internal encoding, inferential closure, and feedback from self-description to self-modification.
