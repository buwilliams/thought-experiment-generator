## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it is pushed to the boundary where the source analogy begins to fail. At that edge, the reasoner is forced to identify a deeper invariant structure shared by source and target; that abstraction was not explicitly present in either domain as previously understood. The novelty comes not from “copying” content across domains, but from creating a new explanatory object: a higher-level relation that constrains both.

If analogy were only transfer, then in the limiting case it would add nothing. A perfect analogy would merely rename known facts; a bad analogy would mislead. So the productive region must lie at the boundary between sameness and breakdown: enough structural overlap for projection, enough mismatch to force criticism.

Push this further. At one extreme, suppose two systems are identical in all relevant respects. Then analogy is epistemically idle: no new knowledge, only redescription. At the other extreme, suppose they share no structure. Then analogy is noise. Therefore, new knowledge appears in the intermediate-but-sharp regime where an attempted mapping partially works and then fails. That failure is load-bearing. It reveals which relations were superficial and which must be preserved for the explanation to survive.

What follows is that analogical reasoning is not fundamentally a mechanism of transport but of **variation and selection**. The analogy proposes a conjectural mapping; criticism exposes where the mapping breaks; repairing it requires formulating a more abstract principle. This repaired principle can then explain phenomena in both domains and often predicts features not noticed before in the target, and sometimes not even in the source.

So the generative step is this: analogy compresses two previously separate domains into a shared structure, and compression creates new questions. What in the source was treated as incidental may become necessary; what in the target seemed brute may become derivable. The new knowledge is the invariant plus its consequences.

From a systems perspective, analogy changes the topology of inquiry. It creates a feedback loop between domains: anomalies in one provoke revisions in the other. The leverage is not in importing answers, but in importing **constraints**. Once the target is seen as instantiating the same abstract pattern, whole spaces of hypotheses open or close.

Thus: **analogical reasoning generates new knowledge precisely by breaking at the limit.** Its epistemic power comes from exposing hidden structure through constrained failure. The source does not donate a finished truth; instead, the collision between source and target produces a new abstraction that neither domain, taken alone, had made explicit. That abstraction is the genuinely new knowledge.
