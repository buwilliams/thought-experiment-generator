## Conjecture

A genuine explanation is distinguished from a sophisticated redescription by whether it identifies a **generative interaction structure** whose alteration would systematically alter the phenomenon.

From the systems-emergence perspective, the key mistake in redescription is to stay at the level of the observed pattern while merely rephrasing it in more abstract, technical, or elegant terms. A redescription may classify, summarize, or restate what happens, but it does not show **how the phenomenon is produced by relations, constraints, and feedback loops among parts**. It leaves the explanandum structurally untouched.

So the conjecture is:

**An explanation is genuine insofar as it captures the load-bearing organization that generates the phenomenon, especially where the phenomenon is emergent; a redescription, however sophisticated, merely tracks the pattern without locating the system-level dependencies that make it occur.**

What follows from this:

1. **Component listing is not enough.**  
   Naming the parts, their properties, or even their local rules does not yet explain an emergent phenomenon. One must show why this network of interactions, and not another, yields the observed behavior.

2. **Predictive and counterfactual grip matters.**  
   A genuine explanation tells us what would change if feedback strengths, connection topology, delays, thresholds, or information flows changed. If no such counterfactual consequences follow, we likely have description, not explanation.

3. **Compression alone is insufficient.**  
   A concise model or elegant restatement can still be redescriptive. Explanation requires not just summarizing the phenomenon but exposing the mechanism of its production at the right level. For emergent phenomena, that “right level” is often not the part but the organized system.

4. **The test is structural intervention.**  
   If changing a purportedly explanatory element forces coherent changes elsewhere in the account and in the predicted behavior, that element is load-bearing. If it can be swapped for a synonym or a metaphor without loss, it was likely rhetorical padding.

This perspective illuminates why many explanations fail despite sounding deep: they redescribe a system-level regularity in psychological, mathematical, or philosophical language but never identify the **interaction architecture** that makes the regularity stable, repeatable, and transformable.

In short:  
**A genuine explanation earns its status by revealing the system’s generative constraints; a sophisticated redescription only mirrors the phenomenon in new language.** The distinction is sharpest when the phenomenon is emergent, because then the explanatory target is not the parts themselves but the pattern of relations that makes the whole do what none of the parts alone can do.
