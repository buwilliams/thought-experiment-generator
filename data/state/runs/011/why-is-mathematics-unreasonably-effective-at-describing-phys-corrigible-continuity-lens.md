Mathematics stays legitimate despite extreme internal change — new objects, new axioms, new formalisms — because these changes occur within visible practices of criticism and correction. Physical theories are allowed to change radically, but they must preserve contact with empirical criticism, explanatory reach, and coherence across transformations of perspective, scale, and measurement. The world itself appears to have stable invariants: symmetries, conservation laws, dimensional relations, dynamical regularities. A reality without persistent structure would not support knowledge at all.

So the “collision” between mathematics and physics is not between a free invention and an alien reality. It is between two systems that have both been selected for tracking invariants under change. Mathematics explores the space of possible structure; physics discovers which of those structures are instantiated, approximately or exactly, in the world.

The load-bearing part of this explanation is the notion of **invariance with visible error correction**. Remove invariance, and mathematics becomes a bag of arbitrary symbol games with no reason to map reality. Remove error correction, and physics loses the capacity to converge on mathematical forms that actually describe nature. Remove the world’s own stable structure, and neither enterprise could accumulate knowledge. These are not decorative details; changing any of them alters the phenomenon to be explained.

This also clarifies why mathematics often works **before** being designed for a physical use. A mathematical structure can remain legitimate internally — because it preserves mathematical invariants — long before a physical theory reveals that the same structure captures a real invariant of nature. The delayed match is not mysterious if both domains are organized around the same selection pressure: retaining what survives criticism across transformations.

So the effectiveness is “unreasonable” only if we expect legitimacy to require local design for immediate use. But institutions can remain trustworthy under rapid change by holding fast to a few deep invariants and making correction routine. Mathematics is exactly such an institution. Physics is another. Their recurring alignment is therefore not an accident but a consequence of a deeper commonality: reality and knowledge alike are structured enough that truth is found by tracking what can remain the same while everything else changes.
