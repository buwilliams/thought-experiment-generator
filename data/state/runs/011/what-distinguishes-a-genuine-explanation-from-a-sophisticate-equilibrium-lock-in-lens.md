## Conjecture

A useful conjecture is this:

**A genuine explanation differs from a sophisticated redescription in that it identifies the load-bearing constraints that make the phenomenon hard to vary — especially the incentive and structural conditions that would reproduce it even if people understood it perfectly and wanted better outcomes.** A redescription, by contrast, tracks the surface pattern in more refined language while leaving the phenomenon explanatorily cheap: you can swap in different wording, motives, or labels without changing what must happen.

From the given lens, this distinction becomes sharper. In collective-action settings, failure is often misdescribed as confusion, apathy, or weak commitment. But if the good is non-excludable and benefits are diffuse, there can be a **stable no-provision equilibrium** in which individually rational choices generate universal underprovision despite widespread preference for the funded outcome. That is explanatory, not merely classificatory, because it points to a structure that constrains behavior. If you change the people’s rhetoric, values, or awareness while leaving the payoff structure intact, the same failure tends to recur. The explanation survives variation; the redescription does not.

So the collision between the perspective and the problem suggests a criterion:

- A **redescription** says what the phenomenon looks like from another angle: “people are disengaged,” “institutions lack trust,” “coordination is difficult.”
- A **genuine explanation** says what system of constraints generates that appearance and what would have to change for the outcome to differ: excludability, concentration of benefits, monitoring costs, sanctioning capacity, feedback timing, etc.

This is why mechanism matters, but not in the thin sense of merely naming one. The mechanism must be **load-bearing**. If removing or altering it changes the phenomenon, it is explanatory. If the account can absorb almost any outcome by redescribing motives after the fact, it is not.

What follows is that explanation is not primarily about adding detail or sophistication. It is about locating the **counterfactual dependency** of the phenomenon: which features of the system are doing the real work? In public-goods cases, the distinction is especially vivid because moralizing redescriptions are easy and often psychologically plausible, yet they fail to explain persistence. Structural underprovision explains why failure can endure even under good intentions.

So: **a genuine explanation is one that reveals why the phenomenon is stable, why preferred alternatives do not arise automatically, and which constraints must be changed to produce a different outcome.** A sophisticated redescription may sound deeper, but if it does not identify those load-bearing constraints, it has not yet explained anything.
