## Conjecture

Gödel incompleteness suggests a structural underprovision of self-knowledge in formal systems, not merely a temporary lack of ingenuity or expressive clarity.

A useful conjecture is this:

**Any sufficiently powerful formal system faces a public-goods problem with respect to truths about its own consistency and completeness: the system can generate many consequences internally, but some globally valuable facts about itself cannot be “funded” by its own rules without changing the system.**

Why this lens fits: a formal system’s proof rules are local mechanisms. They determine what can be derived from given axioms. But statements like “this system is consistent” or Gödel sentences that effectively say “this statement is not provable here” concern the system as a whole. Their benefits are diffuse: if such claims were internally available, they would stabilize trust in the whole enterprise. Yet no individual derivation step can supply that global fact from within, because each step is constrained to operate under the very rules whose adequacy is in question.

So the limit is structural. It is not that the system is confused about itself, nor that it has failed to search hard enough. Rather, self-certifying knowledge is a non-excludable good for the system: if obtainable internally, it would support the entire proof economy. But the architecture of formal derivation creates a stable no-provision equilibrium. The system can prove many specific theorems, while leaving certain self-referential truths unprovable precisely because proving them internally would collapse the distinction between object-level reasoning and meta-level assessment.

What follows:

1. **Self-reference is a leverage point, not a glitch.** Gödel’s construction exposes a feedback loop where the system encodes claims about provability inside itself. This reveals the system’s structure, not a contingent weakness.

2. **Meta-level knowledge is not eliminable.** To know certain things about a formal system, one must often move to a stronger system or external standpoint. That is not philosophical embarrassment; it is the normal way the missing public good gets provisioned.

3. **No final closed circle of self-validation should be expected.** If a system is rich enough to represent arithmetic, there are true statements it cannot prove, and if consistent, it cannot prove its own consistency. The hope for complete internal self-knowledge is blocked by load-bearing constraints in the architecture of formal proof.

4. **Knowledge growth becomes open-ended.** New axioms or stronger frameworks can solve prior limits, but only by creating new meta-questions at the next level. There is no once-and-for-all internally complete settlement.

So Gödel implies that the limits of self-knowledge in formal systems are best understood as arising from structural underprovision: the very features that make a system generative also prevent it from fully underwriting itself from within.
