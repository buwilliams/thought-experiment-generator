## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** An explanation is not merely a compression of information, but a **legible invariant-preserving compression**: it earns the status of understanding when it reduces complexity while keeping visible the small set of constraints that survive error correction across changing cases. Compression without those invariants is only encoding; explanation is compression that remains legitimate under variation.

Here the institutional lens is clarifying. An institution facing rapid change can stay legitimate if it preserves a few clear invariants while making revision routine and visible. Explanation works similarly. A good explanation does not memorize every detail; it compresses. But it cannot compress arbitrarily. It must preserve what is identity-conferring in the phenomenon — the relations, principles, or generative structure such that if they changed, the explanation would have to change too. Those are the load-bearing elements.

So the relationship is:

1. **Compression** removes redundancy.
2. **Understanding** is possession of a compression that tracks the phenomenon’s load-bearing invariants.
3. **Explanation** is the public, criticizable form of that understanding: a compact account that says what must stay fixed, what may vary, and how errors would show up.

This perspective implies that the central issue is not “shorter vs longer,” but **which details can be discarded without losing the capacity to detect and correct mistakes**. A mere summary can be highly compressed yet fragile: when conditions change, it no longer guides action or prediction, and one cannot tell why. By contrast, an explanatory compression retains enough structure to survive change. It tells you what to revise and what to keep.

That makes explanation closer to a legitimate institution than to a zip file. A zip file compresses by exploiting regularity, but it does not distinguish essence from accident. An explanation does. It identifies a small set of stable commitments and permits adaptation everywhere else. In that sense, **understanding is compression with disciplined transparency about its own invariants and correction procedure**.

What follows is that the best explanations are often not the absolutely shortest. They are the shortest accounts that still expose their own error-correcting structure. If a compression becomes so extreme that its preserved constraints are no longer inspectable, trust in it becomes opaque — as with an institution that changes decisions but conceals its rules. Then apparent efficiency undermines legitimacy.

So explanation sits between brute detail and black-box compression. It is successful when it allows a community to reconstruct why the account works, where it would fail, and how it should be revised without losing identity. **Understanding, then, is not compression alone but stable orientation within change: knowing what can be simplified because one has grasped what cannot be abandoned.**
