## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A genuine explanation is distinguished from a sophisticated redescription by whether it identifies the **equilibrium-generating constraints** that make the phenomenon persist even when agents understand it and may dislike it. A redescription tracks patterns in the outcome; an explanation shows why those patterns are **stable under attempted deviation**.

From the perspective of **Equilibrium Lock-In Analysis**, the key test is: does the account reveal a structure such that no participant can unilaterally move to a better state, even though many or all would prefer a different overall arrangement? If yes, it has explanatory force. If not, it is likely only a higher-level paraphrase of what already appears.

This sharpens the distinction in a load-bearing way:

- A **redescription** says what happens, perhaps elegantly, perhaps with rich vocabulary, but leaves open the live possibility that the phenomenon is simply due to error, habit, rhetoric, or bad local choice.
- A **genuine explanation** shows why those apparent alternatives fail — why better intentions, clearer awareness, or isolated decisions do not dissolve the phenomenon.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem suggests that explanation requires more than compression or pattern recognition. It requires locating the **constraint structure**: incentive gradients, coordination problems, path dependence, information asymmetries, enforcement gaps, switching costs, legitimacy loops, or other mechanisms that reproduce the outcome.

What follows is a practical criterion:  
If an account cannot distinguish between:

1. a system that persists because actors are trapped in a mutually reinforcing equilibrium, and  
2. a system that persists only because people have not yet chosen differently,

then it has not yet explained the phenomenon. It has merely redescribed it in more abstract terms.

This also illuminates why many “deep” social, psychological, or philosophical accounts feel unsatisfying. They often redescribe the equilibrium from inside it. They name the norms, discourse, identities, or behaviors that recur, but do not show **why deviation is punished, why cooperation on reform fails, or why local rationality reproduces global undesirability**. Without that, the account lacks discriminating power: it cannot tell us what would break the pattern and what would leave it intact.

So the proposed distinction is:

- **Redescription**: maps the visible regularities of a phenomenon.
- **Explanation**: identifies the hidden stability conditions that make the regularities robust.

On this view, explanation is inherently counterfactual. It tells us not just why the phenomenon occurs, but why nearby alternatives do not. The most explanatory accounts are therefore those that reveal the **lock-in mechanism** — the structural reason the system resists reform. Where there is no such mechanism, the phenomenon may not need “deep” explanation at all; it may be contingent and corrigible. The explanation/redescription boundary is thus marked by whether the account uncovers a real equilibrium constraint rather than merely giving the phenomenon a more elaborate description.
