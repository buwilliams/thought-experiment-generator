## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it preserves a higher-level **control structure** across redescription, not merely when it copies surface relations from one domain to another. What survives the analogy are the **functional invariants**—constraint patterns, feedback roles, error-correction dynamics, optimization pressures, or compositional rules. New knowledge appears when those invariants, once seen as load-bearing in one domain, imply previously unnoticed possibilities, limits, or explanations in another.

This reframes the problem. If an analogy were only “knowledge transfer,” then its legitimacy would vanish under deeper explanation: we would discover that the target already contained everything and the analogy merely decorated it. But many successful analogies do not collapse this way. Their metaphysical mystique disappears—“nature is like a machine,” “mind is like computation”—yet something legitimate remains if the analogy isolates a structure that continues to do explanatory work after redescription.

The key distinction is between:

1. **Mystique that can be stripped away**  
   Surface imagery, metaphorical vividness, historical accident of the source domain.

2. **Higher-level structure that must survive**  
   The organization of dependencies that makes the analogy productive: e.g. conserved quantities, recursive decomposition, selection among variants, negative feedback stabilization.

An analogy generates new knowledge when it functions as a **conjecture about hidden invariants** in the target. It says, in effect: “If this target is governed by the same load-bearing constraints as the source, then further consequences should follow here too.” Those consequences are not contained in advance as explicit target knowledge; they are newly reachable because the analogy compresses many local facts under a single generative schema.

So the novelty does not come from mystical “similarity.” It comes from **re-description that reveals what matters causally or computationally**. Once the invariant is proposed, criticism can test it: if changing supposed source details leaves the target implications intact, those details were nonessential; if altering a structural relation destroys the explanatory yield, then that relation was load-bearing. This is how analogy can be epistemically serious rather than ornamental.

What follows is that analogical reasoning is a tool for **ontology discovery at the level of organization**. It helps identify which kinds, processes, or distinctions are real enough to support cross-domain explanation. It often creates knowledge by making a new problem legible in terms of an already-understood control architecture. That can reveal novel predictions, new interventions, and new impossibility results.

Thus, analogical reasoning generates new knowledge not by transporting content unchanged, but by proposing that two domains share a deeper **functional grammar**. When that grammar survives criticism and redescription, the analogy has done more than transfer knowledge: it has exposed a real abstract structure the target was instantiating all along, and that recognition expands what can be known.
