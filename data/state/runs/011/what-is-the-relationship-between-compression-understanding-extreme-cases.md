## Conjecture

Conjecture: At the boundary, compression and understanding diverge unless the compression preserves counterfactual structure; explanation is the special kind of compression that remains stable when pushed to extremes.

Push the problem to its limits.

At one extreme, maximum compression is a lookup key: a hash, a label, a slogan, “gravity,” “intelligence,” “market forces.” This is tiny, but it explains almost nothing. It lets you point, not understand. Why? Because if you vary the situation, the compressed token does not tell you what would happen. It lacks load-bearing structure.

At the other extreme, minimum compression is the raw totality of data: every observation, every pixel, every trajectory. This contains everything but explains nothing by itself, because it does not distinguish which regularities are real, which differences matter, or how unseen cases would go. Pure storage is not understanding.

So understanding cannot simply be “more compressed” or “less compressed.” The middle is misleading. The boundary shows that the relevant variable is not size of description but what survives under variation.

Now push further: imagine compression to zero length. Only tautologies survive: “things behave as they behave.” Perfect compression here destroys content. Now imagine expansion to infinite detail. You get a perfect chronicle of one world-history, but no principle separating law from accident. In both limits, explanation fails.

This suggests a structural distinction:

- Compression removes redundancy.
- Understanding identifies generative structure.
- Explanation expresses that structure in a form that can answer “what if?” and “why this, not that?”

The load-bearing test sharpens this. In a real explanation, details are constrained: if you alter the law, mechanism, or principle, many consequences change. In a merely compressed summary, details are interchangeable. “Evolution,” “selection pressure,” “efficiency” often function as verbal compression unless tied to a structure that forbids some outcomes and entails others.

So the relationship is this: explanation is compression plus counterfactual depth. It is a representation shorter than the data, but not arbitrarily short; its irreducible content is exactly the structure needed to recover a large space of possible and actual phenomena. Understanding is possession of such an explanation, or the ability to use it to navigate variations.

A good boundary case is mathematics. A proof is often a compression of infinitely many cases into a finite argument. But not every short proof explains. Some prove by trick; others reveal why the result had to be true. The difference is again structural visibility: explanatory compression tracks the source of necessity.

Therefore: the best explanations sit at a constrained optimum. They are as compressed as possible while still preserving the world’s causal or logical dependency structure. Compress further, and they become labels. Expand further, and they become archives. Understanding lives in the narrow band where compression captures not just what happened, but what must, can, and cannot happen.
