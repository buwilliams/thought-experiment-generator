## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Compression, understanding, and explanation are historically related but not identical capacities: explanation arose first as a way to *replace many disconnected observations with a generative account*; compression was later formalized as the reduction of description length; understanding is what you have when a compression is *explanatory*—that is, when it preserves the system’s load-bearing structure and lets you vary conditions without the account breaking.

This becomes clearer if we ask where these ideas came from.

“Compression” came into prominence from coding, storage, and transmission problems: how can the same content be represented with fewer symbols? That origin imports a hidden assumption: if two representations generate the same observable outputs, they are equivalent for our purposes. But explanation was not invented to save space. It was invented to solve a different problem: how to unify appearances, identify what would happen under changes, and expose why some outcomes are possible and others not. Compression inherits from this only a shadow of the original aim.

So compression can be a symptom of understanding without being identical to it. A lookup table over a finite domain can be highly optimized; a curve-fit can summarize data compactly; a latent representation can reduce dimensionality. But these may only compress *regularities in extensional form*. They need not capture the causal, counterfactual, or generative structure that makes the regularity intelligible. They solve the descendant problem—economy of representation—while potentially losing the ancestor problem—grasp of what is really doing the work.

Understanding, then, is what emerged when explanation proved capable not merely of summarizing but of surviving criticism. A person understands a phenomenon when they possess a compact account whose details are load-bearing: if you alter them, the range of phenomena explained changes, and the account makes contact with what would happen in nearby but unobserved cases. This is why understanding feels like “seeing why,” not just “having a shorter description.”

What follows is a useful distinction:

- **Compression without explanation:** shorter encoding, prediction, or categorization, but weak counterfactual reach.
- **Explanation with compression:** a principled reduction in arbitrariness because many facts follow from a smaller set of constraints.
- **Understanding:** the agent-level ability to use such explanations for error-correction, transfer, and invention.

The hidden assumption exposed by the origin story is that brevity is doing the epistemic work. It is not. Brevity matters because good explanations often remove independent assumptions. But the real epistemic achievement is not shorter description length; it is discovering which structure is non-optional.

So the relationship is asymmetric: good explanations usually compress, and understanding usually involves possessing such explanations. But not all compression yields explanation, and not all explanations are valuable because they are compressed. They are valuable because they reveal the deep constraints from which many surface facts follow.
