## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it **breaks an equilibrium lock-in between separately stabilized representations**: each domain, taken alone, is trapped in a locally coherent way of seeing that no single inference inside that domain can easily escape. An analogy creates a temporary cross-domain alignment that lets thinkers import not just conclusions but **new criticism and new generative constraints**, producing conjectures that were not previously available in either domain in that form.

The lock-in is this: within an established conceptual system, the standards of relevance, salient variables, and acceptable explanations reinforce one another. Because each move is evaluated by the same local standards, inquiry tends to remain inside the domain’s existing ontology. No individual inferential step can justify introducing a new structure that the system does not already recognize as meaningful. So even if a better explanatory organization exists, the domain is stuck in a kind of Nash equilibrium: any lone deviation looks unmotivated or illegible from within the current frame.

Analogy can disrupt this. When we say one domain is “like” another, the important transfer is not a stock of facts but a **relational structure**: what counts as a conserved quantity, a bottleneck, a feedback loop, a symmetry, a decomposition into parts and interactions. This imported structure changes what questions can be asked and what counts as a promising conjecture. The result is new knowledge if the analogy reveals a previously unseen constraint in the target domain and survives criticism there.

So the novelty does not come from “copying” knowledge from source to target. It comes from **recombining explanatory roles under load-bearing constraint**. If the mapped relations are genuinely structural, then altering one element forces corresponding reinterpretations elsewhere in the target. That is a sign the analogy is not decorative but epistemically productive. It has opened a path to conjectures the target domain’s prior equilibrium would not generate on its own.

What follows:

1. **Analogies are knowledge-creating when they change the search space.**  
   They do not merely fill a gap; they create new candidate explanations.

2. **Most analogies fail because they do not overcome lock-in.**  
   Superficial similarity transfers labels, not constraints, so no genuinely new criticism becomes possible.

3. **The test of a fruitful analogy is not resemblance but downstream irreversibility.**  
   After the analogy, can we now explain phenomena, pose experiments, or derive incompatibilities that were inaccessible before?

4. **Analogical reasoning is especially powerful in reforming entrenched fields.**  
   Where a domain is equilibrium-locked by its own categories, analogy serves as an external defector strategy: it introduces a coordinated alternative frame that no isolated internal move could supply.

Thus, analogical reasoning creates new knowledge by serving as a mechanism for **escaping representational equilibrium traps**. It is not a conveyor belt for old content, but a structural intervention that enables new conjectures and new criticism.
