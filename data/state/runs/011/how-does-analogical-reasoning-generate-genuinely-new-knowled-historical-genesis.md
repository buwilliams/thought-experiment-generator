## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge not by “moving” content from a known domain to an unknown one, but by exposing and then testing a more abstract structure that neither domain had previously made explicit. What is new is not the borrowed example, but the higher-order explanatory relation conjectured through the comparison.

From an origin-story perspective, the question itself inherits a misleading picture of analogy. It assumes analogy was introduced as a secondary cognitive tool for extending already-justified knowledge into unfamiliar cases. But historically and conceptually, analogy seems to arise to solve a deeper problem: how to think when direct deduction is impossible and observation is under-structured. It replaces the demand for certainty with a method of speculative structure-matching.

That origin matters because it reveals a hidden assumption in the problem: that knowledge must already exist in one place before analogy can operate. But if analogy evolved as a response to explanatory scarcity, then its role is not merely translational. It is generative because it proposes that two situations share a deep constraint, causal organization, or problem-solution logic — and that this commonality has consequences not yet recognized in either case.

What analogy inherited without examination is the prestige hierarchy of deduction over discovery: deduction is treated as “real” knowledge production, while analogy is treated as heuristic ornament. But that framing confuses justification with creation. Deduction preserves truth under fixed premises; analogy helps invent better premises.

The load-bearing feature is this: a successful analogy is not a list of similarities but a conjecture that certain similarities are structurally indispensable. If changing the mapped relation would break the explanatory success in both domains, then the analogy has identified something real. This can generate new knowledge in at least three ways:

1. **Latent structure extraction**: the analogy articulates a pattern implicit in both domains but explicit in neither.  
2. **Constraint export**: once a structural relation is noticed, unexplored consequences follow in the target domain.  
3. **Concept creation**: the comparison may require a new abstract category that reorganizes both source and target.

So the novelty does not come from the source domain alone. It comes from the act of conjecturing that two domains are instances of a deeper explanatory scheme, then criticizing that scheme against both. In this sense, analogy resembles theory formation more than information transfer.

Therefore: analogical reasoning generates new knowledge when it functions as a bridge to an unseen abstraction whose consequences are then testable. It is not epistemically parasitic on prior knowledge; it is a method for creating new explanatory objects. The real product of analogy is often not the conclusion about the target, but the newly discovered structure that reinterprets both source and target at once.
