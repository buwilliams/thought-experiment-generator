## Conjecture

Conjecture: the analogy can be made precise if “self-knowledge” is defined not as introspective acquaintance but as a public-good fixed point in a self-modeling system: a system knows itself to the extent that it can generate and sustain a correct, usable description of its own behavior that is available to its internal decision procedures. The incompleteness-style limit then appears when producing that description is structurally underprovided by the system’s own local incentives or proof resources.

More precisely, treat an agent, formal theory, or institution as a system containing many subprocedures. A self-description is non-excludable internally: once produced, many subsystems can use it. Its benefits are diffuse: improved coordination, error correction, prediction, and planning spread across the whole system rather than rewarding the producing module in proportion to its cost. So even if the whole system would prefer having an adequate self-model, individually rational local procedures may not generate one. This yields a no-provision equilibrium: not mere confusion, but structural underproduction of self-knowledge.

The incompleteness analogy becomes exact when the system’s self-description must be expressed within the same resource-bounded architecture it describes. Then there are true facts about the system’s own operation that no internally licensed procedure can derive while preserving consistency or computational tractability. “Self-knowledge” can therefore be defined as internally actionable self-description under a specified proof/computation budget. On that definition, incompleteness says not just “the system cannot know everything about itself,” but “the system may stably fail to fund the global work needed for adequate self-description.”

What follows is a clean distinction between two failure modes:

1. Epistemic incapacity: there is no internally available derivation or computation of the relevant self-fact.
2. Provision failure: the derivation exists in principle, but no subsystem has sufficient incentive or authority to produce, maintain, and circulate it.

The analogy is load-bearing only if both features matter. If we drop the public-good structure, self-knowledge collapses back into ordinary theorem proving or introspection. If we drop the formal constraints, “incompleteness” becomes a loose metaphor for psychological opacity. Kept together, they explain why self-knowledge is often globally valuable, locally neglected, and formally limited.

So the precise proposal is: define self-knowledge as the provision of correct self-referential models that are internally accessible and decision-relevant; define its limits via proof-theoretic and computational constraints on such provision. Then the central analogy is not between Gödel and vague inwardness, but between incompleteness and the structural underprovision of globally beneficial self-description in self-modeling systems.
