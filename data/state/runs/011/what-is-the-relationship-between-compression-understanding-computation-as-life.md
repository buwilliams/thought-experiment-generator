## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Compression, understanding, and explanation are three views of the same computational achievement: discovering reusable structure that lets a system predict, reconstruct, and intervene in the world with fewer independent degrees of freedom. Compression is the encoding side, explanation is the causal-generative side, and understanding is the agent-level capacity to use that structure across contexts.

From the computation-first perspective, any adaptive system must solve a compression problem. Reality presents a flood of variation; an intelligent or evolving system persists only if it can encode regularities compactly enough to guide action. Genes compress environmental history into heritable strategies; brains compress sensorimotor experience into models; science compresses phenomena into theories. So compression is not merely a convenience of representation but a substrate-independent requirement for successful computation under bounded resources.

But not all compression is understanding. A lookup table can compress data statistically without revealing the generative structure that produced it. Understanding appears when the compression is **load-bearing**: when the compact representation captures constraints such that changing one element changes what follows elsewhere. In that case, the representation is not just shorter; it is structurally coupled to reality. It supports counterfactuals, transfer, and error-correction. That is why explanation matters: an explanation is a compression that preserves the causal or computational organization of the phenomenon, not just its surface regularities.

This suggests a hierarchy:

- **Compression:** reduced description length by exploiting regularity.  
- **Explanation:** compression that models the mechanism or generative process.  
- **Understanding:** the ability of a computational agent to deploy such explanations for prediction, intervention, recombination, and criticism in new situations.

What follows is that the difference between mere pattern-fitting and genuine explanation is not that one compresses and the other does not, but that they compress at different depths. Shallow compression captures correlations; deep explanation captures the process that computes those correlations. The latter is more valuable because it is more portable across environments and more robust under perturbation—exactly what selection, learning, and science reward.

This also dissolves a common opposition between “succinctness” and “understanding.” Good explanations are often compressive because they replace many brute facts with one generative rule. But maximal compression alone can become opaque; an encrypted or highly entangled code may be short without being intelligible. So intelligibility depends on **computational accessibility**: whether the compressed structure can be decomposed and manipulated by the agent. Understanding therefore is not identical to minimum description length; it is compression matched to the inferential architecture of the knower.

So the relationship is: explanation is the kind of compression that earns understanding, and understanding is the capacity to compute with explanations. In evolutionary and cognitive terms, systems that discover such compressions gain adaptive advantage because they can do more with less—less memory, less data, less trial-and-error, and fewer arbitrary assumptions.
