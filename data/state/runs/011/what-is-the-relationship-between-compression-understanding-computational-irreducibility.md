## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Compression, understanding, and explanation are related, but not identical, because in computationally irreducible domains the shortest adequate description may be a *generative rule plus execution*, not a compact summary of outcomes. Therefore, understanding consists less in possessing a compressed prediction of behavior and more in grasping the minimal rule, constraints, and why no shortcut exists.

**Reasoning from the collision of perspectives:**  
Compression is often treated as the mark of understanding: if you can describe many facts with few principles, you understand them. That works in reducible systems, where broad behavior can be derived from compact laws without simulating every detail. But computational irreducibility places a load-bearing limit on this equation. For many systems, there is no substantially shorter route to the outcome than running the process itself. In such cases, lack of compressibility at the level of detailed prediction is not a failure of explanation; it is part of the explanation.

This reframes the relationship:

1. **Compression remains epistemically valuable, but shifts level.**  
   What can often be compressed is not the full trajectory of the system, but the *rule* that generates it. A cellular automaton may require long computation to know its state at step 10,000, yet still be explained by a tiny update rule. The explanatory achievement is locating the correct generative mechanism, not replacing its unfolding with a closed-form shortcut.

2. **Understanding is recognizing where compression stops.**  
   To understand an irreducible system is to know both what is simple and what is not: the underlying rule may be simple, while its consequences are not compressible. So understanding includes a map of compressibility boundaries — what can be derived cheaply, what must be simulated, and which regularities survive at higher scales.

3. **Explanation is stronger than summary.**  
   A compressed summary of outcomes can be shallow if it does not expose the generative structure. Conversely, an explanation can be minimally compressive at the observational level while still deep, if it identifies the rule, the invariants, and the necessity of stepwise unfolding. Explanation answers not merely “how can I state this briefly?” but “what mechanism makes these outcomes, and why can’t they be shortcut?”

4. **Predictive failure may be ontological, not merely practical.**  
   In irreducible systems, unpredictability is structural. This means explanation should not be judged solely by forecast compression. The right standard is whether it correctly captures the system’s load-bearing constraints: the rule, the feedback structure, and the irreducibility itself.

**What follows:**  
The common formula “to understand is to compress” is only conditionally true. A better formulation is: **understanding is compression of the generating principles plus accurate identification of the irreducible residue.** Explanation succeeds not when it eliminates computation, but when it tells us why computation cannot be eliminated.
