## Conjecture

Conjecture: Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it is not treated as a mapping between isolated properties of two domains, but as a transfer of interaction structure that, once embedded in a new system, produces novel consequences through that system’s own feedback loops and constraints.

On this view, the creative step in analogy is not “A is like B, so copy feature x.” It is: “The relational organization that explains behavior in B may also organize phenomena in A.” What gets imported is not content but a generative pattern — a topology of dependencies, bottlenecks, equilibria, amplification, tradeoffs, or control loops. Once that pattern is conjecturally imposed on the target domain, the target’s existing components interact under a new description. This interaction can yield implications that were present in neither domain as previously understood.

So new knowledge arises because the target system is not passive. It has its own stocks, flows, frictions, and hidden constraints. When an analogical structure is mapped onto it, the resulting combination is a new explanatory object. The analogy proposes: “If these relations are load-bearing here too, then these further behaviors should exist.” Those predicted behaviors, problems, or solution paths may be genuinely novel.

This explains why strong analogies are productive even when source and target differ materially. Their parts may be unlike, yet the same higher-order organization may govern both. For example, one does not learn from an analogy by transferring the substance of a fluid to traffic; one learns by transferring a pattern of flow, congestion, threshold effects, and local interaction generating global behavior. That structural conjecture then allows discoveries about traffic that were not already explicit in hydrodynamics and not previously known in traffic studies.

The system-level perspective also clarifies why many analogies fail. If the imported structure is not load-bearing in the target — if changing the mapped relations does not alter the target’s explanatory behavior — then the analogy is decorative, not knowledge-generating. Genuine analogical knowledge therefore depends on criticism: which relations are causally or explanatorily central, and what new predictions survive contact with the target system?

What follows is that analogy is a method of variation and selection in explanation. It creates new knowledge by reorganizing a problem under a conjectured interaction pattern, then exposing that reorganization to criticism. The novelty is real because the explanatory consequences are not just retrieved from memory; they emerge from the collision between imported structure and target-specific dynamics.

In short: analogical reasoning is knowledge-creating when it transfers generative structure rather than superficial similarity, and when the target system, under that structure, yields new consequences that must be discovered rather than merely read off.
