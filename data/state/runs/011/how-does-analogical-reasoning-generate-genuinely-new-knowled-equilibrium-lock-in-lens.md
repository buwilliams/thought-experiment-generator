## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it does not merely copy a known relation from one domain to another, but exposes a **shared structural constraint** that was previously underprovided as an explanatory resource. The novelty comes from making explicit a pattern that neither domain, considered in isolation, had fully articulated.

From the given lens, the key distinction is between **mere confusion** (“we have not yet noticed the answer”) and **structural underprovision** (“the relevant explanatory good is hard to produce because its benefits are diffuse and non-excludable”). A useful analogy often functions like a public good in inquiry. Once someone identifies a deep structural mapping between two domains, many others can use it; but before it is found, no individual fact or local incentive guarantees its production. Each investigator can rationally remain within their own field-specific concepts, leaving the cross-domain pattern unarticulated even though all would benefit from it.

So analogical reasoning is knowledge-generating when it overcomes this equilibrium. It does so by conjecturing that two systems share a **load-bearing organization**—for example, the same feedback form, conservation principle, optimization problem, or informational bottleneck. If that conjecture survives criticism, the result is not just transferred knowledge but a **new abstract object of knowledge**: an explicit structural relation. That relation was not previously contained in either domain as a usable explanation, even if in some weak sense it was “there to be noticed.”

What follows is that the epistemic product of analogy is not primarily a borrowed conclusion but a **new explanatory bridge**. This bridge can have consequences in both directions:

1. It can reveal unnoticed possibilities in the target domain.
2. It can reinterpret the source domain by showing which of its features were genuinely load-bearing.
3. It can define a more general problem-class of which both domains are instances.

That is where the new knowledge resides: in the higher-level abstraction and in the criticism it enables. A bad analogy transfers superficial resemblance; a good one identifies a structural constraint such that changing it would break the explanation in both domains.

Thus, analogical reasoning generates new knowledge when it supplies a missing public good in inquiry: a shareable, non-rival explanatory structure that many could use but that no local description was incentivized to formulate. Its creativity is not mystical and not merely mnemonic. It is the discovery of a real, criticizable abstract commonality that reorganizes what counts as relevant in both domains.
