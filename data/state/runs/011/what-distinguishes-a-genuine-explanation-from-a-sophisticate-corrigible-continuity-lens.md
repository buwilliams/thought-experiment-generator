## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A genuine explanation is what allows a system of thought or an institution to remain *the same while changing*: it identifies a small set of invariant constraints that generate the phenomenon, and it makes error correction possible by showing what would have to be revised if those constraints were wrong. A sophisticated redescription, by contrast, tracks the surface pattern with high fidelity but preserves no load-bearing invariants and offers no disciplined route for correction.

From the given lens, the key issue is legitimacy under rapid change. An institution remains legitimate not by freezing its outward form, but by preserving a few clear invariants while openly adapting everything else. The analogous claim in explanation is this: an explanation earns legitimacy when it preserves a stable core across varying cases and makes visible how corrections occur. If a supposed explanation cannot survive variation except by adding ad hoc qualifications, then it is not explaining; it is restating the phenomenon in more elaborate language.

What distinguishes the two, then, is not elegance, detail, or predictive fit alone, but **constraint structure**.

A genuine explanation:
- identifies **invariants** that remain fixed across changes in circumstance,
- specifies **generative relations** rather than merely redescribing outcomes,
- exposes **error-correcting pathways** — what evidence, criticism, or changed conditions would force revision,
- and contains **load-bearing parts**: altering them changes what the explanation can account for.

A redescription:
- remains close to the observed pattern but does not reveal why that pattern arises,
- can absorb anomalies by rephrasing rather than risking failure,
- lacks clear invariants, or treats every feature as equally important,
- and has few load-bearing commitments because its components are interchangeable.

In systems terms, explanation tracks structure; redescription tracks appearance. The former points to stocks, flows, feedbacks, and constraints that produce behavior. The latter simply names the behavior at a higher level of abstraction. That is why sophisticated redescriptions often feel persuasive: they preserve phenomenological identity without uncovering causal or generative identity.

Under this perspective, the deepest test is: **Can the account remain intelligible and legitimate while visibly correcting itself?** Genuine explanations can, because they distinguish core from periphery. They say, in effect: “These few things must be true; if not, this account fails.” Redescription cannot do this without collapsing, because it has no protected core beyond resemblance to the phenomenon.

So the collision yields this illumination: the difference between explanation and redescription is structurally similar to the difference between a legitimate adaptive institution and a merely image-conscious one. The real thing preserves identity through explicit invariants and routine correction. The counterfeit preserves appearance through rhetorical flexibility.
