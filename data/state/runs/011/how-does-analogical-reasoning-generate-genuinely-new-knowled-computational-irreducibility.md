## Conjecture

Conjecture: Analogical reasoning generates genuinely new knowledge when it compresses two domains into a shared generative rule, and the consequences of that rule in the target domain are computationally irreducible relative to what was previously known there.

On this view, an analogy is not primarily a transfer of stored facts from source to target. It is a conjecture that the two domains are produced by the same or closely related underlying structure. The creative step is the proposal of a new minimal program: “treat this target as if it were generated by that rule.” If the rule is good, it does not merely redescribe known target facts; it generates further consequences that were not already available in explicit form.

Computational irreducibility matters because, even if the source and target share a rule, the target’s behavior may still have to be “run” to be discovered. You do not get the consequences for free just by possessing the analogy. The analogy supplies a compact generative hypothesis, but the implications unfold only through exploration, derivation, simulation, experiment, or proof. That is where genuinely new knowledge appears. The novelty is not mystical transfer; it is the discovery of previously uncomputed consequences of a newly proposed structural identity.

This reframes the epistemic role of analogy. Its power is not that it licenses shortcut prediction. Often it does not. Its power is that it proposes which process to run. In a computationally irreducible world, that is a profound gain: not a closed-form answer, but a better generator of answers.

What follows:

1. Good analogies are judged less by superficial similarity than by load-bearing generative alignment. If changing the shared rule breaks many downstream correspondences, the analogy is knowledge-bearing.
2. Analogies are discovery mechanisms because they alter the search space. They let us import not conclusions but productive operations, questions, and experimental interventions.
3. Newness arises when the target domain contains consequences of the shared rule that had not been noticed, derived, or even posed before. The analogy makes them thinkable and investigable.
4. Failed analogies are still informative: they reveal where the supposed shared generator diverges, thus sharpening ontology and explanation.

So the collision yields this illumination: analogical reasoning creates new knowledge not by moving content intact across domains, but by conjecturing a common computational generator whose consequences in the target must then be unfolded. In irreducible systems, that unfolding is itself the source of novelty.
