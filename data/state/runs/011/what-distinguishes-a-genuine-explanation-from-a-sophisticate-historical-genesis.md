## Conjecture

A useful conjecture is this:

**A genuine explanation is historically and counterfactually anchored; a sophisticated redescription is not.** More precisely: an explanation earns that status when it identifies the *problem situation* from which the phenomenon, concept, or theory arose, the constraints any viable successor had to satisfy, and the specific way the proposed account solves those inherited constraints better than alternatives. A redescription can mimic depth by mapping the phenomenon into elegant language, but it does not expose what the thing is *for*, what prior failure it answers, or what would break if its key structure were altered.

Why this collision of perspectives helps: many concepts become opaque because their origin is forgotten. Once detached from the problem they were invented to solve, their current form looks natural, inevitable, or self-justifying. That forgetfulness creates a niche for redescriptions: they can restate the visible pattern in new terms without recovering the hidden constraints that made the pattern necessary.

So the discriminating test is not mainly “Does this sound deep?” but:

1. **What earlier problem did this account emerge to solve?**
2. **What older account or practice did it replace?**
3. **What assumptions did it inherit without criticism?**
4. **If we change a central posit, what compensating changes become necessary?**

If those questions have substantive answers, the account is likely explanatory. If not, it is likely ornamental.

This also reveals why some mathematically or verbally sophisticated frameworks fail as explanations. They can compress, classify, or predict a phenomenon while leaving untouched the generative question of why this structure, rather than another, came to exist. They redescribe the equilibrium while ignoring the selection pressures that produced it. In systems terms, they report system behavior while missing the feedback structure and historical path dependence that made that behavior stable.

The load-bearing difference is this: in a genuine explanation, details are constrained by the problem history. They are not freely swappable, because each element does work in accounting for both the phenomenon and its emergence from prior error. In a redescription, details are often interchangeable: one vocabulary can be traded for another with little loss except style.

So the conjecture is:

**What distinguishes explanation from redescription is not complexity, abstraction, or predictive fit alone, but whether the account reconstructs the lineage of problems and constraints that made the phenomenon intelligible.** Explanation shows why the phenomenon had to look roughly like this to solve something real; redescription merely shows how to talk about it after the fact.

That suggests a practical criterion: if an account cannot tell an error-correcting origin story — what was wrong before, what pressure forced change, and what hidden constraints shaped the result — then however sophisticated it is, it remains closer to description than explanation.
