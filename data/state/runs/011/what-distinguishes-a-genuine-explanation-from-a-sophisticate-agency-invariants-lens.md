## Conjecture

A useful conjecture is this:

**A genuine explanation is not one that says the same thing more elegantly or at a lower level, but one that identifies the functional invariants and load-bearing constraints that make the phenomenon possible, such that the phenomenon could be varied, manipulated, or embedded in other contexts without losing intelligibility. A sophisticated redescription tracks appearances; an explanation tracks necessity.**

From the given perspective, the key test is: **under deeper redescription, what must survive?** If a higher-level concept loses only its metaphysical mystique but retains its control role, then the concept remains legitimate. If the redescription dissolves the very structure that organized prediction, intervention, or counterfactual dependence, then the original concept was not explanatory in the first place.

So the difference is not “high-level vs low-level,” nor “mechanistic vs abstract.” A higher-level explanation can be genuine if it captures a real control structure. For example, in systems terms, feedback loops, bottlenecks, error-correction regimes, and information flows are often explanatorily prior to microdetail because they are the invariants that survive multiple realizations. Changing the substrate while preserving the organization preserves the phenomenon. That is a mark of explanation.

By contrast, a redescription often increases detail while decreasing constraint. It may translate “X happened because of motive M” into “X happened because neurons fired in pattern N,” but unless pattern N explains why this organization rather than another produced the outcome — and what would change under intervention — nothing has been explained. The phenomenon has merely been redescribed in a different vocabulary.

The load-bearing test sharpens this. In a genuine explanation, details are not ornamental. Altering them changes what follows. The parts are mutually constraining. In a redescription, details can often be swapped out with no loss except rhetorical flavor. That indicates that the account is parasitic on prior recognition of the phenomenon rather than explaining it.

So what follows is:

1. **Depth is not reduction.** An explanation is deeper when it reveals more general constraints, not merely smaller constituents.
2. **Legitimacy of higher-level concepts depends on invariance.** If agency, adaptation, memory, or meaning continue to mark real control structures across redescriptions, they are legitimate explanatory concepts.
3. **Explanatory progress consists in finding the level at which counterfactuals become disciplined.** The right explanation tells us what cannot be changed without changing the phenomenon.

Thus, the collision between this perspective and the problem yields a criterion: **an explanation earns its status by preserving and clarifying the functional organization that governs the phenomenon across redescriptions, while a redescription merely relocates the mystery.**
