## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a limiting case: imagine two explanations of the same phenomenon.

- In **Explanation A**, every part is load-bearing: remove or alter any detail and the explanation loses its ability to account for the phenomenon.
- In **Explanation B**, many details are interchangeable: terms, mechanisms, or examples can be swapped without changing its apparent explanatory force.

Assume both initially seem equally persuasive. Follow the logic.

If every part of A is load-bearing, then A has **high internal constraint**. Its components are not merely adjacent; they mutually determine one another. Each part solves a problem created by the others. This means A excludes many nearby alternatives. Its structure is tight because it is answering not just “what happened?” but “why this rather than something slightly different?” In systems terms, A has many dependency relations and little slack. A change in one part propagates through the whole.

By contrast, if B tolerates free substitution, then its parts are not strongly coupled. The details are decorations, not necessities. The explanation is therefore being carried by a more generic pattern at a higher level of abstraction, while the specific details are underdetermined. If swapping parts does no damage, then those parts were not doing explanatory work in the first place. B may still be useful, but its real content lies only in what remains invariant across substitutions.

So the key structural distinction is this: **load-bearing explanations are characterized by necessity relations among parts; freely swappable explanations are characterized by redundancy, slack, or mere exemplification**.

What follows is sharper. A genuinely deep explanation is not simply one with many details, but one in which the details are *forced* by the problem situation and by one another. This does not mean maximal rigidity is always better. Some domains genuinely require modularity. But where an explanation claims specificity, swappability is diagnostic: it reveals either that the account is overdescribed or that its true explanatory core is thinner than advertised.

The collision between perspective and problem illuminates a test: vary the details and see what breaks. If nothing breaks, then either:
1. the details were ornamental,
2. the explanation is really more abstract than stated, or
3. it is not explaining the phenomenon specifically enough.

Historically, many explanations inherit non-load-bearing details from analogy, rhetoric, or the original context in which they were invented. Over time these details can harden into seeming necessities. The hypothetical scenario strips that away.

**Therefore:** the structural property distinguishing the two kinds of explanation is not complexity but **counterfactual sensitivity**. In a load-bearing explanation, parts are bound by necessity such that nearby alternatives fail; in a swappable explanation, much of the content is contingent packaging around a smaller invariant core.
