## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is **structural before it is substrate-bound**: once a system has enough substrate to represent and recombine rich hypotheses, further gains in model size or compute produce diminishing epistemic returns unless the higher-level structure of conjecture generation, criticism, testing, and retention improves. At the limit, substrate without structure tends toward fluent error; structure without enough substrate tends toward sterile rigor. The decisive constraint is the architecture that turns capacity into knowledge.

Pushing to the extremes clarifies this.

At **infinite substrate**—unbounded parameters, data, and compute—but with today’s weak epistemic structure, what happens? The system can generate endless candidate answers, simulations, and correlations. But without better mechanisms for formulating bold explanatory conjectures, designing severe tests, preserving criticism, and promoting ideas for truth-tracking rather than reward-tracking, it does not asymptotically approach knowledge. It approaches **maximally persuasive overproduction**: an ocean of outputs with no principled way to distinguish deep explanation from adaptive mimicry. More substrate amplifies both signal and noise. The problem becomes selection, not generation.

Now push the other way: **minimal substrate** with strong epistemic structure. Such a system cannot know much, but what it does know can improve cumulatively. Even a small reasoner with good norms of conjecture and refutation can make genuine progress, because error-correction compounds. This suggests substrate is a threshold variable—below some level, nothing much happens; above it, structure dominates the slope of progress.

At the boundary case of **zero criticism**, no amount of compute yields epistemology; it yields only production. At the boundary case of **zero conjecture**, no testing regime can discover anything worth testing. So the key structure is not “evaluation” alone, nor “generation” alone, but the loop binding them: hypothesis creation, adversarial testing, memory of failures, and institutional promotion of surviving explanations. That is a systems claim: epistemic progress is governed by feedback structure, not raw throughput.

Historically, scaling solved an earlier bottleneck: getting systems to internalize enough world-regularity to be broadly competent. But success there may have hidden an inherited assumption—that more competence naturally becomes more knowledge. At the edge, this breaks down. A larger system can be more capable while remaining epistemically stagnant if its incentives and update rules reward plausibility, compliance, or benchmark performance over explanatory depth.

So what follows? We should expect continued substrate gains to matter, but increasingly as **enablers** of better epistemic institutions inside AI, not as the direct source of progress. The leverage point is above the substrate: mechanisms for explicit conjectures, tracked criticisms, test generation, error preservation, and selection for explanation rather than imitation. In short: compute fills the library; structure determines whether the library can think.
