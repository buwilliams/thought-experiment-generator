## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct a case where a system produces almost no polished answers, scores poorly on standard benchmarks, and attracts little attention — yet it reliably generates better *ways of finding and correcting error* than its predecessor. Assume this difference is real. Then the key variable is not answer quality but **error-correcting power**.

From that scenario, epistemic progress can be defined as:

> **An increase in a system’s capacity to generate, expose, discriminate among, and revise conjectures under criticism.**

This definition is independent of fluency, benchmark performance, and popularity because those are all downstream outputs that can improve without any improvement in error correction. A fluent system may merely compress convention. A benchmark-optimized system may exploit narrow regularities. A popular system may track social reinforcement loops. None of these imply improved contact with error.

If we follow the logic strictly, then measurement must also shift from output success to **counterfactual resilience under criticism**. A system has made epistemic progress if, compared with an earlier version, it:

1. **Produces more falsifiable conjectures** rather than more polished assertions.
2. **Surfaces its own assumptions and dependencies** more explicitly.
3. **Distinguishes look-alike explanations** by identifying tests that would separate them.
4. **Revises selectively** — changing what criticism actually targets, while preserving what survives.
5. **Transfers criticism across domains** by recognizing deeper structural errors, not just local mistakes.
6. **Improves search over problem spaces** — finding better questions, not only better answers.

So the relevant metrics are not “How often was it right?” but:
- How many nontrivial alternatives did it generate?
- How efficiently did it eliminate bad explanations?
- How much hidden assumption mass did it expose?
- How often did revision increase coherence without collapsing explanatory reach?
- How robust were its conclusions to adversarial reframing?
- Did it discover new tests, distinctions, or problem decompositions?

Historically, answer production became the proxy because institutions needed visible outputs: exams, publications, products, rankings. These are easy to count. But they inherit an old assumption: that knowledge is mainly a stock of correct statements. If knowledge is instead a **dynamic error-correcting process**, then progress is better understood as improvement in the structure that governs criticism and revision.

The collision between this perspective and the problem illuminates a sharper distinction:

- **Answer production** is a performance variable.
- **Epistemic progress** is a structural variable.

A system can rise in performance while stagnating epistemically, and it can progress epistemically before performance visibly improves. Therefore:

> **The best independent measure of epistemic progress is increased capacity for self-correction under novel criticism, especially where this produces better problem selection, better discriminations among explanations, and more principled revision — even in the absence of improved fluency, benchmark scores, or popularity.**

That is the conjecture.
