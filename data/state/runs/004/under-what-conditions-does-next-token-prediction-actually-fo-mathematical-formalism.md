\[
P(x_{t+1}\mid x_{\le t}) = P(x_{t+1}\mid s_t),
\]

So the key condition is **identifiability from predictive necessity**: if two distinct latent configurations \(z,z'\) with different domain invariants generate the same conditional distribution over all future token continuations, then next-token prediction has no pressure to distinguish them. In that case the invariants are not learnable from prediction alone. Conversely, if invariant differences are exactly what partition futures, then any near-optimal predictor must represent them.

This yields a sharper claim:

1. **Sufficiency:** Prediction is sufficient when the process is approximately Markov in a low-dimensional latent state, the observation map preserves enough information, and the domain invariants are necessary coordinates of the minimal sufficient predictive state.

2. **Failure mode:** Prediction does *not* force real latents when there exist degenerate solutions—surface heuristics, memorized correlations, or alternative compressed states—that match next-token statistics without tracking the invariant structure.

3. **Structural criterion:** The relevant threshold is not model scale alone but the ratio between

Thus the collision between mathematics and the problem suggests: **next-token prediction recovers real latent variables only when invariants are the unique or cheapest sufficient compression of history for forecasting**. Prediction does not magically reveal ontology; it reveals whatever structure is forced by the geometry of conditional distributions.
