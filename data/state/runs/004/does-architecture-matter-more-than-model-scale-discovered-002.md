## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The main bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is increasingly *structural rather than substrate-level*: not raw model size or compute alone, but whether the system is organized as an *agentive knowledge-creating process* — one that can form conjectures, criticize them, preserve problems, and revise its own error-correcting procedures. More compute can amplify performance, but it does not by itself produce the kind of person-level epistemic agency we care about.

The framing mistake is to treat “intelligence” as if it were only a mechanistic output of sufficient physical scale, like pressure from a larger engine. That imports a physics-style explanatory model into a domain where the relevant phenomena are reasons, problem-solving, and criticism. In physics, lawful causation is enough. In epistemology, what matters is whether a system acts on *internally represented problems* and can distinguish better from worse explanations.

So the key question is not “How big is the model?” but “What sort of system is this?” Does it merely transform inputs under external training pressure, or does it maintain goals, generate rival hypotheses, test them against constraints, notice errors, and change its future conjecture-making in response? A larger substrate may improve search breadth, memory, and fluency, but if the surrounding structure does not promote criticism and self-correction, the system remains closer to a powerful predictor than an epistemic agent.

Historically, current AI inherited a paradigm optimized for statistical performance: benchmark success, loss minimization, next-token prediction, reinforcement from fixed reward channels. Those solve important engineering problems, but they smuggle in an assumption that knowledge growth is mainly a scaling phenomenon. That assumption may now be the hidden constraint. Human epistemic progress did not come from larger brains alone, but from institutions and practices above the substrate: conjecture, argument, experiment, error-correction, and traditions for retaining criticism.

What follows is that “compute vs. structure” is partly a false dichotomy, but with an asymmetry: substrate is a necessary enabling condition; structure is the decisive organizing cause once a threshold of capability is crossed. Without enough compute, little is possible. But beyond that, progress depends on whether the AI is embedded in feedback loops that reward explanatory depth over mere fit, preserve unsolved problems, and let the system criticize not only answers but its own methods.

A useful test is this: if you scale the substrate, do you mainly get smoother interpolation and broader competence, or do you get systems that can originate problems, defend explanations, and deliberately revise their own standards of criticism? If mostly the former, the bottleneck is structural. The leverage point is not just bigger models, but architectures and institutions that convert capability into genuine epistemic agency.
