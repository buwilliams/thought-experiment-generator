## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Construct the limiting case: imagine a text stream generated by a hidden process with stable causes \(z\) that govern many different surface realizations. The next token is locally underdetermined unless the predictor infers something about \(z\). Now assume the model has enough capacity and optimization succeeds. What follows depends not on “prediction” in the abstract, but on the structure of the data-generating process.

**Claim:** next-token prediction forces learning of latent variables aligned with real domain invariants only when those invariants are *compressive causes of predictive uncertainty across diverse contexts*. More concretely, prediction is sufficient only if the true hidden variables are the simplest reusable explanation for many token-level dependencies that cannot be captured by shallow surface statistics.

A hypothetical makes the key variable visible. Consider two worlds:

1. **Invariant-needed world.** A latent variable \(z\) (speaker identity, physical state, syntactic role, game position) persists across long spans and systematically constrains many later tokens. Surface forms vary, but the same \(z\) explains them. Without representing \(z\), the predictor must memorize an enormous number of disconnected correlations. With \(z\), the data compresses. Here next-token prediction pressures the model toward a variable corresponding to a real invariant, because that invariant is the best error-reducing and compression-achieving internal organization.

2. **Shortcut world.** The same outputs are statistically predictable from local cues, stylistic regularities, retrieval of near-duplicates, or dense but non-causal correlations. Then prediction can succeed without discovering the real generative variable. The model may learn a sufficient statistic for forecasting that is not an invariant of the domain at all.

So the decisive condition is not merely that a latent variable exists, but that it is **identifiable through predictive necessity**: it must be reused across many contexts, persist enough to matter, and offer a lower-complexity account than any bundle of shortcuts. If alternative representations predict equally well at similar complexity, next-token training alone does not privilege the “real” one.

This suggests a sharper formulation of the truncated conjecture: prediction is sufficient only when the training distribution makes true invariants the dominant *bottleneck-resolving abstractions*. They must sit at the intersection of three pressures:

- **Persistence:** the variable remains relevant over enough context length.
- **Breadth:** it explains many otherwise separate token dependencies.
- **Compression advantage:** encoding it is cheaper than memorizing surface contingencies.

What is illuminated is a systems point: latent-variable learning is not a property of the objective alone but of the objective *coupled to the environment’s structure*. Next-token prediction does not inherently discover reality. It discovers whatever internal states most efficiently reduce predictive error. It reaches real invariants only when reality has been arranged such that the shortest path to prediction runs through them.
