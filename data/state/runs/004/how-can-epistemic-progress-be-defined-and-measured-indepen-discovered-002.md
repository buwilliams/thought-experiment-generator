## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Epistemic progress should be defined not as improved answer production, but as increased **agency in error-correction**: the capacity of a system to form, revise, and govern its own representations in light of criticism, rather than merely emit outputs that track external statistical regularities.

From the free-will lens, the key mistake is to force a person-level concept — *knowing* — into a mechanistic performance model designed for physics-like prediction. Fluency, benchmark scores, and popularity-weighted success all measure how reliably a system is *caused* to produce acceptable outputs under given conditions. They do not by themselves measure whether the system is acting as an epistemic agent: internally modeling a problem, distinguishing appearance from reality, entertaining alternatives, detecting its own errors, and changing its future conduct for reasons.

So the relevant distinction is not between “good” and “bad” answers, but between **externally elicited success** and **internally mediated understanding**.

On this view, epistemic progress occurs when a system gains more of the following:

1. **Counterfactual sensitivity to reasons**  
   It would change its conclusion if a relevant criticism were true, and in ways that preserve coherence across domains.

2. **Model-based self-correction**  
   It can identify why an answer failed, not just that it failed, and alter the generative structure behind future answers.

3. **Deliberative depth**  
   It can compare candidate explanations, track tradeoffs, and reject locally attractive outputs for globally better reasons.

4. **Error ownership**  
   Correction is not merely imposed from outside; the system can incorporate standards that let it detect mismatches itself.

5. **Transfer through abstraction**  
   What is learned in one context improves inquiry in novel cases because the system has captured explanatory structure, not surface patterns.

This suggests measurement should focus on **agency-like epistemic properties**, not output proxies. Useful tests would therefore ask:

- Can the system generate criticisms of its own view before external feedback?
- Can it revise a mistaken framework, not just a mistaken answer?
- Does it preserve improvements under adversarial rewording, domain shift, or social disapproval?
- Can it explain what evidence would change its mind, and then actually change?
- Does correction in one task alter behavior in structurally related tasks?

The historical genesis matters: benchmarks arose to solve the problem of scalable comparison, not the problem of identifying understanding. Their hidden inheritance is behaviorism: if outputs improve, cognition must have improved. But that conflates lawful input-output regularity with epistemic agency.

So the conjecture is: **epistemic progress is best measured as growth in autonomous error-correcting organization** — the extent to which a system is governed by revisable internal models and reasons-responsive criticism, rather than by mere success in producing favored answers. Fluency and benchmarks may correlate with this, but they are not its essence.
