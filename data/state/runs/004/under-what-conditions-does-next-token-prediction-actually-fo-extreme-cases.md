## Conjecture

**Conjecture**

Next-token prediction forces a model to learn latent variables that track real domain invariants **only in the limit where short-horizon surface statistics become insufficient for compression**. More precisely: prediction induces invariant-learning when success on the token stream requires maintaining internal state that is **stable across many local realizations, causally upstream of observations, and repeatedly reused across contexts**. If those conditions fail, next-token prediction can remain purely shallow.

Pushing to the boundaries clarifies this.

At one extreme, imagine a world of purely local text regularities: every next token is predictable from nearby correlations, stylistic habits, and memorized fragments. In that limit, there is **no pressure** to infer deeper latent structure. Prediction succeeds by fitting the surface. The learned “latents” need not correspond to anything real; they are just convenient encodings of token statistics.

At the opposite extreme, imagine a process generated by hidden variables whose effects are **distributed, delayed, and aliased**: many token sequences look locally similar, but diverge over long spans depending on persistent underlying state. Here, local heuristics fail. To minimize predictive loss, the model must infer something like the hidden causes, because only those support compression across all the different surface manifestations. In this regime, latent-variable learning is not optional but instrumentally forced.

So the key condition is a kind of **identifiability under predictive necessity**: the invariant must be the simplest reusable internal state that explains many otherwise disjoint predictive dependencies. Prediction alone is sufficient when the environment has a “bottleneck” structure: a low-dimensional latent state generates a high-dimensional stream, and accurate prediction across long horizons requires tracking that state.

This also reveals when the sufficiency claim breaks. If the invariant is real but **predictively irrelevant**, prediction will not recover it. If it is relevant but only weakly, and a huge family of superficial proxies performs nearly as well, prediction may learn a proxy rather than the invariant itself. And if the training distribution never visits the counterfactual situations that distinguish the true invariant from its shortcuts, then the invariant is underdetermined no matter how capable the learner is.

What follows is:

1. **Prediction is not generically sufficient.**  
   It is sufficient only when the data-generating process makes invariants the best compression strategy for next-token loss.

2. **Long-range, cross-context necessity matters more than realism per se.**  
   A latent becomes learned when it is repeatedly needed, not merely because it exists.

3. **Boundary cases diagnose the mechanism.**  
   If shrinking context length does not hurt much, invariants are probably not being forced. If performance collapses when long-range dependencies are perturbed, the model was likely relying on latent state.

So the collision between next-token prediction and invariants yields this: **prediction learns reality only where reality is the unique efficient route to prediction.**
