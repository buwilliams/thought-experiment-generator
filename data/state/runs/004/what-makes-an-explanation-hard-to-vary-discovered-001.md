## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
An explanation is “all load-bearing” when its parts are linked by **necessitating constraints**: altering one part changes what the explanation predicts, what it rules out, or what other parts must be to preserve coherence. By contrast, an explanation with swappable details is one whose parts are joined only by **thematic fit** or rhetorical continuity, not by constraint propagation.

From this perspective, the key structural distinction is not “amount of detail” but **coupling structure**. In a tightly integrated explanation, each component does at least one of three things:

1. **Constrains adjacent parts** — if this claim changes, others must change too.  
2. **Constrains the explanandum** — it narrows what phenomena the explanation can account for.  
3. **Constrains counterfactuals** — it implies that under altered conditions, different outcomes would follow.

If a detail can be replaced without forcing any compensating revision elsewhere, that detail is not carrying explanatory weight; it is ornamental or redundant.

So the test is: **Does variation propagate?**  
A genuinely load-bearing explanation behaves like a structured system with feedback: modify a premise, mechanism, or boundary condition, and the rest of the account must be reworked. A narrative shell behaves like a collage: details can be swapped while the apparent explanatory shape remains intact.

This suggests a more precise criterion: explanations differ by their degree of **internal compression**. In a strong explanation, many details are not independently chosen; they are jointly fixed by the need to solve the problem. That is why such explanations feel hard to vary. Their details are not arbitrary because they arise from the same underlying structure. In weak explanations, details are loosely attached and underdetermined; multiple substitutions leave the story “sounding right” because the account was never doing much explanatory work.

Historically, many bad explanations survive because they inherit the form of explanation — sequence, labels, mechanism-like language — without inheriting its constraint structure. They answer the social demand for intelligibility, not the epistemic demand for necessity. This is why origin matters: often a once-useful sketch becomes ritualized, and its details persist as inherited decoration.

What follows is that explanatory quality can be probed by **systematic variation**. Ask of each part: if this were different, what breaks? If the answer is “nothing important,” the part was not load-bearing. If changing it forces revisions across the account, then the explanation has real structure.

So the distinguishing property is:  
**Load-bearing explanations are networks of mutually constraining commitments; non-load-bearing explanations are narratives whose details are locally replaceable because they do not participate in a tightly coupled solution to the problem.**
