## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** An explanation is *fully load-bearing* when its parts are tied together by a small set of invariants and constraints such that changing any part either breaks the explanation’s predictive reach, destroys its coherence, or alters what phenomenon it explains. By contrast, an explanation with freely swappable details contains components that are not structurally coupled to those invariants; they function as decoration, implementation choice, or historical residue rather than necessity.

From this lens, the key question is not whether every detail is “included,” but whether each detail participates in preserving the explanation’s **explanatory structure**. The load-bearing parts are the ones that support:

1. **Counterfactual stability** — if this part were different, would the explanation fail to answer “what would happen if…?”  
2. **Constraint satisfaction** — does this part help satisfy the conditions that make the phenomenon possible or inevitable?  
3. **Cross-context transport** — does this part remain necessary when the explanation is applied to nearby cases?  
4. **Compression without loss** — if this part is removed, does the account become vague, merely narrative, or stop distinguishing this outcome from alternatives?

This reframes the issue as one of **structural dependence**. In a genuinely load-bearing explanation, the relation among parts resembles a proof, mechanism, or dynamical model: the components are not just present together but mutually constrained. Their necessity comes from the role they play in maintaining invariants. For example, in a physical explanation, conservation laws, symmetries, or causal bottlenecks are typically load-bearing; surface descriptions like color, naming conventions, or contingent sequencing often are not.

Historically, many explanations accumulate interchangeable details because they descend from storytelling, pedagogy, or local discovery paths. Such details may have helped someone *find* the explanation without being part of what *makes it true*. This is an important distinction: the genealogy of an explanation often leaves scaffolding inside the final account. Structural analysis separates scaffolding from support.

So the distinguishing property is not “specificity” versus “generality,” but **whether the parts are bound by necessity-producing constraints**. A detail is swappable when multiple variants preserve the same invariant structure. It is load-bearing when variation changes the explanatory topology itself.

A practical test follows: map the explanation as a system of dependencies, then perturb each component. If substitutions leave the same predictions, the same impossibility claims, and the same unification across cases intact, that component was never load-bearing. If perturbation forces widespread revision, it was.

Thus the difference between robust explanation and decorative account is the difference between **structure-preserving components** and **replaceable tokens**. The former carry the explanation; the latter merely accompany it.
