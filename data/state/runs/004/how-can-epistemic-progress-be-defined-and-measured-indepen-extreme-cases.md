## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Epistemic progress is best defined not by how often a system produces correct-looking answers, but by how its explanatory structure behaves at the boundaries: under extreme novelty, under adversarial perturbation, under self-critique, and under transfer far beyond its training niche. A system has made epistemic progress iff it has increased its capacity to *generate, preserve, and improve error-correcting explanations* across widening domains, especially where fluency and benchmark habits fail.

Pushing to the limit clarifies the distinction. At one extreme, imagine a system with perfect rhetorical fluency and benchmark optimization but zero ability to detect when its own framing is wrong. As novelty approaches infinity, its performance collapses into elegant imitation. At the other extreme, imagine a halting, unpolished system that can expose hidden assumptions, invent discriminating tests, and revise its own models when they fail. Even with lower surface performance, this second system is epistemically superior because it can enter error-correction loops that the first cannot.

So the relevant unit is not “answer quality” but **distance to breakdown under boundary conditions**. Progress is measured by how far the system can be pushed before it stops improving and starts merely paraphrasing prior structure.

This suggests four non-fluency metrics:

1. **Counterfactual robustness**  
   Can the system preserve the core explanatory insight when the surface form, context, or domain is radically altered? If not, it has patterns, not knowledge.

2. **Error-correction depth**  
   When shown failure, does it merely patch outputs locally, or can it revise upstream assumptions, ontology, and problem framing? Progress means failures become inputs to model improvement.

3. **Frontier extension**  
   Does the system enlarge the class of problems on which it can form testable, compressive explanations? Benchmark saturation without frontier extension is not epistemic growth.

4. **Generative criticism**  
   Can it produce new objections, new tests, and new rival explanations against itself? A system that cannot criticize itself is epistemically bounded even if widely successful.

Historically, benchmark culture inherited a behaviorist proxy: treat observable success as equivalent to cognition. But this confuses selection effects with understanding. Popularity-weighted success is even worse: it measures social propagation, not contact with truth.

At the limit, a definition emerges: **epistemic progress is the increase in a system’s capacity for non-arbitrary explanatory compression that survives criticism and improves through failure**. Measurement should therefore focus on the system’s behavior near failure boundaries, not at equilibrium on familiar tasks.

What this illuminates is simple: fluency, scores, and popularity are middle-regime phenomena. They conceal whether the system possesses the thing that matters — not answer production, but the ability to *undergo genuine knowledge growth*.
