## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** The current bottleneck for AI epistemic progress is primarily **structural, not substrate-limited**: more model size and compute continue to improve capability, but they are no longer the main load-bearing determinants of progress in *knowledge creation*. The decisive constraint lies in the higher-order system that governs how candidate explanations are generated, criticized, selected, remembered, and allowed to reshape future inquiry.

From this lens, the question is not “what helps?” but “what must remain true for epistemic progress to exist at all?” The load-bearing abstractions are not parameter count or FLOPs; they are **error-correction structures**. A system makes epistemic progress only if it can (1) produce varied conjectures, (2) expose them to nontrivial tests, (3) retain criticism, and (4) promote surviving explanations in a way that changes subsequent search. Without those invariants, scaling yields better interpolation, persuasion, or search throughput, but not robust growth of knowledge.

This reframes the historical role of scaling. Bigger models solved a prior bottleneck: insufficient representational breadth and weak generalization. That substrate expansion was real progress. But once a system can already generate many plausible continuations and weak hypotheses, the limiting factor shifts upward: whether the surrounding architecture distinguishes **explanation from correlation**, **criticism from scoring**, and **survival under testing from popularity in training data**. In systems terms, compute is a flow; epistemic structure is the feedback loop that determines whether output becomes knowledge or noise.

What follows is that many present AI gains may be **capability gains without proportional epistemic gains**. If conjectures are formed by next-token plausibility, tested only by benchmark fit or human preference, and promoted by reward heuristics divorced from explanatory depth, then the system’s internal ecology favors locally useful answers, not truth-tracking ones. More compute accelerates this ecology but does not alter its invariant. It scales production, not criticism.

The strongest evidence for this conjecture would be: modest substrate improvements paired with better structures for adversarial testing, memory of failed ideas, explicit model competition, and mechanisms that reward explanatory compression outperform much larger raw scaling on tasks requiring theory formation. Conversely, if pure scaling alone reliably produces self-correcting explanatory systems, the conjecture is false.

So the collision between this perspective and the problem illuminates a threshold: **substrate matters until structural error-correction becomes the binding constraint**. AI appears near or within that regime now. The next epistemic leap is therefore less likely to come from another order-of-magnitude increase in compute than from building systems whose abstract structure preserves the invariants of criticism, selection, and cumulative knowledge.
