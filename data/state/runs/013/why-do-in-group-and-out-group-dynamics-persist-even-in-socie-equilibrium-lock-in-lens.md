## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Even in societies that sincerely reject tribalism, in-group/out-group dynamics persist because *non-tribal social order is itself a public good*—costly to produce, hard to exclude noncontributors from, and vulnerable to stable underprovision.

A society that rejects tribalism typically wants norms like impartiality, universal dignity, restraint in status competition, and fair treatment across lines of identity. But these norms do not enforce themselves. They require costly acts: interpreting others charitably, suppressing retaliatory impulses, investing in shared institutions, punishing one’s own side when it defects, and bearing short-term local costs for diffuse long-term civic benefits. The benefits of this work are broad and non-excludable: everyone gains from lower suspicion and higher trust, including those who do little to sustain it.

That structure creates a familiar trap. For each individual, it is often rational to rely on others to uphold universal norms while oneself defaulting, at least occasionally, to cheaper coalition heuristics: trust the familiar, favor the loyal, excuse one’s side, and treat ambiguous outsiders as risks. These heuristics provide concentrated private benefits—identity, protection, coordination, status—while the costs are dispersed across the whole society. So even when most people *prefer* a genuinely non-tribal order, the society can settle into a stable equilibrium of recurrent boundary-making and factional suspicion.

This perspective distinguishes structural underprovision from mere hypocrisy or bad values. The persistence of tribal dynamics need not mean people are secretly pro-tribal or conceptually confused. It can mean that anti-tribalism is institutionally thin. Where the good is diffuse and non-excludable, moral aspiration alone is an inadequate production mechanism.

What follows is that explicit anti-tribal ideology may remove only the *legitimating rhetoric* of tribalism while leaving the incentive structure intact. Indeed, suppressing overt tribal language can sometimes obscure the real dynamics: people re-form in-groups around politics, class, profession, taste, morality, or epistemic style. The labels change because the underlying coordination problem remains unsolved.

At the extreme case, imagine a society of people who all verbally endorse universal human solidarity but face uncertainty, competition, and finite attention. If no strong institutions reward cross-group cooperation, penalize in-group favoritism, and create repeated positive-sum contact, then under stress nearly everyone will still economize on cognition by using coalition signals. Tribalism reappears not as doctrine but as a low-cost default strategy.

So the illuminating claim is: **tribal persistence is often not primarily a failure of belief but a failure to reliably fund and reproduce the public goods that make non-tribal interaction viable.** The key leverage is structural: build institutions, norms, and feedback loops that make impartiality locally rational rather than merely globally admirable.
