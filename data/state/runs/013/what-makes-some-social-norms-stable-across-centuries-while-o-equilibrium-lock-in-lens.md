## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Some social norms remain stable across centuries when they function as **public goods of coordination and predictability** whose benefits are broad, diffuse, and hard to appropriate individually, and when institutions make contribution to the norm effectively unavoidable. Norms collapse rapidly when these supporting structures weaken, so that maintaining the norm becomes a **non-excludable, underprovided good** that each person prefers others to sustain.

From this perspective, stability is not mainly explained by people deeply endorsing a norm, nor collapse by sudden moral confusion. The deeper distinction is structural: **who bears the cost of compliance and enforcement, who captures the benefits, and whether defection can be privately rewarding while its harms are socially dispersed**.

A norm can persist for centuries if its maintenance is embedded in repeated feedback loops: family transmission, religious ritual, legal reinforcement, reputational monitoring, and material penalties for deviation. In that case, “supporting the norm” is not left to voluntary individual sacrifice. The system solves the public-goods problem by making enforcement and conformity locally rational. People need not agree strongly; they only need to face a world where noncompliance is costly and compliance is legible.

By contrast, a norm becomes fragile when its benefits remain collective but its maintenance becomes optional. If everyone benefits from others continuing to shame violations, teach the custom, or incur personal costs to uphold it, then each individual has an incentive to free ride. The norm may still be widely preferred in the abstract, yet no one has sufficient reason to pay the maintenance cost. This creates a **stable no-provision equilibrium**: each person says, in effect, “the norm should persist, but I need not be the one to defend it.” Once this threshold is crossed, collapse can be sudden because the norm’s apparent strength was really a stock sustained by ongoing contributions that had become structurally underprovided.

This also explains why pressure sometimes reveals fragility rather than causing it. External shocks—war, market change, urbanization, new media, state failure—do not merely challenge norms ideologically. They can **change the excludability structure** of enforcement and the distribution of costs. If monitoring becomes harder, if deviants can exit to new networks, or if elites stop subsidizing enforcement, then the norm’s maintenance reverts to a public good no one can reliably fund.

At the extreme, imagine a norm everyone likes only if everyone else keeps it. If even small acts of enforcement are costly and benefits are diffuse, the norm can disappear almost overnight despite broad preference for its continuation. Conversely, a norm few sincerely admire can survive indefinitely if institutions continuously solve its collective-action problem.

So the key variable is not sincerity but **whether the social system has durable mechanisms for provisioning norm maintenance**. Centuries-long stability marks successful solution of a public-goods problem; rapid collapse marks the exposure of one that had been hidden.
