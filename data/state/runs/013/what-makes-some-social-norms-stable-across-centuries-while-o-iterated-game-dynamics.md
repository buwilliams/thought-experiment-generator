## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms endure across centuries when they are embedded in **iterated interaction structures** that make compliance repeatedly observable, defection legible, and future consequences hard to escape. Norms collapse rapidly when pressure disrupts those conditions—by shortening time horizons, anonymizing behavior, weakening memory, or making reciprocity impossible.

From an iterated game perspective, a norm is not mainly a moral belief; it is a **strategy equilibrium** sustained by repeated encounters. A stable norm persists when people expect to meet again, when others can update on their behavior, and when present gains from violating the norm are outweighed by future losses through retaliation, exclusion, distrust, or missed cooperation. In that sense, “tradition” is often just the visible surface of a deeper game: a long-running system of repeated play.

This illuminates why some norms survive even major shocks. If the underlying network of repetition remains intact—families, neighborhoods, guilds, religious communities, trading circles, courts, or elite circles—then norms can be reconstituted even after war, migration, or economic stress. The key variable is not sincerity but **the shadow of the future**. Centuries-old norms are typically those tied to domains where interaction is dense and recurrent: marriage, inheritance, hospitality, debt, ritual, status, and property. These are not random; they govern relationships people cannot easily exit.

By contrast, norms collapse quickly when a system shifts toward **one-shot or pseudo-anonymous interaction**. Rapid urbanization, digital mediation, state breakdown, labor mobility, frontier conditions, conquest, or technological change can all reduce reputational continuity. Once defections become harder to observe or punish, cooperative norm-following ceases to be a good strategy even for previously compliant actors. Collapse can therefore occur “regardless of character”: people who would sustain a norm in repeated settings may abandon it when the game becomes short-horizon.

An extreme-case test clarifies the structure. In a village where everyone knows everyone and interactions span generations, even costly norms can remain stable. In a setting of total anonymity, transient contact, and no memory, only norms aligned with immediate incentives survive. So the durability of a norm depends less on its ethical content than on whether institutions preserve **memory, visibility, and future stakes**.

What follows is a sharper distinction between **normative legitimacy** and **game-theoretic stability**. Some norms persist for centuries not because they are good, but because repeated play makes deviation expensive. Some worthy norms collapse not because people cease believing in them, but because the interaction structure no longer rewards compliance.

So the central explanatory claim is:  
**The stability of a social norm is primarily a function of whether the surrounding social system continually recreates repeated-game conditions—reputation, reciprocity, observability, and long horizons. Pressure destroys norms when it converts repeated relationships into short-horizon encounters.**
