## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Dominant power structures can self-perpetuate *without explicit collusion* because their persistence is often **computationally irreducible**: the large-scale pattern of advantage is generated by many local, individually rational, rule-following interactions whose aggregate effect cannot be compressed into a simple conspiracy or single causal law. What reproduces domination is not primarily shared intent, but a **minimal generative rule-set** embedded in institutions, incentives, defaults, and learned expectations.

More concretely: if access to resources, legitimacy, and decision-making is routed through existing distributions of status and credibility, then each actor can behave “normally” — hiring for trust, lending for safety, policing by precedent, teaching to standards, voting for stability — while the system iteratively reconstructs the same hierarchy. No beneficiary needs to coordinate explicitly, because coordination is offloaded into the structure itself. The system computes its own continuity.

This perspective shifts explanation away from asking, “Where is the secret agreement?” and toward asking, “What are the smallest recursive rules that regenerate unequal outcomes across many iterations?” Examples of such rules include: preference for familiar signals of competence; risk models trained on historically unequal data; gatekeeping through costly credentials; deference to already-accredited authorities; punishment of deviation framed as neutrality or professionalism. Each rule may appear locally defensible. But when run repeatedly across a population, they produce path-dependent concentration of power.

What follows is that opacity is not accidental. In a computationally irreducible system, even participants inside it may be unable to foresee the full downstream effects of their actions except by living through the iterations. This makes domination easy to deny: because no one act contains the whole pattern, beneficiaries can sincerely experience themselves as merely responding to circumstances. Structural reproduction therefore appears as common sense, meritocracy, or realism rather than domination.

An extreme-case test sharpens this: imagine removing all explicit bigotry or elite coordination while leaving intact inherited wealth, network-based trust, prestige signaling, institutional memory, and unequal error costs. Hierarchy would still likely re-emerge. This suggests that explicit collusion is not the core mechanism; it is only one possible accelerator. The deeper engine is recursive selection through structurally biased rules.

So the explanatory leverage lies not in uncovering hidden meetings of elites, but in identifying and altering the **generative primitives**: who gets the benefit of the doubt, how risk is measured, which credentials count, how failure is punished, how opportunity is queued. If the system is irreducible, prediction of every outcome may be impossible — but criticism can still isolate the rule-set that makes domination the default attractor.
