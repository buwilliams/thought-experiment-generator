## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A coordination failure persists or resolves according to whether the multi-agent system contains enough **computational slack** to discover, transmit, and stabilize a better joint policy. In this perspective, persistence is not mainly about “bad incentives” in isolation, but about a system being trapped in a **locally self-reinforcing computation**: agents keep executing an equilibrium-maintaining algorithm because the information required to switch is too costly, too noisy, too delayed, or too weakly retained. Spontaneous resolution occurs when the system’s information-processing structure crosses a threshold at which alternative coordination becomes computable and selectable.

**What is true under this view:**  
Coordination failure is a kind of distributed computational lock-in. Agents are not merely failing to cooperate; they are implementing a stable error-correcting routine that reproduces the failure. The relevant determinants are therefore structural features of information processing:

1. **Observability:** Can agents detect that an alternative joint pattern exists or that current behavior is inefficient?  
2. **Common knowledge generation:** Can they infer that others also detect this, and that others know that others detect it?  
3. **Experimentation bandwidth:** Is there enough variation, trialing, or exploratory behavior to sample alternative strategies?  
4. **Memory and retention:** If a better pattern appears, can the system store it—in norms, institutions, protocols, reputations, or code?  
5. **Selection pressure:** Does success under the improved pattern propagate faster than regression to the old one?

**What follows:**  
Indefinite persistence is likely when feedback loops compress information: private signals stay private, failed attempts are forgotten, mistrust amplifies ambiguity, and local adaptation rewards short-term defensive behavior. In such systems, each agent’s rational update reproduces collective irrationality. The failure is computationally cheap to maintain and computationally expensive to escape.

Spontaneous resolution is likely when some process increases effective compute at the system level: repeated interaction, visible signals, shared metrics, focal points, trusted intermediaries, interoperable standards, or crises that sharply raise the signal-to-noise ratio. These do not merely “improve incentives”; they reduce the search and coordination cost of a new joint policy. They let the system perform a better computation.

**What this illuminates:**  
The dividing line is not between cooperative and uncooperative agents, but between systems that can and cannot **compute a transition** out of the trap. A market, ecosystem, firm, or polity resolves coordination failure when it can generate enough variation and enough mutual predictability for a superior pattern to become self-replicating. It persists when the old pattern has higher informational fitness than the new one, even if materially worse.

**Extreme-case insight:**  
If agents had perfect observability, zero communication cost, infinite memory, and unlimited ability to test alternatives, most coordination failures would collapse quickly: the better equilibrium would be computable and selectable. If they had none of these, failure would persist indefinitely even when everyone “wants” change. So the core determinant is the system’s capacity to perform distributed computation about alternative futures and lock in the superior one.
