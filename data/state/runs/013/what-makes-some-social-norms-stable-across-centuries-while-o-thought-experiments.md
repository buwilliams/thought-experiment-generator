## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A social norm remains stable across centuries when its enforcement is **distributed, self-reproducing, and tied to many system functions at once**; it collapses rapidly when enforcement is **centralized, costly, and dependent on a narrow justification**. The key variable is not moral strength or age, but **structural redundancy of enforcement**.

**Hypothetical isolating scenario:**  
Imagine two norms, A and B, identical in popularity, age, and stated legitimacy. The only difference is how they are maintained.

- **Norm A** is reinforced by family expectations, ritual, law, status competition, everyday habit, language, and institutions. Violating it carries small penalties from many directions.
- **Norm B** is reinforced mainly by one institution or one ideology—say, the state, a church, or elite fashion. Outside that channel, daily life does little to sustain it.

Assume both norms face equal external pressure: technological change, crisis, elite dissent, and generational turnover.

**Follow the logic:**  
If the sustaining institution of **Norm B** weakens, the norm loses most of its corrective feedback at once. People can defect without being corrected in a hundred tiny interactions. Once enough people visibly defect, expectations flip: what was once costly to violate becomes costly to obey. Collapse is rapid because the norm’s support was concentrated; pressure breaks the main beam.

By contrast, **Norm A** does not depend on any single defense. Even if law changes, family still teaches it; even if elites mock it, daily coordination still rewards it; even if belief weakens, habit and signaling still reproduce it. Pressure must defeat many feedback loops at once. The norm appears “ancient” or “deep,” but what is really deep is the **network of mechanisms that regenerate it**.

Push to the extreme: a norm with **infinite redundancy**—embedded in childrearing, mating, trade, honor, taboo, architecture, and language—would survive even if its original rationale were forgotten. A norm with **zero redundancy**—sustained only by explicit argument—would vanish as soon as argument failed or incentives changed.

**What this illuminates:**  
Durability is often mistaken for truth, sacredness, or cultural essence. But stability may instead indicate that a norm has become **multi-functional infrastructure**: it solves coordination problems, signals loyalty, allocates status, and organizes expectations simultaneously. Such norms persist because removing them creates disorder in several subsystems at once.

Likewise, fast-collapsing norms are often not weak in sentiment but **thin in structure**. They may look dominant right until failure because centralized enforcement can conceal fragility.

So the relevant distinction is not **old vs new** or **good vs bad**, but **structurally overdetermined vs structurally exposed**. Centuries-long stability belongs to norms that are carried by many overlapping feedback loops; rapid collapse belongs to norms whose survival depends on too few.
