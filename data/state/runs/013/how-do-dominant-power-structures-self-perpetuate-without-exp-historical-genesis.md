## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Dominant power structures persist without explicit collusion because they originated as solutions to coordination problems, and in solving those problems they embedded asymmetries into routines, standards, and definitions of competence that now reproduce themselves automatically.

Start with the origin question: what prior problem did a dominant structure solve? Usually not “how do we oppress efficiently?” but “how do we reduce uncertainty, allocate roles, stabilize expectations, and scale cooperation?” Hierarchies, credential systems, property regimes, bureaucracies, professional norms, and cultural scripts emerged because they made complex societies legible and governable. They replaced more local, negotiated, situational forms of order. But they inherited the perspective of the groups designing or benefiting from them, and those assumptions became invisible once encoded into institutions.

What follows is that domination becomes structural rather than conspiratorial. Beneficiaries do not need to meet and agree, because the system already coordinates behavior through incentives, path dependence, and default interpretations of merit, risk, normality, and legitimacy. A hiring manager following “objective” criteria, a lender pricing “creditworthiness,” a school rewarding “professionalism,” or a policymaker defending “fiscal responsibility” may all be acting locally and sincerely while reproducing a historically loaded order. The collusion happened earlier, in effect: in the historical settlement that defined what counts as neutral.

This explains a key puzzle: why unequal outcomes survive even when many participants reject prejudice in the abstract. The answer is that power has migrated from explicit exclusion to infrastructure. Once rules, metrics, and institutions are built around accumulated advantage, they convert yesterday’s victories into today’s common sense. Privilege then appears as competence, incumbency as stability, and redistribution as distortion.

What this perspective illuminates is that self-perpetuation depends less on bad intent than on inherited problem-solutions that continue solving one problem while silently preserving another: the concentration of authority and interpretive power. Systems endure because they train participants to perceive their outputs as deserved and their procedures as natural.

So the hidden assumption is not merely “the powerful protect themselves,” but “the existing way of coordinating society is synonymous with order itself.” That assumption is historically contingent. If the structure was built to solve legibility and coordination under conditions shaped by unequal power, then reform aimed only at attitudes or individual bias will underperform. The leverage point is upstream: redesign the background standards, feedback loops, and institutional defaults through which advantage reproduces itself without needing anyone to conspire.
