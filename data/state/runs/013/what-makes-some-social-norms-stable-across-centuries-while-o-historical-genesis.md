## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms remain stable across centuries not primarily because people keep endorsing them, but because the norm is embedded in solutions to older coordination problems and is continually re-imported through institutions that still solve those problems. Norms collapse rapidly when the problem they originally solved disappears, is solved better by a new structure, or becomes visible as a misfit rather than a necessity.

**Origin-story logic:**  
A norm is rarely born as “a value.” It usually begins as an answer to some recurring difficulty: reducing violence, allocating status, stabilizing kinship, managing inheritance, signaling trustworthiness, coordinating labor, or preserving group identity under uncertainty. Over time, the memory of the problem fades while the rule remains. What survives is not just behavior but a package: stories, rituals, sanctions, identities, and institutions built around it.

This reveals a hidden assumption: we often treat norms as if they persist because of moral agreement. But many persist because they are structurally coupled to systems that still reproduce them — family forms, legal systems, religious authority, property regimes, schooling, prestige hierarchies. The norm appears “natural” only because its origin problem and supporting machinery have become invisible.

**What follows:**  
A norm is likely to be durable when:

1. **It still solves a live problem** people repeatedly face.  
2. **Its function is multiplex** — it solves several problems at once.  
3. **Its support is distributed across institutions**, not dependent on one authority.  
4. **Its rationale can mutate** while the practice remains — inherited duty becomes morality, then identity, then tradition.  
5. **Its violation is legible and sanctionable** at low cost.

A norm is likely to collapse quickly when pressure reveals that:

1. **The original problem is gone** or no longer salient.  
2. **A superior substitute exists** for the underlying function.  
3. **The norm’s inherited justifications become criticizable** once origin conditions are exposed.  
4. **Its enforcement system centralizes and then breaks**, causing a rapid cascade.  
5. **It imposes visible costs without delivering visible benefits** in the present environment.

**Illumination from the collision of perspective and problem:**  
The difference between long-lived and fragile norms is often not “strength of belief” but **continuity of problem-solution fit**. Stable norms are those whose origin problem keeps recurring, even in altered form, so the norm keeps getting regenerated. Fragile norms are often relics: they survived by inertia after their original function was outsourced, technologized, juridified, or morally superseded. Under pressure, they do not merely weaken; they are suddenly seen as unnecessary.

So the key explanatory question is not “How strongly do people believe this norm?” but **“What problem was this norm born to solve, and what now solves that problem?”** That question exposes the hidden inheritance beneath apparent stability.
