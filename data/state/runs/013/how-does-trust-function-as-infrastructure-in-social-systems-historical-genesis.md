## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Trust functions as *inherited coordination infrastructure* — a social substitute for constant verification that evolved to solve the prior problem of unbearable transaction costs in repeated interdependence. It erodes not mainly when people become “less moral,” but when the structures that once made trust a good error-correcting strategy are replaced by systems that externalize risk, sever feedback, and scale anonymity faster than institutions scale accountability.

Origin-first, trust appears not as a virtue floating above society, but as a solution to an older bottleneck: no group can survive if every exchange, promise, or warning must be independently audited from scratch. Trust replaced exhaustive checking. It inherited assumptions from the environments where it first worked well: repeated interaction, local reputation, visible consequences, shared norms, and relatively short causal chains between action and outcome.

So trust is infrastructure in the same sense roads or protocols are infrastructure: it lowers coordination costs, increases speed, and permits complex social activity that would otherwise stall. In systems terms, trust is a stock built from successful interactions, sustained by reinforcing feedback loops: fulfilled expectations generate more willingness to cooperate, which generates more opportunities for fulfilled expectations. It also depends on balancing loops: sanctions, reputation loss, repair rituals, and institutional recourse prevent defection from cascading.

Its erosion, then, is best understood as a structural mismatch between inherited trust mechanisms and newer social environments. Trust decays when the system no longer preserves the conditions under which trusting was adaptive. Key erosive conditions include:

1. **Anonymity without accountability**: interactions scale beyond reputational visibility.
2. **Long causal chains**: people cannot trace who is responsible for outcomes.
3. **Asymmetric opacity**: institutions can observe individuals, but individuals cannot inspect institutions.
4. **Risk externalization**: decision-makers do not bear the downside of failed trust.
5. **Incentive misalignment**: actors are rewarded for appearing trustworthy rather than being trustworthy.
6. **Breakdown of repair mechanisms**: betrayal has no credible remedy, apology, restitution, or sanction.

An extreme-case test clarifies this: in a world of perfect transparency and perfect enforcement, interpersonal trust becomes less necessary because verification substitutes for it; in a world of total opacity and no recourse, trust becomes irrational except within very small enclaves. So trust is neither blind faith nor mere sentiment. It occupies the middle zone where uncertainty is real but bounded by intelligible structure.

What this perspective illuminates is that many contemporary “trust crises” are misdiagnosed as psychological or cultural failures. More often, they are legacy failures: societies are still asking trust to do the work it did in small-scale, feedback-rich settings while operating atop impersonal, high-speed, low-recourse systems. The hidden assumption is that trust can scale automatically. It cannot. It must be re-infrastructured through legible accountability, reciprocal transparency, enforceable repair, and institutions that keep consequences coupled to decisions.
