## Conjecture

Conjecture: Dominant power structures self-perpetuate not mainly through shared intent among beneficiaries, but through a tightly coupled ecology of incentives, standards, identities, and default procedures that make “normal” action reproduce the structure. The load-bearing parts are the ones that, if altered, would force broad compensating changes elsewhere.

Start with the strongest test: remove explicit collusion. If the system still reproduces itself, then coordination must be embedded in structure, not conspiracy. What structure? At least four mutually reinforcing elements seem load-bearing:

1. **Gatekeeping criteria**  
   Institutions decide merit, risk, professionalism, legality, credibility, etc. These criteria appear neutral, but they are calibrated to capacities more available to already advantaged groups. If you changed the criteria, you would have to change recruitment pipelines, training norms, evaluation tools, credential markets, and who counts as “qualified.” That indicates these criteria are not decorative; they are structurally central.

2. **Path-dependent resource accumulation**  
   Wealth, reputation, education, networks, and legitimacy compound over time. Advantage buys error-tolerance, visibility, and access to future advantage. If this compounding were interrupted, inheritance patterns, capital markets, school quality, geographic sorting, and social trust would all shift. So self-perpetuation depends on stocks and feedback loops, not just beliefs.

3. **Local rationality under global injustice**  
   Individuals can behave reasonably by prevailing standards—hire the safest candidate, fund the least risky founder, trust familiar signals, protect property values—and collectively reproduce domination. Change one local decision rule and actors become exposed to risk unless insurance, norms, and accountability systems also change. This shows domination persists because “rationality” is socially engineered, not because everyone secretly agrees.

4. **Legibility and narrative framing**  
   The system teaches people how to interpret outcomes: success as merit, exclusion as unfortunate but necessary, inequality as natural, resistance as destabilizing. If these narratives changed, institutions would need new moral vocabularies, new data practices, and new legitimacy rituals. So ideology is load-bearing not as propaganda pasted on top, but as a compression layer that makes the structure intelligible and acceptable.

What follows is that dominant power is best understood as a **self-repairing system**. Beneficiaries need not coordinate explicitly because deviations trigger corrective pressures: market penalties, professional sanctions, reputational costs, bureaucratic friction, and internalized norms. At the limit, even sincere reformers often become carriers of the structure because they must operate through institutions whose success metrics were built by the old order.

This perspective also distinguishes deep causes from narrative shells. “Prejudice,” “greed,” or “tradition” alone are too interchangeable; swap them out and the system can still persist if the incentive architecture remains. But alter the load-bearing couplings—how resources compound, how institutions certify merit, how risk is distributed, how outcomes are narrated—and the whole structure must either transform or visibly strain to preserve itself.

So the core claim is: power self-perpetuates without collusion when institutions encode historical advantage into ordinary decision rules, and those rules are coupled tightly enough that participating in everyday social life reproduces dominance by default.
