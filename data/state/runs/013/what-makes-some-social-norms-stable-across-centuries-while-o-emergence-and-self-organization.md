## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms persist across centuries not primarily because individuals deeply believe them, nor because institutions continuously enforce them, but because they are embedded in **self-reinforcing interaction structures** that reproduce the norm across many contexts at once. Norms collapse rapidly when those reinforcing loops become coupled to new interaction patterns that flip from stabilizing to destabilizing feedback.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
A norm is not just a shared rule in people’s heads; it is a **system-level equilibrium** sustained by mutually supporting loops among expectation, enforcement, identity, coordination, and visibility. Its durability depends less on the strength of any single component than on the **redundancy and interdependence** of these loops.

A stable norm typically has several features:

1. **Expectation alignment:** people expect others to follow it.  
2. **Low-cost local enforcement:** deviation is noticed and sanctioned in ordinary interaction.  
3. **Identity linkage:** compliance signals belonging, decency, loyalty, or competence.  
4. **Institutional embedding:** laws, rituals, education, and status systems all reproduce it.  
5. **Intergenerational transfer:** children inherit not just the rule but the social situations that make the rule seem natural.  

When these layers reinforce one another, the norm survives even if many individuals privately doubt it. The system carries the belief.

**What follows:**  
Rapid collapse occurs when pressure does not merely weaken the norm “from outside,” but **restructures the network of interactions** so that the same social mechanisms begin amplifying defection instead of compliance. This happens when:

- people newly observe widespread noncompliance,
- sanctions become inconsistent or illegible,
- elite and ordinary behavior diverge visibly,
- alternative identities and coordination points become available,
- communication networks reveal that private dissent is common.

Then a threshold can be crossed: what was once costly to violate becomes costly to uphold. The feedback loops reverse. Public compliance falls, which lowers expectations of compliance, which weakens enforcement, which legitimizes further deviation. Collapse can therefore be sudden even after long apparent stability, because the norm was held in place by a metastable structure rather than by unanimous conviction.

**What this illuminates:**  
The key explanatory variable is not “how strong the norm is” in some intrinsic sense, but **how its reproduction is distributed across the social system**. A norm rooted in many overlapping feedback loops is robust to local shocks. A norm that appears dominant but relies on a narrow enforcement channel, a brittle prestige hierarchy, or widespread preference falsification may be fragile and prone to cascade failure.

**Extreme-case insight:**  
Imagine a norm everyone publicly obeys but almost no one endorses privately. If dissent remains invisible, the norm can last centuries. If one technological or political change makes dissent mutually visible, collapse can be extremely fast. So endurance is not evidence of deep legitimacy, and rapid collapse is not evidence of superficiality. Both are properties of **system structure**.
