## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Trust functions as **coordination infrastructure** in social systems because, under conditions of repeated interaction, it compresses the costs of monitoring, bargaining, and enforcement by making cooperative expectations self-sustaining. In iterated-game terms, trust is not primarily a moral feeling but a **stable equilibrium property** of environments where future encounters matter, defections are observable, memory persists, and reciprocation is feasible. It erodes when the structure of interaction shifts so that the future matters less, signals of defection become noisy, or retaliation and repair become impractical.

### What this perspective illuminates
In one-shot interactions, trust is fragile because exploitation can pay with little downside. In repeated interactions, however, the “shadow of the future” changes the payoff landscape: a short-term gain from defection is outweighed by the long-term loss of future cooperation. Thus trust acts like infrastructure because it supports many transactions at once, invisibly lowering friction across the system.

This means trust is less an attribute of virtuous individuals than a property of **game structure**. Social systems generate trust when they reliably produce:
- **Repeated encounters** rather than anonymous one-offs  
- **Legible behavior** so cooperation and defection can be detected  
- **Memory/reputation** so past conduct influences future treatment  
- **Reciprocal capacity** so actors can reward cooperation and punish betrayal  
- **Sufficiently long time horizons** so future payoffs discipline present behavior

### Structural causes of erosion
Trust decays when these conditions are weakened:

1. **Shortened time horizons**  
   If actors expect exit, churn, crisis, or instability, the value of future cooperation falls. Defection becomes more rational.

2. **Opacity of action**  
   If defection is hard to observe or responsibility is diffused, reputation cannot track behavior. Cooperation loses its enforcement mechanism.

3. **Breakdown of memory systems**  
   High turnover, fragmented institutions, weak records, or reputational overload sever the link between past actions and future consequences.

4. **Asymmetry in reciprocation**  
   If some actors can defect without being punished—or others cannot afford to retaliate—trust collapses unevenly and then system-wide.

5. **Scale without reputation portability**  
   As systems grow, local trust may not generalize unless reputational information travels across contexts.

### Extreme-case implication
At the limit of pure anonymity, zero memory, and no future interaction, trust should collapse regardless of cultural norms or professed values. Conversely, in a small, transparent, repeated community with strong memory and mutual dependence, trust can emerge even among self-interested agents.

### What follows
If trust is infrastructure, then repairing it is less about exhorting people to be better and more about redesigning interaction structures: lengthen horizons, improve visibility, preserve memory, and ensure defections have consequences while cooperation is rewarded. The leverage point is the architecture of repeated play, not the moral psychology of isolated actors.
