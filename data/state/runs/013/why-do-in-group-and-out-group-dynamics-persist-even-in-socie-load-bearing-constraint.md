## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** In-group/out-group dynamics persist in anti-tribalist societies because “reject tribalism” is usually a norm at the level of *declared identity*, while group-boundary formation is a load-bearing feature of coordination, trust allocation, moral signaling, and status competition. Remove explicit tribal markers and the same social functions reappear through new distinctions—often finer, more moralized, and harder to criticize.

Using the load-bearing test: if tribalism were merely a bad inherited narrative, then replacing beliefs with universalist ideals should dissolve the behavior without major downstream changes. But it does not. Instead, when explicit ethnic, national, or sectarian boundaries are suppressed, other boundaries become more salient: educated/uneducated, tolerant/intolerant, informed/misinformed, safe/unsafe, oppressed/oppressor, cosmopolitan/parochial. This suggests the specific labels are interchangeable, but the *role* they play is not.

What is load-bearing?

1. **Coordination under uncertainty.** People need heuristics for whom to trust, imitate, defend, and sanction. A society with no effective grouping would force individuals to evaluate everyone from scratch—an extreme case that does not scale. So some boundary-making is structurally necessary.

2. **Moral order through contrast.** Universalist societies still need visible ways to distinguish cooperators from defectors. If all distinctions are treated as suspect, then enforcement weakens. So anti-tribal norms generate a compensating distinction: those who transcend tribalism versus those still trapped in it. That is already an in-group/out-group split.

3. **Status competition.** If overt dominance by ancestry or faction is disallowed, status competition migrates into prestige around purity of principle, sophistication, or inclusiveness. The prestige hierarchy remains; only its tokens change.

4. **Identity compression.** Large societies are cognitively unmanageable without categories. Even a commitment to “individualism” becomes a category-marking practice, with recognizable speech, tastes, institutions, and enemies.

The integrated explanation is therefore not “people are hypocrites” or “tribal instincts are irrational leftovers.” Those are narrative shells because changing them leaves the main phenomenon intact. A more constrained explanation is that any society must solve recurrent problems of cooperation, prediction, and norm enforcement, and group distinctions are one family of solutions. Anti-tribalism does not eliminate the underlying problem; it changes which distinctions are legitimate and which are taboo.

What follows is counterintuitive: the more a society defines itself against tribalism, the more likely it is to produce **second-order tribalism**—coalitions formed around the repudiation of crude coalitions. These can become especially rigid because they present themselves not as one tribe among others, but as the absence of tribe, i.e. as morality or rationality itself.

So the persistence of in-group/out-group dynamics is not mainly a failure to live up to ideals. It is evidence that boundary-production is structurally load-bearing in social systems, and that suppressing one boundary regime tends to drive boundary formation into subtler, more morally insulated forms.
