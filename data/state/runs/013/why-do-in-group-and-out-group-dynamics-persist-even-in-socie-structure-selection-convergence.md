## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
In-group/out-group dynamics persist in anti-tribalist societies because rejecting *tribal identity as an explicit norm* does not remove the underlying structural problem that group boundaries solve. Any society with stable regularities, scarce attention, asymmetric trust, and distributed knowledge will generate classification systems for coordinating action. If older tribal markers are delegitimized, new markers emerge under morally acceptable descriptions — e.g. “values,” “epistemic standards,” “safety,” “civic responsibility,” or “anti-tribalism” itself.

From the given lens, mathematics and physics are parallel searches over structure: mathematics explores what coherent structures are possible; physics determines which are realized. By analogy, social ideals explore possible normative structures, but actual societies instantiate only those compatible with human cognitive limits, incentive gradients, and coordination needs. So the persistence of in/out dynamics is not evidence that anti-tribal ideals are hypocritical in some superficial sense; it is evidence that the ideal was aimed at the wrong level of abstraction. It attacked one *presentation* of group structure, not the generative conditions that recreate it.

Systems-wise, group formation is a recurring attractor produced by feedback loops:

- **Trust economy:** people rely on proxies for reliability.
- **Information overload:** identity compresses social complexity.
- **Norm enforcement:** groups punish defectors and reward conformity.
- **Moral signaling:** anti-tribal commitments themselves become badges of belonging.
- **Threat detection:** disagreement is redescribed as danger, impurity, or irrationality.

These loops can operate even when everyone sincerely endorses universalism. In fact, explicit anti-tribalism may intensify them, because once crude tribe markers are forbidden, status competition migrates into harder-to-contest symbolic forms. The out-group becomes not “foreigners” or “heretics,” but “the tribal,” “the bigoted,” “the unserious,” “the unsafe,” or “the disinformed.” The boundary remains; only its legitimating vocabulary changes.

An extreme-case test sharpens this: in a society of perfectly interchangeable, fully transparent, omniscient agents, tribal dynamics would vanish because no coarse grouping would be needed. In the opposite limit — finite agents with uncertainty, conflict, and reputation dependence — some boundary-making is unavoidable. Therefore the persistence of in-group/out-group behavior is best explained as a structural consequence of bounded agents doing coordination under uncertainty, not as a removable moral residue.

**What follows:**  
Efforts to eliminate tribalism by denunciation alone will fail and often reappear as subtler tribalism. The more fruitful target is not “grouping” per se but the specific feedback loops that make group boundaries adversarial, self-sealing, and status-extractive. The leverage points are institutional designs that reduce the need for identity proxies: better mechanisms for trust, error-correction, cross-group cooperation, and reversible disagreement. In short: anti-tribal societies still generate tribes because they still need structure; the real question is which structures make difference corrigible rather than hostile.
