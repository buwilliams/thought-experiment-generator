## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Dominant power structures can self-perpetuate *without explicit collusion* when social, economic, and political interactions are sufficiently repeated that individually rational actors learn to preserve the structure as the safest strategy. The system behaves like an iterated game in which defection against the hierarchy is costly, visible to insiders, and only weakly rewarded in the short term, while cooperation with the hierarchy reliably preserves future access, status, and protection.

From an iterated-game perspective, explicit conspiracy is unnecessary because the structure itself supplies the incentives. Elites, gatekeepers, and aspirants interact repeatedly: they hire one another, exchange legitimacy, circulate opportunities, and observe who is “reliable.” In such an environment, support for existing arrangements functions like a cooperative equilibrium. A person need not intend to uphold domination as such; they only need to anticipate that challenging it will damage future payoffs across many rounds.

Three mechanisms follow:

1. **Reputation effects:** Actors who visibly disrupt the power structure gain a reputation for being dangerous, disloyal, or unpredictable. Since future interactions matter, others avoid them. Conformity becomes a signal of trustworthiness.

2. **Reciprocity and retaliation:** Even absent centralized coordination, repeated interaction enables decentralized punishment. Those who challenge the structure may be excluded, denied promotion, ignored, or subtly sanctioned later. Because these responses can be local and informal, the system reproduces itself without any meeting of conspirators.

3. **Shadow of the future:** The longer the expected horizon within elite or semi-elite networks, the more attractive it is to preserve the equilibrium that keeps one inside the game. Short-term moral gains from resistance are outweighed by long-term losses in access. Thus beneficiaries often sustain structures they did not design and may not even endorse explicitly.

What this illuminates is that domination persists less through shared ideology alone than through *repeated strategic embeddedness*. People adapt to the game they expect to keep playing. This also explains why power structures often survive turnover in personnel: the equilibrium is regenerated by incentives, not by unique individuals.

An extreme-case implication clarifies the structure: if interactions became one-shot, anonymous, or non-reputational, self-perpetuation would weaken. Beneficiaries would have less reason to defend the structure reflexively, because dissent would not reliably trigger future penalties. Conversely, when time horizons are long, defection is legible, and gatekeeping is dense, cooperation with hierarchy becomes highly stable.

So the core claim is: **dominant power structures endure without explicit collusion because repeated interaction transforms structural loyalty into the individually rational strategy, and decentralized reputation dynamics substitute for conspiracy.**
