## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A coordination failure persists not mainly because agents are misaligned or change is slow, but because the system lacks *usable feedback loops* that let agents detect, trust, and act on mutual shifts in expectations. It resolves spontaneously when a bottleneck is removed that allows local evidence of changed behavior to propagate into common knowledge faster than defection or caution can erase it.

From this lens, the key question is not “why don’t agents coordinate?” but “what structure prevents corrective feedback from closing the gap between private willingness and public expectation?” In many coordination failures, multiple agents would prefer a new equilibrium, but each relies on governance, norms, prices, reputation, or observation systems that were built for incremental adaptation. If those systems cannot register intent, verify commitments, or punish opportunism quickly enough, then the failure is self-sealing.

So persistence depends on a few structural features:

1. **Observability of others’ shifts.** If agents cannot tell whether others are changing, every move looks risky and isolated.
2. **Credibility of commitments.** If promises are cheap and reversals easy, no one treats signals as actionable.
3. **Speed of feedback relative to cost of mismatch.** If miscoordination is punished faster than learning is rewarded, experimentation dies.
4. **Conversion of local knowledge into common knowledge.** It is not enough that many agents privately see the better equilibrium; they must know that others know it too.

This suggests spontaneous resolution is often not additive — not many small improvements in trust or communication — but bottlenecked. A single change such as a public standard, clearing mechanism, enforceable commitment device, shared metric, or highly visible early adopter can unlock several capabilities at once: signaling, verification, synchronization, and error correction. Once that happens, positive feedback can dominate: each successful coordination event becomes evidence that further coordination is safe.

The extreme case clarifies the structure. If feedback were perfect and instantaneous, inefficient coordination failures could not persist: agents would continuously update into mutually beneficial equilibria. If feedback were absent, even universally preferred alternatives could remain unrealized indefinitely. Therefore the persistence of failure is determined by the quality of the feedback architecture, not merely by preferences.

What follows is that feared rapid change is dangerous only when it outruns or disables the loops by which agents learn whether coordination is working. But rapid change can also *solve* coordination failure if it introduces a new loop that current governance lacked. The central variable is not speed itself; it is whether the system can generate reliable mutual knowledge quickly enough to stabilize a better equilibrium.
