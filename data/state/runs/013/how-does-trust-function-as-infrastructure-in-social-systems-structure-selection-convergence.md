## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Trust is best understood as **epistemic infrastructure** in a social system: a shared compression layer that lets people act on expectations about others without re-verifying everything from scratch. In that sense, trust plays in social life a role analogous to mathematics in physics: it is not a decorative add-on but a structure that makes coordination tractable. What empirical criticism does for physical theories, ongoing social feedback does for trust: it selects which expectations remain usable.

From this perspective, trust is not primarily a feeling but a **system property** that lowers the cost of prediction, cooperation, and repair. A society with functioning trust has reliable interfaces—promises, norms, credentials, institutions, reputations—that allow people to treat many interactions as already “solved.” That enables scale. Without trust, every exchange becomes locally empirical and costly: more monitoring, more contracts, more redundancy, more defensive behavior. So trust functions as infrastructure because it supports flows of information, capital, care, authority, and compliance while remaining mostly invisible when it works.

What erodes trust, then, is not mere disagreement but **persistent failure of predictive fit** across those interfaces. Trust declines when the structures that once made expectations reliable stop being corrigible under criticism. Key erosion conditions include:

1. **Asymmetric error exposure:** when some actors can defect, lie, or externalize costs without comparable consequences. This breaks the feedback loop that would normally align reputation with behavior.  
2. **Opacity at high leverage points:** when institutions controlling consequential decisions become illegible to those affected. If people cannot inspect or contest outcomes, trust loses its error-correcting mechanism.  
3. **Signal pollution:** when indicators of trustworthiness—credentials, branding, metrics, public language—can be cheaply faked or gamed. Then symbols detach from underlying reliability.  
4. **Delayed or suppressed error correction:** when harms are recognized too late, criticism is punished, or repair is absent. Small failures then accumulate into generalized distrust.  
5. **Load beyond design capacity:** in extreme cases—rapid scale, crisis, inequality, informational overload—systems built for repeated local interaction are forced into anonymous, high-speed coordination. Trust mechanisms that worked at small scale cease to track reality.

At the limit, a social system with zero trust does not become merely less pleasant; it becomes **computationally intractable** for ordinary participants. Verification burdens explode. Coordination collapses into coercion, tribalism, or brittle proceduralism.

So the illuminating claim is: **trust erodes when the social system ceases to reliably connect claims, actions, and consequences through visible, corrigible feedback.** Rebuilding trust is therefore less about exhorting sincerity than about redesigning structures so that honesty is legible, defection is costly, criticism is survivable, and repair is faster than decay.
