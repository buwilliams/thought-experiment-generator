## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms persist across centuries when they are embedded in an **equilibrium lock-in**: a structure in which each person’s best response is to comply *because others are expected to comply*, and where deviation carries immediate local costs while the benefits of change are diffuse, delayed, or unattainable by any individual acting alone. Norms collapse rapidly when this mutual-best-response structure has already weakened or was never deep, so that once expectations shift, defection becomes self-reinforcing.

This perspective distinguishes **true stability** from mere inertia. A norm is not durable just because people believe in it, but because it is coupled to enforcement loops: reputation, mating and kinship incentives, access to trade, employment, safety, legitimacy, sacred meaning, or state punishment. These create a Nash-like trap: even if many people privately dislike the norm, no one can improve their situation by unilateral exit. Stability, then, is often a sign not of consensus but of **coordination dependency**.

What follows is that centuries-long norms tend to have three structural properties:

1. **Dense interdependence:** compliance is tied to many domains at once, so violating the norm threatens multiple forms of value simultaneously.  
2. **Distributed enforcement:** punishment does not require a central enforcer; peers, families, markets, and institutions all reproduce the norm.  
3. **Expectation legibility:** everyone can easily tell who is conforming and who is not, making punishment cheap and predictable.

By contrast, rapid norm collapse occurs when pressure attacks not just beliefs but the **equilibrium-supporting structure**. If new institutions reduce punishment, new networks provide alternative communities, or public signals reveal that many others were only complying strategically, then the old norm can pass a tipping point. What looked morally solid was structurally brittle: a contingent equilibrium sustained by common knowledge of compliance, not deep endorsement.

An extreme-case test clarifies this. Imagine a norm everyone hates but everyone can observe and punish deviations from: it can endure indefinitely. Now imagine a norm many sincerely endorse but cannot monitor or enforce: it may erode quickly. So the key variable is less private approval than the **payoff structure of visible coordination**.

This illuminates why some reform efforts fail despite widespread support: they target attitudes while leaving the equilibrium intact. And it explains why some apparently minor shocks cause sudden collapse: they change expectations about what others will do, converting isolated dissent into a new coordinated equilibrium.

So the central claim is: **the longevity of a social norm is primarily a function of how completely it locks agents into mutually reinforcing best responses, and its speed of collapse depends on whether pressure disrupts that lock-in or merely protests against it.**
