## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Trust functions as invisible coordination infrastructure: a shared compression layer that lets people act on expectations rather than constant verification. It erodes not primarily when people become “less moral,” but when the structure of the system makes prediction costly, feedback unreliable, and defection locally rational.

**Hypothetical scenario isolating the key variable:**  
Imagine two societies identical in resources, culture, and formal rules. In both, people depend on strangers for food, transport, payments, care, and information. The only difference is this: in **Society A**, signals about others’ behavior are stable, legible, and quickly corrected when false. In **Society B**, signals are noisy, delayed, manipulable, or disconnected from consequences.

Now assume this is real and follow the logic.

In Society A, trust becomes infrastructure because it lowers the need for monitoring. People can rely on contracts, reputations, institutions, and shared norms as if they were roads or power grids: background systems that make higher-order cooperation possible. The stock of trust grows because successful interactions generate reinforcing feedback. Trust here is not mere belief in goodwill; it is a rational response to a system in which behavior is predictably coupled to consequences.

In Society B, the same people become more defensive. If signals are unreliable, reputation no longer tracks conduct, rules no longer track enforcement, and promises no longer track outcomes. Then verification, hedging, and withdrawal replace easy cooperation. Transaction costs rise. Actors shift from long-horizon reciprocity to short-horizon extraction, because preserving trust yields less payoff than exploiting uncertainty before others do. Distrust spreads as a self-fulfilling equilibrium: each person’s caution becomes evidence to others that caution is necessary.

So the key variable is not trust as an attitude, but **the structural reliability of expectation-formation**.

What follows is that trust erodes under several specific structural conditions:

1. **Signal corruption** — when information channels are flooded, gamed, or decoupled from reality.  
2. **Consequence failure** — when good and bad behavior produce similar outcomes, so the system stops teaching.  
3. **Opacity of decision-making** — when actors cannot tell how outcomes are produced, making motives and rules unreadable.  
4. **Asymmetric impunity** — when some can defect without cost, converting trust from a mutual asset into a vulnerability.  
5. **Volatility** — when rules, identities, or incentives change too quickly for stable expectations to form.  
6. **Scale without translation mechanisms** — when systems grow beyond face-to-face trust but fail to build credible institutional substitutes.

At the extreme case, a social system with zero trustworthy signal and zero consequence coupling does not merely have “low trust”; it loses social bandwidth. Coordination collapses into coercion, kinship, or enclave-based loyalty. Trust, then, is best understood as infrastructure because when it fails, everything else becomes more expensive, slower, and more brittle.

So: trust is what a social system uses instead of constant proof, and it erodes when the structure no longer rewards accurate expectations.
