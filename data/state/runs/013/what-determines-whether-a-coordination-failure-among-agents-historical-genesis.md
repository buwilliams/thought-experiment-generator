## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A coordination failure persists indefinitely when the coordination mechanism now in use is an inherited solution to an earlier problem that no longer exists, while the current agents lack a shared way to notice that the mechanism itself has become the obstacle. It resolves spontaneously when the original function of the mechanism erodes enough that local deviations become legible, imitable, and low-risk.

The origin-story lens shifts the question. Instead of asking only what incentives agents face now, ask: **what problem was the present equilibrium originally solving?** Many persistent coordination failures are not arbitrary traps; they are the remnants of once-useful arrangements. A convention, institution, norm, interface, standard, or hierarchy may have been selected to solve scarcity of information, high mistrust, costly communication, or danger from unilateral deviation. Over time, conditions change, but the inherited structure remains. What persists, then, is not merely failure to coordinate, but fidelity to an old solution.

This illuminates a hidden asymmetry:

- If the old arrangement still visibly solves something, even weakly, agents interpret deviation as recklessness.
- If the old arrangement’s function becomes opaque, agents may still preserve it because everyone has inherited the expectation that “this is how coordination is done.”

So indefinite persistence is likely when three things coincide:

1. **Historical opacity:** agents do not know what prior problem the arrangement solved.
2. **Asymmetric transitional risk:** deviating alone is punished more than conforming together is rewarded.
3. **Institutional inheritance:** the old solution is embedded in training, language, law, software, or status, so agents reproduce it without re-evaluating it.

By contrast, spontaneous resolution becomes possible when changing conditions expose the old solution as unnecessary or counterproductive in a way that can be discovered incrementally. Then small deviations are no longer interpreted as pure defection; they become intelligible experiments. Once one agent’s departure reveals that the inherited constraint was contingent rather than necessary, imitation can cascade.

An extreme-case test clarifies this. Imagine a norm created in a world of zero communication and extreme mistrust. It may be highly rigid and evolutionarily sensible. Now move to the opposite extreme: cheap communication, repeated interaction, high observability. If the norm persists, its persistence cannot be explained mainly by current necessity; it is being sustained by historical lock-in. Conversely, if even under high mistrust and low communication a new pattern emerges, then the old “failure” was not deeply locked but only thinly coordinated.

So the key determinant is not simply preference alignment or payoff structure in the present. It is whether agents can **reconstruct the genealogy of the equilibrium** well enough to separate its original purpose from its inherited prestige. Coordination failures end when people stop treating the existing arrangement as nature and start seeing it as a past answer to a past question.
