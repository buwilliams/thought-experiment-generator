## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Dominant power structures often persist not because beneficiaries consciously coordinate to preserve them, but because *changing* the structure is a public good while *complying with* it is privately rewarded or less costly. Where the benefits of reform are diffuse, delayed, and non-excludable, individually rational actors—including many who dislike the status quo—can remain locked in a stable equilibrium of non-challenge.

This lens separates **structural underprovision** from mere bad faith or apathy. If dismantling domination requires costly acts—speaking up, hiring differently, risking status, refusing informal advantages, building alternatives—then each individual beneficiary faces a familiar incentive problem: they bear concentrated costs for an intervention whose gains are widely shared, including by others who do not contribute. Even modest private incentives to “go along” can therefore outperform stronger but diffuse moral preferences for change.

What follows is that self-perpetuation can arise through **decentralized incentive alignment** rather than conspiracy. The structure reproduces itself through ordinary selections: whom to trust, promote, mentor, cite, believe, protect, or forgive. Each local decision is legible as reasonable from the chooser’s standpoint, especially under uncertainty. But in aggregate these decisions function as a feedback loop: incumbency produces resources, resources produce credibility, credibility shapes standards of merit, and those standards justify further incumbency.

The non-excludability point matters. If one insider takes risks to make the system fairer, they cannot easily reserve the resulting better world for fellow contributors; free riders benefit too. By contrast, the current structure often distributes **excludable** private goods—income, safety, reputation, access, predictability—to those who do not disrupt it. So the reform side is financed like a public good, while the preservation side is subsidized through everyday institutional returns.

At the extreme case, imagine a system where *everyone* privately agrees reform would be better, but no one can capture enough of the gains from their own costly action. Then explicit collusion is unnecessary: the no-reform equilibrium persists even under broad latent opposition. This reveals the hidden structure in less extreme cases. Apparent consent may partly be an artifact of underprovided resistance.

So the illuminating claim is: **dominant power structures self-perpetuate when opposition is organized as a public good and conformity is organized as a private good.** Their durability comes from feedback loops that convert dispersed dissatisfaction into inaction and convert local self-protection into system-wide reproduction.

The leverage point, then, is not mainly moral exhortation. It is changing the payoff structure: lowering the private cost of dissent, making contributions to reform more visible and reciprocated, and converting some benefits of justice from diffuse to attributable. Where reform becomes less like donating to a public good and more like participating in a supported coalition, self-perpetuating dominance becomes less stable.
