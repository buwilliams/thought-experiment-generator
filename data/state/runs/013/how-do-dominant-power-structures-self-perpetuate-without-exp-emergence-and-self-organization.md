## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Dominant power structures persist without explicit collusion because they are reproduced by the system’s interaction architecture: incentives, information flows, status signals, institutional routines, and adaptation pressures generate a self-stabilizing equilibrium in which individually local, seemingly reasonable actions aggregate into globally durable hierarchy.

What is doing the explanatory work is not secret agreement among beneficiaries, but a network of feedback loops.

At the **micro level**, actors respond to immediate constraints: employers minimize perceived risk, schools reward already legible forms of competence, media amplify familiar authorities, institutions copy other institutions, and individuals seek affiliation, security, and advancement within existing rules. Each move can be justified locally. But when these actions are linked, they create **macro-level path dependence**.

Several reinforcing loops help explain self-perpetuation:

1. **Access → performance → legitimacy loop**  
   Those closer to power receive better resources, networks, and forgiveness for failure. These advantages improve outcomes or their appearance, which is then read as evidence of merit, justifying further access.

2. **Recognition → visibility → authority loop**  
   Already recognized actors are more visible; visibility is mistaken for credibility; credibility brings more invitations, appointments, and agenda-setting power.

3. **Norm compliance → institutional fit → selection loop**  
   Institutions define “professionalism,” “reasonableness,” or “leadership” in ways shaped by incumbent groups. People who already resemble those norms are selected, which further entrenches the norms.

4. **Risk management → conservatism → reproduction loop**  
   Gatekeepers often do not optimize for justice or truth but for avoiding blame. Choosing the familiar is safer than backing outsiders. This systematically reproduces existing power without anyone needing to intend domination as such.

5. **Belief adaptation loop**  
   Subordinates and beneficiaries alike update beliefs to make the system intelligible and bearable. Success is moralized, exclusion is individualized, and structural effects are redescribed as natural differences. This stabilizes the system cognitively.

From this perspective, the apparent paradox — “how can domination persist without coordination?” — dissolves. **Collusion is only one mechanism of reproduction, and often not the main one.** In highly structured systems, explicit conspiracy is unnecessary because the environment itself coordinates behavior. The system “teaches” participants how to act through rewards, penalties, templates, and filtered perception.

An extreme-case test clarifies this: if every beneficiary were replaced by morally decent individuals who sincerely reject unfairness, but the incentive structure, evaluative norms, and institutional interfaces remained unchanged, much of the hierarchy would likely reappear. That suggests the causal locus is primarily systemic rather than personal.

So the key illumination is this: dominant power structures are emergent control systems. Their persistence depends less on the intentions of powerful individuals than on the recursive coupling between institutions, beliefs, and selection mechanisms. To disrupt them, one must alter the feedback structure — who gets recognized, what counts as competence, how risk is distributed, how decisions are audited — rather than merely exposing bad actors or demanding better motives.
