## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** In social systems, **trust functions as computational infrastructure**: a compressed, reusable coordination rule that lets agents act without simulating one another in full. Because social interaction is often **computationally irreducible**, trust cannot be replaced at scale by monitoring, contracts, or prediction. It is what makes action possible when the full state of the system cannot be computed in advance. Trust erodes when the system’s structure forces agents to repeatedly inspect, verify, and defend against one another, thereby converting low-cost heuristic coordination into high-cost step-by-step computation.

What this perspective illuminates is that trust is not merely a feeling or moral virtue. It is a **load-bearing simplification**. In a complex society, no one can calculate all downstream consequences, all intentions, or all hidden incentives. Trust substitutes for impossible prediction. It says, in effect: *I will proceed as if your local rule is compatible with mine.* This dramatically reduces the computational burden of social life.

From this follows a structural claim: **high-trust systems are those in which the generative rules of interaction are simple, legible, and recursively reinforced**, so that agents do not need to model each encounter from scratch. Examples include stable norms, credible enforcement, repeated interaction, transparency of roles, and institutions that align local incentives with system-level reliability. These are not guarantees of good outcomes; they are conditions under which trust is a rational compression.

Erosion occurs when the underlying rule-set becomes too opaque, too manipulable, or too adversarial for such compression to remain safe. Then agents are forced into costly recomputation: checking motives, verifying claims, hedging against defection, and building redundant safeguards. **Distrust is what social life looks like when compression fails.** The system still runs, but only by spending more energy on verification, surveillance, bureaucracy, and signaling.

The key structural causes of erosion are therefore not primarily “bad actors” in isolation, but **feedback loops that make local trust increasingly lossy**:  
- incentive structures rewarding short-term extraction over long-term reputation,  
- institutional opacity that prevents agents from inferring stable rules,  
- scale without adequate norm-translation, where interactions lose repeated-game memory,  
- asymmetries of information or power that make trust systematically exploitable,  
- and rapid rule change, which invalidates previously reliable compressions.

An extreme-case test sharpens the point. In a tiny village with perfect memory, trust can be personalized. In a massive anonymous society, that is computationally impossible. Trust must therefore migrate into institutions, protocols, and norms. If those mediating structures fail, no amount of individual goodwill can compensate; the society becomes stuck doing social computation the hard way.

So the collision yields this: **trust is infrastructure because it is a civilization-scale shortcut around computational irreducibility.** Its erosion begins when the system makes that shortcut unreliable.
