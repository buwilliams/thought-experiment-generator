## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A coordination failure persists not primarily because individual agents are irrational, uninformed, or badly motivated, but because the **interaction structure** stabilizes the failure as a self-reinforcing equilibrium. It resolves spontaneously when the system contains enough **feedback asymmetry, observability, or adaptive coupling** for local deviations to propagate into a new coordination pattern.

More concretely: persistence is determined by the **network topology and feedback loops** through which expectations about others are formed and updated. If agents’ incentives depend on anticipated conformity, and those anticipations are generated from local signals that mostly reproduce the status quo, then failure becomes emergent and durable. Each agent may be individually willing to coordinate on the better outcome, yet the system-level pattern blocks transition because no one can reliably infer that enough others will move too. The failure is thus maintained by a **closed loop of mutual hesitation**.

What follows is that spontaneous resolution requires a structural break in that loop, not necessarily a change in preferences. Three system properties matter:

1. **Signal amplification:** Small successful deviations must become visible enough to alter expectations beyond the initial participants.  
2. **Correlation of updating:** Agents must not revise in isolation; some shared event, common signal, or dense connectivity must align belief updates.  
3. **Positive feedback favoring the new equilibrium:** Early adopters must make further adoption easier, safer, or more rewarding.

If these are absent, even repeated evidence that a better coordination point exists may not matter; information dissipates before it reorganizes behavior. If they are present, resolution can appear “spontaneous” even without central control, because once a threshold is crossed the system flips through endogenous cascade dynamics.

The key illumination from the emergence perspective is that “indefinite persistence” and “sudden resolution” are not opposite mysteries but different regimes of the same system. In one regime, feedback loops damp deviations and preserve failure; in the other, they amplify deviations and dissolve it. The explanatory target therefore shifts from agents’ internal states to **the architecture of expectation coupling**: who observes whom, how quickly beliefs update, whether local coordination islands can connect, and whether the cost of being early is borne individually or socialized.

An extreme-case test sharpens this: in a system where every agent perfectly knows all others’ intentions and can adjust instantly, many coordination failures collapse quickly. In a system where every agent sees only noisy local behavior and bears full first-mover risk, failure can persist indefinitely even if everyone prefers the same better outcome. So the determinant is not desire for coordination but the **system’s capacity to convert distributed willingness into mutually recognized momentum**.
