## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A coordination failure persists indefinitely when the system’s feedback structure makes locally rational behavior continuously reproduce the very informational and incentive conditions that justify non-coordination. It resolves spontaneously only when the existing dynamics already contain a path by which small deviations become self-amplifying rather than self-punishing.

From the given lens, mathematics and physics are parallel searches over structure: mathematics explores what coordination structures are possible under logical constraints; social reality selects, by empirical criticism, which of those structures are dynamically stable in actual populations. So the key question is not “why don’t agents choose the better equilibrium?” but “what structural filter makes one equilibrium absorb deviations while another destroys them?”

In systems terms, persistent coordination failure is a **stable attractor**. Its persistence is determined less by preferences in isolation than by coupled stocks and flows: expectations, trust, common knowledge, switching costs, network density, punishment risk, institutional memory. If these variables form reinforcing loops around the bad equilibrium, then even agents who all prefer the better outcome will keep reproducing the worse one. The failure is not a static mistake; it is an equilibrium sustained by error-correcting forces that correct *against* escape.

Spontaneous resolution occurs when the bad equilibrium is only **metastable**: it appears persistent, but hidden within it are positive feedback loops that can flip sign once a threshold is crossed. Examples include enough visible adopters, enough credible commitments, enough repeated interaction, or enough reduction in uncertainty that “waiting” becomes more costly than “moving.” Then a local deviation ceases to be punished and starts serving as evidence to others, producing cascade dynamics.

The mathematics/physics analogy clarifies the “surprising” part. It is not mysterious that coordination sometimes self-resolves: in any world with stable regularities, agents can discover and exploit structures that compress uncertainty. Where the informational geometry of the situation lets actions become public signals, coordination can emerge as an expected convergence property. Where actions remain ambiguous, private, or weakly coupled, no such convergence occurs.

Extreme case: if every agent has perfect knowledge of others’ intentions and zero switching cost, coordination failure cannot persist except by genuine conflict of aims. At the other extreme—infinitesimal trust, high switching cost, noisy observability, and no common knowledge—even universally shared desire for coordination will not dissolve failure. This shows the determinant is not goodwill but **structure of mutual prediction**.

So the core claim is:  
**Coordination failure persists when the system suppresses information that others are ready to coordinate, and resolves when the system makes such information self-reinforcing.**  
The deepest leverage, therefore, is not exhortation but redesign of observability, commitment, and threshold-crossing mechanisms.
