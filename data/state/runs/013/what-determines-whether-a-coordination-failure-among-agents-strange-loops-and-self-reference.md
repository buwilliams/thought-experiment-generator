## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A coordination failure persists when the agents’ representational hierarchy contains a **closed strange loop of mutual modeling**—each agent’s model of others includes a model of how others model itself, and this recursive structure stabilizes expectations around non-coordination. It resolves spontaneously when that loop is **broken, compressed, or reinterpreted** so that agents can shift from “what everyone expects everyone to do” to a higher-order self-description like “we are the kind of system that can change conventions.”

### What is true from this perspective
Coordination failures are not just failures of incentives or information at the base level. They are often failures of **self-reference**. Each agent acts not only on the world, but on a model of the world that includes other agents’ models, which in turn include itself. At sufficient expressive power, the group becomes a system that is effectively **thinking about its own thinking**.

If that recursive loop converges on a fixed point like:

- “No one else will move,”  
- “Everyone knows no one else will move,”  
- “So moving is irrational,”

then the failure can persist indefinitely even when a better equilibrium is obvious. The trap is not merely external; it is embedded in the group’s self-model.

### What follows
Spontaneous resolution occurs when the recursive loop yields a **different fixed point**. This can happen through:

1. **Reflexive signal amplification**: a small act becomes legible as a signal that others will model as legible, creating an upward cascade.  
2. **Meta-level reframing**: agents stop modeling the immediate choice and instead model the situation as a coordination problem, allowing them to act on the loop itself.  
3. **Symbolic compression**: norms, rituals, prices, focal points, or leaders collapse infinite recursive uncertainty into a shared shorthand.

The key determinant is therefore not just payoff structure, but whether the agents’ self-referential models admit a **stable alternative self-description**. A system escapes when it can represent itself as capable of escape.

### Systems framing
In systems terms, persistence is maintained by a **positive feedback loop of recursive pessimism**: expected defection produces actual defection, which confirms the expectation. Resolution requires a structural intervention at the level of **second-order expectations**, not merely first-order preferences.

### Extreme case
At one limit, agents with no capacity for higher-order modeling cannot sustain sophisticated coordination failure; they simply fail locally and repeatedly. At the other limit, agents with unlimited recursive modeling can become paralyzed by infinite regress unless some convention closes the loop. Thus both too little and too much self-reference can block coordination. What matters is a **workable strange loop**: enough self-reference to model common knowledge, but enough compression to let action occur.

### Illuminated point
A coordination failure endures when the group’s strange loop locks identity to stasis: “we are the kind of group that does not move first.” It resolves when the same loop generates a new emergent identity: “we are now the kind of group that can coordinate.” The decisive variable is the structure of collective self-reference.
