## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Trust functions as a form of **coordination infrastructure**: a shared expectation that lowers the cost of exchange, reduces the need for monitoring, and enables agents to act on longer time horizons. Its erosion is not primarily a matter of people becoming worse or more cynical, but of systems shifting into an **equilibrium where distrust is individually rational even when trust would make everyone better off**.

From an Equilibrium Lock-In perspective, trust is not just a feeling or norm; it is a **stock of mutually reinforced expectations**. It allows agents to forgo defensive behaviors because they expect others to do the same. In that sense, trust resembles physical infrastructure: when present, many activities become cheap and fluid; when absent, every transaction requires frictional substitutes — verification, legalism, redundancy, surveillance, branding, signaling, collateral, and exit options.

What this perspective illuminates is the distinction between **trust failure as moral failure** and **trust erosion as equilibrium shift**. In many social systems, each actor may prefer a high-trust world, yet no actor can rationally behave trustingly on their own once enough others are defecting, hedging, or opportunistically exploiting gaps. At that point, distrust becomes self-protective. The system locks in because unilateral trust is punished.

The structural conditions that erode trust are therefore those that **raise the payoff to defection, lower the visibility of reciprocity, or shorten the time horizon over which cooperation pays back**. Examples include:
- high anonymity with low accountability,
- misaligned incentives between agents and institutions,
- large asymmetries of information,
- weak memory or poor reputation transmission,
- repeated experiences of unpunished defection,
- volatility that forces short-term survival behavior,
- scale increases that outstrip mechanisms for legibility and norm enforcement.

These conditions do not merely “damage relationships”; they alter the game structure. Once actors expect non-cooperation, they invest in protective adaptations. Those adaptations themselves — audits, proceduralism, platform intermediation, legal overengineering, constant signaling — can further reduce trust by replacing relational judgment with adversarial compliance. Thus trust erosion often proceeds through a feedback loop: **less trust leads to more defensive structure, which further normalizes distrust**.

The extreme case clarifies the point. In a world of perfect trust, coordination costs approach zero. In a world of zero trust, social life does not end, but it becomes radically more expensive, rigid, and extractive. Function persists only through coercion, exhaustive verification, or enclave-level trust. So trust is not optional cultural decoration; it is what makes complex cooperation scalable without overwhelming friction.

What follows is that restoring trust usually cannot be achieved by exhortation alone. If distrust is equilibrium-rational, then calls for people to “just trust more” are structurally naive. Durable trust repair requires changing the underlying payoff structure: making reciprocity visible, defection costly, memory durable, institutions credible, and long-term cooperation safer than short-term predation.
