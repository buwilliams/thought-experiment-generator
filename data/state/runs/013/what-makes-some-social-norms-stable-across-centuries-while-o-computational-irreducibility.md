## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Some social norms remain stable across centuries not because they are intrinsically stronger, truer, or more efficient, but because they are generated by **simple, recursively reproducible local rules** that can be re-enacted by ordinary people under many changing conditions. Norms collapse rapidly when their continued existence depends on **highly specific coordinating conditions**, elite maintenance, or fragile interpretations that cannot be regenerated once disrupted.

From the perspective of **computational irreducibility**, the persistence of a norm is often not compressible into a short predictive theory. You cannot, in general, infer its long-run survival from stated values, incentives, or formal institutions alone. The norm’s stability is a property of the **ongoing social computation**: repeated interactions, sanctions, imitations, exceptions, reinterpretations, and transmission across generations. To know whether a norm survives pressure, one often has to watch the process unfold.

What this reframes is the explanatory target. Instead of asking, “What equilibrium makes this norm optimal?” the better question is: **What is the minimal generative rule that keeps reproducing this norm despite shocks?** A centuries-stable norm is one whose rule is easy to instantiate repeatedly: e.g. “copy respected elders,” “punish visible defection,” “treat category X as sacred,” “reciprocate hospitality,” “defer in public to rank.” These are low-description-length social algorithms. Because they can run in many environments, they survive regime change, economic shifts, and partial breakdowns.

By contrast, rapidly collapsing norms are often those whose apparent solidity was misleading. They may require a dense support structure: trusted authorities, synchronized belief, costly surveillance, institutional enforcement, or narrow background assumptions. Once pressure removes one key component, the generative process no longer runs. The norm was less a self-reproducing rule than a temporarily sustained output of a larger system.

This suggests a distinction between **surface rigidity** and **deep generativity**. A norm can look rigid because sanctions are severe, yet be structurally brittle if few people can reproduce its logic without centralized enforcement. Another norm can look informal or weak, yet be deeply stable because it regenerates through everyday interaction.

An extreme-case test clarifies the point: if formal justification disappears, if authorities turn hostile, if transmission is interrupted, does the norm reappear anyway from local behavior? If yes, it is likely generated by a simple rule embedded in practice. If no, its previous stability was contingent, not deep.

So the collision of this perspective with the problem yields a concrete claim: **the best predictor of long-run norm stability is not normative legitimacy or current enforcement strength alone, but the simplicity and distributed reproducibility of the rule-set that generates the norm.** Unpredictability in norm change is therefore not merely ignorance; it reflects the fact that social stability is often an irreducible computation carried by millions of interacting agents over time.
