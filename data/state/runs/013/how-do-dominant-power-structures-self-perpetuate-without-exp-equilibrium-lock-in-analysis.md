## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Dominant power structures often persist not because beneficiaries explicitly coordinate to preserve them, but because the system has settled into a **lock-in equilibrium**: each beneficiary, acting independently and even pragmatically, faces local incentives that reward compliance with the existing structure and penalize unilateral deviation. The result is self-perpetuation without conspiracy.

From an equilibrium lock-in perspective, the key claim is: **power reproduces itself when dependence on existing rules, institutions, and expectations makes support for the status quo each actor’s individually rational strategy, even if many actors would prefer a fairer arrangement under conditions of coordinated transition.**

What follows is that self-perpetuation is structural, not merely moral or psychological. Elites may not need to consciously defend the whole system; they only need to respond sensibly to their immediate environment. A firm hires from elite pipelines because they are legible and low-risk. Politicians court donors because campaigns require money. Universities valorize established credentials because those credentials already signal value within the network. Judges defer to precedent, investors to incumbents, media to known voices. No single choice “creates” domination, but together they form a reinforcing loop.

The system persists through several mutually stabilizing mechanisms:

1. **Risk asymmetry:** Beneficiaries who defect from the structure bear concentrated costs, while benefits of reform are diffuse and delayed.  
2. **Coordination dependence:** Better alternatives often require many actors to move together; isolated reformers become less competitive, less credible, or more vulnerable.  
3. **Expectation feedback:** Because everyone expects others to keep using the old hierarchy, it remains rational to invest in it.  
4. **Legibility capture:** Existing power structures define what counts as merit, competence, normality, or realism, making alternatives appear irrational rather than merely unchosen.

This perspective distinguishes **equilibrium constraints** from **contingent failures**. If the structure persists because actors are misinformed, cowardly, or unusually selfish, then better decisions might fix it. But if even enlightened, decent, strategically aware participants would still reproduce the structure absent coordinated change, then the persistence is an equilibrium phenomenon. In that case, moral exhortation alone will predictably fail.

An extreme-case test clarifies the point: imagine every beneficiary privately agreeing the system is unjust, while still facing the same incentives, dependencies, and competitive pressures. If the structure would largely survive anyway, its durability comes from lock-in, not shared ideology alone.

What this illuminates is that anti-domination politics must target **transition mechanics**, not just beliefs. Reform succeeds when it changes payoff structures, reduces first-mover risk, builds coordination capacity, and creates protected pathways into alternative institutions. The leverage point is not persuading one more beneficiary to “do the right thing,” but altering the equilibrium so that preserving dominance is no longer the safest independent move.
