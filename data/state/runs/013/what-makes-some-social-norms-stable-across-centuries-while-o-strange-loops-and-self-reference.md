## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms endure across centuries when they are embedded in a **strange loop of self-reference**: the norm does not merely regulate behavior, but helps define the identity, legitimacy, and interpretive framework of the people and institutions that enforce it. In such cases, obeying the norm reproduces the very agents and narratives that make the norm seem natural, so challenges to it are interpreted through categories the norm itself supplies. By contrast, norms collapse rapidly when they lose this recursive closure—when people can still follow the rule behaviorally, but no longer derive from it a model of who they are, why authority is valid, or what counts as deviance.

What is illuminated by this perspective is that stability is not mainly a matter of repetition or coercion alone, but of **hierarchical self-reentry**. A durable norm operates across levels at once:

1. it structures individual action,  
2. it organizes mutual expectations,  
3. it legitimates institutions, and  
4. those institutions teach and sanctify the norm as part of social reality.

Traversing these levels returns one to the starting point: “we follow this norm because this is the kind of society/person/community we are,” and “we are this kind of society/person/community because we follow this norm.” That loop generates resilience. Pressure at one level is absorbed by reinforcement from the others.

A fragile norm, by contrast, is merely **externally imposed** or locally convenient. It lacks deep self-modeling. Once pressure breaks enforcement, there is no higher-order identity loop restoring it. The system can now describe itself without the norm. At that moment, collapse can be sudden, because what looked like stability was only static repetition, not recursive self-production.

This also explains why some norms persist even when costly or irrational in narrow terms: their function is not just instrumental but **self-descriptive**. They help a society tell itself what it is. And it explains rapid norm cascades: once an alternative loop becomes available—one in which rejecting the old norm better expresses dignity, modernity, justice, or belonging—the recursive machinery flips. Then defection no longer threatens identity; it confirms a new one.

An extreme-case test clarifies the structure. A norm enforced only by surveillance, with no identity or legitimacy loop, should vanish quickly when monitoring fails. A norm internalized so deeply that people feel violation as a threat to selfhood can survive even when enforcement disappears. The difference is not strength of force but depth of self-reference.

So the key variable is: **does the norm participate in a closed loop between behavior, identity, and institutional interpretation?** If yes, it tends toward long-term stability. If no, it remains vulnerable to rapid dissolution under stress.
