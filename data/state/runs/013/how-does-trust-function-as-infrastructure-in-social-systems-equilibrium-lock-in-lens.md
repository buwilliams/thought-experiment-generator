## Conjecture

Conjecture: Trust functions as a non-excludable coordination infrastructure: a shared stock that lowers transaction costs, widens the range of feasible cooperation, and makes higher-order goods—credit, institutions, knowledge-sharing, law, public health, markets—practically operable. Its erosion is therefore often not best explained by “people becoming worse” or merely confused, but by a structural underprovision problem in which the production and maintenance of trust are individually costly while the benefits are diffuse and only partially capturable.

What is true under this lens is that trust resembles a public good more than a private virtue. If I tell the truth, keep commitments, refrain from opportunism, or invest in fair procedures, I bear immediate costs; much of the gain accrues to others and to the system as a whole. A single actor cannot easily exclude others from enjoying the more trustworthy environment that results. So even where nearly everyone prefers to live in a high-trust system, each may rationally underinvest in trust-producing behavior, especially when others’ contributions are uncertain. The equilibrium can settle into stable low trust not because no one values trust, but because no one can safely fund it alone.

This illuminates why trust acts like infrastructure. Like roads or clean air, it is background-enabling: often invisible when abundant, suddenly decisive when degraded. It is a stock built slowly through repeated reliability, legibility of norms, credible enforcement, and shared expectations; it is depleted by betrayal, opacity, impunity, and adversarial incentive design. Once depleted, actors shift to expensive substitutes—monitoring, contracts, collateral, bureaucracy, surveillance, redundancy, in-group favoritism. These are not merely symptoms; they are structural adaptations to low trust that can further entrench it by making good faith less necessary and less rewarded.

The key structural conditions of erosion, then, are those that privatize the gains from defection while socializing the losses from distrust. These include anonymity without accountability, repeated opportunities for one-shot extraction, weak enforcement against opportunism, unequal exposure to risk, information asymmetries, and institutional opacity that makes trustworthy behavior indistinguishable from naive behavior. Extreme case: in a system of perfectly diffuse benefits and fully concentrated costs, trust maintenance collapses entirely unless some mechanism changes the payoff structure.

What follows is a practical distinction: where trust erodes broadly across domains, look first for defects in incentives, enforcement, and observability before attributing failure to culture or motivation. The leverage lies in institutions that make trust contributions visible, reciprocable, and protected from exploitation: reliable adjudication, reputational continuity, norm enforcement, transparency, and arrangements that convert diffuse benefits into partially excludable returns for trust builders. Trust survives not mainly when people are morally better, but when the system does not punish them for acting trustworthily.
