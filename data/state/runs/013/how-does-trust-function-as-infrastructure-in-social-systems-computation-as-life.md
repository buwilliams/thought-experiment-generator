## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Trust is best understood as a **distributed computational infrastructure** that compresses social uncertainty, lowers the cost of coordination, and enables complex collective behavior. In this view, a social system is a network of agents continuously updating models of one another under limited information. Trust functions as a **high-bandwidth prior**: it lets agents act without re-verifying every transaction, motive, or signal. What looks morally or culturally “soft” is structurally equivalent to a protocol layer in a computational system.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
A society with trust possesses a shared mechanism for reducing verification overhead. This is analogous to caching, error-correction, or consensus protocols in distributed systems. Trust stores the results of past interactions and makes them available for future coordination. It is therefore not merely an attitude; it is a **stock of encoded social information** accumulated through repeated successful prediction. Institutions, norms, reputations, contracts, and rituals are all trust-memory devices.

**What follows:**  
When trust is functioning, social computation scales. Agents can specialize, exchange, delegate, and plan across time because they do not need to compute everything from scratch. This enables larger markets, denser institutions, more abstract law, and more ambitious collective projects. Trust thus behaves like infrastructure because it is mostly invisible when working, but its absence forces expensive local workarounds.

**Structural conditions that erode trust:**  
Trust erodes when the system’s informational environment makes reliable prediction harder than opportunism. The key issue is not simply “bad actors,” but a structural shift in incentives, feedback loops, and signal quality.

Core erosive conditions include:

1. **High signal noise:** When agents cannot distinguish honest from manipulative signals, reputational computation degrades. Misinformation, opacity, and identity instability increase verification costs.
2. **Asymmetric accountability:** If some agents can defect without consequence, selection favors exploitative strategies. The system begins computing for extraction rather than cooperation.
3. **Shortened feedback loops for gain, lengthened loops for harm:** If benefits of betrayal are immediate while costs are delayed, hidden, or distributed, trust-destroying behavior is selected.
4. **Loss of shared interpretive protocols:** When groups no longer agree on the meaning of evidence, rules, or fairness, the social system loses interoperability.
5. **Excessive scale without corresponding trust-routing mechanisms:** As systems grow, local trust heuristics fail. Without robust institutions to translate trust across strangers, coordination collapses into suspicion or tribal caching.
6. **Persistent volatility:** In environments of rapid rule change, precarity, or institutional inconsistency, past interaction ceases to predict future behavior. Trust-memory loses value.

**Illumination from the collision:**  
Trust is not the opposite of computation; it is a computational achievement. Its erosion occurs when the social environment selects for adversarial information processing rather than cooperative prediction. At the extreme, a zero-trust society does not become more rational; it becomes computationally overloaded, forced into constant costly verification, shrinking the horizon of cooperation. So the deepest leverage is not exhorting people to “be more trusting,” but redesigning structures so that truthful signaling, reciprocity, and accountability become the evolutionarily stable computations of the system.
