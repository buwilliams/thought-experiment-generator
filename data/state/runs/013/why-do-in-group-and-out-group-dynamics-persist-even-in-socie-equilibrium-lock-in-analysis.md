## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
In-group/out-group dynamics persist in anti-tribalist societies because anti-tribalism often changes the *normative story* people tell about themselves without changing the *coordination incentives* that make group boundary-making individually rational. The result is an equilibrium lock-in: each person may sincerely prefer a less tribal society, yet no one can safely stop using group cues unless enough others stop at once.

**What is structurally true:**  
Group distinctions are not sustained only by hatred, ignorance, or bad values. They also function as low-cost tools for trust allocation, alliance prediction, reputational sorting, and conflict avoidance under uncertainty. In any large society, individuals face repeated problems of: Who is safe? Who is loyal? Who shares norms? Who will reciprocate? When information is limited, visible or narratively salient identities become coordination shortcuts.

Even where explicit tribalism is condemned, this does not remove the underlying need for such shortcuts. It often merely shifts them into more acceptable forms: cultural style, education, politics, moral language, profession, region, or subcultural codes. So “tribe” mutates rather than disappears.

**Why this is an equilibrium rather than just hypocrisy:**  
Suppose one individual tries to act with complete group-blindness. If others still rely on group signals, that individual becomes easier to exploit, worse at predicting coalitions, and less able to secure support. Meanwhile, anyone who openly names persistent group structure risks being accused of reintroducing tribalism. So actors are trapped between two costs: participating in boundary-making, or becoming strategically naive. Since unilateral disarmament is punished, the pattern reproduces itself.

This distinguishes equilibrium lock-in from contingent failure. If the persistence of tribalism were merely due to ignorance or moral inconsistency, better education or stronger exhortation should dissolve it. But if the pattern is equilibrium-based, moral rejection alone cannot eliminate it, because the system still rewards group-sensitive behavior.

**What follows:**  
Anti-tribalist societies often create a second-order tribalism: affiliation around *being the kind of people who reject tribalism properly*. This produces status contests over who is most universalist, least biased, or most morally legible. The boundary is no longer “our ethnicity versus theirs” but “the enlightened versus the suspect.” Thus explicit tribal categories may weaken while new in-/out-group structures form around anti-tribal norms themselves.

**Extreme-case insight:**  
At one extreme, a society with perfect individual-level information and guaranteed impartial enforcement would need few group heuristics; tribalism could genuinely decline. At the other extreme, under scarcity, mistrust, or institutional weakness, group markers become highly adaptive. Real societies sit in between, so public universalism coexists with covert sorting.

**Illumination:**  
The persistence of in-group/out-group dynamics is not evidence that anti-tribal ideals are fake. It is evidence that ideals alone do not overturn equilibria. To reduce tribalism, a society must replace the practical functions of group identity—trust, insurance, coordination, recognition—with institutions that perform those functions better than tribes do.
