## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
A coordination failure persists indefinitely when the agents’ local update rules make successful coordination a **computationally irreducible search problem**: no agent, and often no observer, can infer a shortcut from current state to coordinated equilibrium without the system actually traversing a sequence of interactions. Spontaneous resolution occurs only when the rule structure creates **compressible pathways** — feedback patterns that let local information reliably stand in for global progress.

**What this perspective illuminates**

The usual framing asks whether agents lack information, trust, incentives, or communication. From the standpoint of computational irreducibility, that is incomplete. Even if all those inputs improve, the failure may persist because the system’s next viable move cannot be known in advance except by enacting it. The obstacle is not merely ignorance; it is that the coordination landscape has no tractable shortcut.

In such systems, each agent responds to others’ responses to others’ responses, generating path dependence and recursive uncertainty. Whether a deviation will unlock coordination or trigger punishment, imitation, or collapse may be impossible to determine analytically from the present state alone. So agents rationally remain stuck in locally safe behavior. Persistence, then, is determined less by static preferences than by the **generative rule-set**: who updates when, on what signals, with what memory, threshold, asymmetry, and reversibility.

**What follows**

Persistent failures should be expected where:

1. **Local evidence is non-diagnostic**: an agent cannot tell whether taking a coordinating step is the start of convergence or merely an isolated costly deviation.  
2. **Update rules amplify hesitation**: thresholds, strategic complements, and punishment of failed attempts make exploration expensive.  
3. **History matters strongly**: early fluctuations lock in conventions or mistrust that cannot be bypassed by reasoning alone.  
4. **Search costs exceed local payoff gains**: discovering the path to coordination requires too many sequential trials.

Spontaneous resolution should be expected where:

1. **Small experiments are legible**: agents can test coordination without catastrophic downside.  
2. **Feedback compresses the search**: public signals, focal points, or institutions summarize enough state information to let agents coordinate without simulating the entire strategic future.  
3. **Errors are recoverable**: reversibility allows the system to explore.  
4. **Rule symmetry reduces branching**: fewer distinct contingent responses mean fewer paths must be effectively “computed.”

**Extreme-case insight**

At one extreme, imagine a coordination system whose future can be summarized by a simple indicator — one common clock, one public threshold, one resettable protocol. Coordination resolves quickly because the rule is computationally compressible. At the other extreme, imagine agents whose actions depend on deep, private, history-sensitive expectations about all others. Then resolution may be as hard as running the whole society forward step by step. In that limit, indefinite failure is not anomalous; it is the default.

So the key determinant is: **Does the system’s structure permit a shortcut from local observation to shared expectation, or must coordination be discovered only through unfolding interaction?** Where no shortcut exists, persistent failure is structurally normal.
