## Conjecture

Conjecture: In-group/out-group dynamics persist because they are not primarily caused by explicit tribal beliefs; they are an emergent structural feature of bounded social networks under coordination pressure. Even when a society minimizes tribalist ideology, the underlying mathematics of classification, signaling, and coalition formation still generates partitions.

Let a society be modeled as a graph \(G = (V,E)\), where individuals are nodes and social relations are edges. Each individual has limited attention, trust, and time; these are finite capacities, so edge weights cannot be uniformly high across all pairs. This creates a hard constraint: dense, high-trust subgraphs are easier to sustain locally than globally. From that alone, clustering follows.

Now add coordination. People must choose norms, language, priorities, and interpretations under uncertainty. A useful simplification is to assign each node a state vector \(x_i\) representing beliefs, styles, loyalties, and signals. Interaction rewards partial alignment and penalizes miscoordination. Then repeated local updating tends to minimize local disagreement, producing correlated regions in state space. Those regions are what we call “in-groups.” The complement appears as “out-groups,” not necessarily because of hatred, but because difference is the residual of local convergence.

The key invariant is relative distinction. If the system suppresses one axis of grouping—race, religion, class, nation—agents can still partition along another available dimension, because the structural problem remains: whom to trust, imitate, defend, and coordinate with under finite resources. The labels change; the partition function persists.

An extreme case clarifies this. Suppose a society explicitly abolishes all recognized group categories. If interaction remains sparse, local, and capacity-limited, people still sort by micro-signals: dialect, taste, credentials, moral emphasis, platform, temperament. Conversely, only in the limiting case of complete information, unlimited cognitive capacity, frictionless coordination, and perfectly symmetric interaction would stable in-/out-group structure significantly dissolve. But that is not a human society; it is a degenerate mathematical boundary case.

So anti-tribal norms act not as eliminators of grouping but as transformations on the state space: they can reduce the salience of some partitions, weaken hostility terms, and increase permeability between clusters. They do not remove the graph-theoretic and game-theoretic conditions that generate clustering in the first place.

What follows is important: the persistence of in-/out-group dynamics is less a moral failure than a structural consequence of finite agents embedded in networks. The relevant question is not “How do we eliminate grouping?” but “What system design makes group boundaries less rigid, less zero-sum, and less epistemically distorting?” The leverage lies in changing coupling structure—cross-cutting ties, repeated positive-sum interaction, institutions that reward bridge-building—so that partitions remain, but cease to harden into tribal antagonism.
