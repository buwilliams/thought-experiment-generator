## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms persist across centuries when they encode *compressible solutions to recurrent coordination problems* and are embedded in feedback structures that make local defection costly, visible, or self-correcting. Norms collapse rapidly when they are mainly supported by contingent prestige, coercion, or obsolete material conditions rather than by an enduring problem-solution fit.

From the given lens, mathematics and physics are parallel searches over structure: many formal structures are possible, but only some are selected by reality because they remain stable under empirical criticism. Social norms can be seen similarly. A culture can generate many candidate norms, but long-term survival selects those that fit durable features of human social life: conflict over status, trust under uncertainty, mating and kinship, child-rearing, reciprocity, property, punishment, sacred value protection, and group identity. The “deep fit” of some norms is therefore not mysterious. In any world with repeated interaction, bounded rationality, memory, and interdependence, certain normative structures will be rediscovered because they solve stable coordination problems.

What matters is not merely a norm’s moral rhetoric but its **structural coupling** to incentives, institutions, and cognition. A stable norm typically has several reinforcing properties:

1. **It solves a recurring problem** rather than a temporary one.  
2. **It is legible and teachable** — easy to compress into stories, rituals, taboos, or rules of thumb.  
3. **It is nested in institutions** — family, law, religion, markets, reputation systems.  
4. **It generates self-reinforcement** — conforming reproduces the conditions that make conformity advantageous.  
5. **It degrades gracefully under stress** rather than failing at a threshold.

By contrast, fast-collapsing norms often look stable only because external conditions have been holding them up. Pressure reveals that they were not solving a live problem, or were solving it inefficiently relative to emerging alternatives. Once a substitute norm appears that better fits the underlying structure — for example, new technologies of information, contraception, surveillance, mobility, or trade — the old norm can lose selection support very quickly. Its collapse is not primarily persuasion defeating error; it is a shift in the environment that changes which normative structure is viable.

An extreme-case test clarifies this: imagine infinite enforcement but zero intrinsic problem-solution fit. The norm survives only while force does. Now imagine minimal enforcement but strong fit to repeated cooperation and trust. Versions of the norm reappear even after suppression. That boundary case suggests that durability comes less from declared legitimacy than from structural necessity.

So the key distinction is between **norms that are reality-tracking social technologies** and **norms that are equilibrium artifacts of a passing arrangement**. Centuries-long stability signals that a norm has been selected across many shocks because it tracks a deep regularity of human coexistence. Rapid collapse signals that criticism from changing reality has exposed it as overfit to a vanished world.
