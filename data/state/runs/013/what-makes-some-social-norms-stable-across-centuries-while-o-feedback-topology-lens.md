## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms remain stable across centuries not mainly because people value them highly or because change is slow, but because they are embedded in dense feedback systems that continuously reproduce them across generations. Norms collapse rapidly when pressure disables or bypasses those feedback loops, especially when the norm was being upheld by a small number of bottlenecks rather than by many independent supports.

**What this perspective highlights**

The key question is not “Why did change happen so fast?” but “What structure made the norm vulnerable to fast change once pressure arrived?” A norm can look ancient and immovable while actually depending on a few institutions, incentives, or coordination points: family structure, religious authority, legal enforcement, reputation networks, schooling, marriage markets, or economic necessity. If several of these are tightly coupled, they act as reinforcing loops: conformity brings approval, approval strengthens institutions, institutions teach the norm, and deviation remains costly. Stability then is not mysterious; it is an actively maintained equilibrium.

But this also means apparent durability can be deceptive. If the norm depends on a bottleneck — say, centralized moral authority, local community surveillance, or a narrow economic structure — then removing that bottleneck can unlock multiple changes at once. The collapse appears sudden not because preferences changed overnight, but because the mechanism that converted preferences into behavior stopped functioning. People who were privately unconvinced, marginally compliant, or strategically conforming can then switch quickly, and each visible defection weakens the reputational cost for the next. A reinforcing loop flips sign.

**What follows**

Stable norms tend to have one of two properties:

1. **Redundant feedback support**: multiple institutions and incentives independently regenerate the norm. These are hard to dislodge because pressure must defeat many pathways at once.  
2. **Low dependence on external enforcement**: the norm is internalized, functionally useful, or woven into everyday coordination, so people reproduce it even when supervision weakens.

Rapidly collapsing norms often have the opposite structure:

1. **Centralized enforcement or legitimacy bottlenecks**: remove the church, censor, patriarch, guild, or state rule, and the norm loses its reproduction engine.  
2. **Hidden noncompliance**: many were following the norm only because others seemed to. Once public defection becomes legible, informational cascades accelerate collapse.  
3. **Technological or economic substitution**: a new capability bypasses the old norm’s role, making enforcement costlier and compliance less rewarding.

**Extreme-case test**

Imagine a norm supported by infinite surveillance but zero internal belief: it is stable until surveillance fails, then collapses instantly. Conversely, imagine a norm with zero enforcement but deep practical and moral integration: it can survive enormous pressure. This suggests the deepest variable is not age, nor speed of change, but whether the norm’s persistence comes from distributed learning and mutual reinforcement or from fragile dependence on a few control points.

So the collision of this lens with the problem yields: **centuries-long stability often reflects robust feedback architecture; sudden collapse reveals that what looked like tradition was sometimes only a bottleneck-maintained equilibrium.**
