## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** In-group/out-group dynamics persist in anti-tribalist societies not mainly because people consciously desire tribes, but because abolishing explicit tribal categories does not abolish the *systemic need for coordination, trust, and error-filtering*. When older group markers are delegitimized, these functions reappear through new, more fluid proxies — beliefs, tastes, moral styles, credentials, language norms, or stance toward tribalism itself.

From this perspective, the key question is not “Why are humans still tribal despite better values?” but: **What feedback loops are doing the work that tribal identities used to do, and what happens when a society tries to remove them without replacing their functions?**

Groups are not only identity containers; they are also feedback structures. They help individuals decide whom to trust, whose criticism to weight, where reputational consequences apply, and how norms are enforced. A society that explicitly rejects tribalism often tries to eliminate the *visible markers* of exclusion, but the underlying coordination problem remains. People still need fast heuristics for alliance, reliability, shared knowledge, and mutual protection. If old categories are suppressed, the selection pressure to form new classifiers remains.

This suggests the persistence is not primarily about the *speed* of social change toward anti-tribal norms, but about whether that change **removes feedback loops** that let people safely update across difference. If individuals are told not to sort by group, while institutions provide weak mechanisms for cross-group trust, shared error correction, and nonzero-sum cooperation, then hidden tribal substitutes become attractive. The result is often worse than explicit tribalism in one sense: the grouping process becomes less discussable, more moralized, and harder to correct.

An extreme case clarifies this. Imagine a society that perfectly bans all explicit tribal labels. No one may refer to ethnicity, class, religion, party, or nation. Would grouping disappear? Probably not. People would sort by increasingly indirect signals — vocabulary, humor, risk tolerance, aesthetic codes, epistemic style, institutional affiliation. This implies tribalism is not fundamentally attachment to particular categories; it is a recurrent solution to a coordination bottleneck under uncertainty.

What follows is that anti-tribal progress likely does **not** come from many small exhortations to “be less tribal” alone. That treats the phenomenon as a moral residue inside individuals. The stronger bottleneck is structural: insufficient institutions for positive-sum interaction, criticism across disagreement, and reputation that travels beyond local identity clusters. Resolve that bottleneck, and multiple symptoms may weaken at once.

So the illuminating claim is: **tribal dynamics persist because societies can reject tribalism normatively while still depending functionally on the same sorting mechanisms, merely relocated into less visible channels.** The leverage point is not denunciation of grouping per se, but building feedback-rich institutions where trust, criticism, and cooperation do not require thick in-group boundaries to begin with.
