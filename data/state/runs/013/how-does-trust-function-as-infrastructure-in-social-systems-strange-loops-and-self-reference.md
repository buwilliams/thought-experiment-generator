## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Trust functions as *recursive social infrastructure*: a strange loop in which individuals act on models of others’ models of themselves, and stable cooperation emerges when this loop closes coherently across levels — person, institution, and norm. Trust erodes when these levels cease to refer back to one another consistently, producing self-reference that becomes adversarial, performative, or opaque rather than mutually stabilizing.

More concretely: trust is not merely belief in reliability. It is a system’s capacity to sustain self-referential expectations without collapse. I trust you because I expect that you understand yourself as someone who must remain trustworthy, because you expect that I and others will update on your behavior, and because institutions encode those expectations into repeated feedback. This is infrastructure because it lowers the cost of coordination everywhere else. Like roads or protocols, it is usually invisible until failure forces local workarounds.

From the strange-loop perspective, social order depends on a hierarchy that loops:  
- **Base level:** actions and transactions  
- **Higher level:** reputations, norms, and roles  
- **Meta level:** shared beliefs about how those norms are enforced and interpreted  
When healthy, movement up and down these levels returns to the same conclusion: promises mean something here. The loop compresses uncertainty.

Erosion begins when the loop no longer closes. Three structural conditions matter:

1. **Decoupling of signal from reality.**  
If visible markers of trustworthiness — credentials, branding, moral language, compliance rituals — can be manipulated independently of trustworthy behavior, the system rewards models of trust over trust itself. Self-reference becomes hollow: actors optimize how they are seen as seen.

2. **Opacity at higher levels.**  
Where institutions become too complex for participants to model, people cannot complete the loop from action to norm to accountability and back. Trust then shifts from justified mutual legibility to superstition, tribal heuristics, or cynical disengagement.

3. **Broken feedback and asymmetry.**  
If betrayal is low-cost for powerful actors and highly costly for ordinary ones, the loop becomes one-sided. People are asked to trust institutions that do not have to trust them back. That asymmetry teaches the system to represent itself falsely, which is a deep form of infrastructural decay.

An extreme-case test clarifies this: in a society of perfect surveillance but no reciprocity, compliance may increase while trust vanishes. Conversely, in a tiny village with little formal enforcement but dense mutual legibility, trust can be high. So trust is not reducible to information or force alone. It requires *reflexive alignment* — a structure in which actors, and the system as a whole, can successfully model themselves as bound by the same reality.

Thus the leverage point is not exhortation to “rebuild trust,” but restoring loop integrity: tighter coupling between signal and behavior, intelligible institutions, reciprocal accountability, and consequences that make the system’s self-description true.
