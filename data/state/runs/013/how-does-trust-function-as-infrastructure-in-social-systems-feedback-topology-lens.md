## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** Trust functions as hidden coordination infrastructure because it compresses the need for constant verification, enforcement, and interpretation across a social system. Its erosion is dangerous not merely when it happens quickly, but when changes remove or overwhelm the feedback loops by which trust is calibrated, repaired, and locally verified.

From this lens, trust is not best understood as a sentiment but as a **systemic capacity**: a shared expectation that others, institutions, and signals are reliable enough to enable action under uncertainty. It reduces transaction costs, permits specialization, and allows people to act on representations—contracts, credentials, laws, money, testimony—without rechecking everything from first principles. In systems terms, trust is a stock accumulated through repeated successful interactions, while transparency, accountability, reciprocity, and error-correction are the flows that replenish it.

What structurally erodes trust is therefore not only deception or conflict, but the **breakdown of trust-producing feedback loops**. A system can tolerate some fraud, error, or disagreement if participants can detect failures, assign responsibility, and see credible correction. Trust collapses when those loops are severed: when actions are opaque, harms are diffused, accountability is delayed, institutions cannot visibly self-correct, or signal quality becomes too noisy to distinguish honest from dishonest actors. Under those conditions, people stop relying on shared infrastructure and revert to costly local verification, tribal heuristics, or refusal to cooperate.

This perspective also distinguishes **fast change** from **feedback-destroying change**. Rapid social or technological change is not inherently destabilizing if trust-repair mechanisms scale with it. But if speed outruns auditability, governance, norm formation, and institutional learning, then trust erodes because the system loses its ability to convert surprises into updated, shared knowledge. The danger is not acceleration itself; it is acceleration that disables criticism and repair.

At the extreme case, a society with zero trust must verify everything, enforce everything, and coordinate almost nothing beyond coercion or kinship. That reveals trust’s infrastructural role: without it, social complexity collapses into fragmentation or domination. Conversely, very high trust without criticism becomes gullibility, so durable trust requires not blind belief but **reliable mechanisms for detecting and correcting betrayal**.

This suggests that trust erosion is often driven less by many small moral failures than by a few structural bottlenecks: failures of legibility, accountability, shared reality, and institutional error-correction. Fixing these bottlenecks can restore multiple capabilities at once—cooperation, legitimacy, compliance, innovation—because trust is an upstream enabler. So the central claim is: **trust persists where systems make honesty, competence, and correction visibly testable; it erodes where those tests are blocked, delayed, or rendered meaningless.**
