## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Social norms are stable across centuries when they function as **low-cost, high-fidelity distributed algorithms** for coordinating behavior across generations; they collapse rapidly when changes in the informational environment make them **computationally inefficient, error-prone, or outcompeted by rival coordination algorithms**.

From the computation-first perspective, a norm is not just a belief or rule. It is a **replicable program** running across human minds, institutions, rituals, and incentives. Its stability depends less on whether it is “true” or “moral” than on whether it can keep being **encoded, transmitted, executed, and selected for** under changing conditions.

What follows:

1. **Stability is a property of implementation, not just endorsement.**  
   Norms persist when they are embedded in many substrates at once: family habits, law, religious practice, architecture, language, schooling, status rewards, punishment systems. This redundancy makes the norm robust, like error-correcting code replicated across multiple channels. A norm carried by only one substrate — say, elite opinion — is fragile.

2. **Centuries-long norms compress coordination problems well.**  
   A durable norm often solves a recurring social problem with relatively little cognitive overhead. It gives people a fast heuristic for trust, reciprocity, hierarchy, marriage, property, mourning, or taboo. If a norm reduces uncertainty cheaply, selection favors its retention even if it is imperfect.

3. **Rapid collapse occurs when the environment changes the payoff matrix of information processing.**  
   Technological shifts, urbanization, media, migration, state capacity, contraception, literacy, or platforms can suddenly alter who observes whom, how reputations spread, how sanctions work, and what alternatives are visible. A norm that once compressed social life efficiently may become computationally expensive or maladaptive. Then it is selected against.

4. **Pressure reveals whether the norm’s enforcement was intrinsic or scaffolding-dependent.**  
   In an extreme case, remove punishment, prestige, and shared narrative. Does the norm still reproduce? If not, its apparent moral depth was really dependence on a supporting computation stack. Stable norms survive because people can regenerate them from local information; fragile norms require continuous top-down refresh.

5. **Competing norms are competing protocols.**  
   Norm collapse is often not mere decay but replacement. A new norm wins when it processes identity, coordination, and status more effectively in the new environment. For example, a platform-mediated norm can outcompete a village norm because it scales faster, updates faster, and recruits through denser feedback loops.

So the key distinction is: **stable norms are those whose informational architecture remains adaptive across substrate shifts**, while collapsing norms are those whose replication depended on environmental conditions that no longer exist.

This illuminates why some norms look ancient yet resilient: they are not static relics but **evolutionarily selected computational routines** with strong error correction and cross-context portability. Others shatter quickly because they were only locally fit — efficient for one communication regime, one sanction structure, one reproduction system — and become obsolete when the underlying computation of society changes.
