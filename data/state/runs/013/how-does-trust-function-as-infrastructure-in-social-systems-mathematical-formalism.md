## Conjecture

Conjecture: In a social system, trust functions as a low-entropy coordination field: a distributed stock that reduces the transaction cost of prediction between agents. Structurally, trust is not merely a sentiment but an invariant-enabling relation — it allows commitments, signals, and institutions to retain meaning across time. Its erosion occurs when the system’s error between expected and observed behavior becomes persistently uncorrected, especially under conditions where feedback is delayed, noisy, asymmetric, or manipulable.

Mathematically, let agents be nodes in a graph, with trust weights \(T_{ij}\) on edges representing agent \(i\)’s confidence that agent \(j\)’s future actions will remain within an acceptable prediction interval. Social infrastructure consists not just of roads or platforms but of the network’s ability to support repeated cooperative flows. High average trust increases network conductivity: fewer checks, less redundancy, faster exchange, more long-range coordination. In this sense, trust is like a compression mechanism — it lets agents act on summaries rather than re-verify everything.

The key structural variable is not trust in isolation but the update rule for trust. Suppose \(T_{ij}(t+1)\) depends on:
1. observed reliability,
2. the speed and fidelity of information about breaches,
3. the availability of repair mechanisms,
4. the alignment between local incentives and system-level promises.

Trust remains stable when violations are both rare and corrigible. That is, the system must contain negative feedback loops that convert betrayal, error, or ambiguity into visible correction. Courts, norms, audits, reputational systems, and transparent dispute resolution all serve this function. They do not eliminate failure; they bound its propagation.

Erosion begins when positive feedback loops dominate. This happens when breach yields gain, detection is uncertain, punishment is selective, or narratives can outcompete evidence. Then mistrust is not additive but multiplicative: each local failure lowers confidence not only in one edge \(T_{ij}\) but in the trust-update process itself. Once agents can no longer trust that trust will be repaired, they retreat to costly verification, tribal clustering, or exit. The network fragments.

An invariant emerges: trust requires that promises be more predictive than noise. If the variance of institutional behavior exceeds the tolerance agents can absorb, trust cannot be sustained by exhortation. It must collapse into either coercion or high-cost monitoring.

Extreme case clarifies the structure: in a system of perfect trust, verification costs approach zero; in a system of zero trust, every interaction requires full proof, and large-scale cooperation becomes impossible. Therefore, trust is infrastructure because it occupies the middle layer between individual intention and institutional capacity: it is the hidden medium that makes social scale possible.

So the structural claim is: trust erodes not primarily from isolated dishonesty, but from breakdowns in the mechanisms that keep expectation, observation, and correction tightly coupled.
