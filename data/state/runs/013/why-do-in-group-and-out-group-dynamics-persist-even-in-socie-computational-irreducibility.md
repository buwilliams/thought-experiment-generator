## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Even when a society explicitly rejects tribalism, in-group/out-group dynamics persist because coalition-formation is a **computationally irreducible feature of social coordination**: once many agents must continuously infer trust, loyalty, norm adherence, and threat under local information constraints, no principle like “we are all universalists now” can shortcut the step-by-step generation of affiliation boundaries. Anti-tribal norms do not eliminate grouping; they become one more rule inside the process that generates new groups.

**What this perspective illuminates**

A society is not just a set of beliefs but a running computation. Individuals must decide, repeatedly: Whom do I trust? Whose testimony counts? Who will reciprocate? Who is dangerous? Who shares costly norms? In a large, changing population, these judgments cannot be globally solved in one stroke. They are produced iteratively from countless interactions. That makes boundary formation less like a removable bug and more like an emergent output of the social algorithm.

From this view, tribalism persists not primarily because people are hypocrites or insufficiently enlightened, but because **universal anti-tribal commitments are too abstract to replace the local computational work that groups perform**. Groups compress complexity. They provide heuristics for prediction: shared signals, shared expectations, shared enforcement. Even if old identities are delegitimized, agents still need workable proxies for coordination, so new markers arise—moral style, language, education, taste, institutional affiliation, even one’s stance against tribalism.

**What follows**

1. **Normative rejection of tribalism often shifts, rather than removes, group boundaries.**  
   The society may stop sorting by ethnicity or clan and start sorting by cosmopolitanism, education, moral vocabulary, or attitudes toward inclusion.

2. **The stronger the explicit anti-tribal norm, the more likely a second-order tribalism can emerge.**  
   “People who reject tribalism correctly” become an in-group; “those still trapped in tribal thinking” become the out-group.

3. **Attempts to abolish all boundary formation may fail because they fight an irreducible process at the wrong level.**  
   The relevant question is not “How do we eliminate group formation?” but “What minimal generative rules produce less destructive, more permeable, self-correcting groups?”

**Extreme case**

Imagine a society of fully sincere anti-tribal individuals with no allowed inherited identities. They still must choose collaborators, friends, institutions, sources, and norms. Unless every person has total information about everyone else—a practical impossibility—they will rely on local signals and repeated interactions. Over time, clusters form. That is, even at the limit of perfect ideological anti-tribalism, social computation still generates boundaries.

**Structural implication**

The leverage point is not preaching universality harder. It is redesigning the generative rules: lower the cost of cross-group cooperation, increase overlap among affiliations, reward reversible commitments, and build institutions where disagreement does not map cleanly onto exclusion. If tribal dynamics are computationally irreducible, then the aim is not to shortcut the computation, but to ensure that when it runs, it produces plural, cross-cutting, corrigible forms of belonging rather than rigid hostile camps.
