## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
In-group/out-group dynamics persist in anti-tribalist societies because anti-tribalism, when implemented as a social norm, becomes part of the system’s identity architecture and thereby generates new boundary-making mechanisms. The persistence is not primarily a failure of individual belief or moral consistency; it is an emergent property of any social system that must coordinate trust, norm enforcement, and collective self-description.

**What is true from this perspective:**  
A society is not just a collection of individuals with attitudes. It is a network of repeated interactions, reputational signals, sanctioning loops, and shared classifications. If the system explicitly rejects tribalism, it still needs ways to distinguish cooperators from defectors, insiders from disruptors, norm-followers from norm-violators. Those distinctions create functional boundaries even when the ideology condemns identity boundaries.

So the relevant explanatory target shifts: the question is not “Why are people still tribal despite their ideals?” but “What structural roles do group boundaries play such that they reappear under new names?” The answer is that boundaries are control mechanisms in social systems. They regulate trust, allocate attention, stabilize norms, and reduce coordination costs.

**What follows:**  
Anti-tribal norms can create a feedback loop:

1. The society declares tribalism illegitimate.  
2. Members monitor one another for tribal behavior.  
3. Accusations of tribalism become high-status moral signals.  
4. People cluster around different interpretations of what counts as “tribal,” “inclusive,” or “harmful.”  
5. These interpretive clusters become new in-groups and out-groups.

Thus, explicit rejection of tribalism does not eliminate group formation; it changes the criteria of grouping. The old tribe based on ethnicity, religion, or class may weaken, while a new tribe forms around moral language, epistemic style, political sensibility, or allegiance to anti-tribal norms themselves.

**What is illuminated:**  
The persistence is best seen as emergence from interacting feedback loops:

- **Trust loop:** cooperation requires selective trust; selective trust creates categories.  
- **Norm-enforcement loop:** enforcing inclusion requires excluding some behaviors and often some people.  
- **Identity loop:** shared opposition to tribalism becomes a positive identity marker.  
- **Status loop:** demonstrating non-tribal virtue becomes competitive, producing moral sorting.

At the extreme case, a society that tried to abolish all boundaries entirely would lose the capacity to distinguish friend from predator, contributor from saboteur, norm carrier from norm destroyer. So some boundary-making is structurally unavoidable. The issue is not whether in-groups exist, but whether their formation is corrigible, permeable, and constrained against domination.

**Therefore:**  
In-group/out-group dynamics persist because they are not merely remnants of primitive psychology; they are recurrent system-level solutions to coordination problems. Tribalism survives anti-tribal ideology by migrating from explicit inherited identities to implicit emergent ones. The deepest leverage is not preaching harder against tribalism, but redesigning institutions so that necessary boundaries are low-stakes, revisable, and less able to crystallize into enemy classes.
