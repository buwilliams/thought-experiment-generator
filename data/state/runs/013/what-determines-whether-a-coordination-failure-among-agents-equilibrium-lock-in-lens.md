## Conjecture

**Conjecture:** A coordination failure persists indefinitely when the missing action is the provision of a **diffuse, non-excludable enabling good** and the system lacks a mechanism that converts broad shared benefit into concentrated private payoff, observable commitment, or credible expectation. It resolves spontaneously when some structural change makes early contribution individually rational, even before universal cooperation appears.

This perspective distinguishes a true coordination trap from mere confusion or weak will. If the good is excludable, benefits are concentrated, or contribution is highly legible, then non-provision is often better explained by disagreement, ignorance, or low motivation. But when the good is genuinely non-excludable and its gains are spread thinly across many agents, individually rational agents can converge on a stable **no-provision equilibrium** even while all prefer the world in which the good exists. In that case persistence is not a psychological mystery; it is a structural consequence.

What determines persistence, then, is not mainly the intensity of preference for the good, but the **feedback structure** around first-mover action:

- **Weak private return to contribution** relative to cost  
- **High assurance threshold**: each agent benefits only if enough others also act  
- **Low observability** of others’ intentions or commitments  
- **No exclusion or selective reward** for contributors  
- **Diffuse blame and diffuse gain**, so no one actor internalizes the loss from failure

Under these conditions, the system can sit indefinitely in a locally stable equilibrium. Time alone does not solve it, because waiting is itself individually rational. Repeated interaction may even harden the trap by teaching agents that others also wait.

Spontaneous resolution happens when the structure changes, not merely when people “try harder.” Relevant changes include:

- A fall in the threshold needed for success  
- A rise in contributor-specific benefits  
- A credible signal that enough others will participate  
- A coordinating institution that pools contributions or mandates them  
- A new actor who internalizes enough of the diffuse benefit to act as a subsidizing first mover  
- A shock that increases the visible cost of non-provision

In extreme form, imagine infinitely many agents each receiving nearly zero marginal benefit from their own contribution to a pure public good. Then even unanimous preference for provision does not generate action. At the other extreme, if one agent captures a decisive share of the benefit, provision can occur without broad coordination. The boundary reveals the core principle: **coordination failures persist when preference for the outcome is decoupled from incentive to initiate it**.

So the key determinant is whether the system contains any bridge from collectively preferred outcome to individually rational action. If not, persistence is the default. If yes, spontaneous resolution becomes possible.
