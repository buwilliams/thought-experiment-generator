## Conjecture

**Conjecture:**  
Even societies that explicitly reject tribalism will regenerate in-group/out-group dynamics whenever social life is structured as an iterated game with unequal visibility, repeated interaction, and uncertain trustworthiness. Anti-tribal norms can suppress the *language* of tribalism, but not the strategic pressures that make group boundaries useful as coordination devices.

**Why this follows from iterated game dynamics:**  
In repeated interactions, agents benefit from identifying who is likely to reciprocate, who will defect, and who can be held accountable over time. Where direct knowledge of each person is costly or impossible, group markers become compressed proxies for expected future behavior. An “in-group” is then not primarily a sentimental tribe but a reputational network: a set of agents whose past play is more legible, whose defections are more punishable, and whose cooperation is more likely to be returned.

So even if a society ideologically rejects tribalism, the game does not become one-shot. People still face recurrent dilemmas: whom to trust, hire, defend, share with, forgive, or sanction. If the future matters, they will seek stable signals of reliability. Groups persist because they reduce uncertainty in repeated play.

**What this illuminates:**  
The persistence of in-group/out-group dynamics is less about primitive bias than about the structure of cooperation under incomplete information. Tribalism survives in softened or moralized forms because repeated games reward:
- **Reputation clustering**: trust flows through networks where behavior is observed and remembered.  
- **Reciprocity enforcement**: cooperation is easier where defectors can be excluded next round.  
- **Signal compression**: visible identities substitute for full personal knowledge when societies are large and complex.  

This predicts that explicitly anti-tribal societies often shift from ethnic or kin tribalism to ideological, professional, educational, or lifestyle tribalism. The categories change, but the repeated-game function remains.

**Extreme case:**  
In a perfectly transparent society with universal memory, accurate individual-level reputation, and low cost of cross-group enforcement, coarse group identity would lose much of its strategic value. At the opposite extreme—anonymous, high-turnover, low-accountability environments—cooperation collapses or retreats into tight local cliques. Thus, stronger anti-tribal ideals alone are insufficient; if anonymity rises, trust signals weaken, or punishment of defection becomes unreliable, people will rely more heavily on group heuristics.

**What follows:**  
If a society wants less destructive in-group/out-group dynamics, it must alter the game structure, not merely denounce tribal feeling. The leverage points are:
- improve individual-level reputation systems,
- increase cross-group repeated interaction,
- make defection visible and sanctionable across boundaries,
- lengthen the shadow of the future in shared institutions.

Otherwise, anti-tribalism becomes performative: people reject tribalism in principle while reconstructing it in whatever form best solves the recurring strategic problem of cooperation.
