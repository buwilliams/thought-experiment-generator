# Counterfactual Reasoning

## Summary

Systematically vary the assumptions underlying a problem — remove one, invert one, or replace one with its opposite — and reason through what would follow. What breaks tells you what is load-bearing. What survives tells you what is incidental.

## Full Text

Counterfactual reasoning is the practice of asking "what if this were false?" about each assumption in a problem. It is a form of sensitivity analysis applied to ideas: you are testing which premises are doing real work and which are just inherited from convention.

The method is not to speculate freely. It is to isolate one assumption, hold everything else fixed, and follow the consequences rigorously. If you remove the assumption and the problem collapses, the assumption was structural. If you remove it and nothing much changes, it was decorative — and that is itself informative.

Applied to a problem, this perspective asks:

- What assumptions are so obvious that no one has stated them? State them. Now remove each one in turn.
- What would the world look like if the key relationship in this problem ran the other direction?
- What if the thing that is treated as a constraint were instead a variable? What if the variable were a constant?
- What would need to be true for the problem to not exist at all?
- If the opposite of the proposed solution were implemented, what would fail and why?

The discipline is not to find an answer but to find which assumptions are doing the explanatory work. A good explanation survives counterfactual pressure — you cannot easily vary it without breaking it. A weak explanation dissolves when you push on it.

Counterfactual reasoning is most useful when a problem seems stuck, when consensus has crystallized around an answer, or when the framing itself feels inherited rather than chosen.
