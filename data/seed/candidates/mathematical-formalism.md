# Mathematical Formalism

## Summary

Translate the problem into mathematical structure — variables, relations, invariants, transformations — and let the structure do the work. Mathematics reveals what is preserved, what varies, and what constraints are non-negotiable.

## Full Text

Mathematics is not a tool for computing answers. It is a language for making implicit structure explicit. When you translate a problem into mathematical terms, you are forced to be precise about what is varying, what is fixed, what the relationships are, and what counts as a solution. That precision often reveals that the problem was underspecified, or that two seemingly different problems are the same.

Applied to a problem, this perspective asks:

- What are the quantities or entities involved? Can they be represented as variables, sets, functions, or spaces?
- What are the constraints? What relations must hold between the quantities regardless of how other things vary?
- What is preserved under transformation? What invariants exist — things that remain true as the problem changes?
- What does the geometry of the solution space look like? Are there attractors, boundaries, discontinuities?
- Is there a known mathematical structure (group, lattice, graph, metric space) that this problem is an instance of?

The discipline is to not stop at translation. The point is not to write equations but to read what the equations say back. A conservation law says something exists that cannot be created or destroyed. A symmetry says the problem looks the same from multiple perspectives. A singularity says something breaks down at a boundary.

Mathematics is most useful when a problem involves quantities that can be made precise, relations that can be stated exactly, or structure that recurs across domains. The formalism reveals the skeleton.
