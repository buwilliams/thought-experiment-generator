# Extreme Cases

## Summary

Push the problem to its limits — toward infinity, toward zero, toward maximum and minimum, toward the edge where normal behavior breaks down. What happens at the boundary reveals the structure that is hidden in the middle.

## Full Text

Extreme cases are where explanations break down, and where they break down tells you what they actually assumed. An explanation that only works in the comfortable middle range is not a general explanation — it is a local approximation. Pushing to the limits forces the approximation to reveal itself.

This is not about pathological edge cases for their own sake. It is about using the boundary as a probe. The behavior of a system as a variable approaches its limit — zero, infinity, a phase transition, a singularity — encodes information about the deep structure that is invisible at normal operating conditions.

Applied to a problem, this perspective asks:

- What happens as each quantity in the problem approaches its maximum or minimum possible value?
- At what point does the current explanation stop working? What breaks first?
- Is the breakdown a flaw in the explanation or a genuine feature of the phenomenon?
- What would a solution look like if it had to work at all scales, including the extremes?
- Is there a threshold, phase transition, or critical point? What is special about that boundary?

The discipline is to not dismiss the extreme case as irrelevant because it is unrealistic. The unrealistic is often where the structure is clearest. Thermodynamics at absolute zero. Physics at the speed of light. Economics with infinite agents. These extremes do not occur in practice but they reveal what the theory actually claims.

Extreme cases are most useful when a problem seems smooth and well-behaved — when pushing to the edge will reveal hidden discontinuities or assumptions.
